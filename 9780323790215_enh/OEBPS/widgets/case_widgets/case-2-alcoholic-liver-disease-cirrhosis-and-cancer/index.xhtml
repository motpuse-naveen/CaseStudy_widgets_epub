Alcoholic Liver Disease, Cirrhosis, and Cancer

Learning Objectives

  • 1. Describe the pathology of the three forms of alcoholic liver disease: fatty liver, hepatitis, and cirrhosis.
  • 2. Discuss the mechanisms of alcoholic liver damage.
  • 3. Discuss general aspects of susceptibility factors for and the clinical course of the three types of alcoholic liver damage.
  • 4. Compare and contrast the pathology of nonalcoholic fatty liver (NAFL) and nonalcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH) with alcoholic liver disease and hepatitis C infection.
  • 5. Discuss the epidemiology of and conditions associated with NAFL and NASH.
  • 6. Describe the three microscopic features that are characteristic of cirrhosis.
  • 7. Contrast the two gross morphologic variants of cirrhosis, micronodular and macronodular, and understand why such a classification scheme can be clinically misleading.
  • 8. List the major etiologic agents of cirrhosis.
  • 9. Define the term cryptogenic cirrhosis.
  • 10. Describe the clinical signs and symptoms of cirrhosis, including the causes and consequences of portal hypertension.
  • 11. List the major causes and clinical features of hepatic failure, and describe their physiologic basis.
  • 12. Define the terms hepatic encephalopathy and hepatorenal failure.
  • 13. Know the epidemiology, pathogenesis, pathology, and clinical consequences of the most common liver tumors: benign (cavernous hemangioma, focal nodular hyperplasia, and adenoma) and malignant (hepatocellular carcinoma and cholangiocarcinoma).
  • 14. List the risk factors for developing:
    • a. hepatocellular carcinoma
    • b. cholangiocarcinoma

Scenario

A 57-year-old white man presented to the emergency room with a history of increasing abdominal girth, increasing somnolence, and vomiting blood. Family members related that he had consumed alcohol for many years, drinking up to 1 liter of liquor per day. Two months prior to admission, increasing abdominal girth was noted with the need to purchase new pants. The patient had been increasingly somnolent over the previous few days, although still imbibing alcohol. On the day of admission, he vomited some blood, followed by increased confusion. His past medical history was notable for unknown prior abdominal surgical procedures, for which he received blood products.

The physical examination showed that the patient was a cachectic man who appeared older than his stated age. He had a protuberant abdomen. His blood pressure was 90/60, pulse 120, and temperature 36.8° C. He could not sit up for check of his blood pressure or pulse. His respiratory rate was 24. He was jaundiced. Small spider-like tangles of blood vessels were present over the skin of his neck and chest. Palpable breast tissue was present. Auscultation of his chest revealed a few rhonchi. The cardiac examination was unremarkable. His abdomen was protuberant with shifting dullness, and his spleen was palpable. His liver percussed to a span of 10 cm. A few large blood vessels were noted in the periumbilical area. There was bilateral pedal edema to the mid calves, which was worse on the left side. Neurologically, the patient was somnolent but arousable. He was oriented only to name. On voluntary dorsiflexion of his hands, a flapping motion was noted.

The patient was treated with intravenous fluids and blood transfusions. Blood for cultures was drawn, and an abdominal paracentesis was performed. The fluid obtained was clear and golden yellow. The laboratory reported that the fluid had a total protein of 1.7 gm/dL, an albumin of 1.0 gm/dL, and a white cell count of 180/μL with a differential count of 10% neutrophils, 10% lymphocytes, and 80% monocytes/mesothelial cells. The laboratory also reported that a gram stain of the fluid was negative. Cultures of the fluid were set up. Endoscopic examination of the patient revealed dilated blood vessels in the distal portion of the esophagus and proximal stomach area. There was some adherent blood clot overlying some of these vessels. The vessels were sclerosed by the endoscopist to control the bleeding. After the procedure, the patient was hemodynamically stable but obtunded, arousable only to deep pain. Lactulose, a nonabsorbable sugar, was administered by enema. After 2 days, the patient's mental status improved, and he signed out of the hospital against medical advice.

The patient was followed intermittently for several years; eventually, he had to be readmitted for worsening symptoms, including anorexia and weight loss. A 6-cm homogeneous liver mass was noted on a CT scan. A serum alpha-fetoprotein level was 2500 ng/mL. A biopsy of the liver mass was performed. When given the diagnosis, the patient and family refused further treatment, and the patient died after several weeks. An autopsy was requested.

At autopsy, there was ascites with 2500 mL of clear golden-yellow fluid in the peritoneal cavity and small, bilateral, clear yellow pleural effusions. Esophageal varices were present. The gold-brown liver was cirrhotic, containing one large and several smaller, yellow-green, irregular tumor masses.

Image 1

Ascites - Clinical presentation

Ascites means the collection of excess fluid in the peritoneal cavity. It is manifest clinically as a protuberant abdomen with a positive fluid wave. Also evident in this image is severe muscle wasting, or cachexia, in this individual with cirrhosis.

Question 1
1
What are the pathobiologic mechanisms for developing ascites?

Ascites is one of the consequences of cirrhosis. Ascites is caused by elevation of the portal venous pressure, which leads to increased capillary hydrostatic pressure and leakage of fluid from the vascular space; decreased production of albumin, which leads to lowering of the plasma oncotic pressure and leakage of fluid from the vascular space; sinusoidal hypertension driving fluid into hepatic lymphatics, overwhelming the capacity of thoracic duct drainage; and retention of sodium and water despite an actual increase in total body sodium due to the effects of increased antidiuretic hormone (ADH) and aldosterone levels.


Question 2
2
What is the serum-ascites albumin gradient in this patient?

In this case, serum albumin is 2.8 g/dL, and albumin in ascitic fluid is 1 g/dL. Therefore, the gradient is 1.8 g/dL. Gradients greater than 1.1 g/dL are found in ascites due to cirrhosis or other causes in which the fluid is transudative, because the albumin in ascites fluid is relatively low compared to serum albumin. Gradients of less than 1.1 g/dL are seen in ascitic fluid produced with peritoneal carcinomatosis, tuberculosis, and other forms of serositis in which the ascitic fluid is relatively rich in albumin.


Image 2

Skin, spider telangiectasia - Clinical presentation

On the neck of this individual is a small lesion containing a centrally dilated enlarged blood vessel from which several smaller vessels radiate. These lesions are called spider telangiectasias or spider angiomas.

Question 1
1
Name some other clinical stigmata noted in patients with hepatic failure.

Other stigmata include encephalopathy (associated with hyperammonemia, which impairs neuronal function and promotes cerebral edema), palmar erythema and gynecomastia (due to lack of metabolism of estrogens), coagulopathy (due to decreased production of circulating coagulant proteins), manifestations of hypoalbuminemia (due to decreased production of albumin), and hepatorenal syndrome. Cirrhosis results in both a decrease in the normal function of the liver and an alteration of intrahepatic blood flow, causing portal hypertension. There are several major clinical consequences of portal hypertension, including (1) ascites, (2) creation of portosystemic vascular shunts, and (3) congestive splenomegaly.


Figure 18-9 Major clinical consequences of portal hypertension in the setting of cirrhosis, shown for the male. In women, oligomenorrhea, amenorrhea, and sterility as a result of hypogonadism are frequent. Clinically significant findings are bold faced.


Question 2
2
What type of liver disease is indicated by the laboratory tests in this patient?

This patient had alcoholic liver disease that caused hepatocellular damage. The tests that reflect liver cell injury are elevated levels of aminotransferases, low serum albumin, prolonged prothrombin time, and conjugated hyperbilirubinemia (with bilirubinuria). Furthermore, a high AST/ALT ratio (as in our patient 190:65) is suggestive of advanced alcoholic liver disease. Hyperbilirubinemia is seen in severe alcoholic liver disease. In addition, in alcoholic hepatitis, gamma-glutamyl transpeptidase levels are markedly elevated with normal or only mildly elevated alkaline phosphatase levels. In the latter part of this patient's course, serum alpha-fetoprotein was elevated. This tumor marker, although not specific for hepatocellular carcinoma, is a useful adjunct in the diagnosis and management of liver cancer.

Question 1
1
Esophageal varices are a manifestation of what type of vascular shunt? Name some similar types of vascular shunts noted in patients with cirrhosis.

Esophageal varices are one form of portosystemic vascular shunt seen in patients with cirrhosis. Other examples of portosystemic shunts include hemorrhoids, periumbilical and abdominal wall vascular collaterals (caput medusae), and portal-to-systemic intrahepatic shunts, which contribute to the formation of congestive splenomegaly and ascites. Of these, esophageal varices are clinically the most significant because their rupture may cause massive bleeding and death.

Question 1
1
Describe the geographic and ethnic epidemiologic differences in the incidence of hepatocellular carcinoma and the reasons for these differences.

The incidence of hepatocellular carcinoma worldwide is highly associated with the incidence of hepatitis B and hepatitis C virus infections, and, to a lesser degree, chronic alcoholism and food contaminants such as aflatoxins. The incidence of hepatocellular carcinoma varies from less than 5 cases per 100,000 in areas such as North America, northern and central Europe, and Australia to rates of up to 150 cases per 100,000 in Mozambique, other parts of Africa, and parts of China and Korea, areas of highest seroprevalence for hepatitis B virus infection. Within a given geographic region, the incidence of hepatocellular carcinoma varies markedly among different ethnic groups. The incidence is also greater in men; there is a man-to-woman ratio of 1.5 to 1 in areas of low frequency, and a ratio as high as 3:1 in high-incidence areas.

Question 1
1
In areas of the world where the prevalence of hepatitis B virus infection is high, vertical transmission of the infection from pregnant mothers to their unborn children occurs frequently. What implications does this have regarding the epidemiology of hepatocellular carcinoma in these regions?

In high-incidence regions, the hepatitis B virus carrier state begins in infancy, following vertical transmission of the virus from infected mothers, thus conferring up to a 200-fold greater risk of developing hepatocellular carcinoma by adulthood. In these high-incidence areas, cancer may occur in a non-cirrhotic liver, and it occurs at an earlier age, between 20 and 40 years. In countries where hepatitis B virus infection is not endemic, the association of cancer with cirrhosis is more prevalent, cirrhosis being present in 80% to 90% of cases of hepatocellular carcinoma.

Question 1
1
While cirrhosis is an important contributor to the development of hepatocellular carcinoma, there are certain other diseases that are strongly associated with development of this primary hepatic malignancy. What are they?

Risk factors for developing hepatocellular carcinoma include (1) hepatitis B or hepatitis C virus infections, (2) cirrhosis (cirrhosis from alcoholic liver disease, nonalcoholic fatty liver disease [NASH], and hepatitis C are common in the United States), (3) aflatoxin exposure, and (4) Thorotrast exposure (a radiographic dye used from the 1930s through 1950s).


Question 2
2
What genetic factors are implicated in hepatocellular carcinoma?

There is an increased risk of hepatocellular carcinoma in patients with hemochromatosis, alpha-1 antitrypsin deficiency, and Wilson disease. Additionally, p53 and beta-catenin genes are mutated in 60% and 40% of cases, respectively. The IL-6/JAK/STAT signaling pathway has also been implicated in the pathogenesis of hepatocellular carcinoma.

Image 8

Liver, alcoholic hepatic steatosis (fatty liver) - Medium power

In alcoholic fatty liver, lipid accumulates within the cytoplasm of hepatocytes, creating large clear vacuoles within cells. The nuclei in such cells are compressed to the periphery of the cell. Hepatic steatosis may be associated with slight hepatomegaly and elevations of serum bilirubin and alkaline phosphatase.

Question 1
1
If an individual like the individual from whom this image was made were to completely abstain from drinking alcohol in the future, what would happen to his or her liver?

Alcohol withdrawal and institution of a good diet are adequate treatment, and if an individual abstains from further alcohol ingestion, the histology of the liver and the laboratory tests should return to normal.

Image 9

Trichrome stain

Liver, alcoholic hepatitis - Low power

At low magnification, hepatic steatosis is noted, especially in centrilobular regions, in this case of alcoholic steatohepatitis. The lobular architecture is hard to recognize at this magnification. There is both a chronic and an acute inflammatory infiltrate within hepatic lobules. A connective tissue stain (trichrome stain) highlights the degree of fibrosis that accompanies alcoholic steatohepatitis. Sinusoidal, perivenular, and portal fibrosis shows up as blue-stained areas. With continued injury, fibrous bands bridge between central veins and portal areas and between adjacent portal areas, eventually resulting in alcoholic cirrhosis. Hepatocytes, many showing macrovesicular steatosis, stain red.

Question 1
1
What are the pathobiologic mechanisms responsible for alcoholic liver damage?

Hepatic steatosis from alcohol is thought to result from shunting of substrates away from catabolism within the liver toward lipid biosynthesis, owing to excess Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NADH) production by alcohol and acetaldehyde dehydrogenase, impaired production and secretion of lipoproteins by hepatocytes, and increased catabolism of fat throughout the body. Other detrimental effects of alcohol include: (1) induction of cytochrome P-450, leading to increased biotransformation of certain drugs to toxic metabolites; (2) microsomal oxidation of alcohol with formation of free radicals that react with cell membranes and proteins; (3) direct impairment of microtubular and mitochondrial function and membrane fluidity; (4) conversion of alcohol to acetaldehyde, the major metabolite of alcohol, which induces lipid peroxidation and impairs microtubular function; (5) induction of immunologic destruction, possibly due to alcoholor acetaldehyde-induced alterations of hepatic proteins; and (6) decrease in intrahepatic glutathione, sensitizing the liver to oxidative injury.


Question 2
2
What are the clinical and laboratory changes of alcoholic hepatitis? Would these changes be reversible if this patient were to completely abstain from alcohol in the future?

Alcoholic hepatitis usually appears after a drinking binge and runs the spectrum from asymptomatic to symptomatic with liver failure. Tender hepatomegaly accompanies hyperbilirubinemia and elevated alkaline phosphatase, aminotransferases, and gamma-glutamyl transpeptidase, as well as neutrophilic leukocytosis and occasionally fever. The presence of fibrosis in the liver usually implies irreversible injury. However, further damage and progression to cirrhosis can be avoided if individuals abstain from alcohol ingestion and consume a nutritious diet.

Liver, alcoholic hepatitis - Medium power

Inflammation, hepatic steatosis, and Mallory bodies are present in alcoholic steatohepatitis. Mallory bodies are eosinophilic ropelike inclusions within the cytoplasm of hepatocytes. Ultrastructurally, they consist of tangles of filaments containing cytokeratin and other proteins.

Question 1
1
Are Mallory bodies pathognomonic for alcoholic hepatitis?

Mallory bodies are not pathognomonic for alcoholic liver disease. They can also be present in other liver diseases, including primary biliary cirrhosis, Wilson disease, chronic cholestatic syndromes, and hepatocellular carcinoma.

Severe steatosis and the presence of “chicken wire”–type fibrosis around the central vein are uncharacteristic of hepatitis C infection but are present in steatohepatitis from other etiologies; for example, alcoholic steatohepatitis or nonalcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH).

Question 1
1
What conditions are associated with nonalcoholic fatty liver (NAFL) disease?

Other pathologic features seen in alcoholic and nonalcoholic hepatitis are lobular inflammation, ballooning degeneration and apoptosis, and Mallory bodies, which in cases of NASH are smaller and less conspicuous. Obesity and diabetes mellitus are conditions associated with the development of NAFL and NASH. They are a frequent concomitant cause of liver disease in patients with hepatitis C virus infection or other liver disorders. NASH can also occur independent of other liver disease. It is estimated that one quarter of the U.S. population is affected by this disorder.

Question 1
1
What are some causes of cirrhosis of the liver other than chronic viral hepatitis and chronic alcoholic liver disease?

The etiology of cirrhosis varies, both geographically and socially. In the United States, alcoholic liver disease accounts for well over 50% of the cases. However, other important causes include viral hepatitis (HBV and especially HCV), biliary tract disease, primary hemochromatosis, alpha1-antitrypsin deficiency, and Wilson disease. A significant percentage (10%-15%) of cases are idiopathic (so-called cryptogenic cirrhosis).

Question 1
1
What amount of alcohol consumption is required to develop alcoholic cirrhosis?

Cirrhosis occurs in alcoholics who consume large amounts of alcohol per day over a long period, typically 10 to 20 years. It does not occur in persons who drink small amounts of alcohol occasionally. It should be noted, however, that only 10% to 15% of those at risk develop cirrhosis. Individual, perhaps genetic, susceptibility must exist, but the details are unclear at this point.

Question 1
1
Can you name some other tumors of the liver?

Cavernous hemangiomas are the most common benign liver tumor. Other benign liver tumors include focal nodular hyperplasia, liver cell adenomas, and bile duct adenomas. Hepatocellular carcinoma accounts for approximately 80% to 90% of all primary liver carcinomas. The next most common form of primary liver carcinoma is cholangiocarcinoma, which arises from the bile ducts. Angiosarcoma is a rare, particularly aggressive, malignant mesenchymal tumor of the liver associated with exposure to vinyl chloride, arsenic, and Thorotrast.

Focal nodular hyperplasia of the liver appears as a well-demarcated tan or brown mass within the liver. A very distinctive gross feature of focal nodular hyperplasia is a central stellate fibrous scar within the mass.

Question 1
1
Does focal nodular hyperplasia have any predilection for gender?

Both men and women develop focal nodular hyperplasia. However, the lesion is somewhat more common in women.

Liver cell adenoma

Liver, hepatocellular adenoma - Gross

Characteristically, liver cell adenomas are subcapsular, tan-yellow, often bile-stained, well-demarcated tumors within the liver.

Question 1
1
In what setting are hepatocellular adenomas seen?

They tend to occur in women who use oral contraceptives. They regress if the oral contraceptive is discontinued.


Question 2
2
What mutations are commonly found in hepatocellular adenomas?

HNF-1, beta-catenin, and gp130 mutations are commonly seen in hepatocellular adenomas.

Question 1
1
What are the risk factors associated with cholangiocarcinoma?

Recognized causal influences for developing cholangiocarcinoma are (1) chronic liver fluke infections of the biliary tract, (2) exposure to Thorotrast, (3) primary sclerosing cholangitis, and (4) congenital fibropolycystic diseases of the biliary system. However, most cases of cholangiocarcinoma arise in the absence of any antecedent recognized risk.
