Congenital Malformations

Learning Objectives

  • 1. Define and compare the features of malformation, deformation, disruption, sequence, and syndrome.
  • 2. Know the overall incidence of congenital malformations, and list some of the known causes. Explain how the impact of an insult varies during different developmental periods.
  • 3. List five common birth defects seen in the United States.
  • 4. List the clinical features and major causes of the oligohydramnios sequence.
  • 5. Define agenesis, hypoplasia, and developmental dysplasia.
  • 6. Describe the pathogenesis of immune hydrops and list some clinical manifestations. Describe the clinical intervention often used to prevent this disorder, and explain its mechanism of action.
  • 7. List several causes of nonimmune hydrops fetalis.

Scenario

A 28-year-old woman, estimated to be 39 weeks pregnant by her last menstrual period, presents to the emergency department in active labor. She has been followed by the high-risk obstetric service for the last 2 months because a fetal ultrasound examination had shown oligohydramnios (diminished amniotic fluid) and very small fetal kidneys. Her pregnancy had been otherwise unremarkable, and she denied drug, alcohol, or tobacco use. She had two healthy children at home.

A 2800-g female infant is born 5 hours later by vaginal delivery. Her size is appropriate for the gestational age, and she has Apgar scores of 8 at 1 minute and 8 at 5 minutes. In the delivery room, it is noted that she has an unusual appearance with low-set ears, a flattened nasal profile, inner canthal folds beneath her eyes, and a talipes equinovarus (clubfoot) deformity of the left foot. Several minutes after birth, she develops grunting and chest retractions. She remains dusky even with the administration of oxygen. She is intubated and transferred to the neonatal intensive care unit. A chest radiograph shows bilateral pneumothoraces, and chest tubes are placed. Despite aggressive ventilatory management, she dies 2 hours after birth. Autopsy reveals severe pulmonary hypoplasia and bilateral multicystic dysplasia of the kidneys.

This infant shows the typical facial abnormalities caused by compression secondary to oligohydramnios. Question 1
1
Are abnormal facial features an example of a malformation, a deformation, or a disruption?
Show answer

The abnormal facial features are examples of deformations.


Question 2
2
What are some common causes of oligohydramnios?
Show answer

Amniotic fluid leak, uteroplacental insufficiency, bilateral renal agenesis, and cystic renal dysplasia are common causes of oligohydramnios.

Oligohydramnios sequence, clubfoot deformity - Clinical presentation

This infant has talipes equinovarus (a clubfoot deformity) of her left leg. This deformation results from fetal compression secondary to insufficient amniotic fluid. Other limb defects may be seen, including bowed legs and large, flattened hands.

Question 1
1
Why is a breech presentation seen more commonly with oligohydramnios?
Show answer

The lack of amniotic fluid inhibits the baby's movements, restricting its ability to rotate to a vertex position before delivery.

Pulmonary hypoplasia from the oligohydramnios sequence - Gross

This thoracic organ block from the autopsy examination shows severe lung hypoplasia. The lungs are only about 30% of the size they should be for an infant of this size. Respiratory insufficiency resulting from pulmonary hypoplasia is the most common cause of death associated with the oligohydramnios sequence.

Question 1
1
Why does pulmonary hypoplasia occur with oligohydramnios?
Show answer

It is thought that lung development is stimulated by the lung expansion that occurs from in-utero respiratory movements. A lack of amniotic fluid limits the amount of fluid available to expand the lungs, and limits expansion of the thoracic cage.

Question 1
1
Why do renal abnormalities lead to oligohydramnios?
Show answer

Fetal urine is the main source of amniotic fluid in the latter part of pregnancy. Renal diseases that impair urine production lead to oligohydramnios.

Question 1
1
Describe the pathogenesis of this disorder.
Show answer

Immune hydrops occurs when the red blood cell antigens of the mother are incompatible with those of her fetus, and she mounts an antibody-mediated immune response that crosses the placenta and leads to fetal hemolysis.


Figure 10-10 Pathogenesis of immune hydrops fetalis (see text).


Question 2
2
Is this likely to have been the first pregnancy for this mother?
Show answer

Immune hydrops owing to Rh incompatibility occurs after an Rh­negative mother has been sensitized to Rh-positive blood and has generated anti-Rh antibodies. Usually the sensitization occurs in a previous pregnancy, and first pregnancies are rarely affected by this condition.


Question 3
3
What is the usual preventive measure taken to avoid this condition?
Show answer

Rh-negative mothers are usually administered Rh0(D) immune globulin during each pregnancy and after delivery to eliminate maternal sensitization to Rh-positive red blood cells. Although the exact mechanism of action of Rh0(D) immune globulin is not proven, antibody coating of the fetal cells and blocking of maternal immune responses likely are involved.

Neonate, immune hydrops - Autopsy presentation

Autopsy examination of this infant revealed massive ascites and large pleural effusions. The liver was markedly enlarged, and the lungs were hypoplastic.

Question 1
1
What is the pathogenesis of ascites in immune hydrops?
Show answer

Ascites and generalized edema result from high-output circulatory failure from the anemia and impaired hepatic synthetic function, creating a decreased oncotic pressure.


Question 2
2
Why is the liver enlarged?
Show answer

There is increased extramedullary hematopoiesis to compensate for the hemolytic process. Congestion may also contribute.

Brain, kernicterus - Gross, horizontal section

This section of medulla and cerebellum shows yellow discoloration of several nuclei, including the inferior olives and the dentate. Kernicterus, or bilirubin encephalopathy, occurs in infants with marked hyperbilirubinemia. The immature blood–brain barrier of the neonate allows passage of unconjugated bilirubin, which binds to the lipids in the brain, causing injury.

Question 1
1
What is the source of the hyperbilirubinemia in immune hydrops?
Show answer

The increased bilirubin comes from the hemoglobin that is released from the hemolyzed red blood cells.


Image 12

Fetus, hydrops fetalis in Turner syndrome - Clinical/autopsy presentation

Hydrops fetalis is not a specific disease, but is the condition of generalized tissue edema and serous cavity effusions in a fetus in utero. It has many etiologies in addition to immune hydrops. It is a typical finding in some cases of Turner syndrome (monosomy X), wherein the fetuses often have prominent edema of the distal extremities and of the trunk. There are also large edematous regions around the neck, termed cystic hygroma, as seen here. Much of the fluid accumulation in this condition is thought to be due to developmental obstructions of the lymphatic system. Congenital heart disease, which is also a common finding in this disorder, may contribute to the hydrops.

Question 1
1
Contrast immune and nonimmune hydrops fetalis.
Show answer

Hydrops fetalis occurring from blood group incompatibility is called immune hydrops fetalis, and the other conditions are called nonimmune hydrops fetalis. The etiologies of nonimmune hydrops include infections, structural defects, arrhythmias, and chromosomal abnormalities. Twenty percent of the fatal cases of fetal hydrops are idiopathic.


Image 13

Fetus, hydrops fetalis in parvovirus infection - Clinical/autopsy presentation

This fetus developed hydrops after a congenital infection with parvovirus B19 acquired hematogenously from the mother. Parvovirus preferentially infects and destroys erythroid precursors, leading to anemia.

Infectious Disease Case 3, Image 8 (congenital parvovirus infection)

Question 1
1
What are the clinical manifestations of parvovirus infection in older children?
Show answer

Children develop erythema infectiosum (fifth disease) from parvovirus infection, characterized by a slapped-cheek facial rash, headache, mild upper respiratory tract symptoms, and fever. It is a benign, self-limited disease. Otherwise healthy adults usually have an asymptomatic infection, but if they do manifest symptoms, they may also have arthralgias and arthritis. Patients with an underlying hematologic disorder, such as sickle cell anemia, may develop a transient aplastic crisis, and immunocompromised patients may develop acute or chronic anemia.

With parvovirus infection, the placenta is typically enlarged and edematous. Microscopically, the placental villi show hydropic change. Increased numbers of circulating nucleated red blood cells are visible in the fetal circulation, some of which may contain parvovirus inclusions.

Image 15

Parvovirus inclusion

Liver, parvovirus infection – High power

This fetal liver shows a parvovirus inclusion within the nucleus of an erythroid cell. The liver is often enlarged in this condition because of increased hematopoiesis to compensate for the hemolysis.

Image 16

Fetuses, hydrops fetalis in twin–twin transfusion syndrome - Gross

In this condition, blood from one twin shunts to the other via a vascular communication in the placenta. The recipient twin develops polycythemia and possibly hydrops fetalis, while the donor is smaller and anemic. There is usually polyhydramnios in the amniotic sac of the recipient twin. In this image, the twin on the left shows changes of hydrops.
