Learning Objectives

  • 1. Define and use in the proper context:
    • a. lymphoma
    • b. leukemia
    • c. mixed tumor
  • 2. Understand the concept of the cancer stem cell and how it relates to tumor cell heterogeneity and response to therapy.
  • 3. Understand the concepts of dominant oncogenes and proto-oncogenes.
  • 4. Review the principles of signal transduction in cells. Be familiar with the different classes of growth factors, growth factor receptors, signal-transducing molecules, and nuclear regulatory factors.
  • 5. Compare and contrast the different mechanisms of oncogene activation (point mutations, overexpression, and gene amplification).
  • 6. Describe the use of cytogenetics to identify gene rearrangements, and understand its drawbacks and limitations.
  • 7. Understand the two types of changes in gene structure and expression associated with chromosomal translocations, and give examples of each.
  • 8. Describe the use of molecular diagnostic techniques (FISH and PCR) to identify gene rearrangements, and understand their drawbacks and limitations.
  • 9. Understand the normal functions of the RAS, ABL, MYC, β-catenin, and cyclin D1 gene products; the types of genetic alterations found in their genes; and the types of neoplasms that carry alterations in these genes.
  • 10. Discuss the role of cytogenetics in tumor diagnosis (tumor specificity of particular translocations).
  • 11. Discuss the role of cytogenetics in the distinction between tumor relapse and the occurrence of a second primary tumor.
  • 12. List the types of structural chromosome changes seen in tumors (deletions, translocations, double minute chromosomes) and the oncogenic mechanisms associated with each type (loss of tumor suppressor gene, oncogene activation, gene amplification). Give an example of each and its associated tumor or tumors.
  • 13. Understand the cellular origin of a tumor is a stem cell.
  • 14. Understand the concept of tumor clonality and its usefulness in diagnosis.
  • 15. Compare and contrast the kinetics of tumor growth with the kinetics of tumor cell cycle progression, and understand how this relates to tumor growth fraction.
  • 16. Understand the therapeutic implications of growth fraction in tumors.
  • 17. Understand the concept that tumorigenesis is a multistep process involving the accumulation of genetic alterations.
  • 18. Understand the relationship between tumor progression and cellular heterogeneity within a tumor, as well as the implications in terms of treatment and metastatic potential.
  • 19. List some examples of special markers used to diagnose cancer by immunohistochemistry and flow cytometry.
  • 20. Discuss the use of molecular analysis of immunoglobulin and T-cell receptor gene rearrangements to distinguish cancer versus reactive or other proliferative processes.
  • 21. Discuss the use of molecular diagnostic testing in the setting of cancer diagnosis, prognosis, minimal residual disease evaluation, and diagnosis of hereditary predisposition.

Scenario

A 44-year-old white woman presented with a 2-month history of fatigue and weight loss and with a 2-week history of low-grade fever, early satiety, and left upper quadrant pain. On physical examination, she had no palpable lymphadenopathy, but there was evidence of splenomegaly. Blood cytology revealed a high WBC count of 225,000/mm3, consisting mostly of myeloid lineage cells in different stages of maturation, which included granulocytes, myelocytes, myeloblasts, basophils, and eosinophils. Cytogenetic analysis of spontaneously dividing peripheral blood WBCs revealed the presence of a chromosomal translocation t(9;22), generating the Philadelphia chromosome. RT-PCR analysis indicated the presence of BCR/ABL fusion mRNA transcripts.

The patient was started on imatinib mesylate (Gleevec). Blood cytology done 1 month after the initiation of therapy revealed that the WBC count had returned to the normal range.

Second presentation (4 years later)

The patient presents with a 1-month history of bone pain, weight loss, night sweats, fever, and early satiety. Physical examination reveals the reappearance of massive splenomegaly and this time includes lymphadenopathy as well. Blood cytology reveals a predominance of lymphoblasts. Flow cytometric analysis shows that most peripheral blood mononuclear cells have the cell surface marker characteristics of B lymphoblasts (CD10+ [CALLA], κ−, λ−, TdT+, CD2−, CD7−, CD19+).

RT-PCR analysis indicates the presence of BCR/ABL fusion mRNA transcripts. Cytogenetic and FISH evaluations of bone marrow at the second presentation show the t(9;22) translocation again, as well as additional chromosomal abnormalities typically seen in the blast (acute) phase of chronic myeloid leukemia.

What are some causes of leukocytosis?
Show answer

Leukocytosis may result from reactive (non-neoplastic) proliferation or from neoplastic proliferation (leukemia). There are many causes of reactive leukocytosis, including infections and tissue injury.

Blood smear, initial presentation of chronic myeloid leukemia (CML) - High power

Under high power, myeloid (granulocytic) cells at various stages of maturation can be seen. The cell with a large nucleus, prominent nucleoli, and scant cytoplasm is a myeloblast. Cells with smaller nuclei and numerous cytoplasmic granules are myelocytes. Metamyelocytes have a notched nucleus. A young neutrophil is also seen. The presence of immature cells of the granulocytic series in the peripheral blood is highly abnormal. It results from extensive proliferation and failure of differentiation of precursor cells in the bone marrow, which subsequently spill over into the blood, and is characteristic of CML.

Question 1
1
What genetic alteration is associated with CML?
Show answer

The Philadelphia chromosome [t(9;22)] is the cytogenetic abnormality associated with this disorder. The molecular consequence is translocation of the ABL oncogene on chromosome 9 to the BCR gene on chromosome 22. This leads to constitutive kinase activity.


Question 2
2
What would the karyotype look like?
Show answer

The karyotype abnormality in CML is t(9;22)(q34;q11.2), a translocation between chromosomes 9 and 22. The hybrid chromosome 22 is called the Philadelphia chromosome. The fusion gene is formed by this translocation.


Question 3
3
How is the BCR-ABL fusion protein targeted therapeutically?
Show answer

Understanding of the pathophysiology of CML led to the development of one of the first “targeted therapies,” in which a drug is targeting a particular molecular mechanism of disease. Because targeted therapy aims to impact only the molecular abnormalities and not the normal tissues of the body, there is more specificity to the treatment and often fewer side effects. An analogy would be trying to shoot a target with a missile versus a sniper rifle. Both may hit the target, but the missile will cause more collateral damage. With CML, patients are treated with inhibitors of the BCR-ABL kinase. Eventually, many patients develop mutations that confer resistance to the drug. Second- and third-generation kinase inhibitors that represent separate non-mutated epitopes are then used.


Targeted Therapy Box – Tyrosine Kinase Inhibitor (TKI): A Technical Knockout (TKO) for Myeloproliferative Disorders

Tyrosine Kinase Inhibitor (TKI) Therapy: A Technical Knockout (TKO) for Myeloproliferative Disorders

Mutated, hyperactive tyrosine kinases underlie tumor cell growth and survival in myeloproliferative disorders (MPDs). Recognition of that paradigm has spurred the development of therapeutic agents that can selectively inhibit these rogue enzymes. As a class, these tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs) offer promise as exquisitely specific agents that can reign in unfettered cellular proliferation while leaving normal cellular turnover virtually untouched. The best-known, imatinib, is now the mainstay of therapy for patients with chronic myeloid leukemia (CML).

CML is associated with constitutively active tyrosine kinases. These arise as a consequence of the balanced (9;22) chromosomal translocation that forms the Philadelphia chromosome—fusing genetic fragments from the BCR locus and the ABL oncogene. Basic research spawned out of that fundamental cytogenetic observation led to the deciphering of the structure of the BCR-ABL fusion gene and the detection of the kinase activity of the BCR-ABL protein. Subsequent demonstration that BCR-ABL causes leukemia when expressed in the bone marrow cells of mice identified a possible therapeutic bull’s-eye; the screening of small molecule inhibitors for the unbridled tyrosine kinase activity of the BCR-ABL fusion protein led to the discovery of imatinib, now widely used in the treatment of CML. As a bit of serendipity, in addition to inhibiting the BCR-ABL fusion protein, imatinib also blocks other tyrosine kinases such as platelet-derived growth factor receptor-α (PDGFRα), PDGFRβ, and c-KIT. As a consequence, it also is effective in treating certain other rare MPDs and gastrointestinal stromal tumors (GISTs) associated with activating mutations in those kinase proteins.

The success of TKI therapy for CML forged a new paradigm—and raised hopes—for developing specifically targeted cancer therapies and stimulating new efforts to use TKIs to treat a variety of hematologic malignancies. Clinical trials of novel JAK2 and FLT3 kinase inhibitors are currently under way in patients with primary myelofibrosis and refractory forms of acute myeloid leukemia, respectively, and additional trials are in the planning stage for other myeloid and lymphoid cancers.

Of course, there’s also a cautionary note. Cancer cells are obdurate; with time and opportunity, they tend to acquire additional changes in their mutant tyrosine kinases that render them enzymatically active but insensitive to the TKI. Thus, despite excellent remission and 5-year survival rates, TKI therapy alone fails to completely eliminate CML in the vast majority of patients. Additionally, 20% to 30% of patients eventually develop resistance to the first-line imatinib. Fortunately, medical science is ahead of the game: The new mutations are somewhat predictable, and second-generation TKIs to block those are already available (i.e., dasatinib and nilotinib).



Image 3

Abnormal chromosomes

Philadelphia chromosome, positive chronic myeloid leukemia (CML) - Initial karyotype.

Karyotypic analysis of spontaneously dividing white blood cells from this patient reveals a translocation in every dividing cell in which a piece of the long arm of one chromosome #9 and a piece of the long arm of one chromosome #22 have translocated (exchanged places): t(9;22)(q34;q11.2). The hybrid chromosome #22 is called the Philadelphia chromosome, often abbreviated Ph (or Ph1 in older literature). With this patient’s blood smear, it is diagnostic of CML. No other chromosomal abnormalities are seen in the karyotype pictured.

Question 1
1
How frequently is this translocation seen in CML?
Show answer

More than 95% of cases of CML have this translocation. However, the cases lacking cytogenetic evidence of a Ph chromosome possess an occult BCR/ABL translocation. The BCR/ABL rearrangement, rather than the cytogenetically evident translocation, is the necessary genetic event in CML.

Image 5

Nucleoli

Blood smear, blast phase of chronic myeloid leukemia - High power

The leukocytes are large with high nuclear-to-cytoplasmic ratios and prominent nucleoli. These are all blast cells (i.e., very immature cells) with lymphoid morphology. This appearance is very different from that seen before the patient went into the blast (or acute) phase of the disease.

Question 1
1
How might one determine if this lymphoid malignancy is related to the myeloid malignancy identified in the initial presentation?
Show answer

Even though these are precursor B lymphoid cells, rather than granulocytes, they still possess the Philadelphia chromosome. In addition, they generally show rearrangement of their immunoglobulin heavy chain genes.



Image 6

Chronic myeloid leukemia, blast phase - Flow cytometric analysis

A peripheral blood sample from the patient was stained with antibodies to the CD19, CD10, and immunoglobulin kappa and lambda light chain cell-surface molecules. In a separate sample, the cells were made permeable and stained with antibodies to intracellular CD22 and terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase (TdT). The predominant population, depicted in red, corresponds to the malignant population of cells.

Question 1
1
Based on this staining pattern, what type of cell is now the predominant population?
Show answer

The cells express the pan B-cell markers CD19 and CD22. They also express CD10 and TdT, however, which are found in immature B cells; TdT is not seen in mature B cells. The cells do not express either the kappa or lambda light chain on their surface (i.e., they are surface immunoglobulin negative). This composite of markers is characteristic of immature B cells (pre–B cells).


Question 2
2
How can one determine if a proliferation of B cells is clonal in origin?
Show answer

This can be determined by testing for the presence of a unique immunoglobulin gene rearrangement in the cells.


Question 3
3
What type of cell is depicted in black?
Show answer

The cells depicted in black are CD19+, CD22+, CD10−, TdT−, and lambda or kappa chain positive. This is the phenotype of mature B cells.


Image 7

Chronic myeloid leukemia (CML), blast phase - Karyotype

Karyotypic analysis of peripheral blood mononuclear cells from the second presentation reveals that although the cells have a lymphoid morphology, they still contain the t(9;22) translocation of CML. In addition, they have acquired several new chromosomal abnormalities: extra copies of chromosomes #8, #10, and #19, and a second copy of the abnormal chromosome #22 (the Philadelphia chromosome).

Question 1
1
What is the significance of this Philadelphia chromosome–positive lymphoid leukemia?
Show answer

It implies that the pluripotent stem cell that is transformed in CML has the capacity to differentiate into not only myeloid cells but also lymphoid cells.


Question 2
2
What is the significance of the additional chromosomal abnormalities seen in the second presentation?
Show answer

The tumor cells have accumulated additional genetic changes, and in the process, the disease has become more aggressive (acute leukemia). The efficacy of kinase inhibitors is thought to partially result from decreasing the proliferative drive of these tumors that lead to the accumulation of more aggressive genetic changes.


Image 8

BCR-ABL fusion gene detection by fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH)

The DNA probe for ABL has a red fluorescent tag, and the BCR probe is labeled green. When the two probes are in close proximity, as occurs in the t(9;22) translocation, a yellow color is detected. Pairing of sister chromatids during metaphase may show a single dot or a pair of closely spaced dots.

Question 1
1
The cells from the original chronic myeloid leukemia and the lymphoid blast phase carry the same genetic alteration; what is the nature of the cell type that was originally transformed?
Show answer

This implies that the malignant clone of myeloid cells and the malignant clone of lymphoid cells seen in blast phase arose from the same cell—a pluripotent stem cell.


Image 9


Amplified BCR/ABL fusion transcripts (mRNA-derived cDNA) - PCR products

The polymerase chain reaction (PCR) is a procedure for amplifying specific nucleic acid segments from small amounts of material. In this case, the presence of the BCR/ABL translocation can be detected by using one amplification primer located in exon 9 of the BCR gene and the other located in exon 3 of the ABL gene (see Image 8). Because the regions recognized by each primer have to be on the same cDNA and close to each other for amplification to occur, a PCR product is generated only if the sample contains the BCR/ABL rearrangement. In real-time PCR, the product is detected at the end of each PCR cycle with a fluorescently labeled probe. No product is generated from the negative control, which is a cell line without the BCR-ABL translocation, or from the control lacking RNA. The positive control is a cell line with the BCR-ABL translocation. A strong signal is seen in the patient sample.
