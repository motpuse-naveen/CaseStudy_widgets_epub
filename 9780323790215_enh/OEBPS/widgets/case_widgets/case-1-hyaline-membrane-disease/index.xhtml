Scenario

A newborn girl was delivered at 28 weeks’ gestation (calculated by mother's last menstrual period) to a 25-year-old woman with a history of pregnancy-induced hypertension and continued heavy tobacco use. The mother came to the clinic complaining of cramping. She had a fever of 39°C. The fetal heartbeat was irregular, indicative of fetal distress. Because of concerns about fetal well-being, the delivery was induced. The infant was delivered 4 hours later and weighed 600 g; the Apgar scores were 4 at 1 minute and 7 at 5 minutes. The infant appeared long and thin with a relatively enlarged head size. By measurements, the body weight and crown-heel length were consistent with 25 weeks’ gestational age, and the head circumference was consistent with 28 weeks’ gestation. The gestation was estimated by clinical maturity studies to be 28 weeks.

Sternal retractions were noted, indicating respiratory difficulties, and artificial surfactant was given. The respiratory status did not improve, and the infant was placed on a ventilator. Blood for cultures was drawn, and the infant was begun on antibiotics. Over the course of the next day, increasing ventilatory support was required. The chest radiograph is shown in Image 1. Blood cultures were positive for group B streptococcus. On the second day of life, seizures developed. A head ultrasound examination diagnosed a severe intraventricular hemorrhage. After a discussion with the parents, it was decided that heroic measures would not be instituted, and the infant died at 32 hours of age. An autopsy was done.

Question 1
1
What are the risk factors for hyaline membrane disease?
Show answer

Hyaline membrane disease is basically a disease of premature infants. Approximately 60% of infants born before 28 weeks' gestation, 30% of those born between 28 and 34 weeks' gestation, and less than 5% of those born after 34 weeks' gestation develop this condition. Other risk factors are diabetes in the mother, prenatal asphyxia, and brain injury.


Question 2
2
Would this infant be considered appropriate for gestational age?
Show answer

This infant is small for gestational age, because her birth weight is below the 10th percentile for her estimated age of 28 weeks' gestation. Her intrauterine growth retardation may have resulted from diminished placental blood flow from the maternal pregnancy-induced hypertension coupled with maternal cigarette smoking. This infant shows asymmetric growth retardation with relative sparing of the growth of the brain and head relative to the rest of the body. This asymmetric growth retardation is seen in cases of growth retardation secondary to maternal or placental factors or both. Many fetal causes of intrauterine symmetric growth retardation, such as chromosomal abnormalities and congenital infections, lead to a proportionately small infant.

Question 1
1
What is the pathogenesis of these changes?
Show answer

The fundamental defect is the inability of the immature lung to produce surfactant. Surfactant is essential to reduce surface tension within the alveoli, facilitating their expansion. If surfactant is inadequate, the alveoli collapse. Atelectasis leads to hypoxia, causing epithelial and endothelial injury, resulting in leakage of protein-rich and fibrin-rich exudates into the alveolar spaces. These exudates, together with necrotic cellular debris, form hyaline membranes.

Question 2
2
Which cell type produces surfactant?
Show answer

Type II pneumocytes product surfactant. Production of surfactant greatly increases after 35 weeks' gestation.


Question 3
3
How can hyaline membrane disease be avoided?
Show answer

Reduction of the incidence of hyaline membrane disease can be achieved in two ways. First, attempts are made to delay labor until the lungs become more mature. This approach may be limited by the clinical circumstances. The other strategy is to give the patient glucocorticoids; this induces surfactant synthesis and decreases the risk of respiratory distress syndrome.

Question 1
1
How do hyaline membranes form?
Show answer

Hyaline membranes form from proteinaceous debris, necrotic epithelial cells, and fibrin accumulating in alveolar spaces.


Question 2
2
Are neutrophils prevalent in all cases of hyaline membrane disease?
Show answer

Neutrophils are usually inconspicuous in hyaline membrane disease without concurrent pneumonia.

Question 1
1
In this case, what pathogenetic sequence of events resulted in fetal infection?
Show answer

The pathogenetic sequence likely began with an ascending infection by group B streptococcus, which had presumably colonized the mother's genital tract. The organisms gained access to the amniotic fluid after passing through the placental membranes. The fetus swallowed or aspirated bacteria, resulting in pneumonia and, eventually, sepsis. The infant's difficulty breathing was related to prematurity complicated by pneumonia. Hypoxia secondary to pulmonary disease contributed to the intraventricular hemorrhage.

Question 1
1
What is the pathogenesis of bronchopulmonary dysplasia (BPD)?
Show answer

BPD results from prolonged assisted ventilation at high oxygen concentrations. Oxygen toxicity and mechanical ventilation damage the developing lung, leading to poor alveolar development.


Question 2
2
How have the histologic findings in BPD changed in recent years?
Show answer

Classic BPD histologic findings, including airway epithelial hyperplasia, thickened alveolar walls, and interstitial fibrosis, are no longer seen with improved clinical management. The major histologic finding in new BPD cases is a decreased number of alveoli, also called alveolar hypoplasia, which is thought to result from arrested lung development at the saccular stage.

Question 1
1
What are the clinical sequelae of this disorder?
Show answer

The clinical sequelae include reactive airways disease, exercise intolerance, risk of recurrent infection, and cor pulmonale.


Question 2
2
What is the relationship between hyaline membrane disease and bronchopulmonary dysplasia?
Show answer

Newborns with hyaline membrane disease are treated with high concentrations of ventilator-administered oxygen for long periods of time. This is the major underlying cause of bronchopulmonary dysplasia. Thankfully, the incidence has decreased with gentler administration of oxygen and glucocorticoid therapy.

Question 1
1
What is the relationship between hyaline membrane disease and retinopathy of prematurity?
Show answer

Newborns with hyaline membrane disease are treated with high concentrations of ventilator-administered oxygen for long periods of time. This is the major underlying cause of retinopathy of prematurity, similarly to bronchopulmonary dysplasia.


Question 2
2
What is the pathogenesis of this disorder?
Show answer

Retinopathy of prematurity has a two-phase pathogenesis. First, the hyperoxic phase occurs during the time the patient is on high levels of oxygen. These high oxygen levels greatly decrease levels of vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), leading to apoptosis of endothelial cells. Once the patient returns to room air, there is a spike in VEGF levels that induces neovascularization similar to diabetic retinopathy. Contraction of the neovascular membrane may create enough traction to cause retinal detachment. The use of VEGF inhibitors is under investigation.
