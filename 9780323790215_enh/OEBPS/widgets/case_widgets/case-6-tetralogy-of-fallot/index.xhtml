Tetralogy of Fallot

Learning Objectives

  • 1. Define congenital heart disease.
  • 2. Know the major clinical consequences of left-to-right and right-to-left shunts.
  • 3. Understand the pathophysiology and pathogenesis of pulmonary hypertension in the setting of congenital heart disease.
  • 4. Understand the morphology and pathophysiology of:
    • a. three diseases with left-to-right shunts:
      • 1) atrial septal defect
      • 2) ventricular septal defect
      • 3) patent ductus arteriosus
    • b. two diseases with right-to-left shunts (cyanosis present at birth):
      • 1) tetralogy of Fallot
      • 2) transposition of the great arteries
    • c. two examples of obstructive anomalies:
      • 1) coarctation of the aorta
      • 2) aortic stenosis and atresia

Scenario

A 1-day-old girl needs evaluation because she turns blue when crying. The infant was born after uncomplicated full-term gestation and delivery to a G1, P1 mother with no family history of heart disease. The infant appeared well developed but faintly dusky just after birth. A more intense cyanosis was noted a short while later when the infant began to cry. A systolic murmur was audible. The lungs sounded clear, and the remainder of the physical examination was normal. A chest film showed no cardiac enlargement. The pulmonary vascular markings were diminished. An ECG showed right ventricular hypertrophy. An echocardiogram showed a large ventricular septal defect, a large aorta, and very small pulmonary arteries. There was a severe pressure gradient across the pulmonic valve. Pressures in the right and left ventricles were equal, and no gradient was measured across the septal defect. The infant was started on prostaglandin E1 infusion and a repeat echocardiogram confirmed prior observations. A palliative surgical procedure was performed, and the patient did well postoperatively. At 8 months of age, open-heart surgical repair of the cardiac defects was carried out without complications.

Heart, tetralogy of Fallot (TOF) - Schematic

TOF is a constellation of four anatomic features: (1) ventricular septal defect (VSD), (2) overriding aorta, (3) obstruction to right ventricular outflow, and (4) right ventricular hypertrophy. TOF is the most common form of cyanotic congenital heart disease. This graphic shows a TOF heart with severe pulmonic stenosis and patent ductus arteriosus.

Question 1
1
What are the key anatomic features that determine the severity of the clinical symptoms in TOF?
Show answer

The degree of right ventricular outflow obstruction and the size of the VSD are the key anatomic features that determine the severity of the clinical symptoms. When the pulmonic stenosis is mild and the VSD is large, the physiology is similar to that of VSD. The patient may have left-to-right shunt and no cyanosis. Many of these patients live decades with few symptoms. The more severe the pulmonic stenosis, the more resistance in the pulmonary circuit. If pulmonary resistance exceeds systemic resistance, a right-to-left shunt occurs, and the patient has cyanosis. With extreme degrees of pulmonic stenosis/pulmonic atresia, pulmonary blood flow occurs by retrograde flow through the ductus arteriosus or through bronchial collaterals. In these patients, it is important to keep the ductus open.

Question 2
2
What is a paradoxical embolus, and in what conditions can it occur?
Show answer

The lung normally acts as a filter for thromboemboli arising in the peripheral venous circulation, preventing them from reaching the systemic circulation and causing infarcts. In the presence of a VSD with a right-to-left shunt, such emboli may bypass the lung to enter the systemic circulation and produce a cerebral infarct (e.g., stroke). This is termed a paradoxical embolus and is an important complication of TOF, most commonly seen in young children. Older children may develop cerebral abscesses from a similar mechanism because bacteria fail to be filtered out by the pulmonary vascular bed.

Heart, transposition of the great vessels - Gross, longitudinal section, cut surface

Transposition is actually a highly diverse group of malformations that have in common abnormal connections between the ventricles and the outflow tracts. In the present case, the aorta is connected to the right ventricle, and the pulmonary artery is connected to the left ventricle. Oxygenated blood in the pulmonary circuit mixes with unoxygenated blood in the systemic circuit through the ductus arteriosus, ventricular septal defects, atrial septal defects, or other non-cardiac shunts.

Question 1
1
Is transposition of the great vessels without an accompanying shunt compatible with life?
Show answer

No. Without maintaining a shunt, there is a complete separation of systemic and pulmonary circulations. Oxygenated blood from the lungs would return to the left atrium, flow to the left ventricle, and be pumped by the incorrectly attached pulmonary artery back to the lungs. The deoxygenated blood returning to the heart would simply be pumped back out to the body from the right ventricle by the incorrectly attached aorta. Therefore, there is no useful oxygenation of blood, which is incompatible with life. A shunt is necessary to mix the oxygenated blood into the systemic circulation.


Question 2
2
In a patient with transposition and a shunt, in which direction is the blood being shunted?
Show answer

There is a shunt from the pulmonary circulation to the systemic circulation, which allows oxygenated “red” blood from the lungs to mix with the deoxygenated “blue” blood returning from the systemic circulation, resulting in partially oxygenated “purple” blood and subsequent cyanosis. This shunt can occur via a patent ductus arteriosus or a ventricular septal defect, and prostaglandins are administered to maintain the patent ductus arteriosus in early life. However, surgical intervention is necessary to support long-term cardiac function.

Question 1
1
What simple procedure during a physical examination could uncover this defect?
Show answer

Look for cyanosis in the lower extremities. Blood reaching the lower extremities in patients with pre-ductal coarctation is poorly oxygenated blood shunted through the ductus arteriosus.

This image shows the hyperplasia of smooth muscle and fibrous tissue in an aortic coarctation. The hyperplasia obstructs blood flow into the descending aorta. If the coarctation is severe, the infant is dependent on blood flowing through the patent ductus arteriosus to support perfusion to the lower extremities.

Question 1
1
Do patients with coarctation present with cyanosis?
Show answer

In severe coarctation, obstruction of flow to the descending aorta means that perfusion to the lower extremities is supported by flow through the patent ductus arteriosus. Because this is poorly oxygenated blood coming from the right side of the heart (a right-to-left shunt), the infant develops cyanosis of the lower extremities.
