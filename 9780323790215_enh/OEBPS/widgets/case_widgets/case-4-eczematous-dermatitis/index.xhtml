Spongiotic Dermatitis

  • 2. Compare and contrast the histologic aspects of these conditions.
  • 3. Describe the characteristic clinical features of the different forms of spongiotic dermatitis.
  • 4. Describe the pathogenesis of atopic dermatitis, explaining the formation of the characteristic morphologic features, including the presence of the different cell types.
  • 5. Describe atopic dermatitis as a type of hypersensitivity reaction.
  • 6. Describe the genetic components, if present, in the different forms of spongiotic dermatitis.

Urticaria

  • 3. Describe the clinical features of urticaria.
  • 4. Know the generalized disease of which urticaria may be an expression, and know the prognosis of this particular disorder.

Scenario

A 2-year-old girl is seen by the pediatrician because of redness, scaling, and crusting of the skin of the elbows, hands, and legs. The parents tell the pediatrician that the lesions started 1 week ago with papules and blisters that were very itchy. They also relate that this is not the first time that their child has had these symptoms. Nearly a year ago, she had identical abnormalities, which were examined by a family physician. Now this family physician has advised them to see a pediatrician to confirm the possibility of atopic dermatitis. The mother relates that as a child she had identical lesions. The father does not recall such abnormalities during his childhood. Laboratory investigations of the child show a slightly elevated level of IgE in her plasma and slightly decreased numbers of suppressor T cells. The parents have read about atopic dermatitis on the Internet and learned that affected children are prone to develop bacterial and viral infections that sometimes are life-threatening; this is the main reason why they wanted to see a specialist. Numerous tests are performed to determine the triggers of hypersensitivity reactions in this patient.

The skin of this child displays numerous patches and plaques that are red, scaly, and not very well-defined. These findings are consistent with an eczematous dermatitis. In this child, atopic dermatitis is the most likely cause.

Question 1
1
Describe the different stages of the development of atopic dermatitis.
Show answer

In the early stages of atopic dermatitis, there is a prominent perivascular lymphocytic infiltrate with dermal edema. The most notable changes are in the epidermis in the form of spongiosis (intracellular edema). In the later stages of atopic dermatitis, the epidermis thickens (acanthosis), and hyperkeratosis develops. These histologic features are common to all forms of spongiotic dermatitis. The term spongiotic dermatitis describes a histologic picture that is shared by several distinct clinical entities, including atopic dermatitis, seborrheic dermatitis, nummular eczema, and primary irritant dermatitis.

Question 1
1
Describe the changes in spongiosis.
Show answer

Spongiosis is the accumulation of edema fluid within the intercellular spaces of the epidermis. This change may be seen with any form of acute spongiotic dermatitis.

Question 1
1
Which category of hypersensitivity reactions does atopic dermatitis fall under?
Show answer

Atopic dermatitis is a type IV hypersensitivity reaction.

Question 2
1
Describe the pathogenesis of atopic dermatitis.
Show answer

Antigens at the epidermal surface are presented by Langerhans cells to naïve CD4 T cells. The T cells are activated and develop into effector and memory cells. On reexposure to this antigen, the memory T cells migrate to the site of the antigenic exposure and release cytokines. These cytokines are responsible for the influx of inflammatory cells and subsequent spongiotic dermatitis.

This is a typical example of chronic atopic dermatitis. The antecubital fossa (inner surface of the elbow) shows lichenification (thickening with accentuated skin markings), owing to scratching.

Question 1
1
What would you expect to see histologically in this area of lichenification?
Show answer

A biopsy specimen of the area of lichenification would show a thickened epidermis with hyperkeratosis. Unless there is a superimposed acute process, spongiosis and an inflammatory infiltrate would be absent.

Question 1
1
Does the biopsy specimen confirm what you expected to see?
Show answer

Yes. When any form of spongiotic dermatitis exists over a very long time, the epidermis thickens, and the rete ridges become elongated. This epidermal pattern is called psoriasiform hyperplasia because of the superficial resemblance to the histologic picture seen in psoriasis.

Skin, urticaria – Clinical presentation

This picture shows two wheals, pruritic edematous plaques, surrounded by a slightly erythematous zone characteristic of urticaria. In most patients, the underlying cause cannot be identified. In this patient, the cause was low temperature. The patient has a cold urticaria, here provoked by ice cubes to confirm the diagnosis.

Question 1
1
What are other causes of urticaria?
Show answer

Other causes of urticaria include heat, foods, pollens, pressure, drugs, and insect venoms.


Image 8
Widening of spaces between collagen bundles
Eosinophils

Skin, urticaria – High power

The collagen bundles in the dermis are separated by clear spaces. Eosinophils are present between the collagen bundles. In contrast to atopic dermatitis and other forms of spongiotic dermatitis, the epidermis is relatively spared in urticaria in that no epidermal spongiosis or thickening is generally present.

Question 1
1
In what way is the widening of spaces between the collagen clinically visible?
Show answer

The widening of spaces between the collagen is clinically visible as edema.
