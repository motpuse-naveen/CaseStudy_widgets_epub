Lymphoid Neoplasia I

Learning Objectives

Lymphoid Neoplasia

  • 1. Distinguish between the terms lymphoma and leukemia.
  • 2. Become familiar with the commonly encountered forms of lymphoid neoplasia.

Precursor B-Cell and T-Cell Neoplasms

  • 1. Describe the clinical and hematologic features of acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL). Describe the differences between the B-cell and T-cell types.
  • 2. Understand the immunophenotypic classification of ALL in relation to normal lymphoid maturation.
  • 3. Describe the disease features of ALL that correlate with prognosis.

Peripheral B-Cell Neoplasms - Aggressive Subtypes

  • 1. Diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL):
    • a. Describe the typical clinical features of DLBCL.
    • b. Understand the natural history of DLBCL, the impact of therapy, and the correlation between disease stage and survival.
    • c. Understand the morphologic and genetic heterogeneity seen in this disease.
  • 2. Burkitt lymphoma
    • a. Describe the main demographic subtypes of Burkitt lymphoma, including differences in clinical presentation and association with Epstein-Barr virus.
    • b. Describe the genetic lesions associated with all forms of Burkitt lymphoma.
    • c. Describe the typical morphologic features of this tumor that allow it to be discriminated from other forms of aggressive B-cell neoplasia.
    • d. Understand the stage of lymphoid maturation of Burkitt lymphoma compared to that of lymphoblastic lymphoma.
  • 3. Multiple myeloma (MM)
    • a. Define the proliferating cell, primary anatomic site of involvement, associated serum abnormalities, and natural history of MM.
    • b. Compare and contrast MM and Waldenstrom macroglobulinemia with respect to production of Ig, distribution of lesions, and clinical presentation.
    • c. Compare and contrast MM and monoclonal gammopathies of unknown significance.

Question 1
1
Are these mononuclear cells blasts or reactive lymphocytes?

They are blasts.


Question 2
2
What differentiates lymphoblasts from mature (including reactive) lymphocytes?

Lymphoblasts express terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase (TdT). This enzyme is important in the process of antigen receptor gene rearrangement and is not expressed by mature lymphocytes. In addition, mature B cells express immunoglobulin on their surfaces; lymphoblasts do not.


Question 3
3
How would you describe the cellularity and the cellular composition of the marrow biopsy specimen?

They would be described as hypercellular (packed) and monomorphous.


Question 4
4
How can the leukemic blasts of ALL be distinguished from the myeloid blasts of acute myeloid leukemia (AML)?

Myeloblasts may be distinguished from ALL lymphoblasts on the basis of myeloperoxidase expression. In addition, ALL expresses either B- or T-lymphoid antigens, whereas myeloblasts express myeloid antigens such as CD13, CD14, and CD33.

Question 5
5
What prognostic features have been identified in ALL?

Good prognostic features include age between 2 and 10 years; low white count; early pre-B phenotype; t(12;21); hyperdiploidy. Bad prognostic features include age under 2 or older than 10 years; t(4;11) and other rearrangements involving the MLL gene on chromosome 11; and peripheral blast count greater than 100,000.

Question 7
7
Does this patient have a leukemic process or a T-cell lymphoma?

Both. The patient could be said to have T-cell ALL or lymphoblastic lymphoma.


Question 8
8
Based on the clinical features of such patients, is it important to distinguish between these two processes?

No. Patients diagnosed with T-cell ALL or lymphoblastic lymphoma have essentially the same disease.


Question 9
9
In what other situation does this sort of distinction become nonessential?

It is nonessential in chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL) and small lymphocytic lymphoma (SLL).

Diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL) - High power

DLBCL is a molecularly heterogeneous disease, but it is the most common non-Hodgkin lymphoma. The proliferating cells of all DLBCL are large cells (nuclei larger than histiocyte or endothelial cell nuclei) and have cleared-out chromatin with distinct nucleoli. Beyond that, they are remarkably heterogeneous in regard to nuclear contours, overall size, amount of cytoplasm, pleomorphism, and size of nucleoli, as illustrated in these images. Immunophenotypically, these are mature lymphocytes of B lineage. A common presentation in these lymphomas is an enlarging mass, often located in an extranodal site such as the gastrointestinal tract. DLBCL is an aggressive tumor; however, with intensive chemotherapy complete remission is achieved in 60% to 80% of patients.

Question 1
1
Do these tumors show any translocations?

About 20% of these tumors have a t(14;18) translocation similar to the one seen in follicular lymphomas. About 30% of large cell lymphomas show rearrangement of the BCL6 gene in chromosome band 3q27. MYC rearrangements typically seen in Burkitt lymphoma are present in approximately 5% of cases and portend a worse prognosis.

Small bowel, Burkitt lymphoma - Gross

Classically, two major forms of Burkitt lymphoma have been recognized, African (endemic) and sporadic. More recently, this tumor has been increasingly recognized in the setting of immunodeficiency states, particularly HIV infection. In the African form of the disease, the tumor frequently manifests as a rapidly enlarging mandibular mass, and commonly involves abdominal organs such as the kidney, ovaries, and adrenal glands. In sporadic forms (including in the United States), the tumor usually manifests as an ileocecal mass, as shown here. Both forms are seen most commonly in children or young adults. Burkitt lymphoma represents 2% of non-Hodgkin lymphoma

Question 1
1
What virus is associated with Burkitt lymphoma?

Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) is associated with Burkitt lymphoma.


Question 2
2
How does this association differ among the three types of Burkitt lymphoma?

EBV viral sequences are seen in essentially 100% of endemic Burkitt lymphomas. In contrast, EBV is present in 25% of HIV-related and 15% to 20% of sporadic cases.

Burkitt lymphoma - High power

Burkitt lymphomas are composed of medium-sized cells that are smaller than the cells of diffuse large B-cell lymphoma (DLBCL). Burkitt lymphoma displays a diffuse histologic pattern and a high mitotic index, a feature associated with a high cell death rate. This increased death rate triggers a tissue macrophage response, which creates a so-called “starry sky” appearance. Although this starry sky resemblance is typical of Burkitt lymphoma, it is not diagnostic. Burkitt lymphoma cells also have characteristic morphology in air-dried Wright-Giemsa–stained smears (i.e., the cells have deeply basophilic cytoplasm with lipid-containing vacuoles). Burkitt lymphoma, similar to DLBCL and lymphoblastic processes, behaves aggressively. However, because of its high proliferative rate, it responds well to chemotherapy and lasting remission is achieved in 80% to 90% of cases.

Question 1
1
What translocation is characteristic of these lymphomas?

The translocation t(8;14) is characteristic. Related translocations are t(2;8) and t(8;22).


Question 2
2
What is the molecular consequence of translocation?

All involve juxtaposition of the MYC gene on chromosome 8 and one of the immunoglobulin genes (kappa, heavy chain, and lambda genes on chromosomes 2, 14, and 22). This juxtaposition puts MYC under transcriptional control of the immunoglobulin gene regulatory elements. Because immunoglobulin genes are constitutively expressed in B cells, this causes up-regulation of MYC expression.


Question 3
3
How commonly does Burkitt lymphoma manifest in leukemic form?

Burkitt lymphoma rarely manifests in leukemic form. Leukemic Burkitt lymphoma was previously known as ALL, L3 type. Because it is a tumor of mature B cells, however, it is clearly distinct from ALL despite similar clinical behavior.

Bone marrow smear, skull radiograph, serum protein electrophoresis, multiple myeloma (MM)

MM is a neoplastic proliferation of plasma cells. Although not generally considered a lymphoma because of its unique clinical presentation, it does represent an aggressive tumor of clonal B-lineage cells (in this case, terminally differentiated cells). The plasma cells in MM typically proliferate in the marrow, often causing destruction of bony structures. This proliferation explains the common presenting complaint of bone pain in these patients and the typical—although not diagnostic—punched-out defects seen radiologically. Lymphadenopathy or other mass disease is rare. MM is one of a group of disorders known as plasma cell dyscrasias, which are pathologic processes of the immunoglobulin-secreting cells. MM exhibits a typical monoclonal proliferation of plasma cells, which, in the case of MM, often secrete an abnormal monoclonal immunoglobulin referred to as an M (as in monoclonal) component (also called paraprotein). These immunoglobulins may be identified by serum (or urine) electrophoresis as a monoclonal spike.

Question 1
1
Is the presence of a monoclonal spike diagnostic of MM?

No. A monoclonal spike is not diagnostic of MM.


Question 2
2
Can a monoclonal spike be found in asymptomatic adults?

Yes. A monoclonal spike can be found in patients with monoclonal gammopathy of undetermined significance (MGUS). Patients with solitary plasmacytomas and certain lymphoproliferative disorders can have a monoclonal spike as well.


Figure 13-18 M protein detection in multiple myeloma. Serum protein electrophoresis (SP) is used to screen for a monoclonal immunoglobulin (M protein). Polyclonal IgG in normal serum ( arrow ) appears as a broad band; in contrast, serum from a patient with multiple myeloma contains a single sharp protein band (arrowhead ) in this region of the electropherogram. The suspected monoclonal Ig is confirmed and characterized by immunofixation. In this procedure, proteins separated by electrophoresis within a gel are reacted with specific antisera. After extensive washing, proteins that are cross-linked by antisera are retained and detected with a protein stain. Note the sharp band in the patient serum is cross-linked by antisera specific for IgG heavy chain (G) and kappa light chain ( κ ), indicating the presence of an IgG κM protein. Levels of polyclonal IgG, IgA (A), and lambda light chain ( λ ) are also decreased in the patient serum relative to normal, a finding typical of multiple myeloma. (Courtesy Dr. David Sacks, Department of Pathology, Brigham and Women’s Hospital, Boston, Mass.)


Question 3
3
What are Bence Jones proteins?

Bence Jones proteins are free light chains of the immunoglobulins.


Question 4
4
How does MM produce morbidity?

Other than the pain and fractures produced by the lytic plasma cell lesions, MM produces its deleterious effects in three ways. The first, similar to other hematopoietic neoplasms, is replacement of marrow, resulting in cytopenias. The second is by the pathologic effects of the excess monoclonal immunoglobulin, including renal failure from the toxic effects of Bence Jones proteins (and deposition of amyloid derived from the M protein). Finally, decreased production of normal immunoglobulins predisposes to bacterial infections.
