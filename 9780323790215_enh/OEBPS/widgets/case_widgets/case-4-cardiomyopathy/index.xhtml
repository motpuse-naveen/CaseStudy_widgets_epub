Scenario

The Cook County Medical Examiner's office calls to inform you that they are investigating the sudden death of one of your patients. The patient in question was a 23-year-old man recently evaluated in your office after an episode of syncope that occurred shortly after a funeral for a 21-year-old cousin who had also died suddenly. Your patient's episode of syncope was associated with transient mild dyspnea on exertion and palpitations. There was no associated chest pain. The patient denied prior medical illness. He had never been told he had a heart murmur, and there was nothing in his history to suggest prior acute rheumatic fever. Physical examination revealed a forceful systolic heave and an apical precordial impulse displaced far to the left on the chest wall. The chest radiograph showed cardiomegaly with slight atrial enlargement. An ECG confirmed the presence of left ventricular hypertrophy. His liver enzymes were slightly elevated, but serum markers of cardiac injury (enzymes, troponin) were negative for myocardial infarction. The patient had been referred to a cardiologist for an echocardiogram to rule out hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, valvular heart disease, or coarctation of the aorta.

The medical examiner relates that the patient collapsed suddenly at work while unloading a truck. Extended attempts to revive him were unsuccessful, and he was pronounced dead at the hospital a few hours later.

Question 1
1
Describe the physiology of hypertrophic cardiomyopathy. What would have been found if an echocardiogram had been done?
Show answer

The physiology of hypertrophic cardiomyopathy is that of a muscular ventricle that is difficult to fill. Ejection is forceful but ineffective because the amount of blood in the ventricle is so small. Echocardiogram often shows systolic anterior motion of the mitral valve or other dynamic obstruction to left ventricular outflow.

Myocardial fiber disarray is often seen in hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, particularly in the myocytes of the interventricular septum. Instead of being arranged in parallel arrays, the myocytes appear to be going in many different directions. The individual fibers branch wildly in many different directions.

Question 1
1
What is the pathogenetic basis of hypertrophic cardiomyopathy?
Show answer

Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy can result from over 400 different mutations in at least 9 genes coding for proteins that are integral to the function of the sarcomere. The most commonly mutated genes are β-myosin heavy chain, cardiac TnT, α-tropomysin, and myosin-binding protein C. Most inheritance in autosomal dominant.

Figure 12-31 Causes and consequences of dilated and hypertrophic cardiomyopathy. Some dilated cardiomyopathies and virtually all hypertrophic cardiomyopathies are genetic in origin. The genetic causes of dilated cardiomyopathy involve mutations in any of a wide range of genes. They encode proteins predominantly of the cytoskeleton, but also the sarcomere, mitochondria, and nuclear envelope. In contrast, all of the mutated genes that cause hypertrophic cardiomyopathy encode proteins of the sarcomere. Although these two forms of cardiomyopathy differ greatly in subcellular basis and morphologic phenotypes, they share a common set of clinical complications. LV, left ventricle.


Question 2
2
Are there other types of cardiomyopathy? What must the clinician do before the term cardiomyopathy is applied?
Show answer

The three main categories of cardiomyopathy are hypertrophic, dilated, and restrictive. Before the term cardiomyopathy can be applied, the clinician must rule out cardiac disease secondary to hypertension, coronary artery disease, valvular heart disease, or congenital malformations of the heart.

Dilated cardiomyopathy leads primarily to systolic dysfunction, whereas restrictive and hypertrophic cardiomyopathies result in diastolic dysfunction. Question 1

What does the heart look like in dilated cardiomyopathy? What is the physiology of dilated cardiomyopathy?
Show answer

The heart in dilated cardiomyopathy is large but dilated and flabby. The physiology of dilated cardiomyopathy is that of ineffective contraction. Patients present with progressive congestive heart failure symptoms.


Question 2
2
What is the pathophysiology of dilated cardiomyopathy?
Show answer

Dilated cardiomyopathy may result from a wide range of genetic and non-genetic causes. Genetic causes result from mutations in a wide variety of cytoskeletal, sarcolemmal, and nuclear envelope proteins. Non-genetic causes include myocarditis, alcohol abuse, cobalt toxicity, peripartum cardiomyopathy, and others.

Figure 12-30 Schematic of a myocyte, showing key proteins mutated in dilated cardiomyopathy (red labels), hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (blue labels), or both (green labels). Mutations in titin (the largest known human protein at approximately 30,000 amino acids) account for approximately 20% of all dilated cardiomyopathy. Titin spans the sarcomere and connects the Z and M bands thereby limiting the passive range of motion of the sarcomere as it is stretched. Titin also functions like a molecular spring, with domains that unfold when the protein is stretched and refold when the tension is removed, thereby impacting the passive elasticity of striated muscle.

Targeted Therapy Box – Peripartum Cardiomyopathy: Proscribing Prolactin Promotes Promise

Peripartum Cardiomyopathy: Proscribing Prolactin Promotes Promise

Pregnancy can be problematic in so many ways. Beyond deep vein thromboses, molar pregnancies, eclampsia, and amniotic fluid embolization, peripartum cardiomyopathy (PPCM) occurs with a frequency of roughly 1 in 3000 full-term gestations (higher in some ethnic groups); the outcome can be a severely dilated ventricle with a markedly diminished ejection fraction and congestive heart failure. Treating the heart failure of PPCM during pregnancy is tricky because many of the standard mainstays—warfarin (to minimize secondary thromboses) and angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors or receptor blockers (for afterload reduction)—are teratogenic. Although a majority of women eventually recover normal left ventricular function, a third or more experience a permanent deficit; there is also an overall 5% to 15% mortality rate, with another 5% to 10% of the patients eventually requiring cardiac transplantation.

The pathophysiology of PPCM remains something of a mystery, so prevention and definitive treatment are still evolving:

  • During pregnancy, left ventricular myocardium normally undergoes reversible hypertrophy to accommodate physiologic increases in blood volume and cardiac output; in some cases, this is postulated to drive decompensation of a previously injured and marginally compensated heart. Delivery of the fetus should be followed by complete recovery in the vast majority of such cases—but this does not always occur.
  • Viral myocarditis also can occur during or after pregnancy and can be worse with the relative immunosuppression accompanying gestation. This pathomechanism probably underlies a minority of cases, however, and steroid trials have not shown broad efficacy.
  • Another theory holds that immune responses to circulating fetal cells that insinuate themselves into maternal myocardium (chimerism) are responsible. Unfortunately, therapy with intravenous immune globulin (to dampen immune responses) or pentoxifylline (to attenuate cytokine production) generally has not proved effective.

Enter new data and a therapeutic target. Prolactin is a pituitary hormone that is markedly upregulated after delivery; it is the hormone responsible for inducing lactation and uterine remodeling. In addition to a number of other properties, the full-length 23-kD protein also protects endothelial cells and promotes angiogenesis. Of note, however, a proteolytically cleaved 16-kD fragment of prolactin has precisely the opposite set of effects, causing endothelial cell apoptosis and disrupting capillary vasculature. Indeed, in animal models, increased production of the 16-kD peptide led to a pronounced dilated cardiomyopathy. Bringing the story full circle, increased oxidative stresses seen in PPCM patients are associated with increased proteolysis of the prolactin precursor and elevated circulating levels of the 16-kD molecule.

All of the foregoing suggests that blocking prolactin degradation or reducing its production can ameliorate PPCM. Fortunately, there is already a relatively safe drug on the market that does the latter: Bromocryptine is a dopamine agonist used to reduce prolactin synthesis in the setting of pituitary tumors that cause hyperprolactinemia. Even better, in recent clinical trials in women with PPCM, left ventricular functional recovery was significantly improved with bromocryptine treatment over that with standard heart failure therapy. Although suppression of lactation is a downside to the therapy (with potential physiologic and psychological ramifications), preventing permanent heart failure due to PPCM may be more than a tolerable trade-off.

Question 1
1
Define myocarditis, and list some common etiologic agents.
Show answer

Myocarditis is characterized by inflammation and the consequent degeneration and necrosis of myocardial fibers. The most common causes in North America are viruses, especially influenza and enteroviruses such as coxsackievirus and echovirus. The most important cause in South America is Chagas disease. In addition, myocarditis can also be caused by immune-mediated reactions (e.g., rheumatic fever, drug hypersensitivities) or unknown causes (e.g., giant cell myocarditis).

Cardiac amyloidosis may be a part of generalized amyloidosis such as AA type or AL type. TARGETED THERAPY BOX – AMYLOID: BREAKING UP IS HARD TO DO

Amyloid: Breaking Up Is Hard to Do

Approximately 15 biochemically distinct proteins have the capacity to fold into relatively insoluble β-pleated sheets to produce morphologically identical-looking amyloid fibrils. There are three major forms of amyloid:AL (immunoglobulin light chains), AA (a protein derived from SAA, an acute-phase reactant made in the liver), and Aβ amyloid, found in the cerebral plaques of Alzheimer disease. All forms are associated with a glycoprotein (the P component) that is essential for fibril deposition. Some of these unfolded molecules are directly toxic; thus, immunoglobulin light chains in myeloma bring myocardial cells to a standstill, and the Aβ amyloid in Alzheimer disease plaques is noxious for neurons. The fibrils in all forms get progressively cross-linked, thereby becoming resistant to degradation. The amyloid thus formed progressively accumulates like so much extracellular linoleum, eventually squeezing out normal parenchyma. Typically, the prognosis for patients affected by amyloid has been exceptionally grim; a diagnosis of cardiac involvement usually translates to death within 1 to 2 years.

In all of these entities, the protein aggregation has long been presumed to be relentless and irreversible; at the very best, clinicians could only hope to prevent further accumulation. Accordingly, the traditional approaches to therapy focus on treating the underlying disease—specifically, chemotherapy for myeloma (with AL amyloidosis) and antibiotics for chronic infections or anti-inflammatory agents for autoimmune diseases (with AA amyloidosis).

Some breakthroughs appear imminent, however: Building on an improved understanding of protein folding, research has led to production of a number of useful small molecules—so-called “amyloid breakers,” now entering into clinical trial. Some of these interfere with the binding of chaperone proteins such as substance P and apolipoprotein E4 (apoE4), which can propagate the β-sheet content of Aβ peptides and promote pathogenic fibril formation in the brain: CPHPC is a carboxypyrrolidinyl carboxylic acid derivative that binds to the P component and results in its prompt removal from the circulation thus eliminating an important “nucleation factor” for amyloid propagation. Other agents such as tramiprosate inhibit the interactions between interstitial glycosaminoglycans and Aβ that normally stabilize fibril formation; these breakers are sulfated glycosaminoglycan mimetics. Yet another approach is to stabilize native transthyretin tetramers with drugs like tafamadis or diflunisal to prevent the dissociation of monomeric components (e.g., from mutant isoforms) that can then misfold into amyloid fibrils.

With these approaches, coupled with earlier detection of amyloid deposits before irreversible damage is done, what previously has been an intractable and inexorable process may eventually become manageable.

Extracorporeal Photopheresis: Seeing the Light on Graft-versus-Host Disease

Allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplantation after radiochemotherapy is an important treatment modality for certain leukemias and lymphomas, and graft-versus-tumor effect underlies some of its therapeutic efficacy. Unfortunately, allogeneic transplantation also carries a significant—and potentially life-threatening—risk of graft-versus-host-disease (GVHD). Thus, donor lymphocytes have the capacity to attack a variety of host tissues, leading to intractable diarrhea, pulmonary fibrosis, cutaneous inflammation, hepatobiliary disease, and/or musculoskeletal injury.

Older approaches to control or prevent GVHD include the following:

  • Precise DNA sequence-based donor and host human leukocyte antigen (HLA) matching. Except for transplantation between identical twins, however, mismatch of minor HLA antigens is inevitable, even with sibling transplants.
  • Autologous hematopoietic stem cell transplantation. This modality is not favored currently because graft-versus-tumor effect is lost.
  • Depletion of donor T cells with monoclonal antibodies to CD3 or other T cell markers. Unfortunately, this leads to a reduced graft-versus-tumor effect, poorer overall marrow engraftment, and much more frequent development of Epstein-Barr virus (EBV)-associated B cell tumors, which can be fatal.
  • Administration of immunosuppressive drugs. Such regimens constitute a mainstay of current efforts to control GVHD but carry the usual downside of opportunistic infections and EBV-related malignancies.

When these strategies fail, transplant physicians are increasingly turning to the light—that is, to the use of extracorporeal photopheresis (ECP) to treat white blood cells outside of the body and then return them to the host.

ECP uses 8-methoxypsoralen (8MP), a plant compound that when activated by sunlight cross-links DNA to cause apoptosis of cells. Psoralens have been used since the time of the Pharaohs for a variety of dermatologic conditions; they are the “P” in PUVA (psoralen plus ultraviolet A [UV-A] light) therapy for psoriasis. In ECP, a continuous centrifugal apheresis of blood allows white cells to be separated and externally treated with low doses of 8MP and visible-wavelength light; these are then returned to the patient during the same pheresis session. The ECP approach appears to work not so much by killing proliferating lymphocytes (as had been thought originally) but by generation of regulatory T cells. This may occur by dendritic cell-mediated processing and presentation of the activated and apoptotic cells. The effect is remarkably antigen-specific—there is generally only suppression of the GVHD-causing clones and not a global immunosuppression. The same approach also is effective in treating cutaneous T cell lymphomas and for preventing rejection in solid organ transplants; clinicians are even beginning to see the ECP light for some autoimmune disorders such as Crohn disease and scleroderma!
