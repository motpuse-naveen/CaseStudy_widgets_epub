Scenario

A 45-year-old Native American woman presented complaining of abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, and fever. She had experienced mid-epigastric pain on four or five occasions over the past 6 months. The pain, in the past, was described as a steady but cramping pain lasting several hours, sometimes with radiation to the right subscapular area. A few episodes could be clearly related to eating. The most recent episode of pain, while similar in character to prior episodes, was more severe, had persisted for more than 1 day, and was accompanied by chills, fever, nausea, and vomiting. The patient was on no medications, and her past medical history was notable only for prior hospitalizations for delivery of her five children. Physical examination revealed an obese woman who appeared uncomfortable. Her blood pressure was 110/80, her temperature was 39° C, and her pulse was 110. Her eyes were icteric, and her abdomen was tender in the right upper quadrant, especially on deep inspiration. Her liver was slightly enlarged on percussion. Bowel sounds could be heard. Cardiac, respiratory, and other parts of the examination were normal.

Laboratory results included:

Hematocrit 42%
WBC count 15,000/µL
75% neutrophils
10% bands
15% lymphocytes
AST 85 U/L
ALT 100 U/L
Alkaline phosphatase 240 U/L
Total bilirubin 3.5 mg/dL
Amylase 750 U/L
Lipase 200 U/L
Urinalysis Bilirubin - positive (1+)

She was admitted to the hospital, and after blood for cultures was obtained, parenteral antibiotic therapy with ampicillin and sulbactam was started. An abdominal ultrasound exam showed stones in the gallbladder and dilation of the hepatic and common bile ducts and pancreatic duct.

Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP) was performed, which demonstrated obstruction of the distal portion of the common bile duct near the ampulla of Vater due to an impacted stone. Attempts to remove the stone via the endoscope were unsuccessful. Surgery was scheduled, and a cholecystectomy and exploration of the common bile duct were performed. After removal of a T-tube drain left in the common bile duct at the time of surgery for decompression, the patient did well postoperatively and was discharged.

Question 1
1
What are the pathobiologic mechanisms leading to the development of acute calculous cholecystitis?
Show answer

Acute calculous cholecystitis is due to irritation and inflammation of the gallbladder wall when there is obstruction of bile outflow. Lysolecithin, a hydrolytic product of biliary lecithin produced with bile stasis, is toxic to the gallbladder mucosa. Bile salts are also toxic. Prostaglandins contribute to further damage. Furthermore, inflammation and distention of the gallbladder lead to increased luminal pressure, which compromises blood flow to the wall.

Biliary ducts, choledocholithiasis - Gross, mucosal surface

As in this case, a serious complication of cholelithiasis is choledocholithiasis. Gallstones pass from the gallbladder and through the cystic duct to impact somewhere in the common bile duct, causing choledocholithiasis. This complication leads to dilation of the biliary tree proximal to the obstruction.

Question 1
1
In addition to cholecystitis and choledocholithiasis, what are the complications arising from cholelithiasis?
Show answer

About 70% to 80% of patients with gallstones remain asymptomatic throughout their lives. However, stones can cause colicky pain, acute and chronic cholecystitis, empyema of the gallbladder, gallbladder perforation or fistulae, gallstone ileus, choledocholithiasis with ascending cholangitis and possible liver abscesses, obstructive cholestasis, and pancreatitis.


Question 2
2
What do the laboratory tests indicate?
Show answer

The marked elevation of serum amylase and lipase is consistent with a diagnosis of acute pancreatitis. An elevated alkaline phosphatase with a conjugated hyperbilirubinemia is suggestive of obstructive jaundice due to choledocholithiasis.


Image 5

Cholesterol stone and cholesterolosis of the gallbladder - Gross, mucosal surface

This image shows a round, yellow, pure cholesterol gallstone next to an opened gallbladder, which shows a geographic yellow mucosal surface caused by cholesterolosis of the gallbladder. Cholesterolosis is not a risk factor for gallstone formation. Microscopically, gallbladders affected by cholesterolosis contain numerous triglyceride- and cholesterol-laden macrophages in the lamina propria of the tips of the villi in the mucosal layer.

Question 1
1
Under what conditions do cholesterol gallstones form, and what are the steps in the formation of gallstones?
Show answer

Cholesterol is rendered soluble in bile via aggregation with water-soluble bile salts and water-insoluble lecithins. When cholesterol concentrations increase, supersaturation of bile and nucleation of cholesterol crystals occur. Gallbladder stasis, increased mucin content of the bile, and biliary sludge all act to promote nucleation of cholesterol crystals, which grow by accretion into stones. Risk factors for cholesterol and mixed gallstones include ethnic and geographic factors (e.g., Native American populations, industrialized societies), increasing age, female sex, environmental factors (e.g., oral contraceptive use, multiparity), acquired disorders (e.g., diabetes, obesity, rapid weight loss, diseases of the distal small intestine such as Crohn disease), and hereditary disorders (e.g., hyperlipidemias).

What are risk factors for the development of pigment gallstones?
Show answer

Cirrhosis, chronic hemolytic anemias (such as in patients with sickle cell anemia), bacterial contamination of the biliary tree, and chronic parasitic biliary infections are recognized risk factors for the development of pigment stones.

Question 1
1
What are the causes and postulated mechanisms leading to the development of acute pancreatitis?
Show answer

Alcoholism, along with biliary tract disease (cholelithiasis), accounts for the majority (80%) of cases of acute pancreatitis. About 10% to 20% of cases are idiopathic; there is increasing evidence of several genetic defects. Acute pancreatitis is the result of release of pancreatic enzymes from acinar cells, causing proteolysis, lipolysis, and hemorrhage. Lipase and phospholipase degrade lipids and membrane phospholipids. Released elastase degrades elastic tissue in vessel walls. Pancreatic amylase is also released. Trypsin, a protease, plays a central role in the process by activating all the other proenzymes produced by pancreatic acinar cells. Proposed mechanisms of pancreatic injury and subsequent enzyme release include (1) duct obstruction, (2) direct acinar cell injury, and (3) deranged intracellular transport of pancreatic enzymes.

Question 1
1
What are the major acute complications of acute pancreatitis?
Show answer

About 5% of patients with pancreatitis die during the acute illness. The mortality is higher in patients with other concomitant diseases. Possible sequelae in patients with acute pancreatitis also include development of pancreatic pseudocysts, pancreatic abscesses, duodenal obstruction, and, with repeated episodes of pancreatitis, development of chronic pancreatitis.

Liver, acute cholangitis - Medium power

Acute inflammation is present surrounding the bile ducts, as well as within the wall and lumen of bile ducts in acute cholangitis. Acute cholangitis can result from impaction of small gallstones in the common bile duct (choledocholithiasis) after they have passed from the gallbladder and through the cystic duct into the distal biliary tree.

Question 1
1
What is the prognosis of most patients with gallbladder carcinoma?
Show answer

Carcinomas of the gallbladder are rarely detected at an early stage when they can be surgically cured. The mean 5-year survival is very low, approximately 1%.
