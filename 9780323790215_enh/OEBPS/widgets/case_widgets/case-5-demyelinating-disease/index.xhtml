Demyelinating Disease

Learning Objectives

  • 1. List the two major neurologic disorders associated with demyelination.

Scenario

A 28-year-old woman presented to her physician with complaints of weakness, numbness, and tingling in her left leg. The neurologic abnormalities were preceded by a self-limited, flu-like illness. Physical examination revealed moderate leg weakness, with more subtle weakness in the left leg, and loss of vibratory and position sense in both legs. Lumbar puncture revealed an increased level of IgG and the presence of oligoclonal bands in the cerebrospinal fluid. A magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scan of the head revealed abnormal signal in the periventricular white matter (Image 1). MRI of the spine revealed similar areas of signal abnormality within the spinal cord. She gradually improved over the next several months and was fully ambulatory by 8 months after the initial episode.

Approximately 2 years later, after an uneventful pregnancy, the patient experienced an abrupt loss of vision in her right eye. She was treated with corticosteroids and her vision gradually improved over the next month, although a small visual field deficit persisted. At age 34, the patient developed a tremor, most noticeable when she attempted to pick things up. She also complained of feeling "clumsy" and sometimes had difficulty maintaining her balance. Over the next year her tremor diminished, but she began to experience difficulty speaking (dysarthria) and experienced several transient episodes of double vision (diplopia).

At age 40 while riding as a passenger in a motor vehicle, she was involved in a collision and sustained massive chest trauma. She was transported by helicopter to a local trauma center but died en route and could not be resuscitated. An autopsy was performed.

Postmortem examination revealed evidence of extensive thoracic trauma with multiple rib fractures and a cardiac contusion. Examination of the brain and spinal cord revealed multiple, irregularly distributed lesions within the white matter (Images 2-6).

Question 1
1
What is MS?
Show answer

MS is a sporadic CNS demyelinating disorder possibly caused by autoreactive T cells directed against components of myelin. It is characterized clinically by the development of distinct neurologic deficits separated in time and space (i.e., involving different regions of the nervous system). Although MS can manifest at any age, most cases begin during early and middle adult years. The course of the disease is quite variable. In rare cases, the disease manifests as a fulminant demyelinating process with a poor prognosis. At the other extreme, some patients may experience only minimal neurologic problems after their initial episode. In most cases, MS is characterized by multiple relapsing and remitting episodes associated with variable chronic neurologic deficits.


Question 2
2
How is the diagnosis of MS made?
Show answer

The diagnosis of MS remains fundamentally a clinical diagnosis based on the history of waxing and waning neurologic deficits involving multiple distinct areas of the CNS. Ancillary studies that may be helpful in arriving at a diagnosis of MS include radiographic imaging studies and evaluation of the cerebrospinal fluid (CNS) for evidence of abnormal immunological activity (increased IgG levels, an increase in the IgG synthesis rate within the CSF, and oligoclonal IgG bands). Myelin basic protein is also often present in the CSF during periods of active disease and indicates the presence of active myelin breakdown. None of the ancillary studies is diagnostic of MS, but rather these studies help to support the diagnosis of MS in an appropriate clinical setting.

Question 1
1
What signs and symptoms might correspond to plaque A? Which to plaque B?
Show answer

Plaque A involves portions of the lateral corticospinal tract. Injury to fibers in this area might produce weakness and spasticity on the same side of the body as the cord lesion owing to interruption of signals from upper motor neurons. Plaque B involves the contralateral cuneate fasciculus, which carries ascending sensory fibers from the ipsilateral upper extremity. Injury to these fibers would be manifested clinically as a loss of vibratory and position sense in the upper extremity on the same side as the cord lesion.

Question 1
1
What is known about the cause and pathogenesis of MS?
Show answer

Our current understanding of MS suggests that autoreactive T cells directed against the myelin sheath are responsible for the pathogenesis of this disease. Only a portion of the immunogenetic basis of the disease has been explained. Environmental factors may play a role as well, because MS is more prevalent in northern latitudes than in more tropical climates and individuals emigrating from one area to another after the age of 15 carry with them the same risk of disease as individuals remaining in the original area, suggesting that exposure to an environmental agent early in life plays a role in the development of MS. A role for genetic influences is suggested by (1) the observation that first-degree relatives of MS patients have a 15-fold increased risk of developing MS compared with the general population, (2) its association with certain major histocompatibility complex (MHC ) class II antigens, and (3) the observation that there is a significantly higher concordance rate for MS in monozygotic than in dizygotic twins.

In addition to an increased level of immunoglobulin, CSF obtained from a patient with active disease might contain myelin basic protein, an indicator of active myelin breakdown.

Question 1
1
What is a leukodystrophy? What are some specific types of leukodystrophy?
Show answer

The leukodystrophies are a group of hereditary disorders characterized by abnormal myelin synthesis or turnover, or both. Although the biochemical basis of some forms of leukodystrophy remains incompletely understood, some forms are caused by well-characterized enzyme deficiencies. Examples include metachromatic leukodystrophy (arylsulfatase A deficiency), Krabbe disease (galactocerebroside beta-galactosidase deficiency), and adrenoleukodystrophy (a defect in a peroxisomal membrane–associated protein).


Question 2
2
How do the leukodystrophies differ from multiple sclerosis (MS)?
Show answer

The leukodystrophies differ from MS in many important ways. In addition to their hereditary nature, the leukodystrophies progress relentlessly, usually from childhood, in contrast to the waxing and waning course of typical MS. They involve the white matter in a widespread, symmetric pattern, in contrast to the irregular distribution of the lesions found in MS. Finally, many leukodystrophies involve the peripheral nervous system and the central nervous system (CNS), in contrast to MS, which is limited to the CNS.
