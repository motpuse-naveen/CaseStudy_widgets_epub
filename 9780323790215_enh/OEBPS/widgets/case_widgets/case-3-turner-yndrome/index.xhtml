Scenario

An 18-year-old mother delivered a stillborn female fetus of 23 weeks’ gestational age. The fetus had bilateral cervical cystic hygromas and massive hydrops. An autopsy was performed.

At autopsy, the fetus had coarctation of the aorta, dysgenesis of the ovaries, and a horseshoe kidney. Tissue obtained at autopsy had a 45,X karyotype.

Fetus, Turner syndrome - Clinical presentation

Fetuses with Turner syndrome typically have large, cervical cystic hygromas, owing to abnormal or delayed development of the lymphatic drainage. Cystic hygromas are fluid-filled, dilated lymphatic channels and surrounding connective tissue. These resolve by the time of birth, but the webbed neck of Turner syndrome is evidence of their earlier presence. Turner syndrome neonates often have puffy hands and feet, also related to lymphatic abnormalities. The massive hydrops in this fetus may be related to cardiac or cardiovascular malformation.

Fetal kidney, ureters, urinary bladder - Gross

Fusion of the lower poles of the right and left kidneys has formed a horseshoe-shaped organ. Horseshoe kidneys usually function normally. Various kinds of kidney malformations, including horseshoe kidney, are common in Turner syndrome

Question 1
1
What are the most common abnormalities of the genitourinary system in Turner syndrome?
Show answer

The most common abnormalities are horseshoe kidney and streak (fibrotic) ovaries.


Image 3
Only one sex chromosome

Turner syndrome, 45,X - Karyotype

This is the most common karyotype found in Turner syndrome. Many patients with Turner syndrome have a second karyotypically abnormal cell line with 46 chromosomes, including one normal X chromosome and one structurally abnormal X chromosome.

Question 1
1
How often are pregnancies with the aforementioned karyotype spontaneously aborted?
Show answer

Spontaneous abortion occurs in 99% of pregnancies with 45,X karyotype.

Girl with Turner syndrome - Clinical presentation

Girls with Turner syndrome usually come to medical attention in their early teens because they fail to enter puberty. In addition to the above-listed highlighted features, this teenaged girl has no breast development. Also note the characteristic short stature of Turner syndrome by comparing her shoulder level with the level of the doorknob and the examining table.

1
Is this girl likely to be mentally retarded?
Show answer

Question 2
2
Phenotypically normal females with a 46,XX karyotype have only one active X chromosome; why don’t they have a Turner syndrome phenotype?
Show answer

There are some genes on the inactive X chromosome that remain active throughout life and are presumed to be necessary for normal growth and development. Such genes have functional homologues on the Y chromosome, providing males with two active copies of these genes as well. The inactive X chromosome is selectively reactivated in oogonia before the first meiotic division, so two X chromosomes are presumably necessary for female gametogenesis.

Image 6
An extra X chromosome

Klinefelter syndrome, 47,XXY - Karyotype

This karyotype represents the most common sex chromosome abnormality in males, occurring in about 1:500 live-born boys. It is an important cause of male hypogonadism and infertility.

Question 1
1
What is the state of X chromosome inactivation in Klinefelter syndrome?
Show answer

One X chromosome is inactivated.


Image 7

Klinefelter syndrome - Clinical presentation

Note the long legs and underdeveloped genitalia. Males with Klinefelter syndrome have long legs, hypogonadism with infertility, and normal intelligence. Many go undiagnosed unless they seek a work-up because of infertility.

Question 1
1
Compare and contrast Turner syndrome with Klinefelter syndrome. What similarities do they share?
Show answer

Similarities are as follows: (1) infertility (an important effect of sex chromosome imbalance); (2) evidence of hypogonadism, although this is more pronounced in Turner syndrome than in Klinefelter syndrome; girls with Turner syndrome usually have no secondary sexual development or puberty, whereas boys with Klinefelter syndrome develop facial and pubic hair, although their genitalia remain underdeveloped; (3) normal intelligence (in contrast to autosomal imbalance, sex chromosome imbalance, unless severe, is not associated with mental retardation).


Question 2
2
What are the differences between Turner syndrome and Klinefelter syndrome?
Show answer

(1) Turner syndrome is associated with developmental malformations besides altered sexual development (e.g., developmental abnormalities of the lymphatic system, coarctation of the aorta, horseshoe kidney). Developmental malformations are not increased in Klinefelter syndrome. (2) The developmental abnormalities in Turner syndrome usually lead to clinical diagnosis and cytogenetic confirmation of this condition; in contrast, many males with Klinefelter syndrome are never diagnosed. (3) Klinefelter syndrome is associated with advanced maternal age; Turner syndrome is not. DNA analysis has shown that the extra X chromosome in about half of Klinefelter syndrome patients is of maternal origin, and those cases are strongly associated with advanced maternal age, similar to other trisomies. In contrast, DNA analysis has shown that the single X chromosome in most (approximately 80%) Turner syndrome patients is of maternal origin (i.e., a normal egg was fertilized by a sperm without a sex chromosome).
