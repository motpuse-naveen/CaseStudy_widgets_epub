Coronary Artery Thrombosis and Heart Failure

Learning Objectives

  • Edema and Congestion
    • 1. Define edema.
    • 2. Know the five pathophysiologic categories of edema and give examples of each.
    • 3. Define exudate versus transudate.
    • 4. Understand the central role the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system and renal hypoperfusion play in edema.
    • 5. Define anasarca, dependent edema, and pitting edema.
    • 6. Contrast dependent and generalized edema with respect to etiology.
    • 7. Understand the physiologic effects of edema based upon its location.
    • 8. Define hyperemia, congestion, and nutmeg liver.
    • 9. Understand the pathophysiology of chronic passive congestion (its cause and effects).
    • 10. Define hemorrhage, hematoma, petechiae, purpura, and ecchymoses.
  • Hemostasis
    • 1. Understand the consequences of hemorrhage (based upon location and amount).
    • 2. Define normal hemostasis and thrombosis, and understand the differences between them.
    • 3. Know the general sequence of events in hemostasis.
    • 4. Know the three antithrombotic properties and three prothrombotic properties of endothelium, and list their main mediators.
    • 5. Know the three general reactions platelets undergo, and list their main mediators.
    • 6. Review the coagulation cascade.
    • 7. Know the three main anticoagulants, and understand the role of plasmin.
  • Thrombogenesis
    • 1. Know the Virchow triad (factors predisposing to thrombus formation), and understand each factor's role in the formation of thrombi.
    • 2. Know the common primary and high risk factors for thrombosis secondary to hypercoagulable states; understand the mutation in factor V Leiden.
    • 3. Know the morphology of thrombi (arterial and venous), the distinguishing features between arterial and venous thrombi, and the possible fates of thrombi.
  • Thromboembolic Disease and Pulmonary Thromboembolism
    • 1. Define thrombus, clot, and embolus.
    • 2. List the clinical states associated with increased risk of venous thrombosis. Describe the pathophysiologic basis of such associations.
    • 3. List the most common sites of venous thrombosis. Know which of these sites are the source of clinically significant pulmonary thromboemboli.
    • 4. Describe the common signs and symptoms of venous thrombosis.
    • 5. Relate the outcome of pulmonary thromboembolism to the size of the embolus and the number of emboli. Understand the circumstances in which pulmonary thromboemboli result in infarction.
    • 6. Describe the clinical manifestations and consequences of pulmonary thromboembolism and their approximate frequency. Know how often pulmonary embolism is asymptomatic.
    • 7. Describe the life-threatening events that follow major pulmonary embolism and their pathologic basis.
    • 8. List examples of clinical conditions that give rise to fat embolism, amniotic fluid embolism, and air embolism. Describe the ways in which they significantly differ from pulmonary thromboembolism.
  • Infarction
    • 1. Define infarct.
    • 2. Know the morphology of an infarct (red versus white, septic versus bland), understand the progression of an infarct, and understand the consequences of an infarct (depending upon the location).
    • 3. Know the four major determinants that influence the development of an infarct, and understand the pathophysiology behind each.

Scenario

A 65-year-old man presented to the emergency department with a recent (4-hour) history of severe chest pain radiating to his left arm. He was suspected to have had a "heart attack." Coronary angiography revealed a complete occlusion of the left anterior descending branch about 2 cm from its origin. He was given a therapeutic dose of recombinant human tissue plasminogen activator (t-PA). This treatment restored coronary artery blood flow, and his chest pain improved. Simultaneously, he was started on 1 tablet of aspirin per day.

Seven days later, he noted swelling of both legs and feet and was found to have pitting edema of the legs; his liver was enlarged, and his neck veins (jugular) appeared full. He was given diuretics and asked to consume a salt-restricted diet. Because of considerable weakness, he remained in bed most of the time.

A few days later, he developed sudden pain in the lower right part of his chest, which was aggravated by taking a deep breath. Physical examination revealed that his left leg had developed more swelling than the right. X-ray of his chest showed a faint shadow in the peripheral part of the lower lobe of the right lung. Intravenous heparin was started. Two days later, he became very breathless and died suddenly.

Targeted therapy box: Shooting for PAR: Hitting a Hole-in-One…or a Bogey?

Shooting for PAR: Hitting a Hole-in-One…or a Bogey?

Platelet activation and aggregation play an important role in thrombogenesis, as well as atherogenesis. Thus, platelet activation is a critical component of the pathogenesis of diseases such as myocardial infarction and cerebrovascular stroke. Based on the central importance of platelets, the current standard of preventive care for patients with a previous history of atherothrombotic diseases involves a two-pronged antiplatelet approach:

  • Aspirin blocks platelet thromboxane A2 (TxA2) synthesis by irreversibly inhibiting cyclooxygenase. TxA2 is a potent mediator of platelet aggregation. Although endothelial cyclooxygenase is likewise inhibited by aspirin—with resulting loss of prostacyclin (i.e., prostaglandin I2 [PGI2]) synthesis—the effect on the antithrombotic PGI2 production is transient, because endothelial cells continuously synthesize new cyclooxygenase.
  • Thienopyridine (e.g., clopidogrel) blockade of platelet adenosine diphosphate (ADP) receptors (also called P2Y12). Because ADP also is a major trigger for platelet activation and aggregation, receptor blockade has been shown to be a potent adjunct for preventing recurrent atherothrombosis.

Combined blockade of TxA2 and ADP has well-documented benefits in reducing the risk of recurrent infarction or stroke. This strategy, however, leaves completely unimpeded the activation of platelets by thrombin—probably the most potent pathway that can promote platelet aggregation. Thrombin-mediated activation constitutes an important reason why patients on aspirin and thienopyridines can nevertheless experience recurrent thrombotic events.

Besides generating fibrin from fibrinogen, thrombin activates platelets via the PAR-1 and PAR-4 protease-activated receptors (PARs) that are cleaved by proteases to make a tethered ligand that can efficiently stimulate the digested receptor. Although other PARs can participate in inflammation and pain pathways, PAR-1 is the major thrombin receptor on platelets; hence, it causes prompt and profound platelet activation.

Thus, PAR-1-specific blockade (e.g., with vorapaxar) significantly reduces recurrent atherothrombotic events such as myocardial infarction—even in patients already receiving aspirin and clopidogrel. Fortunately, PAR-1 blockade does not affect the normal coagulation cascade involving thrombin. As might also be anticipated, however, such PAR-1 antagonism is associated with an increased risk of bleeding, including cerebral hemorrhage. Consequently, although going for PAR might seem like a clear winner, this approach will need to be carefully evaluated for individual patients in light of some of the potential (hemorrhagic) traps.

Yet another mechanism of platelet aggregation is cross-linking through their cell surface glycoprotein IIb/IIIa receptors by fibrinogen. Accordingly, antagonists (e.g., abciximab, eptifibatide, tirofiban) of these glycoprotein receptors also can be used as powerful antiplatelet agents. These agents can be administered only intravenously, however, so they typically are used for selected clinical indications in the hospital setting (e.g., to prevent acute vascular stent thrombosis), and not for widespread outpatient atherothrombosis prevention.


Targeted Therapy box: Aspirin – An old actor with a new role

Aspirin: An Old Actor with a New Role

Rubor (erythema), tumor (swelling), calor (warmth), and dolor (pain) have long been recognized as classic elements of inflammation. These are active features driven by a host of mediators that affect the behavior of blood vessels, inflammatory cells, and nerves. In particular, metabolites of arachidonic acid such as the prostaglandins and leukotrienes play important roles. Consequently, drugs that target the phospholipase-mediated release of arachidonic acid precursors (e.g., glucocorticoids) or the production of prostaglandins (e.g., cyclooxygenase [COX] inhibitors, such as aspirin) have a salubrious effect on inflammation. These drugs, however, are not without side effects.

Fortunately, several new targets are in the therapeutic cross-hairs thanks to an improved understanding of the normal pathways by which inflammation is intrinsically modulated.

  • Until recently, the natural resolution of inflammation was considered a passive process; the prevailing view was that once the inciting stimulus was removed by the inflammatory response, the signals driving the activity just progressively “fizzled out.” It is now clear, however, that the pathways triggered early in inflammation also set into motion the production of mediators that ultimately actively shut down inflammation. Thus, arachidonate precursors that can give rise to pro-inflammatory leukotrienes—via the action of 5-lipoxygenase—also can be converted to anti-inflammatory lipoxins through the subsequent activity of 12-lipoxygenase. Somewhat counterintuitively, then, upstream inhibition of the 5-lipoxygenase enzyme can actually prolong and augment inflammation by removing the substrate for a later enzyme that actively promotes resolution.
  • Still other natural anti-inflammatory compounds—called resolvins and protectins—are generated from omega-3 fatty acids such as eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA) and docosahexaenoic acid (DHA). Found in superabundance in cold-water fish, these essential polyunsaturated fatty acids have long been associated with beneficial effects in atherosclerosis, asthma, and cancer. When EPA and DHA are sequentially modified by cytochrome P-450 and lipoxygenase enzymes, they form potent anti-inflammatory products that block neutrophil and monocyte recruitment and mediator production. A number of the resolvins also are impressively analgesic, interfering with both peripheral and central pain pathways. Consequently, several resolvin analogues are in clinical trials; although they show clinical promise, their availability is several years away.

Fortuitously, aspirin, an over-the-counter drug that augments resolvin production, is already on hand. The well-described mechanism of action for aspirin is the irreversible acetylation of COX proteins. Although this prevents the production of the pro-inflammatory prostaglandins, the acetylated protein can be the first enzymatic step in producing resolvins! Thus, aspirin is not only anti-inflammatory but also pro-resolving; the other NSAIDs, which merely block (but do not acetylate) COX, do not have this effect. It is more than ironic that one of the oldest (and cheapest) of drugs may be at the cutting edge of the future of anti-inflammatory agents.


Question 3
3
How do the various natural anticoagulants act?

There are three natural anticoagulants: (1) The protein C system generates active protein C that inactivates cofactors V and VIII; protein C itself is activated by thrombin after the latter binds to thrombomodulin on the endothelium. (2) Antithrombin is activated by binding to heparin-like molecules on the endothelium; activated antithrombin causes proteolysis of active factors IX, X, and XI, and thrombin. (3) Plasmin cleaves fibrin; it is derived from its circulating precursor, plasminogen, by the action of t-PA, which is synthesized by endothelial cells.

Question 1
1
What is the difference between a postmortem clot and a thrombus?

Postmortem clots are not attached to endothelium; they are gelatinous, rubbery, dark red at the ends, and yellowish elsewhere. Thrombi are attached to endothelium and are traversed by pale gray fibrin strands that can be seen on cut section; they are more firm but fragile.


Question 2
2
What stage in the formation of a thrombus is an important target of the currently used antithrombotic medications?

The most important stage in thrombogenesis that is inhibited by current antithrombotic medications is platelet aggregation. This crucial step requires binding of platelets by fibrinogen molecules, which attach to platelets at the GPIIb/IIIa receptor. Different antithrombotic drugs inhibit platelet aggregation in different ways. Aspirin inhibits synthesis of thromboxane A2. Clopidogrel inhibit ADP-mediated structural alterations in the GPIIb/IIIa receptor, preventing binding of fibrinogen to this receptor. Direct GPIIb/IIIa inhibitors are also available.

What is the thrombus made of?

The thrombus is made of fibrin, platelets, and red blood cells.

2
What causes arterial thrombosis? What causes venous thrombosis?

Arterial thrombosis is caused by endothelial damage (e.g., atherosclerosis, vasculitis); venous thrombosis is caused by stasis (sluggishness) of blood flow. Both types of vessels are affected in hypercoagulable states such as antithrombin or protein C deficiency.

Question 1
1
What are the various fates of thrombi?

The fates of thrombi include propagation, embolism, dissolution, and organization with recanalization.


Question 2
1
Which of these fates is clinically most significant in the arterial circulation versus the venous circulation?

The most significant problem with arterial thrombi is propagation leading to luminal obstruction, resulting in infarction of the tissue supplied. Important examples include myocardial and cerebral infarction. The most significant problem with venous thrombi is the possibility of potentially fatal embolization into the pulmonary circulation.

Question 1
1
What are the major similarities between a myocardial and a cerebral infarct?

The major similarity is in the etiology. Both types of infarcts are commonly caused by thrombotic occlusion of the arteries supplying them. Thrombi usually form on the same underlying disease process (i.e., atherosclerotic arterial disease). Also, the early histologic reactions, such as neutrophilic infiltration and granulation tissue formation, are common to both.


Question 2
2
What are the major differences between a myocardial and a cerebral infarct?

A myocardial infarct typically features coagulative necrosis, which heals by fibrosis and leaves behind a fibrous scar. In contrast, a cerebral infarct is typically liquefactive necrosis, in which dead tissue is digested without being replaced by fibrosis, leaving behind a cystic, cavitary lesion.


Question 3
3
What is the mechanism of formation of hemorrhagic infarcts in the brain?

Brain infarcts can be pale or hemorrhagic. Hemorrhagic infarcts are due to arterial occlusion followed by reperfusion. Examples are embolic occlusion followed by fragmentation of emboli or occlusive vasospasm that later is relieved.


Image 8


Liver, chronic passive venous congestion - Gross, cut surface

This condition is caused by resistance or obstruction to the outflow of venous blood from the liver, as may occur in chronic right heart failure (congestive heart failure). The area surrounding the central veins ("centrizonal") becomes intensely congested, and the hepatocytes in the central zone may become necrotic owing to hypoxia. These centrilobular areas appear as the dark red spots on the cut surface. The alternating pale areas represent the periportal hepatocytes, which have sustained a lesser degree of hypoxia. This gross appearance is also called "nutmeg liver." In the hepatic lobules, blood flows from the periportal to the central zones, and the centrilobular areas are more vulnerable to hypoxia than the peripheral hepatocytes.

Question 1
1
What is the pathogenesis of pulmonary edema?

Left ventricular failure (e.g., caused by a myocardial infarct) causes pump failure, and secondarily there is impaired flow of blood from the lung to the left atrium. Increased hydrostatic pressure in pulmonary alveolar capillaries and subsequent transudation of fluid into alveoli result. Pulmonary edema in other cases may also result from damage to alveolar capillaries (e.g., in acute respiratory distress syndrome).


Question 2
2
How does this type of edema differ from the edema seen in acute inflammation?

The fluid in pulmonary edema is a transudate (i.e., it is protein poor, has low specific gravity, and does not contain inflammatory cells). Edema in inflammation is an exudate.

Question 1
1
Did this patient have clinical features suggestive of pulmonary thromboembolism?

Yes. He had deep vein thrombosis in his left leg, which most likely was the source of an embolus. His chest pain that was exaggerated by breathing suggests pleural inflammation overlying an infarct in the right lower lobe. Massive pulmonary thromboembolism was the probable cause of his death.


Question 2
2
Why are some infarcts red and others pale?

Red infarcts result from hemorrhage into the necrotic area. This hemorrhage is likely to occur in tissues that have a loose texture and dual blood supply (e.g., lung). Pale infarcts occur in compact tissues and tissues in which the collaterals do not readily refill the necrotic area (e.g., heart).

The thrombi are red because of entrapped erythrocytes, and they have a laminated appearance, reflecting varying composition of the thrombus, which relates to the rate of blood flow at the time the thrombus was forming.

Question 1
1
What conditions predispose to venous thrombosis?

Venous stasis caused by prolonged immobilization (e.g., in hospitalized patients after surgery) or by congestive heart failure predisposes to thrombosis.


Question 2
2
What is the most common source of clinically significant pulmonary emboli?

The most common source of pulmonary emboli are the large, deep veins of the leg at or above the knee joint. These include popliteal veins, femoral veins, and iliac veins. Thrombi in these vessels often do not produce local symptoms. In contrast, thrombi in superficial veins often produce pain, edema, and varicose ulcers, but they usually do not embolize.


Question 3
3
What is the most common symptom associated with such venous thrombi?

There are no symptoms in about 50% of cases. Local pain and edema occur in the remaining cases.

On the left side, airspaces and alveolar septa can be seen; however, the right half looks solid and eosinophilic. Question 1
1
What is the most common symptom of pulmonary embolism?

There are usually no symptoms. Most pulmonary emboli (60% to 80%) are clinically silent because of their small size and because of the dual blood flow through the bronchial circulation. With time, these emboli organize and are incorporated into the vessel wall.


Question 2
2
How and when does pulmonary thromboembolism cause sudden death?

If more than 60% of the pulmonary circulation is obstructed by emboli, the patient is at a high risk of sudden death owing to acute right heart failure (cor pulmonale) or shock (cardiovascular collapse).


Question 3
3
When does pulmonary thromboembolism result in infarction?

The possibility of developing pulmonary infarction is higher in a previously diseased lung, especially in the setting of sluggish bronchial arterial flow or prior pulmonary congestion owing to left heart failure.

The outlines of the pulmonary alveoli can be seen, but the structural details have been obscured in this area of coagulative necrosis. There are no intact nuclei. Question 1
1
What is the risk of recurrence of pulmonary thromboembolism?

Generally, a patient who has had one pulmonary embolus is at a higher risk of having more.


Question 2
2
In what respects does fat embolism significantly differ from a typical venous pulmonary thromboembolism?

Fat embolism occurs after fractures of long bones, major soft tissue trauma, or severe burns. Most patients with fat embolism are asymptomatic, similar to patients with venous thromboembolism. In cases (<10%) that are symptomatic, besides pulmonary insufficiency, patients also develop neurologic symptoms, skin rashes, and sometimes anemia and thrombocytopenia. Microscopically, the emboli consist of fat or marrow particles.

Question 3
3
In what respects does amniotic fluid embolism significantly differ from a typical venous pulmonary thromboembolism?

Amniotic fluid embolism is a grave condition, with mortality greater than 80% because of respiratory insufficiency, shock, disseminated intravascular coagulation, seizures, and coma. This condition is a rare complication of labor (1 in 50,000 deliveries). Microscopically, pulmonary vessels contain squamous cells and mucin (contents of amniotic fluid) derived from fetal skin and intestinal tract.
