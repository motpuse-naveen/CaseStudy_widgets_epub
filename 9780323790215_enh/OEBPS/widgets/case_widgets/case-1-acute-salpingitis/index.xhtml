Acute Salpingitis

Learning Objectives

  • 1. Define the terms edema, exudate, and transudate.
  • 2. Describe the vascular changes in acute inflammation and the consequences at each step of acute inflammation; relate to heat and redness.
  • 3. Know the mechanisms behind increased vascular permeability, and know which vessels are affected.
  • 4. Describe the steps involved in extravasation of leukocytes from the blood, where integrins and selectins act.
  • 5. Define chemotaxis and leukocyte activation.
  • 6. Describe phagocytosis and the role of opsonins.
  • 7. List the chemical mediators of inflammation, and know their sources and immediate effects
  • 8. Compare and contrast acute versus chronic inflammation with respect to causes, nature of the inflammatory response, and tissue changes.
  • 9. Compare and contrast the clinical settings in which different types of inflammatory cells (e.g., neutrophils, eosinophils, monocyte-macrophages, lymphocytes) accumulate in tissues. Compare and contrast the contents of neutrophil and eosinophil granules.
  • 10. Describe the differences between the various cell types (i.e., labile, stable, permanent) in terms of their regeneration potential. List examples of each cell type.
  • 11. Compare and contrast regeneration and fibrosis, and define the terms angiogenesis and scar formation.
  • 12. Describe the systemic manifestations of inflammation and their general physiology, including fever, leukocyte left shift, and acute phase reactants.

Scenario

An 18-year-old woman presents to the emergency department with severe abdominal pain, which has been progressive over the last 3 days. Her menstrual period was due about the time her symptoms started. She admits to being sexually active with several partners in the past 2 months with inconsistent use of birth control. Physical examination reveals marked lower abdominal/pelvic tenderness and a purulent vaginal discharge. She is febrile (39° C) at the time of examination. Her CBC is significant for a WBC count of 15,000 with 82% polymorphonuclear neutrophils (PMNs) and 6% bands and metamyelocytes.

Question 1
1
Which cardinal signs of inflammation can you see here?

Redness (rubor) and swelling (tumor) can be seen; not seen are heat (calor), pain (dolor), and loss of function.


Question 2
2
What chemical mediators are responsible for increasing the blood flow to an area of inflammation?

Histamine, prostaglandins (PGI2, PGE, and PGD2), and nitric oxide are the chemical mediators responsible for increasing the blood flow to an area of inflammation.

Question 3
3
The surface of the tubo-ovarian mass is not only red but also shaggy. What is responsible for the shaggy appearance?

The shaggy appearance is caused by exudation of fibrinogen, which gets deposited as fibrin on the surface. This is a manifestation of increased vascular permeability.

Figure 3-2 Formation of exudates and transudates. A, Normal hydrostatic pressure (blue arrow) is about 32 mm Hg at the arterial end of a capillary bed and 12 mm Hg at the venous end; the mean colloid osmotic pressure of tissues is approximately 25 mm Hg (green arrow) , which is equal to the mean capillary pressure. Therefore, the net flow of fluid across the vascular bed is almost nil. B, An exudate is formed in inflammation, because vascular permeability increases as a result of increased interendothelial spaces. C, A transudate is formed when fluid leaks out because of increased hydrostatic pressure or decreased osmotic pressure.

Question 1
1
Why does this patient have leukocytosis?

In patients with acute inflammation, the blood neutrophil count is increased because these cells are mobilized from the storage pool of neutrophils in the bone marrow by the action of interleukin-1 (IL-1) and tumor necrosis factor (TNF). Later in the course of inflammation, there is increased production of neutrophils in the bone marrow owing to the production of granulocyte macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF).

Question 1
1
What steps are involved in the exit of neutrophils from the blood into tissues?

There are three steps: (1) in the vessel lumen, margination, rolling, and firm adhesion; (2) transmigration across the endothelium; and (3) migration in interstitial tissue toward a chemotactic stimulus.

Figure 3-4 The multistep process of leukocyte migration through blood vessels, shown here for neutrophils. The leukocytes first roll, then become activated and adhere to endothelium, then transmigrate across the endothelium, pierce the basement membrane, and migrate toward chemoattractants emanating from the source of injury. Different molecules play predominant roles in different steps of this process: selectins in rolling; chemokines (usually displayed bound to proteoglycans) in activating the neutrophils to increase avidity of integrins; integrins in firm adhesion; and CD31 (PECAM-1) in transmigration. ICAM-1, Intercellular adhesion molecule 1; PECAM-1 (CD31), platelet endothelial cell adhesion molecule-1; TNF, tumor necrosis factor.


Question 2
2
What adhesion molecules are relevant for emigration of inflammatory cells?

The following molecules are relevant: (1) selectins, including E-selectin and P-selectin on endothelium and L-selectin on neutrophils; P-selectin and E-selectin bind to sialyl Lewis X on the neutrophils, and L-selectin binds to mucin-like molecules on endothelium; (2) immunoglobulin family molecules, including ICAM-1 and VCAM-1 on endothelium; these bind to integrins (e.g., LFA-1, Mac-1, and VLA-4) on leukocytes.

Question 3
3
At what stages do adhesion molecules function?

Selectins are involved in rolling. Integrins are involved in firm adhesion. PECAM-1, another adhesion molecule expressed on leukocytes and endothelial cells, is involved in transmigration.



Question 4
4
What is the long-term clinical consequence of this infection?

This patient is at increased risk for a future infertility and ectopic pregnancy due to scarring that will result in impaired passage of an egg through the tubal lumen.

Question 1
1
What does the presence of plasma cells indicate about the duration of the inflammatory response in this case?

The presence of plasma cells (along with lymphocytes and macrophages) indicates that chronic inflammation is present.


Question 2
2
What mechanisms do neutrophils use to kill microbes?

Two mechanisms are used: (1) oxygen-dependent and (2) oxygen-independent. Oxygen-dependent mechanisms are the most important. They involve generation of superoxide O2-via the NADPH system; this gives rise eventually to H2O2 and OH-. Also generated are myeloperoxidase-dependent HOCl radicals. Oxygen-independent mechanisms include lysozyme, lactoferrin, and defensins.


Question 3
3
Name any defects in neutrophil-killing mechanisms.

Three important defects are (1) defects in adhesion owing to genetic deficiency of β2 integrins (LAD-1); (2) defects in chemotaxis or phagocytosis from an inherited impairment in assembly of microtubules (e.g., Chediak-Higashi syndrome); and (3) defects in microbicidal activity owing to a genetic deficiency of the NADPH oxidase system (called chronic granulomatous disease) or genetic deficiency of myeloperoxidase.

Lung, chronic inflammation - Medium power

This image is not from the patient described in the case, but it is provided to illustrate features that are characteristic of chronic inflammation: infiltration by mononuclear cells, fibrosis, and tissue destruction. The mononuclear cells—in this case, mainly lymphocytes—have aggregated to form a lymphoid nodule. They are also scattered in the areas of fibrosis. Fibrous tissue has replaced the normal lung parenchyma that has been destroyed. Only a few alveolar spaces, separated by markedly fibrotic septa, remain. The normal lung architecture is completely destroyed.

Question 1
1
How do these tissue changes differ from those in acute inflammation?

Chronic inflammation is characterized by (1) infiltration by mononuclear cells (lymphocytes, monocytes, and plasma cells); (2) tissue injury, mediated largely by inflammatory cells; and (3) repair by granulation tissue, leading to scarring.

Question 1
1
Under what conditions are eosinophils prominent in the inflammatory infiltrate?

Eosinophils are prominent in the inflammatory infiltrate in allergic inflammation, parasitic infestations, and some forms of chronic inflammation.


Question 2
2
What are the contents of eosinophil granules?

Eosinophil granules contain major basic protein and eosinophil cationic protein; both are toxic to tissue cells.


Question 3
3
What factors or products are secreted by activated macrophages?

The following are secreted by activated macrophages: (1) enzymes such as acid and neutral proteases, which cause tissue damage; (2) plasma proteins such as complement components and coagulation factors; (3) reactive oxygen species and nitric oxide; (4) arachidonic acid; and (5) cytokines (e.g., interleukin-1 [IL-1], tumor necrosis factor [TNF], and growth factors such as FGF, EGF, and PDGF). These cause fibrogenesis, deposition of extracellular matrix, and angiogenesis.
