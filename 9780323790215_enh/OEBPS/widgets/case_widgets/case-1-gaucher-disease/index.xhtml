Scenario

A 49-year-old woman complains of increasing pain in her right leg and hip. At presentation, she can no longer bear weight on her right leg. Her medical history is unremarkable except that her obstetrician had noted mild splenomegaly during her third pregnancy, when she was 30 years old. She declined any work-up at that time and has felt well since except for some recent abdominal discomfort and the hip pain. Family history reveals that she is Jewish, that her three children are healthy, and that her two siblings are healthy except that her younger brother has "back trouble."

Physical examination reveals marked splenomegaly. Laboratory data include a low platelet count and anemia. Radiologic evaluation of her right hip and leg shows lytic lesions and a probable linear fracture in the femoral head and neck as well as an Erlenmeyer flask deformity of the distal femur. After a transfusion of platelets, she is taken to surgery, where a pathologic fracture of her right femoral neck is confirmed and a bone marrow biopsy is performed. After a frozen-section diagnosis of the bone marrow is made by a surgical pathologist, a splenectomy and a right hip replacement are performed.

Erlenmeyer flask deformity

Upper leg and knee, Gaucher disease - Radiograph

This image shows an Erlenmeyer flask deformity of the distal femur. Widening of the distal femur and thinning of cortical bone, especially in the long bones and vertebrae, are characteristic of Gaucher disease. The bony abnormalities are considered to be related to the numerous lipid-laden macrophages (Gaucher cells) in the bone marrow, but the mechanism of bone deformation and destruction in this disease remains unclear.

Describe the process of normal phagolysosomal fusion and the pathophysiology of lysosomal storage diseases.

Normally, phagocytic cells such as macrophages ingest (phagocytose) pathogens, dying cells, and other debris. This material is stored in a vesicle called a phagosome, made from pinched off portions of the cell membrane. Also stored are autophagosomes filled with worn-out organelles from that cell, such as mitochondria that need to be discarded. The phagosome fuses with a lysosome, which contains digestive enzymes synthesized in the endoplasmic reticulum and trafficked through the Golgi apparatus. The ingested material is then degraded by the lysosomal enzymes.


Figure 5-9 Synthesis and intracellular transport of lysosomal enzymes.

In lysosomal storage diseases, enzymes crucial for the digestion of various substances within the phagolysosome are missing or non-functional due to a mutation in the gene for that enzyme. Without the proper lysosomal enzymes, the substances cannot be digested and they accumulate in the lysosomes, impairing cellular function. Additionally, impaired autophagy (digestion of Golgi) leads to the continued use of worn out Golgis, which results in oxidative damage and free radical generation that causes cellular damage.


Figure 5-10 Pathogenesis of lysosomal storage diseases. In the example shown, a complex substrate is normally degraded by a series of lysosomal enzymes (A, B, and C) into soluble end products. If there is a defi ciency or malfunction of one of the enzymes (e.g., B), catabolism is incomplete and insoluble intermediates accumulate in the lysosomes. In addition to this primary storage, secondary storage and toxic effects result from defective autophagy.

3
What therapeutic approaches may be used to treat Gaucher disease?

The mainstay of treatment is enzyme replacement therapy (ERT) to reduce glucocerebrosidase accumulation. More recently, substrate reduction therapy (SRT) drugs have been approved that decrease the synthesis of the glucocerebrosidase, the substrate for the deficient enzyme. Before the advent of ERT and SRT, splenectomy was commonly used. Additionally, because the fundamental defect resides in phagocytic cells arising from the bone marrow stem cells, allogeneic stem cell transplantation can also be curative. However, it is only used rarely since the advent of ERT.


TARGETED THERAPY BOX: Cutting Gordian Knots – Salvaging Misfolded Proteins

Cutting Gordian Knots: Salvaging Misfolded Proteins

Misfolded proteins are increasingly recognized as the root cause of a wide array of human diseases. Clearly, mutations in the primary amino acid sequence can cause loss of enzyme, or of structural function, as catalytic sites or motifs for protein-protein interactions are disrupted; in some cases, misfolded proteins are recognized as defective and even degraded through endoplasmic reticulum (ER) or ubiquitin-proteasome pathways. Cystic fibrosis, Marfan syndrome, Fabry disease, muscular dystrophy, and retinitis pigmentosa all fall into this general category; misfolded tumor suppressor gene products such as p53 can even contribute to malignancy. In other cases, misfolded proteins cause pathology by accumulating. Excessive intracellular accretion can trigger ER stress responses that culminate in apoptotic suicide; diseases in this group include α1-antitrypsin deficiency, amyotrophic lateral sclerosis, Huntington disease, and diabetes. Conversely, excess misfolded extracellular proteins can cause cell death or dysfunction through the formation of amyloid aggregates, as in Alzheimer disease and Creutzfeld-Jakob spongiform encephalopathy (prion disease).


Gaucher disease (GD) is the most common of the lysosomal storage disorders; it also is an excellent candidate for therapeutic approaches that influence protein folding. GD results from a variety of recessive genetic mutations (more than 150!) leading to misfolded β-gluocerebrosidase enzymes that are either inactive or are prematurely degraded in theER. In most cell types, low-level residual enzyme activity is sufficient to prevent cellular dysfunction. Cells responsible for catabolizing large amounts of glucocerebroside, however, are overwhelmed. Consequently, macrophages progressively accumulate nondegraded glucosylceramide from the membranes of senescent leukocytes and red cells. Besides causing organomegaly, the macrophages also are activated by their glycolipid burden, elaborating pro-inflammatory cytokines. Thus, GD causes bone destruction, thrombocytopenia due to hypersplenism, and central nervous system (CNS) dysfunction attributed to microglial cell cytokine production. Worse, in type II disease, a complete absence of enzyme activity leads to infantile neuronal dysfunction and death consequent to the inability to recycle critical membrane constituents.


The treatment option currently in widest use is somewhat less than ideal. Enzyme replacement therapy(ERT), using recombinant enzymes, takes advantage of the fact that terminal mannose-6-phosphate glycosylation of glucocerebrosidase allows the protein to be bound to surface mannose receptors, endocytosed, and specifically targeted to the lysosomal compartment. Because defective macrophages are the central pathologic problem, ERT restores enzyme activity precisely to where it needs to go. Unfortunately, this therapy is extremely expensive ($300,000 annually) and requires regular intravenous administration, and the enzymes do not cross the blood-brain barrier for treating CNS deficiency.


To the rescue comes the class of pharmacologic chaperones, small molecules that stabilize or reactivate improperly folded glucocerebrosidase. Studies show that if only 10% of normal enzyme activity can be restored, the vast majority of GD-associated pathologic effects can be ameliorated. These agents (isofagomine, N-octyl-β-valienamine) are orally available and inexpensive and cross the blood-brain barrier, making them potentially valuable in therapy for all forms of GD. 1
What are causes of low platelet counts in patients with Gaucher disease?

Low platelet counts may be due to marrow compromise by Gaucher cells or to accelerated destruction of platelets by an enlarged spleen.

Question 1
1
In what other tissues may Gaucher cells accumulate?

Gaucher cells may accumulate in any part of the mononuclear phagocyte system (e.g., liver, lymph nodes, tonsils). In the neuronopathic forms, Gaucher cells are found in the Virchow-Robin spaces in the brain.

1
What is the source of the glucocerebroside accumulation in these cells?

In the spleen, the stored glucocerebrosides are derived from the breakdown of cell membranes of red blood cells and white blood cells.
