Learning Objectives
  • 1. List the clinical settings in which hematopoietic stem cell transplantation is useful.
  • 2. Describe the underlying mechanism of graft rejection and graft-versus-host disease (GVHD).
  • 3. Define the graft-versus-leukemia (GVL) effect, and describe its likely mechanism.
  • 4. Define natural killer cells, and distinguish them from T cells in their interactions with class I major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules.
  • 5. Describe how GVHD can be prevented, and know the complications that arise from the currently used method of GVHD prevention.
  • 6. Describe and distinguish the target organs and tissue changes in acute versus chronic GVHD.

Scenario

A 35-year-old man with acute myelogenous leukemia received allogeneic stem cell transplant from an human leukocyte antigen (HLA)–matched unrelated donor. The donor was female and was gravida 3, para 3. Further post-transplant treatment consisted of daily cyclosporine and methotrexate IV on days 1, 3, 6, and 11. His post-transplant course was complicated by severe pancytopenia, moderate mucositis, and fever, requiring treatment with broad-spectrum antibiotics. On day 15, the patient had early signs of marrow engraftment; circulating neutrophils were noted on the peripheral blood smear. On day 17, the patient developed hyperbilirubinemia, profuse diarrhea, and a maculopapular rash on his face, trunk, proximal extremities, palms, and soles that involved approximately 70% of his body surface area. Biopsy specimens of the skin and rectal mucosa were obtained. The patient was treated with methylprednisolone. Within 1 week, the hyperbilirubinemia, rash, and diarrhea had improved; the methylprednisolone dosage was slowly tapered but continued along with cyclosporine for several weeks due to flares of rash and diarrhea. At day 20, the patient received a 2-week course of ganciclovir for cytomegalovirus prophylaxis. At day 40, he developed fever to 102° F with shaking chills. He was started empirically on broad-spectrum antibiotics, and a blood culture drawn at the time of the temperature spike revealed Pseudomonas aeruginosa. With antibiotic therapy, the infection cleared. By day 100, the patient’s clinical status was stable, and the methylprednisolone was tapered without any flare in rash, diarrhea, or hyperbilirubinemia.


Image 1

Acute graft-versus-host disease (GVHD), skin, post–hematopoietic stem cell transplant - Clinical presentation

This patient is 1 month post transplant. There is a diffuse erythematous rash on his trunk, limbs, palms, soles, and face. GVHD remains the major impediment to successful allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplantation; 10% to 30% of patients with sustained engraftment die of acute GVHD and its complications. Consider the profound immunosuppressive therapy that accompanies the transplant procedure.

Question 1
1
Besides an incompatible graft, how else might such a rash be explained?
Show answer

Rashes may be caused by infection, especially viral, in these immunosuppressed patients. Drug-induced rashes are also common in hospitalized patients.


Question 2
2
What are the primary target organs of acute GVHD?
Show answer

The primary targets are skin, gut epithelium, bile ducts, and lymphoid tissue.


Image 2

DNA analysis of engraftment after hematopoietic stem cell transplantation - graph

Short tandem repeats (STRs) are repeats of a two- to six-nucleotide sequence (e.g., TCAG) that occur in tandem (TCAGTCAGTCAGTCAG). STR are found throughout the human genome at conserved loci and are polymorphic in size. One person may have 14 repeats at a certain loci, and another person may have 20 repeats. For engraftment analysis, total genomic DNA from white blood cells in the blood of the recipient and the donor is purified. Multiplex polymerase chain reaction (PCR) is performed with fluorescently labeled primers targeting 10 STR loci. The products are separated by size via capillary electrophoresis and analyzed with a laser, which detects the fluorescent primer. Only three loci are shown in this figure. Allele 3 at locus 2 and allele 8 at locus 3 are unique to the donor.

Question 1
1
Was engraftment successful?
Show answer

Yes. Engraftment was successful.


Question 2
2
What is the significance of the difference in the pattern at 2 weeks and 6 months?
Show answer

At 2 weeks, some host cells are still present, giving rise to the recipient peaks for alleles 5 and 6. At 6 months, all DNA seems to be of donor origin.


Question 3
3
What are the primary determinants of successful hematopoietic stem cell transplantation?
Show answer

The primary determinants are (1) closeness of the human leukocyte antigen (HLA) types, (2) severity of the graft-versus-host disease, and (3) ability of the host to withstand the conditioning regimen.


Image 3

Dependence of acute graft-versus-host disease (GVHD) on human leukocyte antigen (HLA) disparity - Graph

The importance of HLA matching in GVHD is illustrated. The figure shows the number of mismatched HLA loci versus the incidence of GVHD. Reactivity of donor lymphocytes present in the bone marrow graft against host tissues is the basis of acute or chronic GVHD. Therefore, the greater the number of target antigens, the higher the incidence (and the greater the severity) of GVHD.

Question 1
1
To prevent GVHD, can one simply deplete donor marrow of lymphocytes prior to transplantation?
Show answer

One can deplete donor T cells, but unfortunately, the catch is that this impairs engraftment.


Question 2
2
We have learned that donor T cells are necessary for bone marrow engraftment. What might be the mechanism for this phenomenon?
Show answer

The exact mechanism is not known, but two possibilities are likely: (1) the donor T cells activated by host antigens could secrete cytokines like granulocyte macrophage colony-stimulating factor (GM-CSF) that help the growth of stem cells, and (2) the donor T cells could inactivate host anti-donor natural killer cells and T cells.


Question 3
3
Does the presence of donor lymphocytes in the inoculum have other benefits?
Show answer

Yes. It appears that donor T cells mediate an antileukemia effect (graft-versus-leukemia). Thus, if T cells are depleted from the marrow, there is a greater likelihood of leukemia relapse (in patients who have leukemia).

Bone marrow, post bone marrow transplant - Low power

The left frame shows markedly hypocellular marrow post purging with chemotherapy or immediately post transplantation. Rare hematopoietic elements remain among the surviving adipose component. The right frame shows normocellular marrow 3 months post transplantation. Engraftment has clearly occurred. Functionally, erythroid and granulocytic compartments are essentially reconstituted. Immune function, however, may be compromised for much longer. If graft-versus-host disease (GVHD) supervenes, the period of immunologic recovery is even more prolonged.

Question 1
1
What are the major risks to the patient during this interval, and what special support does the patient require?
Show answer

Immediately after transplant, the host marrow and immune system are suppressed due to the conditioning regimen used for the transplant (i.e., chemotherapy, radiation). The major risks are infection and bleeding. The special support needed is blood transfusions, antibiotics, and isolation from microbes by placing patients in laminar-flow rooms.

Question 2
2
How would you expect lymphoid tissues to appear during early GVHD? Late GVHD?
Show answer

Early during GVHD, the donor cells are activated in the host lymphoid tissues, and they undergo proliferation, so the lymphoid tissue appears full of activated cells. Later, however, there is hypoplasia and atrophy of the lymphoid tissue; lymph nodes, spleen, tonsils, and thymus all look empty.

Due either to the condition underlying the transplant or to pretransplant preparative treatment, there is a severe pancytopenic state for 2 to 4 weeks following the bone marrow transplant.

Question 1
1
How long does impairment of immunologic function last post transplant with acute graft-versus-host disease (GVHD)?
Show answer

Impairment lasts from months to about a year with acute GVHD, and it lasts even longer in those who develop chronic GVHD.


Image 6
Enlarged epithelial cell
Intranuclear inclusion

Lung, cytomegalovirus (CMV) pneumonitis post bone marrow transplant - High power

CMV-infected respiratory epithelial cells are 2 to 4 times larger than normal and often have an 8 to 10 µm intranuclear inclusion that is eccentrically placed and surrounded by a clear halo, resulting in an owl's-eye appearance.


Image 7
Spongiosis
Early bulla formation
Mononuclear infiltrate
Apoptotic keratinocyte

Skin, acute graft-versus-host disease (GVHD) - Medium power

The skin can serve as a useful early warning system for acute GVHD. The characteristic rash generally appears within 6 weeks of transplantation. Histologically, one sees vacuolar degeneration and necrosis of basal cells and acanthocytes. Individual apoptotic keratinocytes are found (right), and these are often seen in association with lymphocytes (termed satellitosis). The resultant loosening of intercellular junctions creates increased spaces between keratinocytes, a feature histologically called spongiosis. As these spaces coalesce, small blisters (bullae) may form. In extreme cases, this can lead to widespread epidermolysis.

Question 1
1
Within the skin, what are the actual initial target cells?
Show answer

The actual target cells are cells in the basal layer. These are the stem cells of the skin.


Question 2
2
With what other conditions are the histologic findings consistent under these clinical circumstances?
Show answer

Viral infections of the skin can produce a similar clinical picture and may be difficult to distinguish from GVHD.


Image 8
Scattered apoptotic epithelial cells
Mononuclear inflammatory infiltrate

Colon, mild acute graft-versus-host disease (GVHD) - Medium power

Another primary target of acute GVHD is the entire gastrointestinal tract. The most severe changes are often found in the distal ileum. Dilation of the mucosal glands is the first change. Also found are individual apoptotic epithelial cells in intestinal crypts, frequently in close apposition to mononuclear cells.

Question 1
1
“Also found are individual apoptotic epithelial cells in intestinal crypts, frequently in close apposition to mononuclear cells.” What does this remind you of?
Show answer

The histology of colonic epithelium in acute GVHD is reminiscent of epithelial damage in the skin. In both cases, lymphocytes cause apoptosis of epithelial cells.


Question 2
2
By analogy to skin, can you guess what the original target cell is thought to be?
Show answer

The target cells are the stem cells within the crypts.


Question 3
3
Do you think there is an increased chronic inflammatory infiltrate of the lamina propria?
Show answer

No. There is only a modest chronic inflammatory cell infiltrate. This seems paradoxical in view of the active GVH reaction. It seems that a lot of the damage in GVHD is mediated by release of cytokines from the donor anti-host cells.


Question 4
4
What clinical symptoms might be associated with GVH effects on this target system?
Show answer

The primary symptom of colonic epithelial damage is bloody diarrhea.

Image 10

Colon, severe acute graft-versus-host disease (GVHD) - High power

The epithelial lining of the gland is attenuated and disorganized. The gland lumen is filled with sloughed epithelial cells and debris. Note the relative sparseness of the mononuclear cell infiltrate despite the severe damage. This is often characteristic of GVHD.

Question 1
1
What does this indicate about the patient's immune status?
Show answer

The paucity of immune lymphocytes in the colon suggests that the patient may be immunosuppressed by GVHD and immunosuppressive drugs such as cyclosporine.


Question 2
2
What, if not direct cytotoxicity by the infiltrate, might account for the severe damage?
Show answer

Cytokines released from the sensitized cells cause damage. These could include tumor necrosis factor (TNF) and interleukin (IL)-1.

In extreme cases, focal mucosal gland loss becomes widespread and progresses to total denudation of the mucosal surface. Such cases can be fatal.

Image 12
Liver parenchyma hepatocytes
Portal triads

Liver, acute graft-versus-host disease (GVHD) - Low power

Note that the portal triads contain a significant mononuclear infiltrate, though, as in other affected tissues, it is not robust. The major affected cell in the liver is within the portal triads. Hepatocellular damage can also be found, but it is less typical and harder to separate from effects of cytotoxic drugs or infections.

Question 1
1
The major affected cell in the liver is within the portal triads. Which is it?
Show answer

In the liver, bile duct epithelium is the primary target in GVHD.


Question 2
2
What might be a clinical liver sign in this patient?
Show answer

Jaundice might be a sign.

Liver, acute graft-versus-host disease (GVHD) - High power

The small bile ducts show changes ranging from irregular cell and nuclear shape and disorganization to necrosis. Lumens may contain necrotic debris but rarely have bile plugs. Of all the changes in the liver, these are the ones most closely correlated with GVHD.
