Scenario

An 18-year-old man visited the dermatologist after developing itchy, scaly, red papules on the skin of his elbows, scalp, and knees over several months. When the scale was lifted from one of the plaques, multiple minute bleeding points became visible. A biopsy was performed at the edge of one of the lesions and confirmed the clinical impression. Therapy was subsequently started and resulted in partial remission of the patient's symptoms. In the following years, the patient developed involvement at other sites including the abdomen and intergluteal cleft. While vacationing in a tropical area, the patient found that swimming improved his symptoms.

Image 1
Well-demarcated plaques with silver-white scales

Skin, psoriasis – Clinical presentation

The skin shows typical well-demarcated plaques covered by silver-white scales. The scaling is surrounded by erythema. These lesions can be described as erythematosquamous lesions.

Question 1
1
Which other two common dermatoses can show erythematosquamous lesions?
Show answer

The other common erythematosquamous dermatoses are eczematous dermatitis and dermatomycoses. The silver-white color and the sharp demarcation are very characteristic of psoriasis. Eczematous dermatitis is often less well circumscribed, and it can include vesicles in addition to erythema and desquamation, especially in early stages. In dermatomycoses, the lesions often are round with a pronounced red, scaly border.

Skin, psoriasis – Low power

A biopsy specimen of one of the lesions shows psoriasiform epidermal hyperplasia with elongated rete ridges. Hyperkeratosis is present, and the superficial dermis contains a perivascular infiltrate.

Question 1
1
At low magnification, what is the histologic differential diagnosis?
Show answer

The differential diagnosis should include psoriasis, chronic spongiotic dermatitis, and dermatomycoses. Features that would suggest psoriasis at low power include uniform hyperplasia of the rete ridges with thinning of the epidermis above the dermal papillae. At high power, parakeratosis (retention of nuclei in the stratum corneum), the absence of a granular layer, and neutrophils in the stratum corneum are typical. In chronic spongiotic dermatitis, the hyperplasia of the rete ridges is less regular, and the granular layer is generally expanded. The inflammatory infiltrate is generally scant. Dermatomycoses can be excluded with a periodic acid–Schiff stain, which highlights fungal organisms, typically in the stratum corneum.

Skin, psoriasis – High power

Neutrophils are present in a thick layer of parakeratosis, and the stratum granulosum is almost absent.

Question 1
1
What are the groups of neutrophils within the stratum corneum called?
Show answer

These groups of neutrophils are Munro microabscesses.


Question 2
2
What would be the diagnosis if these microabscesses were much larger?
Show answer

The diagnosis would be pustular psoriasis.

Skin, lichen planus – Clinical presentation

Lichen planus presents with pruritic, purple, polygonal papules. Often there is confluence of the papules. Most frequently, the lesions are seen on the wrists and the lower arms. Patients with lichen planus often also have mucosal lesions.

Question 1
1
Describe the clinical presentation of mucosal lesions in lichen planus.
Show answer

Mucosal lesions in lichen planus affect the oral mucosa and the genital mucosa, especially the glans penis. The mucosal lesions are clinically different from the purple, polygonal papules seen on the skin. White, reticulated (net-like) lesions can be seen on the oral and genital mucosa.

Skin, lichen planus – High power

High magnification of a skin biopsy specimen shows a thickened epidermis with marked hyperkeratosis and an expanded granular layer. A brisk, band-like lymphocytic infiltrate is present at the dermal-epidermal junction with destruction of the basal keratinocytes. The necrotic keratinocytes are deeply eosinophilic and shrunken. These characteristic cells are typical of lichen planus and are known as colloid or Civatte bodies. Lichen planus generally resolves spontaneously a few years after onset. It typically leaves zones of postinflammatory hyperpigmentation.

Question 1
1
Considering the histology of an active lesion, explain why lichen planus leaves hyperpigmentation.
Show answer

In active lesions of lichen planus, there is an impressive destruction of the basal layer of the epidermis. In this layer, keratinocytes contain the largest amount of melanin pigment. The pigment is present in the keratinocytes just above the nucleus to prevent nuclear damage from UV radiation. As keratinocytes are destroyed, they release their melanin pigment into the upper dermis where it is taken up by macrophages. The melanin-containing macrophages give the clinical appearance of hyperpigmentation.
