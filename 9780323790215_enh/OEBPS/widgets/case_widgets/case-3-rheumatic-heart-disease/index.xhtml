Rheumatic Heart Disease

Learning Objectives

  • 1. Review cardiac valve anatomy. Define valve stenosis and valve insufficiency.
  • 2. Describe the pathology and natural history of mitral valve prolapse. Know the characteristic murmur of mitral valve prolapse and the most important complications associated with mitral valve prolapse. Know how common this condition is.
  • 3. Describe the etiology and clinical symptoms of acute rheumatic fever.
  • 4. Describe the pathology and natural history of acute rheumatic fever and rheumatic heart disease. Describe an Aschoff body. Know which parts of the heart are affected in acute rheumatic fever. Describe the long-term consequences.
  • 5. Describe the pathology and natural history of chronic rheumatic heart disease and know which valves are most often involved. Describe the pathology and pathophysiology of postrheumatic mitral stenosis.
  • 6. Define valvulitis, endocarditis, and vegetation. List the components of a vegetation.
  • 7. Compare and contrast acute endocarditis, subacute endocarditis, and nonbacterial thrombotic endocarditis with respect to:
    • a. predisposing conditions
    • b. valves involved
    • c. morphology of the vegetations
    • d. complications

Scenario

A 10-year-old girl was brought to a pediatrician because of a mild sore throat. The pediatrician noticed some redness, swabbed her throat, tested for group A streptococci in the office, sent blood for anti-streptolysin O titer, and prescribed treatment. The child returned 3 weeks later with fever and painful swelling of the knee joints, followed by pain and swelling of elbow joints. Physical examination revealed a pericardial friction rub and a murmur suggestive of mitral regurgitation. The anti-streptolysin O titer was elevated over the previous value. The child was hospitalized and treated with antibiotics and anti-inflammatory drugs. The patient recovered and did well over the next several decades.

In her 30s, the patient began to have dyspnea on exertion, which slowly increased in severity in her 40s. In the months prior to her hospital admission for this and related symptoms, she had awakened at night feeling short of breath. She had one fainting spell, followed by an episode of temporary loss of vision in one eye. On admission to the hospital, she was very short of breath and had coughed up some blood-tinged sputum. Physical examination and laboratory tests suggested narrowing of the mitral valve orifice. X-ray of her chest showed an enlarged left atrium and pulmonary edema. Cardiac surgery was offered, but she refused. She was treated medically for several days and discharged, feeling better but with some residual limitation of cardiac function.

The week before her next admission, she went to a dentist because of tooth pain, and underwent a dental extraction. She returned to the hospital with high spiking fevers and overt signs and symptoms of heart failure.

Acute rheumatic fever - Schematic

The inflammatory lesions of acute rheumatic fever affect all three parts of the heart: endocardium (especially the valves), myocardium, and epicardium. Endocardial involvement leads to the formation of vegetations (called verrucae) along the line of closure of the affected valve. The mitral valve is commonly affected, although in many cases multiple valves are involved. Myocardial involvement takes the form of myocarditis with widely disseminated focal inflammatory lesions (called Aschoff bodies) scattered about in the interstitium, often in a perivascular location. The pericarditis of acute rheumatic fever begins with similar inflammatory lesions of the epicardial connective tissues, and, in many cases, is associated with a fibrinous or serofibrinous exudate. The pericarditis of acute rheumatic fever usually resolves without lesions, but, in rare cases, it can result in a constrictive pericarditis with dense fibrous adhesions that bind the parietal to the visceral pericardium.

Question 1
1
What is the significance of the patient's history of rheumatic fever as a child? What are the primary symptoms of acute rheumatic fever?
Show answer

The patient's childhood acute rheumatic fever is the cause of her present-day mitral stenosis. Post-rheumatic changes account for most cases of mitral stenosis. The major clinical manifestations of acute rheumatic fever are migratory polyarthritis, carditis, subcutaneous nodules, erythema marginatum, and Sydenham chorea. The attack rate for acute rheumatic fever is approximately 3% after infection with group A beta-hemolytic streptococci.

Question 1
1
What is the etiology of these inflammatory lesions?
Show answer

Acute rheumatic fever results from a host immune response to group A Streptococcal antigens that cross-react with host proteins. After a streptococcal infection (pharyngitis), antibodies to streptococcal M proteins develop. These antibodies cross-react with cardiac glycoprotein antigens. This causes complement activation and recruitment of inflammatory cells that cause both T-cell and antibody-mediated damage to heart tissue.


Question 2
2
Explain their predominantly interstitial location.
Show answer

The glycoproteins with which the antibodies react are located predominantly within interstitial connective tissue.

Aschoff or Anitschkow cells
Aschoff body

Heart, Aschoff body - High power

Inflammatory lesions are found widely scattered in the valves, pericardium, and myocardium of patients who die of acute rheumatic fever (ARF). The cellular aggregate seen here is typical of the myocardial lesions found, and is called an Aschoff body (AB). The typical AB contains plump histiocytes (Aschoff or Anitschkow cells), some of which are multinucleated (Aschoff giant cells). The predominance of histiocytic cells, some epithelioid, suggests that the AB is a form of granuloma. Aschoff bodies may also contain lymphocytes and plasma cells. A central zone of fibrinoid necrosis is seen in ABs in early stages of ARF. Widespread fibrinoid necrosis and inflammation of the valve substance leads to damage of the overlying endothelial cells. With endothelial damage, thrombosis is initiated, and vegetations form along the closing edge of the valve where the leaflets slam together. This endocardial damage heals with dense fibrous scars, which progress over time to look like the valves in the images that follow.

What is the basis of thrombosis in this setting?
Show answer

In the dilated atrium with a stenotic mitral valve, the blood stagnates. Stasis is a factor in thrombogenesis. In addition, there may be endocardial damage resulting from regurgitant jets; this can also predispose to thrombosis.

Question 3
3
Why is this patient feeling so short of breath? Why is her left atrium enlarged? Why are her lungs abnormal? Why does she get short of breath at night? Does she have heart failure? What kind of heart failure does she have?
Show answer

The patient is feeling short of breath because she has severe mitral stenosis. There is fusion of the commissures and fibrotic closure of the mitral valve. As the cross-sectional area of the orifice of the affected valve grows smaller, there is progressive obstruction to blood flow from the left atrium to the left ventricle. Valve orifices smaller than 2 cm2 are usually symptomatic. Blood begins to back up first into the left atrium, which becomes enlarged. Pressure increases in the atrium and pulmonary vascular tree. Elevated pulmonary vascular pressure leads to pulmonary edema and explains her dyspneic symptoms. Eventually the pressure backs up into the right heart. With longstanding severe mitral stenosis, permanent structural changes in the pulmonary vasculature may limit flow even after the valve is fixed. Severe mitral stenosis also leads to decreased filling of the left ventricle and low cardiac output, leading to her symptoms of left heart failure.

Question 1
1
What is the significance of atrial fibrillation in this patient?
Show answer

Atrial fibrillation is a dire consequence in patients with aortic and mitral stenosis. In the normal heart, left atrial contraction contributes little to left ventricular filling. In the case of mitral stenosis, the left atrium has a major responsibility for pumping blood across the tiny mitral orifice and filling the left ventricle. Atrial fibrillation takes away almost all of the beneficial effects of atrial contraction during diastole; cardiac output can acutely decrease, precipitating acute pulmonary edema. Atrial fibrillation also predisposes to the formation of thrombi, as seen in the Image 4.

Lung, hemosiderin-laden macrophages - High power

Chronic left heart failure of many different etiologies is often associated with elevated left atrial pressure and elevated pulmonary venous and pulmonary capillary pressure. With mitral stenosis, left atrial pressures can be particularly high. When alveolar capillary pressure exceeds plasma oncotic pressure, fluid leaks out into the alveoli and the patient develops pulmonary edema. With severe chronic pulmonary congestion, there can also be intermittent alveolar hemorrhage. These hemosiderin-laden macrophages are the residue of prior alveolar hemorrhage; they represent microscopic evidence of chronic severe left heart failure.

Question 1
1
What would most likely be the causative organisms? What are the criteria for diagnosis of infective endocarditis? What should be done to prevent endocarditis? List three general factors that predispose to endocarditis.
Show answer

Endocarditis is a possibility any time a valve is hemodynamically abnormal. In previously abnormal hearts, as in this case, streptococci, most often S. viridans, are often causative. Staphylococci can also cause endocarditis and are often identified in cases of acute endocarditis. Although most cases of infective endocarditis are due to gram-positive bacteria, gram-negative organisms, fungi, and a long list of other organisms have been reported. In many cases, the bacteria form biofilms, which make it very difficult to penetrate with antibiotic therapy. Patients with abnormal heart valves and congenital shunts should have antibiotic prophylaxis before procedures likely to produce bacteremia. Conditions that predispose to endocarditis include: (1) abnormal flow (e.g., hemodynamically abnormal valves, congenital shunts), (2) bacteremia (e.g., instrumentation, intravenous drug abuse, surgery), and (3) abnormal immune response (e.g., neutropenia, immunodeficiency, immunosuppression).

Targeted Therapy Box – Biofilm: We’ve been slimed

Biofilm: We’ve Been Slimed

Bacteria exist either as single-celled “planktonic” free-floating organisms or as biofilms—complex adherent multicellular communities in a self-produced exopolysaccharide matrix “slime.” Biofilms occur anywhere that liquids and solids meet; good examples are the slippery surface of a boulder in a stream and the grunge that accumulates in a waste pipe. Significantly, roughly 80% of all human infections—including wound infections, catheter infections, endocarditis, urinary tract infections, and infections of implanted devices—are caused by biofilms. The numbers translate into almost 100,000 biofilm-related deaths each year in the United States.

The cell-cell interactions of bacteria in biofilms are regulated by the microbial production of quorum-sensing proteins that bind to specific receptors and coordinate the subsequent communal behavior. Up to 15% of the microbial genome is differentially regulated in the free versus biofilm forms of the same microorganism (approximately half are activated and the other half repressed). As a consequence of these genetic differences, bacteria in biofilms are over 1000 times more resistant to antimicrobial agents than their planktonic counterparts. This is ascribed to a less permeable microenvironment (slime), decreased microbial growth rates with a small population of “persister” cells, and upregulation of stress responses, in addition to a more efficient transfer of antimicrobial resistance genes. Also noteworthy (and disquieting) is that bacteria cultures in hospital microbiology labs—forming the basis for standard antibiotic sensitivity analysis—are more akin to planktonic bacteria than they are to biofilm.

Clearly, a biofilm infection poses a significant problem. Not only is the microbe hunkered down in a difficult-to-penetrate slime, and has upregulated genes that allow it to resist antimicrobial eradication, but the host response is likely to be prolonged, with significant potential “innocent bystander” damage. At present, the time-honored approach is to remove whatever surface is harboring the biofilm, followed by aggressive antibiotic treatment. Although this is reasonable for a catheter-based infection, it is substantially more problematic for an implanted heart valve or orthopedic prosthesis.

Accordingly, although not yet widely utilized, effective disruption of bacterial biofilms is a more-than-worthy therapeutic goal. A number of new therapies are under active (and even frenzied) investigation in animal models:

  • Silver-coating (silver has broad antimicrobial activity) or antibiotic impregnation of catheters is of some benefit in frustrating the initial colonization that precedes biofilm formation.
  • Applied electric currents or ultrasound vibration approaches also augment antibiotic therapies by partially disrupting biofilm colonies, and by increasing bacterial membrane permeability.
  • More sophisticated tactics involve antisense molecules that silence the bacterial genes that are upregulated in biofilms, or synthetic decoy molecules that interfere with the quorum-sensing apparatus.
  • Perhaps most exciting is the possibility that bacteriophage viruses can be conscripted to the battle; besides being potent and selective bacterial adversaries, these agents can contribute to the normal regulation of biofilms by producing polysaccharidases that hydrolyze slime.

Question 1
1
What are the components of a vegetation? What are the predisposing conditions for their formation?
Show answer

A vegetation is an intracardiac thrombus. They often form on the valves. They form when the valves are hemodynamically abnormal or there is injury to the endocardium. Vegetations may occur secondary to diseases such as rheumatic fever, lupus, or congenital heart diseases. Hypercoagulable states sometimes associated with chronic illnesses can also predispose to cardiac vegetations.

Figure 12-24 Comparison of the four major forms of vegetative endocarditis. The rheumatic fever phase of rheumatic heart disease (RHD) is marked by small, warty vegetations along the lines of closure of the valve leaflets. Infective endocarditis (IE) is characterized by large, irregular masses on the valve cusps that can extend onto the chordae (see Fig. 12-25 A ). Nonbacterial thrombotic endocarditis (NBTE) typically exhibits small, bland vegetations, usually attached at the line of closure. One or many may be present (see Figure 12-26 ). Libman-Sacks endocarditis (LSE) has small or medium-sized vegetations on either or both sides of the valve leaflets.

Question 1
1
What other complications are encountered in infective endocarditis?
Show answer

The complications of infective endocarditis may be cardiac or extracardiac. Cardiac complications include valve insufficiency, myocardial abscesses (ring abscesses), suppurative pericarditis, and, rarely, a hemopericardium. Embolic complications include infarcts and abscesses. Their location depends on whether the right-side or left-side valves are affected. Renal complications include, in addition to infarcts and abscesses, focal glomerulonephritis (owing to microemboli) and diffuse glomerulonephritis secondary to antigen-antibody complex formation.


Question 2
2
What might be a typical social history for a patient with tricuspid valve endocarditis?
Show answer

Right-sided endocarditis typically occurs in intravenous drug abusers because pathogens enter the venous circulation and reach the right side of the heart.
