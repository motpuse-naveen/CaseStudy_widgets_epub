Scenario

A 29-year-old motorcyclist skidded on an icy patch in the road and was thrown onto the pavement. Witnesses reported that, after impact and a brief period of consciousness, the patient became disoriented and rapidly lost consciousness. Emergency medical personnel stabilized the patient and transported him to a local hospital.

In the emergency department, the patient was found to have a fracture of the right lateral skull. Ophthalmologic examination revealed a dilated right pupil. An emergent CT scan revealed an accumulation of blood between the brain and the skull (Image 1). While the patient was being prepared for emergency surgery, his breathing became labored and gasping, and progressed to respiratory arrest. Resuscitative efforts were unsuccessful.

Postmortem examination confirmed the presence of a skull fracture involving the squamous portion of the right temporal bone. Approximately 100 mL of fresh blood was present between the dura mater and the skull (Image 2). Additional changes included generalized brain edema, right uncal gyral herniation, hemorrhage in the right medial occipital cortex, cerebellar tonsillar herniation, and flattening of the ventral brainstem.

Head, skull fracture and hemorrhage ¬ CT scans

These CT images, taken in the emergency department, reveal evidence of a right lateral skull fracture and associated hemorrhage in the epidural space. Epidural hematomas, or accumulations of blood between the external surface of the dura mater and the inner surface of the skull, are almost always traumatic in origin. They typically follow a fracture of the lateral skull, associated with laceration of one or more branches of the middle meningeal artery.

Question 1
1
What are the three main types of hemorrhages or hematomas that occur between the brain and skull? Which types are most often the result of trauma?

The three main types of hemorrhages or hematomas that may be located between the brain and skull are subarachnoid, subdural, and epidural. Subdural and epidural hemorrhages are almost always the result of trauma. Although subarachnoid hemorrhage is most often the result of trauma, it may also be caused by rupture of an aneurysm or an arteriovenous malformation hemorrhage.

Question 2
2
Given the history and physical findings of this patient, which type does he have? What is the etiology of this lesion? Why was emergency surgery necessary?

This patient has an acute epidural hematoma, which occurs after rupture of one of the meningeal arteries (usually the middle meningeal artery), almost always as the result of skull fracture. Emergency surgery is necessary because if the epidural hematoma is not drained, it produces, in rapid succession, uncal herniation, tonsillar herniation, medullary compression, respiratory arrest, and death.

Question 1
1
Why are lateral skull fractures so often associated with tears of the middle meningeal artery?

The branches of the middle meningeal artery lie in contact with the inner surface of the lateral skull area and are commonly torn when the bone in this area fractures. The lateral part of the skull contains the squamous portion of the temporal bone, a thin bone segment that is especially vulnerable to fracture in the setting of local blunt force injury.


Question 2
2
Why are epidural hematomas dangerous?

Because they are arterial in origin, epidural hematomas tend to enlarge very rapidly. This rapid enlargement is manifested clinically by rapid loss of consciousness after a brief lucid interval, as was the case with this patient. Similar to any expanding intracranial mass, epidural hematomas cause an increase in intracranial pressure that is associated with decreased perfusion of the brain and potentially catastrophic compression of vital respiratory centers (e.g., the cardiorespiratory centers in the brainstem).

Question 1
1
What is the pathogenesis of subdural hematomas?

Subdural hematomas develop between the arachnoid mater and the inner surface of the dura whenever tears occur in the veins (bridging veins) connecting the surface of the brain to one of the dural sinuses (usually the superior sagittal sinus). Such tears are often caused by rapid deceleration of the head, as might occur in a head-on motor vehicle collision. Preexisting cerebral atrophy increases the risk of subdural hemorrhage. In some cases of subdural hematoma, particularly hematomas occurring in elderly individuals, there may be no clear history of previous head trauma. The hematomas may organize over time to form encapsulated masses between the arachnoid and inner surface of the dura mater; these are termed chronic subdural hematomas. Chronic subdural hematomas are often bilateral.

Question 2
2
How is this different from the pathogenesis of epidural hematomas?

Epidural hematomas are caused by arterial tears, whereas subdural hematomas are of venous origin.


Figure 28-10 Epidural hematoma (left) in which rupture of a meningeal artery, usually associated with a skull fracture, leads to accumulation of arterial blood between the dura and the skull. In a subdural hematoma (right) , damage to bridging veins between the brain and the superior sagittal sinus leads to the accumulation of blood between the dura and the arachnoid.

Question 3
3
What is the other type of hemorrhage that can develop between the surface of the brain and the skull?

Subarachnoid hemorrhage, or bleeding into the space between the pia and arachnoid mater, is the third type of hemorrhage that may occur between the surface of the brain and the skull and is also usually caused by head trauma. Traumatic subarachnoid hemorrhages are usually caused by bruises of the surface of the brain, termed contusions. Such areas of brain injury cause leakage of blood into the superficial brain parenchyma and, from there, into the adjacent subarachnoid space. Subarachnoid hemorrhage may also occur in the absence of trauma. Ruptured saccular (berry) aneurysms (discussed in Case 1: Cerebrovascular Disease), are the most common cause of spontaneous subarachnoid hemorrhage.

Question 1
1
In addition to expanding mass lesions and generalized brain parenchymal edema, what is another important cause of increased intracranial pressure?

Any process that obstructs the normal flow of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) can increase intracranial pressure. Examples include tumors and inflammatory conditions within the ventricular system and processes that interfere with normal absorption of CSF by the arachnoid granulations (e.g., meningitis, organizing subarachnoid hemorrhage). The CSF accumulates behind the point of obstruction in such conditions, causing expansion of the ventricles (obstructive hydrocephalus) and increased intracranial pressure.

Question 1
1
What are some important consequences of medial temporal herniation?

The most important clinical consequence of medial temporal herniation is compression of the ipsilateral third cranial (oculomotor) nerve, which travels between the medial surface of the temporal lobe and the edge of the tentorium cerebelli. Because parasympathetic fibers controlling papillary constriction travel in the periphery of the oculomotor nerve, compression of this structure causes the ipsilateral pupil (in this case, the right pupil) to dilate owing to unopposed sympathetic input to the pupillary muscles. Ipsilateral pupillary dilation is an important clinical manifestation of temporal lobe herniation. Herniation of the temporal lobe may also compress the posterior cerebral artery, causing hemorrhagic infarcts in the posterior cerebral artery territory (medial and inferior occipital lobe) on the same side as the herniation. Patients who survive such episodes may sustain permanent loss of vision because of ischemic injury to the primary visual cortex, located on the medial aspect of the occipital lobe.

Question 1
1
What are the important consequences of cerebellar tonsillar herniation?

Cerebellar tonsillar herniation is a potentially catastrophic lesion resulting from associated compression and distortion of the adjacent brainstem, which contains vital respiratory centers. Compromise of these areas may cause respiratory arrest, a common terminal event in patients with expanding intracranial lesions. Caudal displacement of the cerebellum and brainstem may also be associated with hemorrhagic necrosis of the cerebellar tonsils and with secondary hemorrhage within the brainstem (Duret hemorrhage, illustrated in the next Image 9).

Question 1
1
What is the pathogenesis of this form of hemorrhage?

The pathogenesis of Duret hemorrhage has been the source of considerable debate. The most critical factor in its development is probably caudal displacement of the brainstem relative to the basilar artery, particularly whenever there is asymmetric mass effect above the tentorium cerebelli. Such caudal displacement causes kinking of the penetrating median and paramedian pontine arteries that branch off the basilar artery and resultant necrosis and hemorrhage in the territories supplied by those vessels.


Question 2
2
How does the appearance of Duret hemorrhage differ from that of a primary brainstem hemorrhage?

In contrast to primary pontine hemorrhages, which usually form globular hematomas, the Duret hemorrhage is characterized by irregular areas of hemorrhage and parenchymal softening in a median and paramedian distribution reflecting compromise of the penetrating pontine arteries supplying these areas.

Question 1
1
What are some common sites for brain contusions?

Although they may occur anywhere on the surface of the brain, contusions are encountered most commonly in the frontal poles, the orbital surfaces of the frontal lobes, the temporal poles, the inferior temporal lobes, and the region of the Sylvian fissure.


Question 2
2
What is the difference between a coup and contrecoup contusion?

The term coup contusion refers to a contusion occurring immediately at the site of impact (in the absence of a skull fracture). These contusions tend to occur most frequently in situations in which a relatively immobile head is struck by a rapidly moving object (e.g., a baseball bat). In contrast, contrecoup contusions are contusions that arise on the surface of the brain at a site some distance from the point of impact. These contusions occur most commonly when a moving head strikes a relatively immobile object (e.g., a falling head striking the floor). It is impossible to reliably distinguish a coup from a countercoup contusion and the nature of the traumatic insult based on examination of the brain alone.


Question 3
3
What would be the appearance of the cerebrospinal fluid in this patient?

The cerebrospinal fluid would be bloody. As noted previously, trauma is the most common cause of subarachnoid hemorrhage.
