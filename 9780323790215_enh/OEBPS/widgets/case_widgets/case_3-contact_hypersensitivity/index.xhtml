Contact Hypersensitivity

Learning Objectives

  • 1. Define the four main categories of hypersensitivity reactions and recall multiple examples of each type.
  • 2. Understand the difference between polymorphonuclear and mononuclear inflammatory infiltrates and in which settings you would expect to see each.
  • 3. Describe the pathophysiology of type IV hypersensitivity reactions, including the role of antigen-presenting cells, naïve CD4+ cells, IFN-γ, and macrophages.

Question 1
1
What subtype of T cells are these?
Show answer

In most forms of delayed hypersensitivity, CD4+ T cells are predominant; however, in certain types of delayed hypersensitivity (e.g., contact hypersensitivity), CD8+ T cells have also been shown to be involved. In this case, there is a mixture of CD4+ and CD8+ T cells.

Given the edema shown in Image 2, what elements suggest the process is chronic as opposed to acute inflammation?

Pathophysiology of contact hypersensitivity - Schematic

Legend: Diamond, Hapten (contact allergen); E, effector T lymphocyte; M, memory T lymphocyte; N, näive lymphocyte.

Langerhans cells are the principal antigen-presenting cells in the epidermis. When a contact allergen comes into contact with the skin, the Langerhans cell internalizes it and expresses it on its membrane. The Langerhans cells migrate to lymph nodes through lymphatics. In the lymph node, the Langerhans cells present the antigen to naive lymphocytes (CD4+ T cells, TH1), which then proliferate into memory and effector cells. These cells enter the circulation and home into the dermis in the elicitation phase. They secrete the cytokines IL-2, which causes T cells to proliferate, and IFN-γ, which activates monocytes-macrophages. The monocytes damage the cells in the epidermis.

Question 1
1
To which category of hypersensitivity reactions does contact hypersensitivity belong?
Show answer

Contact hypersensitivity is a type IV (cell-mediated) hypersensitivity reaction.


Table 6.1 Mechanisms of Hypersensitivity Reactions


Type Immune Mechanisms Histopathologic Lesions Prototypical Disorders
Immediate (type I) hypersensitivity Production of IgE antibody → immediate release of vasoactive amines and other mediators from mast cells; later recruitment of inflammatory cells Vascular dilation, edema, smooth muscle contraction, mucus production, tissue injury, inflammation Anaphylaxis, allergies, bronchial asthma (atopic forms)
Antibody-mediated (type II) hypersensitivity Production of IgG, IgM → binds to antigen on target cell or tissue → phagocytosis or lysis of target cell by activated complement or Fc receptors; recruitment of leukocytes Phagocytosis and lysis of cells; inflammation; in some diseases, functional derangements without cell or tissue injury Autoimmune hemolytic anemia, Goodpasture syndrome
Immune complex–mediated (type III) hypersensitivity Deposition of antigen-antibody complexes → complement activation → recruitment of leukocytes by complement products and Fc receptors → release of enzymes and other toxic molecules Inflammation, necrotizing vasculitis (fibrinoid necrosis) Systemic lupus erythematosus, some forms of glomerulonephritis, serum sickness, Arthus reaction
Cell-mediated (type IV) hypersensitivity Activated T lymphocytes → (1) release of cytokines; inflammation and macrophage activation; (2) T cell–mediated cytotoxicity Perivascular cellular infiltrates, edema; granuloma formation, cell destruction Contact dermatitis, multiple sclerosis, type 1 diabetes; tuberculosis

Ig, Immunoglobulin.


Question 2
2
What other diseases result from type IV hypersensitivity reactions?
Show answer

T-cell mediated hypersensitivity plays a role not only in contact dermatitis but also type 1 diabetes, multiple sclerosis, inflammatory bowel disease, rheumatoid arthritis, and psoriasis.
