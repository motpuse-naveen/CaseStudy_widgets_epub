Crescentic Glomerulonephritis

Normal Reference Range Table

Learning Objectives

  • 1. Describe the usual clinical presentation of rapidly progressive (crescentic) glomerulonephritis (RPGN).
  • 2. Describe the major histologic glomerular findings in RPGN.
  • 3. Know that RPGN may be divided into three general types, based on immune causation and presence or absence of antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibody (ANCA):
    • a. type I: anti–glomerular basement membrane disease (ANCA negative).
    • b. type II: immune complex–mediated disease, (eg, systemic lupus erythematosus, postinfectious glomerulonephritis) (ANCA negative).
    • c. type III: pauci-immune deposit disease (ANCA positive).
  • 4. List the diseases that may be associated with the clinicopathologic presentation of RPGN.

Scenario

A 27-year-old white man who was previously in good health presented to his family physician with increasing fatigue and red urine. There was no history of previous illness, and review of systems was negative. Physical examination was negative except for hypertension (165/110 mm Hg). Urinalysis revealed 2+ protein and 2+ blood; his serum creatinine was 1.8 mg/dL.

A week later, the patient continued to have intermittent bouts of hematuria and began to notice ankle swelling and generalized malaise, nausea and vomiting, and decreasing urine output. He presented to a hospital emergency department and was noted to be almost anuric. On physical examination, his blood pressure was 170/110 mm Hg. An abdominal sonogram showed kidneys of normal size with no evidence of hydronephrosis. Laboratory examination revealed the following:

Urinalysis: Protein 2+
Blood 4+
Glucose negative
Microscopy >40 RBCs/HPF (normal, 0–2 RBCs/HPF)
10 WBCs/HPF (normal, 0–2 WBCs/HPF)
5–10 RBC casts/LPF (normal, 0 casts/LPF)
Hematocrit 38%
Creatinine 3.9 mg/dL
BUN 102 mg/dL
Liver serology Normal
ANCA, ANA, HIV Negative

ANCA, Antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibody; ANA, antinuclear antibody; BUN, blood urea nitrogen; HIV, human immunodeficiency virus; HPF, high-power field; LPF, low-power field; RBCs, red blood cells; WBCs, white blood cells.

The patient was admitted to the intensive care unit with acute renal failure and fluid retention. An emergency renal biopsy was performed. Based on the light microscopic and immunofluorescence findings, plasmapheresis was begun. Over the ensuing 2 weeks, the patient's creatinine decreased to 1.7 mg/dL with a reduction in peripheral edema and resolution of hematuria.

Kidney, crescentic glomerulonephritis - Medium power

The glomerulus on the left shows a crescent. The crescent is composed of parietal epithelial cells that have proliferated and some mononuclear cells derived from blood. The crescent distorts and compresses the glomerulus. The crescent is a result of severe glomerular injury, but it tells little about the pathogenesis of the injury. Immunofluorescence (IF), electron microscopy (EM), and serologic tests are required to determine the cause of injury.

Question 1
1
With what renal syndrome did this patient present? What is the most common cause of this entity?
Show answer

This patient presented initially with two of the three symptoms associated with acute nephritic syndrome (hypertension, hematuria, and evidence of renal failure [ie, increased creatinine]). The most common cause of acute nephritic syndrome is post-streptococcal (postinfectious) glomerulonephritis. This is an immune complex–mediated disease with immunoglobulin G and complement 3 visible along the glomerular basement membrane by IF ("lumpy-bumpy pattern") and large, subepithelial humps visible by EM.

Question 2
2
What features of the history, physical examination, and laboratory evaluation are consistent with acute renal failure?
Show answer

Features consistent with acute renal failure are as follows: oliguria/anuria (by history and observation), uremia/azotemia (by laboratory tests), nausea and vomiting (by history), fluid retention or volume depletion (by history and physical examination), and ankle edema and hypertension (by physical examination).

This patient had red blood cells (RBCs) and RBC casts in his urine; this is pathognomonic of glomerular disease.

Question 1
1
How would immunofluorescence (IF) help in the diagnosis?
Show answer

IF findings can help classify rapidly progressive glomerulonephritis (RPGN) into three categories: type I RPGN (anti-GBM) has a linear pattern; type II RPGN is caused by immune complexes and has a granular pattern; and type III RPGN is defined by lack of anti-GBM or immune complex etiology, so IF is normal.

Question 2
2
What other laboratory test can narrow the diagnosis in RPGN?
Show answer

Antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibody (ANCA) testing can narrow the diagnosis in RPGN. ANCA is negative in type I and type II RPGN, and positive in type III RPGN.

Image 4

Glomerulus, crescentic glomerulonephritis, glomerular basement membrane, staining with anti–immunoglobulin G - Medium power

This is an immunofluorescence (IF) section from the patient's biopsy specimen treated with antiserum against immunoglobulin G (IgG). IF is a good technique to rapidly categorize crescentic glomerulonephritis (CGN). Type I CGN (anti–glomerular basement membrane [GBM] disease) shows linear fluorescence with IgG. Type II CGN (immune complex disease) shows granular deposits of immunoglobulin and complement; the pattern depends on the disease process (eg, systemic lupus erythematosus, immunoglobulin A). Type III CGN (pauci-immune) shows no evidence of immune deposits by IF or electron microscopy (EM).

Question 3
3
Why was plasmapheresis done in this case?
Show answer

This patient had type I (anti-GBM) CGN. This diagnosis is supported by IF and EM and the absence of antineutrophil cytoplasmic antibodies. Plasmapheresis was done to remove the circulating anti-GBM antibodies that damage the kidney.
