Renal Cancer

Learning Objectives

  • 1. Describe the gross difference between adenoma and renal cell carcinoma.
  • 2. With respect to renal cell carcinoma, be familiar with:
    • a. the gross and microscopic morphologic appearance.
    • b. typical clinical features, especially the classic triad; know whether the symptoms are seen in all patients.
    • c. mode of spread.
    • d. associated paraneoplastic syndromes, especially polycythemia.
  • 3. Be familiar with the histologic type and typical clinical features of carcinoma of the renal pelvis.
  • 4. Know that urothelial tumors of the renal pelvis are often associated with multiple tumors involving the pelvis, ureters, or bladder.
  • 5. Know what renal tumor is seen in infants.

Scenario

A 63-year-old man was found to have microscopic hematuria on urinalysis done as part of a pre-employment physical examination. The remainder of the urinalysis was normal, as was the physical examination. The patient did not complain of any fever, weight loss, pain, malaise, weakness, or urinary tract symptoms. Laboratory results showed the following:

Electrolytes Normal
Hct 57%
Hb 19 g/dL
BUN 12 mg/dL
Creatinine 0.7 mg/dL
WBC 7450/mm3 with a normal differential
Urine cytology Negative

BUN, Blood urea nitrogen; Hb, hemoglobin; Hct, hematocrit; WBC, white blood cell count.

Work-up for the hematuria was begun with noninvasive imaging studies, which included a renal ultrasound examination and computed tomography scan. A mass was seen in the left kidney with both imaging studies. The patient underwent nephrectomy.

The multilobular, yellow, and grossly hemorrhagic appearance is typical of an adenocarcinoma of the kidney, also more commonly known as a renal cell carcinoma (RCC). The white bands surrounding and dividing the nodules are dense fibrous tissue.

Question 1
1
What is the classic triad of presenting symptoms of renal cell carcinoma?
Show answer

The classic triad of presenting symptoms is (1) costovertebral angle pain, (2) palpable mass, and (3) hematuria. These symptoms are seen together in only 10% of patients, however. The most reliable symptom is hematuria, which is seen in 90%.


Question 2
2
What is the typical age and sex of patients with RCC?
Show answer

Patients are typically in their 60s to 70s. The male-to-female ratio is 3:1.


Question 3
3
Are there hereditary forms of kidney cancer?
Show answer

The majority of RCC is sporadic; however there are some hereditary associations. Patients with von Hippel-Lindau disease have a greatly increased risk of RCC. Hereditary leiomyomatosis and renal cell carcinoma is an autosomal dominant disease caused by a mutation in the fumarate hydratase gene that manifests as papillary RCCs and skin and uterine leiomyomas. Hereditary papillary carcinoma exhibits mutations in the MET oncogene. Birt-Hogg-Dube syndrome is autosomal dominant and involves mutations in the BHD gene manifesting as a variety of RCCs, skin lesions, and pulmonary cysts/blebs.

Question 1
1
How does this appearance compare with that of Wilms tumor (Image 6)?
Show answer

Wilms tumor has blue cells with sparse cytoplasm. Renal cell carcinoma has clear cells and abundant cytoplasm.





Question 2
2
How does the tumor spread?
Show answer

A tendency to invade the renal vein and grow as a cord, sometimes extending to the heart via the inferior vena cava, is characteristic of this tumor. Renal cell cancers may metastasize widely before local symptoms are noted.


Question 3
3
What paraneoplastic syndromes are associated with renal cell carcinoma?
Show answer

Renal cell carcinoma is a great mimic. It may cause polycythemia, hypercalcemia, Cushing syndrome, and amyloidosis.

A yellow-white, papillary tumor fills the pelvicalyceal system in the renal pelvis at one end of this kidney. The image of the urinary bladder also shows a large, exophytic tumor in the bladder. Question 1
1
What would the microscopic appearance of this tumor be?
Show answer

The microscopic appearance of renal pelvis and bladder tumors is papillary urothelial carcinoma.

Question 1
1
What genetic abnormalities and malformation syndromes are associated with Wilms tumor?
Show answer

Wilms tumors are associated with deletion of WT1 (WAGR and Denys-Drash syndromes) or WT2 (Beckwith-Wiedemann syndrome) loci on chromosome 11.


Question 2
2
What is the survival rate for Wilms tumor?
Show answer

The survival rate for Wilms tumor is 90% with treatment combining chemotherapy, radiation, and surgery.

Wilms tumor is the most common pediatric kidney tumor. Notice the blue cells of Wilms tumor with tubule formation. Compare with the abundant clear cytoplasm of renal cell carcinoma. Wilms tumor is a round blue cell tumor.

Question 1
1
Name four other round blue cell tumors.
Show answer

Other round blue cell tumors are (1) neuroblastoma, (2) Ewing sarcoma, (3) lymphoma, and (4) embryonal rhabdomyosarcoma.
