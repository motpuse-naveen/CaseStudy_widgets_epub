  • 3. Describe phagocytosis and the role of opsonins.
  • 4. Compare and contrast the oxygen-dependent and oxygen-independent mechanisms of microbial killing and the relative importance of these two pathways. Know the disease that best exemplifies a leukocyte defect in oxygen-dependent microbicidal systems.

Scenario

A 3-year-old Hispanic boy was admitted to the hospital with fever, cough, lymphadenopathy, and a 2-cm abscess on the right side of his face. He had not responded to regular antibiotics and local treatment. Significant past medical history included an uncomplicated normal vaginal delivery at term, recurrent otitis media (middle ear infections) that did not readily respond to antibiotic therapy, and two previous hospitalizations for staphylococcal pneumonia. He had one brother who died at 2 years of age. During the present admission, the patient had persistent fever but a normal chest x-ray. Biopsy of the lesion on his face revealed tissue necrosis and mononuclear cell infiltrates (predominantly monocytes and macrophages), and cultures grew 3+ Burkholderia cepacia. Laboratory data included a WBC of 16,200 with 85% neutrophils, a negative HIV test, and normal T-cell and B-cell subsets in the peripheral blood. Blood IgG levels were increased; however, IgM and IgA levels were normal. T-cell proliferation responses to mitogens and recall antigens were normal, as were the levels of complement factors in the blood. Neutrophil function tests revealed normal chemotaxis and phagocytosis and normal expression of CD11b/CD18 (Mac-1) and sialyl Lewis X on the neutrophil surface. The test for neutrophil respiratory burst activity (measured by chemiluminescence) was abnormal.

Question 1
1
What is the basis of defective neutrophil function in CGD?
Show answer

There is impaired microbicidal activity, owing to absence of NADPH oxidase; there is no oxidative burst, and superoxide O2− is not produced. Consequently, the downstream products, such as H2O2 and OH−, are also deficient.


Question 2
2
How does this differ from myeloperoxidase deficiency?
Show answer

In CGD, H2O2 cannot be formed, owing to a deficiency of the NADPH oxidase system; in myeloperoxidase deficiency, H2O2 is formed, but it cannot be converted into reactive halides such HOCl−.

Question 1
1
Explain the basis of the other laboratory tests performed in this case.
Show answer

This patient had recurrent infections that he was unable to clear despite antibiotic treatment. The infections were caused largely by extracellular bacteria, which suggests a defect in neutrophil function. The total neutrophil count was increased, suggesting that there was no defect in neutrophil production. CD11b and sialyl Lewis X, required for extravasation, were normal. IgG and complement components act as opsonins and are required for phagocytosis; their levels were normal as well. CD11b, also the receptor for C3b opsonins, was normal. Chemotaxis and phagocytosis were also shown to be normal. There was no defect in neutrophil production, extravasation, or ability to engulf bacteria.
