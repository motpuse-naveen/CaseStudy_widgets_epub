Scenario

A 52-year-old man from Florida presents to his physician with a mass on his lower lip that has been growing for several months. He has a 30-year history of heavy pipe smoking. He spends a lot of his time in the sun on the golf course. He finally decided to see his doctor because the mass became painful and made it difficult to smoke his pipe. A biopsy was performed. The patient subsequently underwent resection of the mass.

Image 1
Ulcerating squamous cell carcinoma

Lip, squamous carcinoma - Clinical presentation

This image shows an irregular ulcer with hemorrhagic raised margins on the lower lip. Actinic radiation (sunlight) and pipe smoking are predisposing factors to cancer of the lower lip. The prognosis for lip cancer is good; the 5-year recurrence-free rate is about 90%.

Question 1
1
What is the relationship between location and prognosis for cancers of the oral cavity?
Show answer

The prognosis is best for carcinomas of the lip and has traditionally been poorest for carcinomas of the floor of the mouth and the base of the tongue. However, the discovery that HPV-driven oropharyngeal SCCs respond very well to radiation has improved the outcomes in these patients.


Question 2
2
What are the risk factors for “traditional” oropharyngeal squamous cell carcinoma?
Show answer

Smoking and alcohol use are the major risk factors. Their effect is synergistic. Cancer of the lower lip is also related to actinic radiation.

Lip, squamous carcinoma - Low power

Microscopic examination of the lesion on the lip shows areas of well-differentiated neoplastic squamous cells with large areas of keratinization. The tumor is invasive; finger-like projections and islands of tumor extend into the underlying stroma.

Question 1
1
What is leukoplakia, and how is it related to oral cancers?
Show answer

Leukoplakia refers to any whitish mucosal patch or plaque caused by epithelial thickening. Microscopically, leukoplakias show a range of changes, varying from hyperkeratosis with or without epithelial dysplasia to carcinoma in situ. The latter (about 5% to 15% of leukoplakias) can progress to invasive cancer.

This image depicts a large exophytic tumor arising from the tonsil. Most tumors of the tonsil are not as pronounced as this example and may appear as a slight asymmetry on endoscopy.

Image 4
Tonsillar mass, high-power

This tumor arises from the tonsillar crypts. At low power, the tumor is very “blue” because it is non-keratinizing and there is a higher nuclear:cytoplasm ratio. At high power, nuclear pleomorphism can be appreciated. Necrosis can also be seen in these tumors. This tumor could be categorized as “poorly differentiated” because it does not recapitulate normal squamous mucosa. However, for reasons that will be discussed later, the term should perhaps be avoided in certain cases of oropharyngeal squamous cell carcinoma.

Question 1
1
How does this case differ morphologically from our patient with SCC of the lip (image 2)?
Show answer

The SCC in our patient was very well-differentiated and formed keratin. It was a “pink” tumor. This SCC is very “blue,” it is not well-differentiated, and is not keratinizing. As we will see, these two tumors have a completely different pathogenesis.

What is the pathophysiologic implication of staining for p16?
Show answer

p16 staining is a surrogate marker for infection by HPV virus.

Question 2
2
What is the clinical implication of HPV infection in oropharyngeal SCCs?
Show answer

It is now clear that there are two distinct neoplasia pathways leading to SCC in the oropharynx. The “classic” SCCs (30%) are typically keratinizing and well-differentiated, and result predominantly from smoking and excessive alcohol use. The second pathway (70%) occurs in young patients with no smoking history and is driven by HPV infection (particularly HPV subtype 16). It occurs predominantly in the tonsils, base of tongue, and soft palate. It results in non-keratinizing SCCs that appear “poorly differentiated.” The term “poorly differentiated” typically implies a poorly-behaving tumor. In the case of HPV-related oropharyngeal SCCs, the term “poorly differentiated” may be misleading because these patients respond extraordinarily well to radiation therapy. Patients with HPV-related SCC have a 5-year survival of 90% compared to 60% to 80% with HPV-negative oropharyngeal SCCs. HPV vaccines may also prevent development of this tumor in the first place, as they do in the cervix.

Benign tumor

Parotid gland, pleomorphic adenoma - Gross, cut surface

Most tumors of this kind manifest as rounded, well-demarcated, yellow-white masses that rarely exceed a diameter of 6 cm. In some cases, the capsule is not fully developed, and the tumor extends to the margin of resection. The recurrence rate with adequate parotidectomy is about 4%, but with attempted enucleation only, recurrence approaches 25%.

Question 1
1
How frequently does this tumor occur in other salivary glands?
Show answer

Of pleomorphic adenomas, 80% occur in the parotid gland, and 10% occur in the submandibular gland. The remaining 10% are generally encountered in the minor salivary glands of the oral cavity, nasal cavity, and paranasal sinuses.

Question 1
1
How often are these tumors malignant?
Show answer

These tumors very uncommonly are malignant; carcinomas arise in only 2% to 3% of pleomorphic adenomas (called “carcinoma ex pleomorphic adenoma”). In contrast, these tumors are very prone to local recurrence. If enucleated, about 25% recur because of microscopic protrusions at the tumor edges.


Image 8
Cartilage-like area
Myoepithelial cells
Tubular structure

Salivary gland, pleomorphic adenoma - High power

The tumor is composed of epithelial elements dispersed within a background of loose myxoid tissue containing islands of fibrocartilage. Closer examination highlights the fibrocartilage, strands of myoepithelial cells with small dark-staining nuclei, and epithelial elements forming tubules. Rarely, these tumors undergo malignant transformation.

Question 1
1
Why are these tumors sometimes called mixed salivary gland tumors?
Show answer

These tumors are called mixed because they show epithelial, myoepithelial, and mesenchymal differentiation.


Question 2
2
Does the likelihood of malignancy relate to tumor location?
Show answer

Generally, the likelihood of malignancy in all salivary gland tumors is inversely related to the size of the gland. Only a few (15%) tumors in the parotid gland are malignant, whereas more than 50% of tumors in minor salivary glands are malignant.

Grossly, most Warthin tumors are round-to-oval encapsulated masses, 2 to 5 cm in diameter. The cut surface reveals cystic spaces filled with a mucinous or serous secretion.

Question 1
1
What is the distribution of this tumor?
Show answer

This tumor is virtually restricted to the parotid gland. In 10% of cases, it is multifocal, and in 10% it is bilateral.

Question 1
1
What is a risk factor for development of Warthin tumor?
Show answer

Smokers have eight times the risk for developing Warthin tumor when compared with nonsmokers. Warthin tumor used to occur predominantly in men; however, the increased incidence in women smokers occurring over the last 50 years has paralleled the increase in Warthin tumors in female patients. (In 1953, the male-to-female ratio was 10:1 compared with 1.2:1 now.)

Higher power examination clearly shows the dense lymphoid stroma with a germinal center and eosinophilic epithelial cells.

Question 1
1
How common is malignancy in these tumors?
Show answer

Malignancy is very uncommon.


Question 2
2
What is the most common malignant salivary gland tumor?
Show answer

Mucoepidermoid tumors are most often high grade and malignant. They are characterized by the MECT1-MAML2 fusion gene in more than 50% of cases.
