Colonic Adenoma and Carcinoma

Learning Objectives

  • 1. Define a neoplasm. Contrast neoplastic growth with hyperplasia, metaplasia, and dysplasia.
  • 2. Describe the dysplasia-to-carcinoma in situ sequence and the clinical significance of carcinoma in situ.
  • 3. Know the basic principles of the nomenclature of benign and malignant processes.
  • 4. Define and use in the proper context:
    • a. adenoma
    • b. papilloma
    • c. polyp
    • d. cystadenoma
    • e. adenocarcinoma
    • f. sarcoma
  • 5. Compare and contrast benign and malignant tumors with respect to:
    • a. demarcation from surrounding tissue (i.e., capsule, local invasiveness)
    • b. rate of growth
    • c. degree of differentiation (explaining the meaning of differentiation when referring to a tumor)
    • d. distant spread (metastases)
  • 6. Understand the clinical significance of invasiveness and metastasis.
  • 7. Describe the anatomic pathways used by tumors in metastatic spread. Know which pathways are more commonly used by carcinomas versus sarcomas.
  • 8. List some common sites of distant metastases. Know which organs are least commonly involved in metastases.
  • 9. Describe the concept of a tumor suppressor gene (recessive oncogene), and list some examples.
  • 10. Discuss the concept of tumor and tissue specificity.
  • 11. List some inherited syndromes associated with a genetic predisposition to cancer.
  • 12. Compare and contrast clinical features of cancer in patients with an inherited predisposition versus sporadic cancer, in terms of multiple primary tumors, age at onset, and genetic alterations.
  • 13. Describe the clinical features associated with inherited syndromes resulting from mutations in RB, P53, NF1, APC, PTCH, and BRCA-1.
  • 14. Understand the relationship between DNA mismatch repair and mutation rates. Know which inherited condition is associated with defects in mismatch repair.
  • 15. Contrast chromosome instability with microsatellite instability and explain how the differences are manifested in the cancer cell karyotype.
  • 16. Describe the seven fundamental changes in cell physiology associated with the malignant phenotype: self-sufficiency in growth signals, insensitivity to growth inhibition, evasion of apoptosis, genomic instability, immortalization, sustained angiogenesis, and invasion and metastasis.
  • 17. Understand the concept that tumorigenesis is a multistep process involving the accumulation of genetic alterations.
  • 18. Correlate the molecular and morphologic features of colon carcinoma as an example of tumor progression.
  • 19. List the sequential steps involved in local invasion by tumor cells leading to the growth of metastatic deposits.
  • 20. Know the cellular and molecular properties that allow tumor cells to invade the extracellular matrix. List some relevant enzymes that facilitate metastases.
  • 21. Understand why some organs may be more prone to be sites of metastases than others, and understand the roles played by chemokines and adhesion molecules in guiding metastasis formation.

Scenario

A 28-year-old white man presents with a 3-week history of nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and bloody stools. Sigmoidoscopy reveals the presence of multiple polyps distributed throughout the colon and a 2×2 cm mass in the descending colon. An x-ray of his chest reveals multiple bilateral opacities in the lung fields. His liver is enlarged and appears to be nodular. Biopsy specimens are obtained of the colonic mass and several of the polyps. Family history includes a maternal uncle and maternal grandfather who died of colon cancer.

Question 1
1
What genetic alterations are seen in patients with this syndrome?
Show answer

Patients with familial adenomatous polyposis are born with loss or mutation of one copy of the APC gene, which is on the long arm of chromosome #5 (5q). All of their cells are deficient in one of the two copies of the APC gene.

Question 2
2
What features distinguish a benign from a malignant growth in the colonic mucosa?
Show answer

Benign tumors are well circumscribed, slow growing, well differentiated, and noninvasive, and they do not metastasize. Malignant tumors are invasive, and they may metastasize. They may be well differentiated or poorly differentiated. Because of invasion, their margins are irregular.

Question 1
1
To what organs might this tumor metastasize?
Show answer

Colon carcinoma may spread by lymphatics to the regional lymph nodes or by the bloodstream to the liver. Eventually, metastasis may occur into the lungs. Intraperitoneal spread may also occur in the peritoneal cavity.


Question 2
2
Are there any tumor markers in the serum associated with this tumor?
Show answer

Carcinoma of the colon is associated with the presence of carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA) in the serum. However, this marker is not specific for colon carcinoma. CEA may also be present in the serum in patients with gastric cancer or breast cancer. Some non-neoplastic conditions may also be associated with CEA in the serum.

Question 1
1
What biochemical activities are often exhibited by invasive cancer cells?
Show answer

Invasive cancer cells must be able to bind to basement membrane and extracellular matrix by laminin receptors and fibronectin receptors. To degrade the extracellular matrix, they produce proteolytic enzymes such as collagenase type IV and plasminogen activator.

Figure 17-49 Morphologic and molecular changes in the adenoma-carcinoma sequence. Loss of one normal copy of the tumor suppressor gene APC occurs early. Individuals born with one mutant allele are therefore at increased risk of developing colon cancer. Alternatively, inactivation of APC in colonic epithelium may occur later in life. This is the “first hit” according to the Knudson hypothesis (Chapter 7). The loss of the intact second copy of APC follows (“second hit”). Other changes, including mutation of KRAS, losses at 18q21 involving SMAD2 and SMAD4, and inactivation of the tumor suppressor gene TP53, lead to the emergence of carcinoma, in which further mutations occur. Although there seems to be a temporal sequence of changes, the accumulation of mutations, rather than their occurrence in a specific order, is most critical.

Question 1
1
How frequently are mutations in P53, RAS, and APC seen in other types of malignancies?
Show answer

p53 is the most common gene mutated in human cancer. It is mutated in more than 50% of all malignant tumors. RAS is the most commonly mutated proto-oncogene in human cancer. It is mutated in about 15% to 20% of malignancies. APC mutation is seen in very few other tumors (e.g., gastric and esophageal cancers).


Question 2
2
What are the functions of P53 and RAS in normal cells?
Show answer

P53 is called the “guardian of the genome.” When cells sustain DNA damage, the normal P53 gene causes cell cycle arrest in G1, providing time for repair of DNA damage. If repair is successful, cells enter the cycle again; if unsuccessful, the p53 gene causes cell death by apoptosis. RAS in normal cells is a signal-transduction molecule. When cells receive a growth-promoting stimulus, RAS-GDP is converted to RAS-GTP. The latter is the active form of RAS, and it sends growth-promoting signals to the nucleus. Normally, RAS-GTP is rapidly converted to its inactive form by hydrolysis of GTP to GDP.


Figure 7-25 Growth factor signaling pathways in cancer. Growth factor receptors, RAS, PI3K, MYC, and D cyclins are oncoproteins that are activated by mutations in various cancers. GAPs apply brakes to RAS activation, and PTEN serves the same function for PI3K.


Figure 7-30 The role of p53 in maintaining the integrity of the genome. Activation of normal p53 by DNA-damaging agents or by hypoxia leads to cell cycle arrest in G 1 and induction of DNA repair by transcriptional upregulation of the cyclin-dependent kinase inhibitor CDKN1A (encoding the cyclin-dependent kinase inhibitor p21) and the GADD45 genes. Successful repair of DNA allows cells to proceed with the cell cycle; if DNA repair fails, p53 triggers either apoptosis or senescence. In cells with loss or mutations of the p53 gene , DNA damage does not induce cell cycle arrest or DNA repair, and genetically damaged cells proliferate, giving rise eventually to malignant neoplasms.

Question 4
4
Which are associated with tumor progression?
Show answer

The subsequent mutations in RAS, LOH18q (SMAD4), and P53 result in tumor progression (i.e., the tumor cells acquire new properties that make the cells more abnormal in behavior).


Question 5
5
How does genomic instability influence neoplastic transformation?
Show answer

Genomic instability contributes to neoplastic transformation indirectly by predisposing to mutations in proto-oncogenes and tumor suppressor genes.


Question 6
6
Where does hereditary nonpolyposis colon cancer fit into this picture?
Show answer

Hereditary nonpolyposis colon cancer results from an inherited mutation in certain DNA repair genes. This mutation causes genomic instability and predisposes to mutations in other genes that are more directly related to transformation.


Figure 17-50 Morphologic and molecular changes in the mismatch repair pathway of colon carcinogenesis. Defects in mismatch repair genes result in microsatellite instability and permit accumulation of mutations in numerous genes. If these mutations affect genes involved in cell survival and proliferation, cancer may develop.

Question 2
2
What is the relationship between cirrhosis of the liver and primary liver cell cancer?
Show answer

Cirrhosis, regardless of its etiology, is a very important risk factor for liver cancer. This association is believed to result from the extensive regeneration of liver cells that characterizes cirrhosis. Dividing cells are at an increased risk of mutation.

Question 1
1
What determines the homing of cancer cells into various organs?
Show answer

Why certain cancer cells metastasize to specific organs is unclear. Anatomic considerations are important (e.g., cancer of the breast usually spreads first to the draining lymph nodes). Other factors that are specific to the tissues are also important. Tumor cells may bear receptors for molecules on the endothelial cells specific to a given organ. Alternatively, certain tissues may not favor metastases because their microenvironment may inhibit the proteolytic enzymes needed for invasion by the tumor cells.

Image 12

Neurofibromatosis type 1 - Clinical presentation

Neurofibromatosis is an example of an inherited disorder that illustrates many of the concepts discussed for familial adenomatous polyposis, especially tumor progression, recessive tumor suppressor genes, inherited predisposition, and the difference between a benign and a malignant process. Affected individuals develop multiple benign neurofibromas that arise within or are attached to the nerve trunks in the skin. Neurofibromas may also be found internally, attached to nerves. On the right side of the neck and shoulder of this patient, extensive subcutaneous neurofibromas have formed pendulous masses termed plexiform neurofibromas. Patients with neurofibromatosis have an increased risk of developing malignant peripheral nerve sheath tumors. This condition arises from mutations in the NF1 tumor suppressor gene.

Question 1
1
What is the biochemical function of NF1?
Show answer

NF1 encodes a GTPase-activating protein (GAP) that facilitates the inactivation of RAS by converting RAS-GTP to RAS-GDP. NF1 down-regulates the function of RAS.

Image 14

Neurofibroma – Low power

This image is from an incisional biopsy specimen of a large thigh mass in a patient with neurofibromatosis. The tumor is composed of spindle-shaped cells that have the features of Schwann cells and fibroblasts. The cells are arranged in a wavy pattern with bundles of collagen in between (“shredded carrot” pattern), and they are relatively uniform in shape and size. The nuclei are elongated and wavy. There is no nuclear pleomorphism, and mitotic figures are very rare. This is a typical neurofibroma.

Question 1
1
How would the two NF1 alleles in these cells compare with the NF1 alleles in the normal cells of this patient?
Show answer

In a neurofibroma, it is likely that both NF1 alleles would be inactive. By comparison, the normal cells of the patient would have only one inactivated copy of NF1. This inactive NF1 would have been inherited in the germline, providing the first hit, according to the two-hit hypothesis of tumor development.

Question 2
2
What route of metastasis is commonly used by sarcomas?
Show answer

Sarcomas typically spread by invading blood vessels.


Question 3
3
What factors induce vascularization (angiogenesis) of the tumor?
Show answer

There are several factors, but the two most important are basic fibroblast growth factor (bFGF) and vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF). These are produced by tumor cells themselves or by host cells (e.g., macrophages that accumulate in tumors). Anti-VEGF therapy can be used in many tumor types to prevent angiogenesis, but while this may help control the tumor, it is not curative.
