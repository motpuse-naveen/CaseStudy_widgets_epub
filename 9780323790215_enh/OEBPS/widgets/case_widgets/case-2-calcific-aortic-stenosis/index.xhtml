Calcific Aortic Stenosis

Scenario

A 70-year-old man was brought to the hospital for evaluation of chest pain. He described feeling weak and dizzy after a walk around the block that morning. This episode of dizziness was followed by 10 to 15 minutes of chest pain that quickly resolved when he rested. He also described a 2-month history of similar but shorter episodes of chest pain and dizziness after working in his yard. He had been told several years ago that he had a slight heart murmur, but he had not been evaluated further. He had been otherwise healthy all of his life.

Physical signs and auscultation suggested aortic valve narrowing. His lungs were clear to auscultation. A chest radiograph showed an enlarged heart and a vague area of calcification at the base of the heart in the region of the aortic valve. Cardiac enzymes and other laboratory tests were normal. An ECG showed atrial fibrillation and left ventricular hypertrophy.

His valve was replaced, and he did well for several years. He then began to have signs and symptoms of aortic regurgitation.

Heart, aortic valve - Gross

Large, dense, calcific nodules arise in aortic valve cusps. The calcific masses approach, but usually do not involve, the free edges of the cusps. The primary process here is progressive dystrophic calcification of the fibrous tissue of the valve. The cusps are distorted and frozen in a nonphysiologic position by the densely calcified nodules. The cusps can just barely open during systole and may not be able to close fully with diastole. In this case, the commissures of the valve are not fused. Fusion of the commissures would suggest a preexisting inflammatory process, such as prior rheumatic heart disease or a prior bout of infective endocarditis. Degenerative calcific aortic stenosis is the most common cause of aortic stenosis in patients 60 years old. The term degenerative calcific aortic stenosis is generally used when the valve has three equal cusps. Stenotic aortic valves in younger age groups are more likely to be bicuspid valves with secondary calcific changes or postrheumatic valves. Aortic stenosis in children is almost always due to a severe congenital deformity of the valve. The older patient in our scenario likely had an aortic valve that looked like the one shown.

Question 1
1
Why does this patient have chest pain?

He has chest pain because the pressure in his aorta is not adequate to fill his intramyocardial coronary arteries during diastole. Hypoperfusion of subendocardial zones leads to ischemia, cardiac dysfunction, and angina. The problem is worsened by left ventricular hypertrophy and elevated left ventricular diastolic pressure. Left ventricular hypertrophy in response to the elevated pressure load means the heart has to work harder and use more oxygen. Elevated left ventricular diastolic pressure also limits blood flow, particularly to the endocardial zone.


Question 2
2
What determines the severity of his symptoms?

The severity of his symptoms is determined by the degree of pressure decrease across this valve, which is determined by the size of the orifice remaining.


Question 3
3
What are some reasons why the patient might feel dizzy?

The patient probably feels dizzy because his brain is inadequately perfused. In the same way that myocardial blood flow is limited by the pressure decrease across the valve, perfusion of the brain may also be limited.

Heart, left ventricular hypertrophy in calcific aortic stenosis - Gross, longitudinal section, cut surface

The ventricle is working very hard to overcome a severe outflow tract obstruction. The anatomic manifestation of that added work is concentric left ventricular hypertrophy. The left ventricular wall is thickened, and histologic sections would show hypertrophy of the individual cardiac myocytes. Although high pressures are generated by this ventricle during systole, the pressure decrease across the stenotic aortic valve limits flow to the coronary arteries and systemic circulation. Aortic stenosis creates problems on both sides of the ischemia equation (supply vs. demand). Supply (coronary artery blood flow) is limited by the pressure decrease across the valve, and demand (myocardial oxygen demand) is increased in the hypertrophied ventricle.

Question 1
1
List other conditions that are associated with concentric left ventricular hypertrophy.

Hypertension, aortic coarctation, and some cases of hypertrophic cardiomyopathy are other conditions associated with concentric left ventricular hypertrophy.

Raphe

Heart, bicuspid aortic valve - Gross

This aortic valve has two uneven cusps. Dense calcific nodules have distorted the cusps of the valve. As in degenerative calcific aortic stenosis (in normal three-cusp valves), the primary process is progressive dystrophic calcification of the dense fibrous tissue of the valve. The process is accelerated in bicuspid valves by the abnormal hemodynamics of the two uneven cusps. Aortic stenosis associated with degenerative changes in bicuspid valves is generally seen in younger patients compared with the usual form of degenerative calcific aortic stenosis. The two cusps shown are somewhat thickened by fibrosis, but their commissures are not fused. Note calcification of the raphe where the third commissure should have formed. A bicuspid aortic valve is found in 1% to 2% of the population, and could be considered the most common form of congenital heart disease, depending on how congenital heart disease is defined. Bicuspid valves are generally not stenotic or symptomatic at birth, but because they are hemodynamically abnormal, they are more prone to calcification and endocarditis. They may have some degree of incompetence.

Image 4

Thrombi
Metal valve strut

Heart, thrombosed mechanical valve prosthesis - Gross

One of the metal struts of this mechanical prosthetic valve is visible above an accumulation of tan-to-dark brown thrombi. These partially organized thrombi extend from the rim of the prosthesis into the orifice of the valve. The occluder (the portion of the mechanical valve that blocks back-flow across the valve) of the valve is held in an open position by the organizing thrombus partially filling the valve orifice. Mechanical heart valves are generally more durable and can last longer than bioprosthetic valves. Their primary disadvantage is that patients must receive continuous anticoagulation therapy to prevent thrombi from forming on the nonbiologic surfaces of these valves. Mechanical valves can also damage red blood cells and may be associated with a microangiopathic hemolytic anemia.

About 30% of bioprosthetic valves fail within 10 years.
