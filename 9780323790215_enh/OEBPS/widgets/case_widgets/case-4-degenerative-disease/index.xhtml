Scenario

A 72-year-old woman was brought to the physician by her daughter because of increasing confusion and forgetfulness over the past several years. She had become lost while returning from her neighborhood grocery store several times over the past year, prompting concerns about her safety. She had difficulty remembering names and she had become increasingly depressed and uncommunicative.

Neurologic examination revealed a confused patient oriented only to person. She was able to recall three items immediately after naming them but could not remember them after 5 minutes. She did not recognize her daughter. No focal neurologic abnormalities were identified. A CT scan of her head revealed generalized cerebral atrophy (Image 1) but no focal lesions. Her laboratory studies, including cerebrospinal fluid examination, revealed no abnormalities.

Over the next 2 years, her cognitive status progressively deteriorated to the point that she required constant skilled nursing care, and she was transferred to a nursing home. Her course there was complicated by recurrent decubitus ulcers and several episodes of pneumonia; she died 3 years after admission following the development of severe bronchopneumonia. Permission for an autopsy was obtained.

At autopsy, the brain weighed 1000 g (normal 1200 g). There was generalized cerebral cortical atrophy (Image 2) accompanied by a dramatic loss of neurons in the pyramidal cell layer of the hippocampus. Special stains (Images 3, 4, and 5) revealed well-developed neurofibrillary tangles and neuritic plaques in the hippocampus and in multiple areas of the cerebral neocortex. Amyloid deposits were also identified in the walls of multiple blood vessels in the leptomeninges and brain parenchyma.

Head, generalized cerebral atrophy – CT scan

A CT scan of the patient's head showed a dramatic loss of parenchyma, manifested here by generalized widening of sulci and marked enlargement of the lateral ventricles (hydrocephalus ex vacuo). Scans of this type are useful in excluding focal lesions, such as neoplasms and infarcts; these processes can also cause cognitive impairment. Although the radiographic changes present in this case are consistent with Alzheimer disease (AD), they are neither specific nor invariably present; definitive diagnosis requires histologic confirmation.

Question 1
1
What is AD?
Show answer

AD is a degenerative disease of the central nervous system (CNS) characterized clinically by progressive cognitive impairment and memory loss. The disease is rare in individuals younger than 50 years but is the most common cause of dementia in patients older than 65 years. Structural changes in AD include variable degrees of cerebral cortical atrophy and accumulation of neurofibrillary tangles, neuritic plaques, and a form of amyloid known as beta-amyloid within the brain (discussed subsequently). The cause of AD is unknown. Approximately 85% of cases of AD are sporadic, although familial forms of the disease (approximately 15% of cases) are now well documented and have provided investigators with important insights into the pathogenesis of the disease.


Question 2
2
What are some other causes of dementia?
Show answer

Other causes of dementia in elderly patients include other neurodegenerative disorders (e.g., Pick disease, Parkinson disease and related Lewy body disorders, Huntington disease), infections (e.g., neurosyphilis, AIDS-associated dementia, Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease), vascular disease, brain trauma, tumors, demyelinating disease, and toxic-metabolic disorders.

Brain, hippocampus, Alzheimer disease (AD), silver stain – Very low power

This section of hippocampus is stained with a type of silver stain called a Sevier-Munger stain, which is used to highlight neurofibrillary tangles and neuritic (senile) plaques, two of the most important microscopic changes in AD. The pyramidal cell layer of the hippocampus, particularly the CA1 region, is a good place to look for these structures, although they are difficult to appreciate at this magnification. Neurofibrillary tangles and neuritic plaques are consistent features in the hippocampus in AD; identical changes can also be seen in otherwise normal, older individuals, although usually in lower numbers. The definitive diagnosis of AD, at least from a morphologic standpoint, requires the identification of significant numbers of plaques and tangles in the cerebral neocortex.


Image 4

Neurofibrillary tangles
Neuritic plaques

Brain, cerebral cortex, Alzheimer disease (AD), silver stain – Medium power

This higher power image of the brain, stained with another type of silver stain designed to highlight neurofibrillary tangles and neuritic plaques, illustrates the morphology of these structures in the cerebral cortex. The neurofibrillary tangles are densely staining deposits within neuronal cell bodies; they are composed of structures termed paired helical filaments (PHF), an abnormal form of the tau protein. The neuritic plaques, also highlighted in this image, are composed of abnormal, PHF-containing neuronal cell processes, termed dystrophic neurites, surrounding a core containing beta-amyloid. As noted previously, the histologic diagnosis of Alzheimer disease (AD) is based on the presence of significant numbers of such plaques and tangles in the cerebral neocortex.

Question 1
1
What are PHFs?
Show answer

PHFs are composed of an abnormally hyperphosphorylated and aggregated form of tau protein, a normal cellular constituent believed to play an important role in the assembly of axonal microtubules. In Alzheimer disease (AD), it accumulates within neuronal cell bodies to form neurofibrillary tangles and within swollen, dystrophic neuritic processes in the neuritic plaques. It can also be detected in neuritic processes in the cerebral cortex in the form of delicate neuropil threads.


Question 2
2
What is the source of the beta-amyloid in the core of the plaque?
Show answer

Accumulations of beta-amyloid protein within the central nervous system (CNS) are another consistent feature of AD. The beta-amyloid is derived from a precursor molecule termed beta-amyloid precursor protein (beta-APP). Beta-APP is a protein of uncertain function that is normally expressed on the surface of neuronal membranes. It is converted to beta-amyloid via the action of the enzymes beta-secretase and gamma-secretase. In AD, beta-amyloid deposits are found in the cores of neuritic plaques and in the walls of parenchymal and leptomeningeal blood vessels (amyloid angiopathy).


Targeted Therapy Box – Amyloid: Breaking up is Hard to Do

Amyloidosis

Amyloid: Breaking Up Is Hard to Do

Approximately 15 biochemically distinct proteins have the capacity to fold into relatively insoluble β-pleated sheets to produce morphologically identical-looking amyloid fibrils. There are three major forms of amyloid:AL (immunoglobulin light chains), AA (a protein derived from SAA, an acute-phase reactant made in the liver), and Aβ amyloid, found in the cerebral plaques of Alzheimer disease. All forms are associated with a glycoprotein (the P component) that is essential for fibril deposition. Some of these unfolded molecules are directly toxic; thus, immunoglobulin light chains in myeloma bring myocardial cells to a standstill, and the Aβ amyloid in Alzheimer disease plaques is noxious for neurons. The fibrils in all forms get progressively cross-linked, thereby becoming resistant to degradation. The amyloid thus formed progressively accumulates like so much extracellular linoleum, eventually squeezing out normal parenchyma. Typically, the prognosis for patients affected by amyloid has been exceptionally grim; a diagnosis of cardiac involvement usually translates to death within 1 to 2 years.


In all of these entities, the protein aggregation has long been presumed to be relentless and irreversible; at the very best, clinicians could only hope to prevent further accumulation. Accordingly, the traditional approaches to therapy focus on treating the underlying disease—specifically, chemotherapy for myeloma (with AL amyloidosis) and antibiotics for chronic infections or anti-inflammatory agents for autoimmune diseases (with AA amyloidosis).


Some breakthroughs appear imminent, however: Building on an improved understanding of protein folding, research has led to production of a number of useful small molecules—so-called “amyloid breakers,” now entering into clinical trial. Some of these interfere with the binding of chaperone proteins such as substance P and apolipoprotein E4 (apoE4), which can propagate the β-sheet content of Aβ peptides and promote pathogenic fibril formation in the brain: CPHPC is a carboxypyrrolidinyl carboxylic acid derivative that binds to the P component and results in its prompt removal from the circulation thus eliminating an important “nucleation factor” for amyloid propagation. Other agents such as tramiprosate inhibit the interactions between interstitial glycosaminoglycans and Aβ that normally stabilize fibril formation; these breakers are sulfated glycosaminoglycan mimetics. Yet another approach is to stabilize native transthyretin tetramers with drugs like tafamadis or diflunisal to prevent the dissociation of monomeric components (e.g., from mutant isoforms) that can then misfold into amyloid fibrils.


With these approaches, coupled with earlier detection of amyloid deposits before irreversible damage is done, what previously has been an intractable and inexorable process may eventually become manageable.

Targeted Therapy Box – Plaque Attack: Untangling Alzheimer Disease

Tumors

Plaque Attack: Untangling Alzheimer Disease

Alzheimer disease (AD) is the most common cause of dementia, characterized as cognitive decline and short-term memory loss; it is also the sixth most common cause of death in the United States. With more definitive, life-prolonging treatments for cardiovascular and neoplastic disease, in combination with the general aging of the population, the incidence of AD can be expected to increase dramatically in the coming decades. For the United States, this is projected at 1 million additional patients each year, with approximately 15 million afflicted persons by 2050. Clearly, the coming deluge of dementia provides a strong impetus to develop improved therapies; even better would be to check AD in its very earliest stages.


Current first-line therapies are intended to ameliorate the progressive cognitive decline of AD. Because memory is regulated through cholinergic pathways, cholinesterase inhibitors (e.g., donepezil, rivastigmine) are prescribed to increase acetylcholine levels. In addition, because glutamate influences long-term potentiation—important for learning and memory—another class of drugs (e.g., memantine) that block N-methyl-d-aspartase (NMDA) receptors, which regulate glutamate levels, has emerged. Unfortunately, these drugs provide at best only modest gains (1% to 5% improvement on various AD assessment scales), with side effects that adversely affect up to half of the patients. Moreover, cholinesterase inhibitors and NMDA antagonists only treat the symptoms and do not attack the underlying causes. With time, ongoing neuronal injury soon overtakes any transient pharmacologic benefit.


By comparison, drugs in the pipeline target putative AD pathogenic pathways. The prevailing view is that a transmembrane amyloid precursor protein (APP) is sequentially processed by β- (extracellular) and γ- (intramembranous) proteases called secretases. These generate a 42-amino-acid peptide (Aβ) that forms toxic oligomers, or misfolds into insoluble amyloid plaque. Of note, an alternate pathway involving sequential α- and γ-secretase cleavages does not result in the pathologic Aβ peptide. Besides direct neurotoxicity, Aβ complexes also induce increased phosphorylation of intracellular tau, a protein that stabilizes neuronal microtubules; hyperphosphorylated tau then aggregates into cytoplasmic neurofibrillary tangles, and the microtubule architecture collapses.


In light of the central role envisaged for Aβ, β- and γ-secretase inhibitors and α-secretase stimulators are all reasonable targets. Indeed, β-secretase inhibitors (e.g., thiazolidinediones) improve AD scores but may be of limited value because neuregulin-1 (involved in myelination) is a normal β-secretase substrate. Unfortunately, γ-secretase inhibitors (e.g., teranflurbil) have not yet lived up to their potential; indeed, recent trials suggest that γ-secretase activity may be critical to maintain neuronal membrane homeostasis. On the other hand, a number of α-secretase stimulators (e.g., etazolate, byrostatin-1, a green tea polyphenol extract called epigallocatechin-3-gallate) are promising in clinical studies. Other approaches have been designed to inhibit Aβ aggregation (e.g., tramiprosate) or harness the immune system to engulf and degrade plaques and oligomers; anti-Aβ monoclonal antibodies such as bapineuzumab and solanezumab may accomplish this. Finally, inhibition of tau phosphorylation (NP-03112) or aggregations are viable strategies currently in clinical trials.


Thus, although AD is still a depressingly progressive and still largely intractable disorder, the foregoing advances in the fundamental understanding of its pathogenesis have provided several encouraging lines for attacking the plaques.



Question 3
3
What molecular abnormalities have been identified in patients with familial forms of AD?
Show answer

Approximately 15% of AD cases are familial. One important group of familial AD cases is associated with mutations in the gene encoding beta-APP, located on chromosome 21. Although the role played by beta-amyloid deposition and neuronal injury in AD is a subject of considerable controversy, available evidence strongly suggests a relationship between abnormal beta-amyloid deposition and AD. A second group of familial AD cases has been linked to mutations in genes encoding the cellular proteins presenilin-1 and presenilin-2, located on chromosomes 14 and 1. Mutant forms of presenilin are associated with an increased production of beta-amyloid and are targets for cleavage by apoptosis-associated enzymes (caspase proteases).


Question 4
4
What is another genetic factor associated with an increased risk for AD?
Show answer

Although not strictly associated with familial AD, the expression of the e4 allele of apolipoprotein E (ApoE) has also been associated with an increased risk of AD. The mechanism whereby e4 expression increases the risk of AD is unclear.

Question 2
2
What is Parkinson disease?
Show answer

Parkinson disease, or idiopathic parkinsonism, is a degenerative disease associated with a loss of dopaminergic neurons in the pars compacta of the substantia nigra. Similar to Alzheimer disease, most cases of Parkinson disease are sporadic, although rare familial forms also exist. In addition to the motor abnormalities noted in this patient, 10% to 15% of patients with Parkinson disease also develop dementia.


Question 3
3
Are there any other causes of parkinsonism?
Show answer

Similar motor abnormalities are a feature of many other neurodegenerative diseases (e.g., progressive supranuclear palsy, corticobasal degeneration, “multiple system atrophies”), certain toxic insults (exemplified by the development of severe parkinsonism in individuals exposed to MPTP, a toxin produced during the illicit synthesis of home-brewed meperidine), and some cases of cerebrovascular disease. A condition known as postencephalitic parkinsonism, which followed some cases of influenza during the 1914-1918 pandemic, is usually also included compulsively in the list of causes of parkinsonism; it is virtually nonexistent today.

Lewy bodies

Brain, substantia nigra, Lewy bodies – High power

This image illustrates the histologic hallmark of Parkinson disease – Lewy bodies in the cytoplasm of pigmented neurons of the substantia nigra. Lewy bodies have a characteristic target-shaped appearance within the substantia nigra and are composed of alpha synuclein associated with other proteins, such as ubiquitin and neurofilament. Identical inclusions may also be encountered in other sites in the central nervous system, including the locus caeruleus and, particularly in cases with dementia, the cerebral cortex. An occasional Lewy body may also be encountered as an incidental finding in the substantia nigra of neurologically normal elderly individuals.

Question 1
1
What is HD?
Show answer

HD is an autosomal dominant disorder characterized clinically by the development of abnormal, involuntary, writhing or jerking movements (choreoathetosis) accompanied by progressive dementia. Manifestations usually develop during adulthood, although juvenile cases are well recognized. Cases that manifest during childhood are more often characterized by rigidity than by involuntary movements, but, similar to adult-onset cases, they progress inexorably over several years. Morphologically, the major change in HD is loss of medium-sized spiny neurons in the neostriatum, manifested grossly by severe atrophy of the caudate nuclei and, to a lesser degree, the putamen. Cerebral cortical atrophy may also be evident, particularly in the frontal lobes.


Question 2
2
What is the nature of the mutation in patients with HD?
Show answer

HD is caused by a mutation in the HD gene, located on the short arm of chromosome 4, which encodes a protein known as huntingtin. HD is caused by expansion of a CAG base pair repeat within the gene, an example of a triplet-repeat mutation. As in the case of numerous other triplet-repeat disorders, the larger the number of CAG repeats, the earlier the onset of the disease.

Question 1
1
What is ALS? What are the clinical manifestations of upper motor neuron loss in ALS?
Show answer

ALS, also known as motor neuron disease or Lou Gehrig disease, is a degenerative disorder characterized by a spontaneous, progressive loss of upper motor neurons in the cerebral cortex and lower motor neurons in the anterior horns of the spinal cord. Typically a disease of adulthood, most cases are sporadic, although familial variants also exist (approximately 15% of cases). Manifestations of upper motor neuron loss in ALS include spasticity, abnormally brisk deep tendon reflexes, and a Babinski sign (upgoing toes after application of painful pressure to the sole of the foot).

Question 1
1
What are the clinical consequences of lower motor neuron loss in ALS?
Show answer

Loss of lower motor neurons is associated with development of weakness and atrophy in muscles innervated by these neurons. In addition to causing devastating weakness in the extremities, this lower motor neuron loss is associated with brainstem dysfunction (abnormal speech, impaired swallowing) and respiratory failure.

Question 1
1
What is CJD?
Show answer

CJD is one of a group of uncommon diseases caused by the accumulation of an abnormal form of prion protein. Normal prion protein (designated PrPc) is a 30-kD protein of uncertain function encoded by a gene on chromosome 20 and expressed in normal neurons. Prion-associated diseases occur when PrPc undergoes a conformational change to an abnormal form, designated PrPsc. The presence of PrPsc induces conformational changes in other PrPc molecules, which causes progressive neuronal loss and the development of neurologic abnormalities. The conformational change to PrPsc can occur spontaneously, presumably accounting for sporadic cases of CJD. Certain mutations in the gene for PrPc also occur that are associated with an unusually high rate of spontaneous conformational change to PrPsc; such mutations are believed to be responsible for the less common but well-recognized familial forms of CJD. Rarely, the introduction of PrPsc from external sources (e.g., contaminated neurosurgical instruments, corneal transplants) has been associated with the development of CJD.


Figure 28-31 Pathogenesis of prion disease. α -helical PrP c may spontaneously shift to the β -sheet PrP sc conformation, an event that occurs at a much higher rate in familial disease associated with germ line PrP mutations. PrP sc may also be acquired from exogenous sources, such as contaminated food, medical instrumentation, or medicines. Once present, PrP sc converts additional molecules of PrP c into PrP sc through physical interaction, eventually leading to the formation of pathogenic PrP sc aggregates


Question 2
2
What are some other examples of prion-related neurologic disease in humans?
Show answer

CJD is not the only example of prion-associated human disease. Other examples include kuru, Gerstmann-Straussler-Scheinker syndrome, and fatal familial insomnia, all extremely rare conditions. Variant CJD is a prion-related neurologic disease that has been linked to the ingestion of beef from cattle with bovine spongiform encephalopathy, another prion-associated disease. It typically affects younger patients when compared with conventional CJD.
