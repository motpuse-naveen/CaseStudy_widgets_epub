Ovarian Tumor

Learning Objectives

  • 1. Describe the pathogenesis and clinical features of polycystic ovarian disease.
  • 2. List the three classifications of ovarian tumors, and cite the relative incidence of ovarian tumors.
  • 3. Describe and contrast the pathologic and clinical features of ovarian serous and mucinous cystadenomas, borderline tumors, and carcinomas.
  • 4. Describe the usual clinical presentation and pathologic features of mature cystic teratomas.
  • 5. Describe the two most common types of sex cord–stromal tumors: granulosa-theca cell tumors and fibrothecomas.
  • 6. Define Krukenberg tumor.

Scenario

A 52-year-old, fit, slim former dancer goes to her internist because of vague abdominal pain and a feeling of fullness. Her medical history is noncontributory. Her gynecologic history includes menarche, age 13; coitarche, age 18; lifetime sexual partners, three; no intravenous drug use or transfusions. Her internist examines her, but can find no problems, so he sends her to a gastroenterologist. The gastroenterologist examines her, finds no problems, and orders upper and lower gastrointestinal studies, which are negative. The woman continues to see these physicians, with worsening symptoms, over 5 months; it is decided that because no disease has been found, she needs a psychiatric consultation to aid with her developing anxiety and depression. The psychiatrist knows he needs to exclude organic disease before he treats emotional symptoms, so he performs a physical examination. He notes abdominal fullness with a fluid wave, consistent with ascites. He also performs a pelvic examination. A 10-cm left adnexal mass is easily felt. Cytologic examination of the ascitic fluid is performed (Image 1). At laparotomy, a tumor is diagnosed by the pathologist by doing a frozen section (Images 2–4). The tumor is found to have spread to her other ovary, omentum, and numerous sites on her peritoneum. She receives chemotherapy, but dies 1 year after diagnosis.

Image 1

Cluster of neoplastic cells in ascitic fluid

Ascitic fluid cytology, serous carcinoma - High power

Cytologic examination of ascitic fluid often can provide the diagnosis in patients with unexplained ascites. Note the papillary arrangement of the tumor cells. Although simple in this case, differentiation of tumor cells from benign mesothelial cells in cytologic preparations can be extremely difficult.

Question 1
1
Why is ovarian carcinoma often discovered at an advanced stage?

The relatively hidden location of the ovaries allows tumors to spread before becoming clinically apparent.

Question 1
1
Serous cystadenocarcinomas are considered ovarian tumors of surface epithelial origin; what are the origins of the other two groups of ovarian tumors?

Germ cells and ovarian sex cord–stromal cells are the origins of the other two groups of ovarian tumors.


Question 2
2
Where are high-grade ovarian serous carcinomas believed to arise from?

Historically, it was believed that serous tumors arose from surface epithelial inclusion cysts that became malignant. However, it is now thought that at least some of these tumors arise from the epithelium of the fallopian tube.


Figure 22-30 Schematic diagram of the pathogenesis of ovarian epithelial tumors. Type I tumors progress from benign tumors through borderline tumors that may give rise to a low-grade carcinoma. These include low-grade serous, endometrioid and mucinous carcinomas. Type II tumors arise from inclusions cysts/fallopian tube epithelium via intraepithelial precursors that are often not identifi ed. They demonstrate high-grade features and are most commonly of serous histology. STIC, serous tubal intraepithelial carcinoma.


Question 3
3
How does this new understanding of serous carcinoma pathogenesis change patient management?

Women at high risk for ovarian cancer, such as BRCA mutation carriers, now receive prophylactic bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy instead of bilateral oophorectomy.

Question 1
1
How does this tumor differ histologically from a serous tumor of low malignant potential?

In serous cystadenocarcinoma, the epithelium destructively invades the stroma.


Question 2
2
How common are serous cystadenocarcinomas?

Serous cystadenocarcinomas are the most common malignant ovarian tumor, comprising 40% of all ovarian cancers.


Image 5

Cysts

Ovary, serous cystadenoma - Gross, cut surfaces

Ovarian serous cystadenomas are typically unilocular and have a smooth inner surface. Occasional serous cystadenomas may have several locules, as in this example. Note the lack of friable papillae. Friable papillae are characteristically seen in serous cystadenocarcinomas or serous tumors of low malignant potential.

Image 6

Cilia

Ovary, serous cystadenoma - High power

As stated for Image 5, most serous cystadenomas have a smooth inner surface lined by a bland epithelium reminiscent of fallopian tube epithelium. Note the cilia on several of the bland epithelial cells.

Question 1
1
What is the prognosis for a serous cystadenoma?

The prognosis is excellent; excision is curative.


Image 7

Inner cyst surface with papillae

Ovary, serous borderline tumor - Gross, cut surface

In contrast to serous cystadenomas, serous borderline tumors have numerous friable papillae, often covering the inner cyst surface.

Question 1
1
What histologic characteristic distinguishes serous borderline tumors from serous cystadenomas?

Serous tumors of low malignant potential are lined by complex papillary structures covered by epithelium with nuclear atypia and mitotic activity, whereas serous cystadenomas are lined by a flat, bland epithelium.


Image 8

Papillae with atypical epithelium

Ovary, serous tumor of low malignant potential (serous borderline tumor) - Low power

The papillae of serous borderline tumors are covered by a complex, atypical epithelium similar to that seen in serous cystadenocarcinomas. Serous borderline tumors, however, lack stromal invasion.

Question 1
1
What is the prognosis for a completely excised ovarian borderline tumor that is confined to the ovary?

The prognosis is excellent; 100% are cured.

The outside surface of this tumor is smooth, similar to a serous cystadenoma; however, on bisection, the typical multilocular pattern of a mucinous cystadenoma can be seen. Benign, endocervical-like, mucinous epithelium lines the locules.

Question 1
1
How does a typical mucinous cystadenoma differ in gross appearance from a typical serous cystadenoma?

Serous cystadenomas are typically unilocular and filled with thin straw-colored fluid, whereas mucinous cystadenomas are typically multilocular and filled with thick mucinous fluid.


Question 2
2
How does a typical mucinous cystadenoma differ in gross appearance from a serous cystadenocarcinoma?

Serous cystadenocarcinomas typically have solid and cystic components filled with friable papillae.

Where else in the body do teratomas arise?

They occur in the testis and, rarely, in the mediastinum, pineal gland, and sacrococcygeal region.

Which of the three groups of ovarian tumors is represented by this tumor? Is it benign or malignant?

This is a germ cell tumor. It is benign.


Question 2
2
Are there any malignant germ cell tumors of the ovary?

Malignant germ cell tumors of the ovary include immature teratomas, dysgerminomas, and yolk sac tumors, among others.

Question 1
1
Define Meigs syndrome.

Meigs syndrome comprises hydrothorax, ascites, and an ovarian tumor.


Question 2
2
Fibrothecomas are discovered in which age group of patients most commonly?

Fibrothecomas are most commonly found in middle-aged to elderly women.

Question 1
1
Granulosa cell tumors may also be associated with endometrial hyperplasia or carcinoma. Why?

These tumors often produce large amounts of estrogen, resulting in unopposed estrogen stimulation of the endometrium and subsequent endometrial hyperplasia.


Image 14

Call-Exner bodies

Ovary, granulosa cell tumor - Medium power

Granulosa cell tumors are composed of polygonal cells arranged in sheets, trabeculae, or follicle-like structures containing eosinophilic material (Call-Exner bodies).

Question 1
1
Is this tumor considered an ovarian tumor of surface epithelial origin, germ cell origin, or sex cord–stromal origin?

This tumor is considered an ovarian tumor of sex cord–stromal origin. It originates from the estrogen-producing granulosa cells which line the ovarian follicles.


Image 15

High-power view
Signet ring cells
Mucin stain

Ovary, Krukenberg tumor (metastatic gastric carcinoma) - Gross, cut surface

Krukenberg tumors are characteristically bilateral, markedly enlarged ovaries infiltrated with metastatic signet ring cell carcinoma from the stomach. The intracytoplasmic mucin of the signet ring cells stains pink with a mucin stain.

Question 1
1
What other tumors commonly metastasize to the ovaries?

Endometrial carcinoma, fallopian tube primaries, breast carcinoma, and other gastrointestinal carcinomas commonly metastasize to the ovaries.
