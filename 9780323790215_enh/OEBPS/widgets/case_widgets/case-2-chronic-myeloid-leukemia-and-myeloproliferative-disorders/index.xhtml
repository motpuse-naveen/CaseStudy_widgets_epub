Learning Objectives

Chronic Myeloproliferative Disorders (CMDs)

  • 1. Define the neoplastic stem cell in CMDs. List the different CMDs, and discuss the basis for the classification of these disorders.

Chronic Myelogenous leukemia (CML)

  • 1. Describe the age of the patients; presenting clinical findings, including physical and hematologic data; natural history; and cytogenetic and molecular abnormalities in CML. List features that distinguish CML and leukemoid reaction.
  • 2. Define the blast phase of CML. Describe different cell types that may be involved in this process. Understand the implication of these different types of blast phase in CML to the putative stem cell that undergoes neoplastic transformation.
  • 3. Compare and contrast cytogenetic findings in CML and CML in blast phase. Know the karyotypic and molecular defect of the Philadelphia (Ph) chromosome and other types of leukemias with which it has been associated.
  • 4. Be familiar with the various methods available for detecting the BCR/ABL gene rearrangement.

Polycythemia Vera

  • 1. Describe the age of the patients; presenting clinical findings, including physical and hematologic data; and natural history of polycythemia vera. Describe the primary aim of therapy in this disease.
  • 2. Define the spent phase in polycythemia vera, and compare and contrast blast transformation of polycythemia vera and CML.
  • 3. Distinguish polycythemia vera, secondary polycythemia, and relative polycythemia.

Primary Myelofibrosis

  • 1. Describe the age of the patients; presenting clinical findings, including physical and hematologic data; and the natural history of primary myelofibrosis. Describe the primary aim of therapy in these patients.
  • 2. Understand the terms myelofibrosis, primary myelofibrosis, and secondary myelofibrosis. Compare and contrast the role of the fibroblast and the myeloid elements in this disorder.

Essential Thrombocythemia (ET)

  • 1. Describe the age of the patients; presenting clinical findings, including physical and hematologic data; and the natural history of ET.
  • 2. Describe how ET is treated and what the indications are for therapy.

Scenario

First Presentation

The patient is 47-year-old woman who presents to your office complaining of early satiety. She gives a history of a 5 to 10 lb unintentional weight loss. She notes associated night sweats and fevers up to 100° F. Your physical examination demonstrates a woman in no acute distress. Her spleen is markedly enlarged, but no peripheral lymphadenopathy is noted. The CBC reveals:

Hb 14.5 g/dL
WBC 80,000/mm3
Plt 750,000/mm3

RT−PCR analysis was performed on peripheral blood, which showed BCR-ABL fusion mRNA transcripts. Cytogenetic evaluation of bone marrow shows the t(9;22) translocation.

The patient was started on imatinib, with a decrease in her white cell count and symptoms.

Second Presentation (4 Years Later)

The patient presents with a 1-month history of bone pain, weight loss, night sweats, fever, and early satiety. Physical exam reveals the reappearance of massive splenomegaly, and this time, lymphadenopathy as well.

Blood cytology reveals a predominance of lymphoblasts. Flow cytometric analysis identifies the majority of peripheral blood mononuclear cells as having the cell surface marker characteristics of B-lineage lymphoblasts (CD10+ [CALLA), κ immunoglobin light chain-, λ immunoglobin light chain-, TdT+, CD2−, CD7−, CD19+].

RT−PCR analysis was again performed on peripheral blood, which showed BCR-ABL fusion mRNA transcripts. Cytogenetic evaluation of bone marrow shows the t(9;22) translocation as well as additional chromosomal abnormalities typically seen in the blast phase of chronic myelogenousleukemia.

1
Are these findings most consistent with a leukemic or a reactive process?

These findings are most consistent with a leukemic process.

Question 2
2
List some of the hematologic and clinical findings in this case that permit you to reach this conclusion.
Show answer

Findings include high white blood cell count with a major fraction of immature neutrophilic elements, weight loss, night sweats, and fevers.


Question 3
3
What is the reason for this patient's early satiety and for her night sweats, fever, and weight loss?
Show answer

Early satiety is due to a markedly enlarged spleen compressing the stomach. The other symptoms are due to a hypermetabolic state resulting from turnover of the vast number of myeloid cells.


Question 4
4
What is the cytogenetic abnormality associated with this disorder?
Show answer

The Philadelphia (Ph) chromosome [t(9;22)] is the cytogenetic abnormality associated with this disorder.


Question 5
5
How specific is this finding?
Show answer

It is very specific. Although this translocation can be seen in acute lymphoblastic leukemia, the latter is not a differential diagnostic consideration here.


Question 6
6
Is it found in other chronic myeloproliferative disorders?
Show answer

No.


Question 7
7
What is the common etiology in all myeloproliferative neoplasms?
Show answer

The common etiology is the presence of mutated, constitutively active tyrosine kinases or other signaling pathways that lead to growth factor independence.

Image 2

Philadelphia chromosome translocation

Philadelphia (Ph) chromosome–positive chronic myelogenousleukemia (CML) - Initial karyotype

Karyotypic analysis of spontaneously dividing white blood cells from this patient reveals in every dividing cell a translocation in which a piece of the long arm of one chromosome 9 and a piece of the long arm of one chromosome 22 have translocated (exchanged places): t(9;22)(q34;q11.2). The hybrid chromosome 22 has been named the Ph chromosome. No other chromosomal abnormalities are seen in the karyotype.

Question 1
1
In light of this patient’s blood smear, is it diagnostic of CML?
Show answer

The presence of the Ph chromosome on this patient’s blood smear is diagnostic of CML.


Image 3

Translocation

Philadelphia (Ph) chromosome translocation - Schematic

Although the Ph chromosome translocation — t(9;22) — has been principally associated with chronic myelogenousleukemia (CML), it may also be found in acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL).

Question 1
1
Does the presence of the Ph chromosome in ALL portend a good or bad prognosis?
Show answer

Historically, Ph+ B-ALL portended a worse prognosis. However, current treatment with BCR-ABL kinase inhibitor therapy in combination with chemotherapy has greatly improved outcomes in children.


Question 2
2
What is the molecular consequence of the t(9;22)?
Show answer

The molecular consequence is translocation of the ABL oncogene on chromosome 9 to the BCR gene on chromosome 22. This leads to constitutive kinase activity.


Figure 13-32 Pathogenesis of chronic myeloid leukemia. Breakage and joining of BCR and ABL creates a chimeric BCR-ABL fusion gene that encodes a constitutively active BCR-ABL tyrosine kinase. BCR-ABL activates multiple downstream pathways, which drive growth factor-independent proliferation and survival of bone marrow progenitors. Because BCR-ABL does not interfere with differentiation, the net result is an increase in mature elements in the peripheral blood, particularly granulocytes and platelets.


Question 3
3
Do all cases of CML have a Ph chromosome?
Show answer

No. The Ph chromosome is absent in approximately 5% of cases. These cases lacking cytogenetic evidence of a Ph chromosome possess an occult BCR/ABL translocation, however. The BCR/ABL rearrangement, rather than the cytogenetically evident translocation, is the necessary genetic event in CML.


Question 4
4
What methods are available for detection of the gene rearrangement in CML? What are the advantages and limitations of the various methods?
Show answer

Methods include conventional karyotype analysis (identifying the Ph chromosome), reverse transcriptase polymerase chain reaction [RT-PCR] (identifying BCR-ABL fusion mRNA), and fluorescence in situ hybridization [FISH] (identifying the BCR-ABL fusion gene). Conventional cytogenetic analysis has the advantage of looking at the entire chromosome complement of the tumor for assessment of any other abnormalities. Conventional cytogenetics would not detect occult BCR/ABL rearrangement, however, and requires living dividing cells. RT-PCR is rapid, highly sensitive, and easy to perform; it is limited only by the integrity of the RNA in the sample. FISH analysis is rapid, easy to perform, and sensitive, although not as sensitive as RT-PCR. Both of these molecular analyses have the advantage that they do not require living cells. They all have the disadvantage, however, of looking at only a single genetic lesion, and they provide no information about other genetic events. RT-PCR has emerged as the diagnostic test of choice for many clinical labs, and it can also be used to quantify response to treatment and detect minimal residual disease following bone marrow transplant.




Question 5
5
How is the BCR-ABL fusion protein targeted therapeutically?
Show answer

Understanding of the pathophysiology of CML led to the development of one of the first “targeted therapies,” in which a drug is targeting a particular molecular mechanism of disease. Because targeted therapy aims to impact only the molecular abnormalities and not the normal tissues of the body, there is more specificity to the treatment and often fewer side effects. An analogy would be trying to shoot a target with a missile versus a sniper rifle. Both may hit the target, but the missile will cause more collateral damage. In CML, patients are treated with inhibitors of the BCR-ABL kinase. Eventually, many patients develop mutations that confer resistance to the drug. Second- and third-generation kinase inhibitors that represent separate non-mutated epitopes are then used.

Chronic myelogenous leukemia
Chronic myelogenous leukemia, blast phase

Blood smears, chronic myelogenous leukemia (CML), blast phase - Medium power

The natural history of CML is a chronic stable phase of variable length followed by a transformation to a more acute immature process (acceleration or blast phase). The advent of BCR-ABL kinase inhibitors has led to sustained hematologic remissions in more than 90% of patients. While this is likely not a “curative” therapy because it does not destroy the CML stem cell, it controls blood counts and greatly decreases the risk of transformation (which accounts for most adverse outcomes in these patients). Hematopoietic stem cell transplantation is highly effective in young patients with controlled disease, with a cure rate of approximately 75%.

Question 1
1
What is the prognosis of blast transformation of CML?
Show answer

The prognosis is unfavorable. When blast phase has occurred, the clinical course is generally rapidly progressive. Kinase inhibitor therapy and transplantation at this time generally confer little benefit.


Image 5


Chronic myelogenous leukemia (CML) blast phase - Flow cytometric analysis

Blast phase of CML generally takes one of two forms: myeloid (two thirds of cases) or precursor B lymphoid (one third of cases). Myeloid blast phase resembles acute myelogenous leukemia, whereas lymphoid blast phase closely resembles acute lymphoblastic leukemia. These can usually be distinguished by flow cytometry, as shown in this image.

Question 1
1
Is this a lymphoid or myeloid blast phase?
Show answer

Expression of the B-cell antigen CD19, the common acute lymphoblastic leukemia antigen (CALLA) CD10, and the lymphoid blast marker terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase (TdT) indicates that this is a precursor B-cell lymphoblastic phase. This immunophenotype is indistinguishable from that seen in precursor B acute lymphoblastic leukemia. A myeloid blast phase would consist of cells expressing myeloid antigens such as CD13, CD33, or myeloperoxidase.


Question 2
2
What does the existence of this type of blast phase imply about the nature of the transformed cell in CML?
Show answer

It implies that the pluripotent stem cell that is transformed in CML has the capacity to differentiate into not only myeloid cells but also lymphoid cells.


Question 3
3
Would these cells still possess the Philadelphia (Ph) chromosome? Would they show immunoglobulin gene rearrangement?
Show answer

Even though these are precursor B lymphoid cells, rather than granulocytes, they still possess the Ph chromosome. In addition, they generally show rearrangement of their immunoglobulin heavy chain genes.

Chronic myelogenous leukemia (CML), blast phase - Karyotype

Karyotypic analysis of peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs) from the patient’s second presentation reveals that although the cells have a lymphoid morphology, they still contain the t(9;22) translocation of CML. In addition, they have acquired several new chromosomal abnormalities: extra copies of chromosomes 8, 10, and 19 and the abnormal chromosome 22 (the Philadelphia chromosome).

Question 1
1
What is the significance of the additional chromosomal abnormalities seen in the patient’s second presentation?
Show answer

The tumor cells have accumulated additional genetic changes, and in the process, the disease has become more aggressive (acute leukemia). The efficacy of kinase inhibitors is thought to partially result from decreasing the proliferative drive of these tumors that lead to the accumulation of more aggressive genetic changes.

Megakaryocytes
Maturing erythroid cells

Blood smear, polycythemia vera (PV) - Very low power

PV is a myeloproliferative disorder characterized by excessive production of all three myeloid cell lines (granulocytic, erythroid, and megakaryocytic). In most patients, the most prominent expansion is in the erythroid lineage, producing the erythrocytosis (shown in this image) that is the cardinal feature of this disease. Leukocytosis and thrombocytosis may also be present. The bone marrow shows a panhyperplasia, although an expansion of erythroid precursors and of large hyperlobated megakaryocytes is most prominent.

Question 1
1
What are the main clinical manifestations of PV?
Show answer

The main clinical manifestations of PV, are thrombosis and hypertension due to increased blood viscosity.


Question 2
2
What is the underlying molecular abnormality in PV?
Show answer

PV is strongly associated with activating mutations in the JAK2 tyrosine kinase. Constitutive activation leads to JAK/STAT signaling, which includes many hematopoietic growth factor receptors as well as the erythropoietin receptor.


Question 3
3
What are the treatment and prognosis of PV?
Show answer

The most common therapy is phlebotomy with the aim of rendering patients iron deficient. Chemotherapy may also be used in some instances. The median survival is prolonged (10-20 years). JAK2 inhibitors are currently being used in clinical trials and offer the potential of future targeted therapy.


Question 4
4
Does PV undergo any type of transformation? Is the rate of transformation related to the form of therapy?
Show answer

Blast phase supervenes in 2% of patients. The low end of this range is seen in patients who are treated with phlebotomy, whereas much higher rates of blast phase occur in patients treated with chemotherapy. A second type of transformation is known as the spent phase. This phase represents progressive marrow fibrosis with eventual marrow failure and death from the consequences of cytopenia. This phase occurs in 10% to 20% of patients.


Question 5
5
How may PV be distinguished from secondary erythrocytosis?
Show answer

All forms of secondary erythrocytosis result from increases in erythropoietin, either physiologic or pathologic. The erythroid proliferation in PV is autonomous and results in decreased production of erythropoietin by the kidney.

Essential thrombocytopenia, bone marrow
Megakaryocytes

Blood smear, essential thrombocythemia (ET) - High power

ET is the least common and most benign of the chronic myeloproliferative disorders. Although it is a stem cell defect similar to the other myeloproliferative disorders, the cellular expansion is largely restricted to the megakaryocytic lineage, resulting in megakaryocytic hyperplasia in the bone marrow and marked thrombocytosis (commonly>1 million/μL).

Question 1
1
What are the clinical manifestations of ET?
Show answer

The clinical manifestations of ET are hemorrhage (most commonly) and thrombosis. The paradoxic presence of hemorrhage in a state with increased platelets is due to abnormalities of platelet function in this disorder.


Question 2
2
What is the molecular pathogenesis of ET?
Show answer

ET is caused by activating mutations that directly or indirectly promote tyrosine kinase receptor signaling, leading to platelet hyperproliferation. The most common mutations are activating mutations in the JAK2 receptor (same receptor mutated in PV), MPL (a receptor normally activated by thrombopoietin), or calreticulin (an endoplasmic reticulum protein which may activate JAK/STAT signaling).


Question 3
3
What is the prognosis of ET?
Show answer

The median survival of ET patients is similar to that of age-matched controls.


Question 4
4
Is it easy to distinguish ET from reactive thrombocytosis?
Show answer

No. It may be extremely difficult to distinguish ET from reactive thrombocytosis, especially when the platelet count is only modestly elevated. No generally used laboratory tests are capable of making the distinction except in the few ET cases that possess karyotypic abnormalities.


Question 5
5
What is the treatment for ET?
Show answer

Some oncologists do not treat ET routinely until the appearance of complications. “Gentle chemotherapy” can be used to reduce the number and function of the platelets in ET to prevent thrombosis and hemorrhage.

Blood smear, primary myelofibrosis (PM) - Medium power

This peripheral smear shows several of the changes characteristic of PM. One such abnormality is the presence of poikilocytosis, including many teardrop cells (dacryocytes). In addition, the presence of circulating normoblasts (left) and myeloblasts (right)—a condition known as leukoerythroblastosis—can be readily identified. Although leukoerythroblastosis is typical of PM, it can also be seen in various other conditions, such as metastatic cancer to the marrow (or any other myelophthisic process), and is not diagnostic in and of itself. Definitive diagnosis of PM requires bone marrow analysis, which typically reveals the development of obliterative marrow fibrosis (right) with the loss of normal marrow elements. The deposition of fibrous material becomes more apparent with a reticulin stain of the marrow (left). In addition to the characteristic changes in the peripheral blood, certain morphologic and functional changes in the spleen are typical of PM. On gross inspection, the spleen (second feature) is frequently markedly enlarged. Extramedullary hematopoiesis in the spleen (remaining features) is thought to be responsible for the architectural distortion found in patients with PM.

Question 1
1
What is the clinical course of PM?
Show answer

Patients with PM experience progressive marrow fibrosis and failure, resulting in death from cytopenia after a median survival from diagnosis of 5 years. Blast phase occurs in 5% to 10% of patients.


Question 2
2
What is the molecular abnormality in PM?
Show answer

Similar to essential thrombocythemia, mutations in JAK2, MPL, and calreticulin either directly or indirectly activate receptor tyrosine kinase signaling. The neoplastic megakaryocytes release inappropriate amounts of fibrogenic factors (transforming growth factor-beta [TGF -beta] and platelet derived growth factor [PDGF]) that lead to fibrosis in the bone marrow


Question 3
3
How do PM and the spent phase of polycythemia vera (PV) differ?
Show answer

The spent phase of PV has essentially identical hematologic and pathologic features to PM, emphasizing the relatedness of these disorders. Patients with the spent phase of PV tend to have a rapid, progressive, downhill course.
