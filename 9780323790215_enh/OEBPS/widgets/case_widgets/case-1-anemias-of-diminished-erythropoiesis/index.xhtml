A 65-year-old man presented to his physician with increasing fatigue and weakness. He recently began experiencing shortness of breath with performance of routine physical activity. The patient had no personal or family history of anemia, unusual dietary habits, or bleeding. On physical examination, pallor and tachycardia were noted without other abnormal findings. Image 1

Peripheral blood in iron deficiency (100x)

Peripheral blood smear, normal - High power

The normal peripheral blood smear illustrates the usual circulating blood elements. The red blood cells are uniform in size with an average diameter of 7 microns. The central pallor is about one third of the cell diameter. A lymphocyte, a segmented neutrophil, and multiple platelets are also seen. In iron deficiency anemia, the red blood cells are decreased in number and are smaller than normal (microcytic). The central area of pallor is increased (hypochromia), a reflection that the cells contain less than normal amounts of hemoglobin. The red blood cell changes shown in the image are an example of microcytic, hypochromic anemia. In addition, considerable variation in the size (anisocytosis) and shape (poikilocytosis) of these cells may be observed. The complete blood count report of such a patient would show a decrease in the mean cell volume and usually in the mean cell hemoglobin concentration. Microcytic hypochromic anemia is typical—but not diagnostic—of iron deficiency anemia. These cellular changes are the result of failure of the red blood cell to produce adequate hemoglobin.

Iron deficiency anemia may be characterized as what type of maturation defect?

Iron deficiency anemia may be characterized as a cytoplasmic defect.


Question 2
2
What is the functional classification of iron deficiency anemia? What is the morphologic classification of iron deficiency anemia?

Iron deficiency is an example of ineffective erythropoiesis. The marrow has increased numbers of erythroid precursors, but they are incapable of maturing appropriately, resulting in decreased production of mature red blood cells. Iron deficiency is morphologically an example of microcytic hypochromic anemia, along with thalassemia minor and some examples of anemia of chronic disease.

Bone marrow, iron deficiency anemia, Prussian blue stain - High power

The two frames in this image represent Prussian blue stains of bone marrow aspirates in normal (left) and iron-deficiency (right) states. Prussian blue is a special stain that colors hemosiderin (a storage form of iron) blue. The frame on the left shows a macrophage in which cytoplasm stains deep blue because of a heavy concentration of storage iron. Such cells are known as hemosiderin-laden macrophages. Iron accumulates within the macrophages, normally the major site of iron storage, as a byproduct of the breakdown of senescent red blood cells. Iron is recovered from the macrophages and delivered by transferrin to the normoblasts to be used in the synthesis of hemoglobin. Also shown in this frame are a few nucleated red blood cells containing tiny iron granules staining blue; these cells are called sideroblasts.

Question 2
2
What is the most common cause of iron deficiency anemia in the United States?

Chronic blood loss is the most common cause of iron deficiency anemia in the United States. Classic sources of chronic blood loss include heavy periods in younger to middle-aged women and older patients with an undiagnosed colon cancer. Other causes of iron deficiency include dietary deficiency (rare in developed countries), impaired absorption (e.g., sprue, chronic diarrhea, post-gastrectomy), and increased requirement (e.g., growing children, pregnant women).

Question 1
1
What are the major causes of megaloblastic anemia?

Folate and B12 deficiency are the major causes. Chemotherapeutic agents can also cause megaloblastic anemia. Other causes of anemia include reticulocytosis, alcoholism, liver disease, dyserythropoietic bone marrow disorders, and hypothyroidism, but the mean cell volume rarely reaches levels higher than 110 to 115 fL (µm3) in these disorders. These disorders are not due to impaired DNA synthesis.

Question 2
2
What is the pathogenesis of pernicious anemia?

B12 absorption in the small bowel requires intrinsic factor, which is synthesized by parietal cells in the gastric fundus. In pernicious anemia, autoantibodies to parietal cells and intrinsic factor cause a chronic atrophic gastritis and decrease the amount of intrinsic factor being produced. This leads to impaired B12 absorption.

Major cytologic features

Bone marrow biopsy specimen, megaloblastic anemia - Low power

Megaloblastic anemia is another example of ineffective erythropoiesis, although in this disease, the maturation defect is not restricted to the erythroid lineage (i.e., this represents ineffective hematopoiesis). This bone marrow biopsy specimen (10×) shows a hypercellular marrow typical of megaloblastic hematopoiesis. The hypercellularity is due to the marrow response to peripheral cytopenias. The first feature (aspirate smears of bone marrow) shows the difference between normoblastic (left) and megaloblastic (right) maturation. In megaloblastic erythropoiesis, there is a nuclear maturation defect in erythroid precursors. Morphologic differences include a larger cell, larger nucleus, and finer chromatin than in normoblastic maturation. The second feature shows some of the major cytologic features of megaloblastic states discussed earlier; specifically, giant bands and megaloblastic erythropoiesis. Some erythroid precursors show irregular nuclear contours, termed dyserythropoiesis.

Question 1
1
What is the biochemical basis of delayed nuclear maturation in megaloblastic anemias?

Folate is required for the synthesis of deoxythymidylate monophosphate, which is required for DNA synthesis. Vitamin B12 is essential for the synthesis of the biologically active form of folic acid, tetrahydrofolate. In vitamin B12 deficiency, there is an internal folate deficiency.

Question 2
2
What are the similarities and differences between the effects of folate deficiency and vitamin B12 deficiency? Can these two be distinguished on the basis of changes in the blood smear or marrow aspirate?

Folate deficiency and vitamin B12 deficiency produce megaloblastic anemias that are a result of abnormal nuclear maturation. In most cases, folate deficiency is related to inadequate dietary intake, whereas vitamin B12 deficiency involves failure to absorb vitamin B12 normally, as in pernicious anemia. Folate and vitamin B12 deficiency have similar bone marrow and blood presentations. Serum vitamin B12, folate, and red blood cell folate levels are necessary to differentiate the two processes. In addition to causing megaloblastic anemia, vitamin B12 deficiency leads to neurologic changes resulting from damage to the ascending and descending fibers of the posterolateral spinal tracts. Folate deficiency does not affect the nervous system.



Image 5


Red cell maturation - Schematic

The red blood cells in the bone marrow undergo a maturation process, the purpose of which is to create cells full of hemoglobin. This maturation process is diagrammatically represented in this image. The red blood cell precursor (normoblast) begins as a large cell with a high nuclear-to-cytoplasmic ratio (N/C ratio) (left side of diagram). At this stage of maturation, the cytoplasm is very blue because the cell contains high concentrations of mRNA. (RNA stains blue with the Wright stain.) Over the course of 72 hours, the normoblast matures: the cell becomes smaller, the chromatin condenses, and the nucleus is finally extruded from the cell. While these cytologic changes are proceeding, the cytoplasm acquires an increasing concentration of hemoglobin owing to the incorporation of heme (protoporphyrin and iron) into the alpha and beta globin chains. As a result, the cell becomes redder, reflecting the increased concentration of hemoglobin. After extrusion of the nucleus, the new red blood cell—or reticulocyte—remains in the marrow for about 24 hours. Understanding this sequence permits conceptualization of the changes associated with certain disease states. Deficiencies that alter nuclear maturation (i.e., folate deficiency, vitamin B12 deficiency) delay progression of the nucleus through the normal cell cycle and lead to the formation of abnormally shaped red blood cells, which, on average, have a higher volume (increased mean cell volume [MCV]) because of relatively unimpaired cytoplasmic growth and maturation. Such changes are often identified as nuclear maturation defects. If the deficiency primarily affects cytoplasmic maturation—as in the case of iron deficiency—the cells, on average, are smaller (decreased MCV).

Question 1
1
Why does the normoblast contain increased mRNA?

The normoblast has increased mRNA because it is assembling the cellular machinery to engage in protein production; specifically, the hemoglobin alpha and beta globin molecules.


Question 2
2
How does one explain the marked hypercellularity of the marrow in megaloblastic anemia if the cell cycle is slowed down?

While each proliferating cell is progressing through the cell cycle at a considerably slower rate than normal, a much higher proportion of marrow cells are cycling than in normal marrow.

Peripheral blood smear

Bone marrow, normal and aplastic anemia - Low power

Aplastic anemia is a misnomer because it represents a panaplasia of the marrow. From the standpoint of the erythroid compartment, it is an example of a proliferative defect of erythropoiesis because few red blood cell precursors are present in the marrow to support red blood cell production. The precursors that are present (if any) seem to mature appropriately. Because there is no maturation defect, the anemia is bland, with little anisopoikilocytosis. A bone marrow biopsy specimen showing normocellular marrow is depicted in the left panel of the image. Normally, approximately 50% to 60% of the marrow space is hematopoietic elements, while the fat cells make up the remaining 40%. These percentages are age dependent, with the proportion of marrow fat increasing with age. The right panel depicts a bone marrow biopsy specimen in aplastic anemia. The marrow space is virtually devoid of hematopoietic elements. The higher magnification feature corroborates the hypocellularity (<5%) of the marrow. The residual cellular composition includes some myeloid elements, plasma cells, and lymphocytes. The hypocellularity of the marrow is reflected in the peripheral blood smear (second feature), which shows granulocytopenia (the nucleated cell shown is a lymphocyte) and leukopenia. (Normally, at this magnification, you should be able to identify five to seven leukocytes.) There is ample space between red blood cells, a reflection of this patient’s anemia, and few, if any, platelets.

Question 1
1
What is the pathogenesis of aplastic anemia? How is it treated?

Aplastic anemia is characterized by anemia, neutropenia, and thrombocytopenia. It appears to result from an immunologically mediated suppression of multipotent myeloid stem cells, perhaps after they have been antigenically altered by some environmental factors. Alternatively, primary stem cell abnormalities may play a role. As a result, there is inadequate production or release of the differentiated cell lines. Some cases follow exposure to chemicals, drugs, or viral infections (particularly hepatitis). Bone marrow transplantation is curative. In cases where transplantation is not possible, immunosuppressive therapy is implemented.

How does pure red cell aplasia differ from aplastic anemia? What are its major causes?

Pure red cell aplasia differs from aplastic anemia in that it represents a selective depletion of red cell precursors with intact granulocytic and megakaryocytic lineages. Most cases are immune mediated and may be associated with a variety of conditions, including neoplasia, infection, drug and toxin exposure, and idiopathic. An important cause of transient pure red cell aplasia is infection with parvovirus B19

Bone marrow, increased iron

Blood smear, anemia of chronic disease (AOCD) - High power

The red cells in AOCD are reduced in number and may be normocytic, as shown in the image, or mildly microcytic and minimally hypochromic, as shown in the first feature. The anemia is usually of mild to moderate degree. Anemia of chronic disease develops in the setting of persistent systemic inflammation and essentially leads to the sequestration of iron inside of “constipated” macrophages. The increased storage iron is reflected as increased serum ferritin; thus, increased serum ferritin in conjunction with decreased serum iron is characteristic of AOCD.

Question 1
1
What is the ferritin level in iron deficiency anemia?

In iron deficiency anemia, serum ferritin is decreased, allowing distinction from AOCD.


Question 2
2
What is the etiology and pathogenesis of AOCD?

AOCD is caused by persistent systemic inflammation such as chronic microbial infections (e.g., osteomyelitis, endocarditis, lung abscess), chronic immune disorders (e.g., rheumatoid arthritis), or neoplasia (e.g., lung or breast carcinoma, Hodgkin lymphoma). This chronic inflammation leads to the release of IL-6, which upregulates a liver protein called hepcidin. Hepcidin causes macrophages to sequester iron instead of transporting it to the bone marrow for erythropoiesis, also decreasing iron absorption/transport in intestinal epithelial cells. Erythrocyte precursors sense a low iron state because the serum iron is low even though there is ample total body iron being stored. Without available iron, the erythroid precursors are unable to develop and the patient becomes anemic. Interestingly, AOCD may actually be an evolutionary mechanism to prevent bacteria from getting hold of the iron that they need to carry out their pathogenic actions during chronic infections.






Image 8


Blood smear, anemia of renal failure - Medium power

Anemia of renal failure results directly from a deficiency in erythropoietin production, making it unique among the many types of anemia. Because of the decreased erythropoietin, there is decreased proliferation of erythroid precursors in the marrow. Anemia of renal failure falls into the broad category of hypoproliferative anemias. The anemia is generally normochromic/normocytic with little anisopoikilocytosis, as illustrated in this image.

Question 1
1
How is anemia of renal failure treated?

Most patients respond well to exogenous erythropoietin administration, assuming other factors interfering with erythropoiesis (e.g., iron deficiency) are absent.
