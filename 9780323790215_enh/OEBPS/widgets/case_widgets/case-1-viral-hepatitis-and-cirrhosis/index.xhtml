Viral Hepatitis and Cirrhosis

Learning Objectives

  • 1. Describe the relative frequency, epidemiology, route of transmission, incubation period, and tendency to form a carrier/chronic state of infection with:
    • a. hepatitis A virus (HAV)
    • b. hepatitis B virus (HBV)
    • c. hepatitis C virus (HCV)
    • d. delta virus (HDV)
  • 2. Define the term carrier state as it relates to viral hepatitis infection.
  • 3. Differentiate between coinfection and superinfection with HDV.
  • 4. Discuss the role of the immune response in causing acute liver damage in viral hepatitis.
  • 5. Discuss the use of serologic tests in differentiating between the various forms of viral hepatitis and in determining the state of the disease (i.e., asymptomatic and symptomatic acute hepatitis and carrier and chronic states of infection).
  • 6. Describe the pathology of acute hepatitis, subfulminant and fulminant hepatitis, and chronic hepatitis.
  • 7. List multiple causes, other than viral infections, of subfulminant and fulminant hepatic failure.
  • 8. Describe the pathology of cirrhosis due to chronic viral hepatitis, and recognize that the use of the term postnecrotic cirrhosis is clinically misleading.
  • 9. Classify the various categories of liver disease associated with drug and toxin exposures, and list some of the more important agents responsible for these.

Scenario

A 30-year-old black homosexual man presented to the local clinic complaining of anorexia, nausea, vomiting, and malaise. He noted the onset of anorexia and nausea a week prior to presentation but attributed it to the flu. However, intractable symptoms developed, such that he had not kept anything down for the past 2 or 3 days and had lost 10 pounds. His past medical history was pertinent for multiple sexual partners. His sexual practices included unprotected anal intercourse and anal-oral sex. He had a history of adequately treated syphilis and the usual childhood diseases. There was no history of intravenous drug use. He admitted to drinking alcohol. Physical examination revealed a thin man who looked ill. His blood pressure was 110/80 lying down, which decreased to 90/60 upon sitting. His pulse changed from 84 lying down to 108 when sitting. His temperature was 37.5° C, and his respiratory rate was 20. His eyes were yellow. There was mild right upper quadrant abdominal tenderness. The liver percussed to 3 cm below the costal margin with a total span of 13 cm. No spleen was palpated. Cardiac, respiratory, and other parts of the examination were normal.

He was admitted to the hospital for volume repletion and antiemetics. After the patient was stable, he was discharged home with warnings that his sexual partners might be at risk for a similar infection and should be seen by a physician. When seen in the clinic 3 months after discharge, the patient was much better. His liver enzymes had normalized. The anti-delta virus antibody titer had returned from the reference laboratory and was negative.

Question 1
1
What are the differences between routes of transmission of HBV and hepatitis C (HCV) viruses and hepatitis A virus (HAV)?
Show answer

Hepatitis A infection (infectious hepatitis): This RNA virus is spread through a fecal-oral route, because the virus is shed predominantly in the stool. Cases of HAV are commonly seen as sporadic outbreaks in restaurants where a food-handler has a subclinical or early infection or in other close-contact settings. In less developed countries, where sanitation and poor hygiene are problems, HAV is usually contracted as a childhood disease, and most adults show serologic evidence of prior exposure.


Figure 18-10 Temporal changes in serologic markers in acute hepatitis A infection. HAV, Hepatitis A virus.

Hepatitis B infection: This DNA virus is spread parenterally and through intimate contact, whereby HBV is transferred to mucosal or cutaneous surfaces, as may occur during various sexual activities. Except for intravenous drug users, medical personnel, the homosexual community, and individuals who have received multiple blood products, HBV is not very common in the United States. It is endemic in areas such as Africa and Southeast Asia where neonatal infections are common due to vertical transmission from an infected mother to her fetus. An estimated one-third of the world population is infected with HBV.

Hepatitis C infection: Like HBV infection, HCV, which is an RNA virus, is a major cause of liver disease worldwide. HCV is primarily spread parenterally. It is only rarely spread through contact-associated mechanisms such as during sexual intercourse, and it is even less frequently spread through vertical transmission.

Question 1
1
What are the potential clinical outcomes and their relative frequencies in patients with acute infections with HBV? How does this differ from infections with hepatitis A virus and hepatitis C virus?
Show answer

Most patients with hepatitis B have either subclinical or minimally symptomatic acute infections, and they completely recover from their acute infection. Very few become carriers. Approximately 5% of infected adults develop chronic hepatitis. A higher percentage of acutely infected neonates and small children go on to develop carrier states/chronic hepatitis B infections. About one third of patients with chronic hepatitis B infections eventually develop cirrhosis. Patients with hepatitis C infections have a much greater chance of developing chronic hepatitis (greater than 50% of infected adults), and of these individuals, a significant percentage go on to develop cirrhosis. Patients with hepatitis A viral infections never develop a carrier or chronic state after their acute illness.

Question 1
1
What clinical laboratory abnormalities, including serologic results, are present in acute viral hepatitis due to HBV?
Show answer

In acute viral hepatitis, the liver function test results are consistent with hepatocellular damage. Aspartate aminotransferase and alanine aminotransferase, intracellular hepatic enzymes, are released into the blood when liver cells die. Levels of both are significantly elevated in acute viral hepatitis. In acute hepatitis B, HBsAg and anti-HBc are positive. Anti-HBc is of the IgM type. Alkaline phosphatase and another enzyme, gamma-glutamyl transpeptidase, are elevated in intrahepatic and extrahepatic cholestatic diseases. They are only mildly elevated in acute viral hepatitis. A conjugated hyperbilirubinemia with an elevated total and direct bilirubin and bilirubinuria are also usually present in cases of acute viral hepatitis.


Question 2
2
What are the differences in the laboratory results in the other states of infection with HBV (i.e., recovery following an acute infection, carrier/chronic states of infection)?
Show answer

The presence of circulating HBsAg, HBeAg, and HBV DNA is indicative of active HBV infection, but it is the presence of the IgM fraction of anti-HBc that is indicative of an acute infection. If the anti-HBc is positive, but the IgM fraction is negative (i.e., patient has IgG anti-HBc), this would be compatible with a carrier/chronic infection with HBV. With chronic infection and progressive liver injury, in addition to the continued presence of HBsAg, HBe antigen and HBV DNA are also persistently present in the blood. Anti-HBs, which is a neutralizing antibody and thus confers immunity to reinfection, is a marker that is detected several weeks to months after resolution of the acute illness and disappearance of HBsAg from the blood. Anti-HBs is also detected in individuals who have responded to hepatitis B vaccination. In the window period between the disappearance of HBsAg and development of anti-HBs, the IgM fraction of anti-HBc may be the only positive marker of disease. Anti-HBe appears after disappearance of HBeAg and is indicative of waning disease and recovery.

Question 1
1
Can you name some causes, other than hepatotropic virus infections, of a similar clinical picture of fulminant hepatic failure?
Show answer

Viral hepatitis and drug or toxin exposure (e.g., acetaminophen, Amanita phalloides mycotoxin) are the main causes. Other causes include ischemia, hepatic vein obstruction (Budd-Chiari syndrome), massive malignant infiltration of the liver, Wilson disease, and acute fatty liver of pregnancy.

Question 1
1
What are the potential outcomes of patients with fulminant hepatitis?
Show answer

Many patients with subfulminant or fulminant hepatitis are severely ill and die of acute liver failure. Depending on the cause and their clinical condition, liver transplantation may be the only option for some patients. A small number of patients may go on to recovery if the cause of the liver injury has been removed and supportive medical care is given.

Question 1
1
Do patients who are carriers of HBV manifest any signs or symptoms of infection, and can they transmit viral hepatitis to another individual?
Show answer

Most patients are totally asymptomatic or only minimally symptomatic and are detected during evaluation of abnormal laboratory tests performed for other reasons. These patients may have normal or only minimally elevated aminotransferase levels. They, by definition, are HBsAg positive and can thus transmit viral hepatitis to another individual. Other individuals, who are also considered to be carriers of the virus, develop chronic hepatitis with persistence of HBeAg and HBV DNA in the blood.

What are the possible outcomes of patients with chronic liver disease due to viral hepatitis? What might one expect from a patient with a liver biopsy that looks like this?
Show answer

Patients with chronic viral hepatitis may completely recover at some point, or they may have chronic hepatitis indefinitely. Of the latter group, some will go on to develop cirrhosis. This is more common in patients with chronic hepatitis due to hepatitis C virus than in patients with hepatitis due to HBV. This biopsy specimen shows little in the way of active liver injury; that is, interface hepatitis (piecemeal necrosis) or fibrosis. Thus, this patient does not have significant chronic liver injury and may benefit from medical therapy aimed at inducing recovery.

How have new drugs changed the treatment of hepatitis C?
Show answer

Previously, patients were treated with ribavirin and interferon with only moderate response rates. Recently, protease and polymerase inhibitors have been approved by the Food and Drug Administration (FDA), leading to a cure rate of greater than 90%.


Targeted Therapy Box – Hep to Go: Beyond Ribavin and Interferon for Hepatitis C

Tumors

Hep to Go: Beyond Ribavirin and Interferon for Hepatitis C

Hepatitis C (“hep C”) infections result in chronic disease in more than 80% of affected persons, of whom 20% to 30% experience progression to cirrhosis. Consequently, hepatitis C is the most common cause of chronic liver disease in the United States and is the number-one indication for liver transplantation. Even worse, 5% of chronically infected patients develop hepatocellular carcinoma.

At the time of the initial infection with hepatitis C virus (HCV), most patients do not mount a sufficiently robust initial immune response to clear the RNA virus, and with a low-fidelity viral polymerase, HCV acquires new mutations at a prodigious rate. Moreover, HCV makes a protease that can cleave adaptor molecules, and its core protein inhibits intracellular kinase signaling, both of which normally drive an antiviral response.

Preventing the initial infection is clearly the best way to avoid the sequelae, but the search for a vaccine has been impeded by poorly immunogenic envelope proteins and a heterogeneous mixture of viral subspecies with minimal antigenic overlap. Accordingly, the current clinical approach focuses on frustrating viral replication and augmenting the host innate response:

  • Ribavirin is a nucleoside analogue that the viral polymerase incorporates into the viral genome, thereby promoting “lethal” mutagenesis; it can pair with cytidine and uridine (mimicking guanine and adenine bases), leading to a theoretical “error catastrophe.” Ribavirin also can inhibit cellular inosine monophosphate dehydrogenase, resulting in guanosine triphosphate (GTP) depletion and inhibition of viral replication.
  • Interferon-α is a cytokine that drives a number of innate immune responses that assist in viral eradication by improved natural killer cell activity and by inhibition of HCV replication.

Unfortunately, the standard two-drug cocktail is expensive ($20,000 for the typical 48-week course) and has many untoward side effects such as cytopenias, fatigue, and depression. In addition, it is effective in only about 40% of patients. Much effort, therefore, is being devoted to the development of next-generation therapies such as protease inhibitors. These therapies are based on the current understanding of the molecular basis of viral replication.

After infection, the HCV genome initially is translated as a single long polyprotein that has to be cleaved by a specific viral serine protease (NS3) for virus assembly to occur. Consequently, use of selective protease inhibitors constitutes an attractive form of adjunctive therapy. Indeed, when added to interferon and ribavirin, protease inhibitors such as telaprevir and boceprevir greatly reduce viral replication in almost 70% of cases, and in many patients, HCV is undetectable within 12 to 24 weeks. Such triple-compound regimens are likely to become the standard of care within a short period of time; moreover, in a fashion analogous to that for the highly active antiretroviral therapy (HAART) for human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) infection, triple-drug cocktails also may squelch HCV infections—from so many different directions—that they never get the chance to mutate.

Question 1
1
Can you name some different diseases that would result in a pattern of cirrhosis similar to that in cirrhosis from chronic viral hepatitis?
Show answer

This is macronodular cirrhosis. Viral hepatitis (HBV and HCV) is the most common cause of macronodular cirrhosis. In addition, Wilson disease and alpha-1-antitrypsin can cause macronodular cirrhosis. Micronodular cirrhosis (nodules <3 mm) classically occur secondary to alcoholism, non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH), and hemochromatosis. Liver disease from other causes, including drugs and toxins and biliary cirrhosis from any cause, can look similar in an advanced stage of the illness. Eventually, the history and certain laboratory tests may be the only clues as to the original cause of liver injury.

Question 1
1
Can you describe the characteristics of cirrhosis, whatever the cause?
Show answer

Cirrhosis is defined by the presence of fibrosis that disrupts the parenchymal architecture diffusely by creating interconnecting fibrous bands that separate parenchymal nodules of regenerative hepatocytes.
