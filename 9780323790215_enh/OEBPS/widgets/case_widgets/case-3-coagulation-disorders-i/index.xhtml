Scenario

A 7-month-old boy presents to the pediatric emergency department with a markedly swollen right knee that is tender. He has recently started crawling. His physical examination is unremarkable except for his right knee and some large healing bruises on his lower legs. On further questioning, his parents state that these kinds of bruises have been noticed off and on since his birth. The family history is significant for a bleeding tendency in the infant’s maternal uncle, who had died at a young age because of massive bleeding.

Question 1
1
What are the two main components of the hemostatic system?

The hemostatic system can be divided into primary and secondary components. Primary hemostasis involves formation of a platelet plug at the site of vascular injury. Primary hemostatic disorders include qualitative or quantitative defects in platelets. Platelet defects are usually acquired; especially common are thrombocytopenia and platelet dysfunction resulting from ingestion of drugs such as aspirin. Rare inherited platelet defects also occur. A common inherited disorder of primary hemostasis is von Willebrand disease. von Willebrand protein is a complex multimer necessary for normal binding of platelets to subendothelial collagen; deficiencies or defects in this protein inhibit normal platelet plug formation despite a normal platelet count. Secondary hemostasis involves activation of the clotting cascade and formation of a fibrin clot. Secondary hemostatic disorders usually involve deficiencies in clotting factors. The most common inherited clotting factor deficiencies are hemophilia A and B. Acquired clotting factor problems are more common; the most common is due to ingestion of oral anticoagulants.

How are the clinical manifestations of primary versus secondary hemostatic disorders different?

Disorders of primary hemostasis typically manifest as mucocutaneous bleeding (easy bruising, nosebleeds, menorrhagia); deep tissue bleeding and hemarthroses are uncommon. Disorders of secondary hemostasis typically manifest as skin, muscle, or joint bleeding and severe postoperative bleeding. Deep tissue bleeding may be spontaneous or in response to trauma, depending on the severity of the defect.

Image 2

Clotting cascade - Schematic

The clotting cascade is initiated by the tissue factor pathway and propagated by the intrinsic pathway. Hemophilia is due to a deficiency of either factor VIII (hemophilia A) or factor IX (hemophilia B). The severity of clinical bleeding is correlated with the extent of deficiency of these clotting factors. The genes for factor VIII and factor IX are located on the X chromosome. Factor VIII is synthesized by endothelial cells lining the sinusoids of the liver. It has a short half-life of 12 hours. It is carried on von Willebrand factor (a large multimeric glycoprotein), which protects it from proteolysis.

Question 1
1
What is the mode of inheritance of hemophilia A and B?

The mode of inheritance is X-linked recessive. Females are carriers, and males are affected. Rarely, females may be affected if there is extreme lyonization of the X chromosome or if they have Turner syndrome.


Question 2
2
What is the molecular basis for hemophilia?

There may be point mutation, a deletion, or a substitution in the gene, leading to decreased factor VIII or IX synthesis.

Question 1
1
What laboratory studies should be ordered in this case?

Laboratory investigation is crucial in the proper treatment of bleeding disorders. Screening coagulation tests PT and activated PTT should be ordered along with a complete blood count that should include a platelet count. Hemophilia patients have prolonged activated PTT with a normal PT. Generally, the extent of prolongation of the activated PTT does not correlate with the severity of factor deficiency. If there is a known family history of hemophilia A or B, assay of factor VIII or factor IX should be ordered. If the family history is negative, a factor VIII assay should be ordered first; if normal, factor IX should be assayed because factor VIII deficiency is more common than factor IX deficiency. This particular patient had an activated PTT of 50 seconds (reference range 23-33 seconds). His factor VIII was 0%.


Question 2
2
How do factor levels correlate with disease severity in hemophilia A?

Hemophilia patients are divided into three levels of severity: (1) mild (factor VIII activity 6%-35% of normal), (2) moderate (factor VIII 1%-5%), and (3) severe (factor VIII < 1%). Patients with mild disease bleed only after moderate to severe trauma or surgery. Moderate disease is associated with a hemorrhagic tendency with mild trauma or minor surgery and occasional spontaneous hemorrhages with joint bleeds. Severe disease is associated with spontaneous bleeding, including joint hemorrhages (especially knees). This patient belongs to the severe category and requires aggressive management.


Question 3
3
How would this patient’s bleeding tendency be managed?

Hemophiliacs are treated with factor replacement therapy. Patients with mild disease can be treated with desmopressin (DDAVP), which acts on endothelial cells to release stored von Willebrand factor, which increases the available factor VIII in circulation. Patients with moderate to severe hemophilia A are treated with recombinant factor VIII replacement therapy.


Question 4
4
What is the major side effect of replacement therapy?

Patients with severe hemophilia who do not have factor VIII in their plasma make an antibody against factor VIII when exposed to factor VIII concentrates because factor VIII is foreign to their immune system. Patients who have low-titer antibody are treated with immune tolerance therapy by a high dose of recombinant factor VIII to overcome the immune system. Patients who have high-titer antibody are difficult to treat. They may be treated with (1) recombinant factor VIIa (which initiates the tissue factor pathway and avoids the need for the intrinsic pathway), (2) activated factors that bypass the requirement of factor VIII in the intrinsic pathway, or (3) porcine factor VIII.
