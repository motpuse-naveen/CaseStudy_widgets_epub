Learning Objectives
  • 1. Define meningitis, and describe the location and character of the inflammatory process.
  • 2. List possible routes of access of infectious organisms into the central nervous system (CNS).
  • 3. Contrast the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) findings characteristic of bacterial meningitis, aseptic (viral) meningitis, and chronic meningoencephalitis (tuberculous meningitis). Describe the CSF findings in viral encephalitis.
  • 4. Describe the clinical presentation and pathology of acute bacterial meningitis, and contrast this with the pathologic findings in viral meningitis. Know the sequelae of acute bacterial meningitis.
  • 5. Define the relationship between patient age and the most common etiologic organisms for bacterial meningitis.
  • 6. Compare and contrast acute bacterial meningitis and chronic meningoencephalitis.
  • 7. Define the conditions that predispose to the development of a brain abscess, and describe the clinical and pathologic features.
  • 8. Describe the clinical and pathologic findings in tuberculosis of the CNS.
  • 9. List the common causes of viral encephalitis, and describe the pathologic changes in encephalitis due to arbovirus and herpes simplex virus.
  • 10. Discuss the three basic patterns of fungal infection in the CNS (chronic meningitis, vasculitis, parenchymal invasion), and describe the specific pathologic features of cryptococcal meningitis.
  • 11. Discuss the four major characteristics of the Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome, and note the organism most commonly associated with this syndrome.

Scenario

In December, a 3-year-old girl was admitted to the hospital with symptoms of headache, fever, and lethargy. One week prior to admission, the child had been noted to have upper respiratory symptoms, including purulent rhinorrhea and cough. Three days prior to admission, she complained of symptoms consistent with otitis media. A course of amoxicillin was prescribed following consultation with a local pediatrician. Symptoms persisted, with the development of fever and emesis by the evening of admission.

On physical exam, the patient was listless and had a fever of 38.5° C. A lumbar puncture was done and the following values were obtained from the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF):

The parents consented to an autopsy. Significant findings on internal examination were limited to the central nervous system (Images 2 and 3).

Question 1
1
What organisms most commonly cause bacterial meningitis in neonates, children, and adults?
Show answer

The bacterial organisms causing meningitis may be divided as follows: Neonates: group B Streptococcus, Escherichia coli, and Listeria; Infants and children: Streptococcus pneumoniae and Neisseria meningitides; Adults: S. pneumoniae and gram-negative rods.

Question 2
2
What was the clinical diagnosis in this patient, and what factors predisposed her to develop meningitis?
Show answer

The clinical diagnosis is bacterial meningitis secondary to S. pneumoniae. This bacterium is a gram-positive, lancet-shaped coccus, often occurring in pairs. The patient had an upper respiratory tract infection associated with otitis media, predisposing her to the development of meningitis.

Question 1
1
Why is the exudate most pronounced adjacent to the meningeal vasculature?
Show answer

The inflammation reaches the central nervous system through the meningeal vasculature; therefore, one would expect a concentration of white blood cells at this location.


Question 2
2
What is the significance of the change in the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) glucose during the patient's hospital course?
Show answer

In a significant minority of patients with bacterial meningitis, the CSF glucose is normal at the time of diagnosis. In this case, the glucose was normal, but it decreased because of progression of the disease. The organism was not sensitive to the first antibiotic used; therefore, the glucose decreased as expected with bacterial meningitis.

Brain, bacterial meningitis - Low power

Bacterial meningitis is typically characterized by a purulent exudate confined to the leptomeninges. The exudate expands the meningeal space between the pia and arachnoid and may extend into the perivascular Virchow-Robin spaces. However, direct extension into the brain is rare. The bacteria most commonly causing bacterial meningitis in children under 10 years are Streptococcus pneumoniae and Neisseria meningitides. In the newborn period, group B Streptococcus, Escherichia coli, and Listeria are the most common. In adults, S. pneumoniae and gram-negative rods are the most common.

Question 1
1
What is the pathogenesis of arachnoid fibrosis as a sequela of bacterial meningitis?
Show answer

Arachnoid fibrosis (scarring) occurs following organization of the inflammatory response to the bacteria in the meninges.

Brain, tuberculous meningitis - Gross

In tuberculous meningitis, the exudate is much thicker than in pyogenic meningitis. It has a gelatinous character and typically accumulates at the base of the brain, as seen in this image. The thick exudate may obliterate the cisterns and encase cranial nerves. Arteries running through the exudate may show obliterative endarteritis with inflammatory infiltrates in their walls and marked intimal thickening. These changes can lead to ischemic injury to the brain.

Cerebral necrosis may occur as a result of direct extension of the organism into the brain or as a result of vasculitis with secondary ischemic necrosis.

Brain, tuberculoma - Gross, coronal section

A tuberculoma is shown in the temporal lobe. It is seen as a well-circumscribed intraparenchymal mass that may have effects similar to those caused by any other intracranial mass, and it may therefore mimic a tumor. It is a destructive lesion and may be associated with tuberculous meningitis.

Brain, herpes simplex virus (HSV) meningoencephalitis - Medium power

HSV produces a hemorrhagic meningoencephalitis with inflammation in both the meninges and the brain parenchyma. Note the hemorrhage surrounding the perivascular lymphocytic infiltrate. The virus directly infects cells in the cerebral cortex, causing necrosis and a glial reaction. This reaction produces a glial nodule. Although viral inclusions are not visible in these images, the virus may be identified by special staining techniques, culture, or polymerase chain reaction amplification.

Question 1
1
Is it possible to see viral inclusions in HSV1 or HSV2 encephalitis?
Show answer

One may see viral inclusions in both HSV1 and HSV2 encephalitis. These inclusions may be found in both neurons and glia.

Question 1
1
What are the various methods by which bacteria may seed the brain and produce an abscess?
Show answer

The main routes of bacterial transmission to the central nervous system that result in abscess formation include hematogenous dissemination, direct implantation from trauma, and local extension, as from the paranasal sinuses.

Brain, fungal (cryptococcal) meningitis - Gross, coronal section

The cerebral cortex shows multiple small cyst-like structures throughout the gray and white matter, corresponding to dilated perivascular (Virchow-Robin) spaces. These lesions have been aptly described as soap bubble lesions and contain mucoid material characteristic of cryptococcal meningitis.

Question 1
1
What is the route of infection with Cryptococcus neoformans?
Show answer

Cryptococcus is inhaled and initially causes a pulmonary infection that undergoes hematogenous dissemination to the central nervous system.

Brain, cryptococcal meningitis, mucin stain - Medium power

The wall of the cryptococcal organism contains large amounts of mucin, which stains red with this special stain. Question 1
1
In what clinical setting would you expect to see cryptococcal meningitis?
Show answer

Cryptococcal meningitis occurs in the setting of immunosuppression. It is particularly common in those with AIDS.
