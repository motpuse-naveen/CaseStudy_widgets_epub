Learning Objectives

  • 1. Recognize the territories that supply the anterior, middle, and posterior cerebral arteries and the vertebrobasilar system
  • 2. Describe the gross and microscopic patterns of central nervous system (CNS) focal infarcts, global hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy, and watershed (border-zone) infarcts.
  • 3. Describe the process of organization of an infarct in the CNS.
  • 4. Describe the relative roles of thrombosis and embolism in causing CNS infarcts. Identify three important sources of emboli.
  • 5. Explain the basis of hemorrhage in a hemorrhagic infarct. Compare and contrast with primary intraparenchymal hemorrhage.
  • 6. List common risk factors for, and major sites of, primary intraparenchymal hemorrhage.

Scenario

An 81-year-old, right-handed man awoke one morning unable to move his right arm or leg and unable to speak. He awakened his wife, who immediately called an ambulance. The patient was taken to the emergency department, where right-sided paralysis and aphasia (inability to speak) were documented. His previous medical history included a transmural myocardial infarct at age 75 and two episodes of transient, right-sided weakness and speech difficulty during the past year. The patient was admitted to the hospital, stabilized over the next 10 days, and discharged to a rehabilitation facility. The patient's right hemiparesis persisted, and he remained aphasic. He died suddenly, approximately 18 months after his initial presentation.

Postmortem examination revealed three-vessel coronary atherosclerosis and a remote apical myocardial infarct, accompanied by an organized intramural left ventricular thrombus. Severe carotid atherosclerosis was also present. The brain contained a large, cavitary lesion in the left lateral frontal area (Images 1 and 2). Moderate-to-severe atherosclerosis was present in the intracranial vessels (Image 3).

Question 1
1
How does an infarct become cystic? How long does it take for an infarct to undergo cystic change?
Show answer

The infarct becomes cystic because of phagocytosis of the necrotic, lipid-rich tissue by macrophages, which migrate into the infarct during the first week. Evolution of the infarct to a cystic defect is usually complete by about 6 months.

Question 1
1
What are the major risk factors for the development of cerebral atherosclerosis?
Show answer

The major causes of atherosclerosis in the CNS are the same as the causes elsewhere in the body; they include hypertension, smoking, hyperlipidemia, diabetes, and aging.


Question 2
2
What is a likely cause of our patient's frontal lobe infarct?
Show answer

There are two major possibilities to consider in this case: (1) embolic occlusion or (2) local thrombotic occlusion of a branch of the left middle cerebral artery. Embolic occlusion is much more common than local thrombosis in the middle cerebral artery territory (in contrast to the vertebrobasilar system, where thrombi are a common cause of infarcts). From a practical standpoint, it is often impossible to show the occlusive lesion at the time of autopsy, particularly in the case of old infarcts.


Question 3
3
What are possible sources of emboli in this patient?
Show answer

Possible sources of emboli in this patient include the organized mural thrombus in the left ventricle of the heart, as well as atherosclerotic plaques involving more proximal arteries (especially the carotid arteries). Local thrombosis of intracranial vessels is less common than embolic occlusion of vessels supplying the cerebral hemispheres, but it can occur. The vertebrobasilar arterial system, in contrast, is a common site for local thrombus formation.


Question 4
4
Explain the transient episodes of right-sided weakness and speech difficulty during the year before the development of the infarct.
Show answer

The episodes of transient weakness and speech difficulty likely reflect brief episodes of vascular occlusion by small embolic fragments, originating either from the heart or from atheromatous plaques in more proximal arterial segments. Such episodes are termed transient ischemic attacks (TIAs). This term denotes a self-limited episode of neurologic dysfunction lasting a few seconds to 24 hours (but usually <30 minutes). Approximately 20% to 25% of patients with TIAs develop a CNS infarct during the next 5 years.

Question 1
1
How does the appearance of this acute infarct differ from that of the remote infarct?
Show answer

In contrast to the cavitary defect that characterizes the remote infarct in our patient, in the acute setting, the necrotic tissue is swollen. This swollen appearance is caused by the accumulation of fluid, within the necrotic cells (cytotoxic edema) and within the interstitium, the latter owing to leakage of fluid into the affected tissue from damaged blood vessels (vasogenic edema). In severe cases, this edema may increase the intracranial pressure to life-threatening levels.

Question 1
1
How do brain parenchymal infarcts organize?
Show answer

Central nervous system (CNS) parenchymal infarcts organize via an influx of macrophages into the area of necrosis. The macrophages ingest the lipid-rich, necrotic parenchyma (see image 7), ultimately converting the area to a fluid-filled cavity (liquefactive necrosis). As noted previously, this process is usually complete by about 6 months after the development of the infarct.

The presence of lipid-laden macrophages, characterized by foamy cytoplasm, distinguishes organizing infarcts from acute infarcts. Organization of the infarct begins within the first week and usually continues for up to 6 months.

1
Is this an acute, organizing, or remote infarct? Why?
Show answer

Although the distinction between an acute and organizing infarct can be difficult on the basis of gross examination, this pontine lesion likely represents an organizing infarct because it is well demarcated from the adjacent viable parenchyma. The borders of acute infarcts tend to be less well defined than the borders of organizing infarcts. The lesion has not yet cavitated however, and is not a remote (healed) infarct at this stage. As noted previously, local thrombosis is an important cause of infarcts in areas of the brain supplied by branches of the vertebrobasilar system.

Question 2
2
What factors contribute to the hemorrhagic appearance of some infarcts?
Show answer

Several factors may contribute to the development of significant hemorrhage in central nervous system (CNS) infarcts, of which two of the most important are (1) anticoagulation and (2) reperfusion of nonviable tissue. Reperfusion may occur when blood enters the necrotic area from vessels in adjacent vascular territories, or when the initial occlusive lesion disappears shortly after the development of irreversible tissue damage. The latter scenario is particularly common in the case of CNS infarcts caused by embolic events. Infarcts caused by venous obstruction are also quite hemorrhagic because of the stasis caused by compromise of venous drainage.

Brain, pons, remote lacunar infarct – Gross, horizontal section

The term lacunar is used to describe small infarcts - by convention, infarcts measuring less than 1.5 cm in greatest dimension. As might be anticipated, such infarcts are a manifestation of small vessel disease and are particularly common in hypertension. In addition to the pons, the basal ganglia, thalamus, and cerebral white matter are common sites for these lesions. The cystic appearance of this infarct indicates that it is a remote (healed) lesion.


Image 12


Brain, global hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy – Gross, coronal section

Another important pattern of hypoxic-ischemic central nervous system injury is that of global hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy, a pattern of injury that may develop after a period of total circulatory arrest. This is the brain from a patient who died approximately 5 days after a cardiac arrest. The patient was found down and was vigorously resuscitated en route to the hospital. Although a sinus rhythm was established, the patient remained comatose and ventilator-dependent throughout his hospital stay. Note the generalized swelling and irregular discoloration of gray matter, features indicative of irreversible, widespread brain injury caused by total circulatory arrest.


Image 13

Borderzone infarct

Brain, border-zone infarct – Gross, coronal section

In some cases, a global decrease in central nervous system perfusion pressure may cause damage limited to localized areas of the brain. Arterial border zones (i.e., the junctions between two vascular territories) are especially vulnerable to decreases in global perfusion pressure and may be the only site of permanent tissue injury after a hypotensive episode. Preexisting vascular disease (e.g., atherosclerosis) increases the likelihood of border-zone injury during systemic hypotension. The hemorrhagic lesion in the right cerebral hemisphere in this case lies at the boundary between the anterior and middle cerebral artery territories. The term watershed infarct is sometimes used, less appropriately, to describe such lesions.

Question 2
2
What is meant by the term selective vulnerability?
Show answer

The term selective vulnerability is used to refer to the increased sensitivity of local areas of the brain to injury following a global insult. The cortical injury pattern present in this case illustrates the increased vulnerability of arterial border zone regions to a global decrease in perfusion pressure. Other areas of special vulnerability to hypoxic ischemic injury include portions of the pyramidal cell layer of the hippocampus (especially the CA1 area) and the Purkinje cell layer of the cerebellum. Local excitotoxicity probably plays an important role in the development of neuronal injury in a number of these selectively vulnerable areas.

Cardiovascular Disease Case 4, Image 11 (brain, thalamic hemorrhage, gross)

Question 1
1
What is the most common predisposing factor to spontaneous brain parenchymal hemorrhage? What lesion is felt to be the source of such hemorrhages?
Show answer

Hypertension is the most common cause of spontaneous brain parenchymal hemorrhage. So-called “hypertensive hemorrhages” are believed to originate in tiny aneurysms, termed Charcot-Bouchard microaneurysms, usually located in small arterial channels in the area of the basal ganglia or thalamus. These Charcot-Bouchard microaneurysms are extremely difficult to show in postmortem tissue and should not be confused with saccular aneurysms, discussed later.


Image 15

Hematoma

Brain, primary pontine hemorrhage – Gross, horizontal section

In addition to the cerebral hemispheres, the pons is occasionally the site of a primary hemorrhage. Similar to their counterparts in the cerebral hemispheres, primary pontine hemorrhages appear as globular hematomas that displace and compress adjacent parenchyma.

Question 1
1
Compare the expected patterns in focal infarcts, border zone infarcts, and global hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy.
Show answer

Focal infarcts are located in the distribution of a specific arterial branch. Border zone (watershed) infarcts are located at the distal borders of major cerebral arteries and are usually found in the parasagittal frontoparietal region (the border of the regions supplied by the middle and anterior cerebral arteries) or, less commonly, in the temporo-occipital region (the border of the regions supplied by the middle and posterior cerebral arteries) or parieto-occipital region (the border of the regions supplied by the anterior and posterior cerebral arteries). Global hypoxic-ischemic encephalopathy appears acutely as necrotic neurons; with organization, there is segmental neuronal dropout with gliosis. Susceptible areas include the pyramidal layer of the hippocampus (particularly Sommer sector) and the Purkinje cells of the cerebellum. If hypoxia-ischemia is severe, there is also diffuse cortical pseudolaminar necrosis (necrosis of layers of cortical neurons).

Brain, arteriovenous malformation – Gross, coronal section

This image illustrates another important cause of spontaneous brain parenchyma hemorrhage, arteriovenous malformation (AVM). AVMs are the most dangerous of the various forms of intracranial vascular malformation by virtue of their tendency to undergo spontaneous, often catastrophic, hemorrhage. AVMs may also cause neurologic abnormalities (e.g., seizures) in the absence of hemorrhage.

Question 1
1
What is the cause of hemorrhage in a primary intraparenchymal hemorrhage?
Show answer

A primary intraparenchymal hemorrhage is due to rupture of a vessel. In the case of hypertensive hemorrhage, the most common type, the rupture is thought to be of a Charcot-Brouchard–type aneurysm that forms at small artery bifurcations. These aneurysms have been shown to increase in the brain with age and with the duration of hypertension.


Image 17


Brain, right cerebral hemisphere, subarachnoid hemorrhage – Gross, medial surface

In addition to the intraparenchymal hemorrhages discussed previously, spontaneous hemorrhage may also occur in the subarachnoid space. The most common cause of spontaneous (non-traumatic) subarachnoid hemorrhage is rupture of a saccular (berry) aneurysm.

Question 1
1
What would the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) contain in a case of subarachnoid hemorrhage?
Show answer

The CSF would contain blood in the case of subarachnoid hemorrhage.


Question 2
2
What is another important cause of subarachnoid hemorrhage?
Show answer

Trauma is another important cause of subarachnoid hemorrhage; it is a more common cause of subarachnoid hemorrhage than saccular aneurysms.

Brain, saccular aneurysm – Gross, ventral surface

The brainstem and cerebellum are distorted by a large saccular aneurysm, which lies near the junction of the left vertebral and basilar arteries. Saccular, or berry, aneurysms are most commonly encountered at branch points in the internal carotid, middle cerebral, or anterior cerebral arteries, but they may also involve the posterior circulation, as in this example. Although sometimes attributed to areas of congenital weakness at arterial branch points, saccular aneurysms are most commonly encountered in the middle adult years and are exceedingly rare in children.

Question 1
1
What are the most common locations for a saccular aneurysm?
Show answer

The most common locations are the junction of the anterior cerebral artery and anterior communicating artery (40%), the middle cerebral artery (34%), and the junction of the middle cerebral artery and posterior cerebral artery.




Question 2
2
In what diseases is there an increased risk of berry aneurysms?
Show answer

Autosomal dominant polycystic kidney disease, Ehler-Danos type IV, neurofibromatosis type I, and Marfan syndrome predispose to berry aneurysms.

Targeted Therapy Box: Polycystic kidney disease: A failure to communicate

Tumors

Polycystic Kidney Disease: A Failure to Communicate

Autosomal dominant adult polycystic kidney disease (ADPKD) affects 0.1% to 0.4% of the population and underlies 5% to 10% of all cases of end-stage renal disease. At present, the therapeutic options for patients with end-stage disease are the same as for patients with other kidney diseases: either chronic dialysis or transplantation.


ADPKD results from mutations in PKD1 and PKD2, genes that code for the polycystin-1 and -2 proteins, respectively.


  • Polycystin-1 and -2 are expressed by the epithelial cells of the distal nephron and the collecting ducts, as well as by bile duct epithelium and endothelial cells (possibly explaining the hepatic cysts and vascular aneurysms that also occur in ADPKD).
  • Polycystin-1 (PC-1) is part of the multi-subunit primary cilium, a nonmotile hairlike organelle that projects from the apical face of the epithelium and senses mechanical forces such as shear generated by fluid movements. When stimulated, the primary cilium triggers a series of events that increase cytoplasmic calcium.
  • Polycystin-2 (PC-2) is a calcium channel; it is another component of the primary cilium complex and also is found in the endoplasmic reticulum. PC-2 regulates the uptake of calcium from the extracellular milieu and from intracellular stores in the endoplasmic reticulum.

In general, the mutations responsible for ADPKD decrease PC-1 or PC-2 function, resulting in abnormally low intracellular calcium levels. This effect has several consequences, but among the most important is a higher level of intracellular cyclic AMP (cAMP), which stimulates epithelial proliferation and activates the cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator (CFTR), leading to increased fluid secretion. Tubular epithelium proliferation and increased fluid secretion underlies the formation of cysts, which eventually replace the normal parenchyma.


Fortunately, this pathogenic pathway also suggests therapeutic opportunities. Current attention is focused on modulating intracellular cAMP levels through blockade of the vasopressin pathway. Vasopressin (also known as antidiuretic hormone) binds to the V2 receptor and promotes water absorption in the collecting duct epithelium by stimulating adenyl cyclase—the enzyme that catalyzes the formation of cAMP. Selective V2 receptor antagonists such as tolvaptan decrease intracellular cAMP and reduce proliferation and fluid secretion, while avoiding complications related to antagonism of V1 receptors (which regulate heart rate and blood pressure). V2 antagonists are now in phase three clinical trials; it is hoped that these agents will slow or prevent the currently inexorable progression of ADPKD.
