Learning Objectives
  • 1. List the cell types present in the various parts of the tracheobronchial tree. Be familiar with various forms of cellular differentiation and their possible relationship to specific types of lung cancer.
  • 2. Describe the relationship between lung cancer and cigarette smoking and other environmental hazards (e.g., radon.).
  • 3. Describe the four major histologic forms of lung cancer and their biologic behavior patterns (relative to prognosis and therapy). Understand the importance of distinguishing non–small cell lung carcinoma and small cell lung carcinoma.
  • 4. Describe the usual histologic sequence of the development of lung cancers. Appreciate that lung cancers arise through a series of molecular events and that some of these may be of diagnostic and prognostic importance.
  • 5. Describe the common location, typical histology, clinical manifestations, and cell of origin of bronchial carcinoid. Know whether its etiology is related to smoking.
  • 6. Know that the lung and pleura are common sites of metastatic spread of tumors arising elsewhere. Know which tumors frequently metastasize to the lungs.
  • 7. List the important causes of serous and inflammatory effusions in the pleural space.
  • 8. Describe the major primary tumor of the pleura and the environmental agent associated with it. Understand the role of smoking in asbestos-induced lung cancer and mesothelioma.
  • 9. List the major diseases associated with tobacco use and abuse.
  • 10. List the major cancer types associated with tobacco use.
  • 11. Understand the nature of the carcinogens and other harmful products in tobacco smoke and the mechanism by which they promote carcinogenesis.

Scenario

A 64-year-old male factory worker presented with a history of chest pain and hemoptysis. He had lost over 7 pounds in the last 4 weeks and complained of feeling "run down and having no energy." He had smoked heavily since adolescence. The only relevant past history was a squamous cell carcinoma of the larynx 3 years previously, which had been treated with surgery and radiation. Physical examination revealed a cachectic, nervous man with swollen fingertips and decreased breath sounds on the right side of the chest. An x-ray showed a large, right upper lobe mass with central cavitation. CT scans indicated that the mediastinal nodes were enlarged. Sputum cytology revealed a moderate number of metaplastic and dysplastic squamous cells and rare malignant cells. Laryngoscopy indicated no evidence of recurrence of his original tumor. Bronchoscopy revealed several thickened white areas, from which biopsy specimens were obtained, and a large, polypoid mass obstructing the right upper lobe bronchus. Mediastinoscopy with biopsy of an enlarged node was performed. He received radiation therapy to the chest, but his condition deteriorated, and he died 4 months later. An autopsy was performed.

Question 1
1
What signs and symptoms would result from brachial plexus involvement?
Show answer

Pancoast tumor may be associated with pain in the region of the scapula, ribs, and vertebrae and weakness of the upper extremity with wasting of the muscles of the hand. Pain in the distribution of the ulnar nerve may be prominent. Involvement of the local segment of the sympathetic chain results in a characteristic set of signs and symptoms known as Horner syndrome (enophthalmos, ptosis, miosis, and anhidrosis).

Question 1
1
What type of cell is this, and what causes its staining properties?
Show answer

Cells with these characteristics are squamous cells. The presence of high–molecular-weight keratins in terminally differentiated squamous cells causes the characteristic orange-red appearance of the cytoplasm. Specifically, the cytologic features support that this is a malignant squamous cell – squamous cell carcinoma.

Another characteristic finding on cytologic examination is the presence of epithelial "pearls." These consist of highly keratinized concentric whorls of cells. As the pearls become larger, the cells in the center undergo necrosis.

Question 1
1
Epithelial pearls are characteristic of what tumor type?
Show answer

They are characteristic of squamous cell carcinoma.

Question 1
1
Can peripherally arising adenocarcinomas be diagnosed by bronchoscopy?
Show answer

Yes. In some cases, tumor may spread in the sub-bronchial lymphatics and be detected by bronchial biopsy, exfoliated cells may be detected in bronchial or bronchioloalveolar lavage fluids, or a transbronchial biopsy specimen of the tumor may yield a histologic diagnosis.


Question 2
2
What are the major causes of cavitary lesions in the lung?
Show answer

Pulmonary tuberculosis is the most common cause. A malignancy with cavitation, especially squamous cell carcinoma, is the next most common. Other causes are uncommon and include lung abscesses and Hodgkin lymphoma.

Question 2
2
Can metastatic squamous cell carcinoma from a distant site metastasize to the lungs and mimic primary squamous cell carcinoma of the lung?
Show answer

Yes. The distinction can be difficult histologically. Evidence for a metastatic lesion includes the presence of multiple nodules, a well-circumscribed appearance, and existinence primarily within lymphovascular spaces. Clinical and radiologic correlation is helpful. A HPV-related metastastic squamous cell carcinoma from the cervix or head and neck may be distinguished using tumor HPV subtyping.

Question 1
1
Why are heavy smokers at risk for the development of multiple tumors of the upper aerodigestive tract?
Show answer

These lesions result from the so-called “field effect”; the entire upper aerodigestive tract has been damaged by exposure to tobacco-related carcinogens, develops multiple preneoplastic lesions, and is at increased risk for the development of one or more cancers.

Question 1
1
What other preneoplastic lesions occur in the respiratory epithelium?
Show answer

The recognized preneoplastic lesions leading to squamous cell carcinoma include high-grade dysplasia and carcinoma in situ. A precursor lesion of peripheral adenocarcinoma is atypical adenomatous hyperplasia (AAH), although not all foci of AAH will progress to an invasive carcinoma.


Question 2
2
Are these lesions limited to the epithelium of large bronchi?
Show answer

Preneoplastic squamous lesions are mainly present in large and medium-sized bronchi. Occasionally, they may be present in small bronchi and bronchioles.


Question 3
3
What is their significance?
Show answer

The preneoplastic lesions dysplasia and carcinoma in situ are regarded as evidence of smoking-related damage, because they are rarely present in the lungs of lifetime nonsmokers.

Is lepidic growth diagnostic of primary adenocarcinomas of the lung?
Show answer

Lepidic growth is characteristically seen at the edges of primary lung adenocarcinomas arising peripherally, but it may be seen with other tumor types, including metastatic tumors.


Question 2
2
What is the difference between a lepidic adenocarcinoma and adenocarcinoma in situ?
Show answer

The lepidic pattern is a noninvasive pattern seen in both lepidic adenocarcinoma and adenocarcinoma in situ. According to the 2011 classification of lung tumors, the distinction is made by the size of the tumor. Tumors measuring 3 cm or less with a pure lepidic pattern and no evidence of invasion are classified as adenocarcinoma in situ. These tumors have a nearly 100% disease-free survival after resection. Tumors larger than 3 cm with a pure lepidic pattern and no evidence of invasion are classified as adenocarcinoma, lepidic pattern. The term bronchoalveolar carcinoma is no longer used.

Question 1
1
Describe the molecular genetics of lung adenocarcinomas.
Show answer

Subclassification of non–small cell lung carcinoma into adenocarcinoma and squamous cell carcinoma has become extremely important because new targeted therapeutics can be given to patients with lung adenocarcinomas harboring specific mutations and prolong patient survival. In general, lung adenocarcinomas harbor mutations that lead to oncogenic gain-of-function alterations in growth factor receptor signaling pathways. These include activating mutations in the EGFR receptor (which are targetable with EGFR inhibitors such as erlotinib) and EML4-ALK translocations (which are targetable with the tyrosine kinase inhibitor crizotinib). Other mutations include KRAS (most common but not currently targetable), ROS, MET, and RET.

Lesions of the Upper Respiratory Tract

Non–Small Cell Lung Cancer: Pathologic Details Can Beat the Devil

Non–small cell lung cancers (NSCLCs) constitute a group of epithelial malignancies that are pathologically and clinically distinct from the neuroendocrine-derived small cell lung carcinomas. Although histologically disparate from one another—they include squamous cell carcinomas, adenocarcinomas (comprising 50% of NSCLCs), and poorly differentiated large cell carcinomas—these tumors all share the depressing characteristic of responding poorly to standard cytotoxic chemotherapy. Indeed, failure to control NSCLC by surgical resection is associated with a miserable prognosis. The 5-year survival rate with even limited (less than 3 cm), localized stage I disease is only 50%; in the presence of distant metastases, less than 1% of patients survive to 5 years.

The picture is brightening, however, with the recognition that subsets of NSCLCs carry specific oncogenic mutations in potentially targetable molecules.

  • Roughly 10% of adenocarcinomas in non-Asian persons (and roughly 50% of adenocarcinomas in Asian populations!) express constitutively activating mutations in epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR). EGFR is upstream of the mitogen-activated protein (MAP) kinase, Akt, and Janus kinase pathways, and activation leads to augmented cell proliferation. Of interest, in whites, these activating mutations tend to occur in adenocarcinomas arising in female patients who have never smoked; thus, the epidemiology strongly suggests a pathogenesis for such tumors distinct from that for other, smoking-related lung cancers. Moreover, EGFR mutant status strongly predicts improved progression-free survival with the administration of receptor tyrosine kinase inhibitors such as gefitinib or erlotinib.
  • Another, nonoverlapping subset of adenocarcinomas (roughly 5%) have acquired a mutant fusion protein (EML-ALK) that combines portions of the echinoderm microtubule-associated protein-like 4 (EML4) and anaplastic lymphoma kinase (ALK) molecules. The resultant chimeric protein is a constitutively activated kinase that drives cellular proliferation along MAP kinase pathways. Of importance, the kinase inhibitor crizotinib significantly improves patient survival when tumors contain the EML-ALK fusion protein.

Consequently, a better appreciation for the molecular underpinnings of NSCLC potentially allows exquisitely targeted therapy. To take advantage of such Achilles’ heels, however, current state-of-the-art pathologic diagnosis mandates careful ancillary evaluation in any new case. If the pathologist can identify mutant EGFR (by polymerase chain reaction techniques), increased EGFR expression (by immunohistochemical analysis), or EML-ALK fusion rearrangement (by fluorescence in situ hybridization [FISH]), the oncologist has some improved and more selective weapons to bring to bear.

Question 1
1
Can brain metastases be clinically silent?
Show answer

Yes. Depending on location and number, they frequently may be clinically silent until they reach a large size.


Image 18
Elevated nail beds

Fingers, clubbing - Clinical presentation

Clubbing of the fingers (also known as hypertrophic pulmonary osteoarthropathy [HPO]) is associated with some pulmonary carcinomas, especially adenocarcinomas. It is characterized by painful periosteal elevation at several bony and joint sites, especially the fingers. Elevation of the nail bed (shown here) to form a pronounced curvature is characteristic. HPO is reversible, and frequently it resolves spontaneously soon after complete surgical resection of the tumor.

Question 1
1
Are other diseases associated with HPO?
Show answer

Yes. Various chronic pulmonary diseases and some congenital heart diseases, especially diseases with increased pulmonary blood flow, may be associated with HPO.


Image 19
Giant cell
Focal necrosis

Lung, large cell carcinoma - High power

Large cell carcinomas are poorly differentiated or undifferentiated pulmonary carcinomas. In some tumors, giant cells are prominent. Rapidly growing tumors, such as this one, may have focal areas of necrosis. They must be distinguished from small cell carcinomas, which usually lack evidence of differentiation at the light microscopic level but which have markers of neuroendocrine differentiation. The diagnosis of large cell carcinoma is essentially a diagnosis of exclusion.

Is there a specific cell type that gives rise to large cell carcinomas?

No. Although large cell carcinomas show no evidence of differentiation by routine light microscopy, special studies, including mucin stains and electron microscopy, may reveal that they are very poorly differentiated examples of other types of lung carcinomas.

Lung, mesotheliomas - Low power

Mesothelial cells are biphasic and may resemble epithelial or fibroblast cells. The malignant tumors (mesotheliomas) that arise from mesothelial cells may mimic these biphasic appearances. Mesotheliomas may be epithelioid (left) or sarcomatoid (right). The appearance of epithelioid mesotheliomas may closely resemble that of adenocarcinoma of the lung.

Question 1
1
How can epithelioid mesotheliomas be distinguished from adenocarcinoma?
Show answer

A panel of immunohistochemical stains can be used to differentiate epithelioid mesotheliomas from adenocarcinomas. Stains positive in mesothelioma include WT-1, D240, and calretinin, and stains positive in adenocarcinoma include MOC-31, CEA, and BerEp4. Additionally, evidence of mucin formation would be diagnostic of adenocarcinoma. Although not used often clinically, electron microscopic evidence of long, branching microvilli is characteristic of mesotheliomas.
