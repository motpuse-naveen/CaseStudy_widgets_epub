Learning Objectives

  • 1. Understand the five categories of diarrheal diseases, and list the major causes in each category.
  • 2. Know the major malabsorption syndromes and the many organ systems affected by the consequences of malabsorption responsible for the various pathologic effects:
    • a. alimentary tract
    • b. hematopoietic system
    • c. musculoskeletal system
    • d. endocrine system
    • e. epidermis
    • f. nervous system
  • 3. Know the following aspects of celiac sprue:
    • a. definition and prevalence in various ethnic groups
    • b. pathogenesis
    • c. clinical features
    • d. pathology (gross and microscopic features)
    • e. complications (T-cell lymphoma and gastrointestinal tract carcinoma)
  • 4. Know the characteristic clinical and microscopic features associated with:
    • a. giardiasis
    • b. Whipple disease
    • c. tropical sprue

Scenario

A 44-year-old white man presented with a 7-month history of increasing diarrhea. The frequency of his bowel movements had increased to five to seven per day, and his stools were yellow and malodorous and floated at the top of the water in the toilet. He had occasional abdominal cramping, but no tenesmus, melena, or bleeding. His appetite was good, but he had experienced gradual weight loss. His bowel movement frequency would decrease on fasting and would increase with food intake. A family history of a brother with chronic diarrhea was elicited.

Stool tests revealed a stool output of 4128 g/day (normal 100-200 g/day) with fat excretion of 17 g/day (normal <5 g/day). Microscopic examination for ova and parasites and cultures for bacterial pathogens and acid-fast bacilli were negative. Blood testing showed mild anemia (hematocrit 36), hypoproteinemia (4.9 mg/dL), and hypoalbuminemia (3.4 mg/dL). Colonoscopy showed normal mucosa. Esophagogastroduodenoscopy showed flat-appearing mucosa in the duodenum. Biopsy specimens were obtained. Serum tests for IgA antiendomysial and anti-IgA tissue transglutaminase (IgA-tTG) antibodies were positive. The patient was discharged with appropriate dietary instructions. His diarrhea resolved, and his weight began to increase.

Bacilli, cross section

Small bowel, Whipple disease - Low power

Numerous macrophages are seen throughout the lamina propria. With the routine hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) stain, the cytoplasm of these cells has a pale, foamy appearance. A periodic acid-Schiff (PAS) stain highlights the cytoplasmic inclusions, staining them a deep red. An electron micrograph reveals that these inclusions are rod-shaped bacilli. The large, round, empty spaces in the lamina propria are dilated lymphatic channels owing to lymphatic obstruction by macrophages.

Question 1
1
What is the clinical triad seen in Whipple disease?
Show answer

The triad is diarrhea, weight loss, and arthralgias.
