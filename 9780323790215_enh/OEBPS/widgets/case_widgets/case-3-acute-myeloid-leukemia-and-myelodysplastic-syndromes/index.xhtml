Scenario

A 40-year-old man presented to the emergency department with a complaint of large amounts of bright red blood in his stool. On questioning, he reported the gradual onset of fatigue over the last several weeks and the appearance of numerous small red dots on his skin the day before. Physical examination revealed a well-developed man in no acute distress. The only significant physical findings were tachycardia (heart rate 104 beats/min), tachypnea (respiratory rate 27/min), orthostatic hypotension, pallor of skin and mucous membranes, and numerous petechiae. Laboratory values were:

Hb 6.5 g/dL
Hct 17.9%
WBC 2,200/mm3
Plt 4,000/mm3
PT 14.6 sec
PTT 42.4 sec
Fibrinogen 118 mg/dL (ref. range 190 - 400)
D-dimer 7.8 µg/mL (nl < 0.5)

An examination of a peripheral blood smear revealed 11% abnormal immature cells, and a bone marrow aspirate and biopsy were subsequently performed.

The low-power view of the bone marrow core biopsy specimen reveals a markedly hypercellular bone marrow (normally, approximately half of the marrow space should be occupied by fat cells). The high-power views of the aspirate smear (features) show a uniform population of immature abnormal cells. These are large cells with abundant purple cytoplasmic granules and sometimes bilobed nuclei. Some of the cells contain multiple Auer rods. Cytogenetic analysis of the marrow revealed t(15;17).

Question 1
1
What is the diagnosis in this case?
Show answer

The diagnosis is acute promyelocytic leukemia.


Question 2
2
What associated syndrome is indicated by the clinical presentation and laboratory data, and how does it relate to the patient’s primary diagnosis?
Show answer

The elevated PT, PTT, and D-dimers; decreased fibrinogen and platelet count; and clinical evidence of bleeding all indicate the presence of disseminated intravascular coagulation (DIC). Acute promyelocytic leukemia is almost always associated with some degree of DIC at presentation, and this complication is responsible for much of the early morbidity and mortality in this disease. The coagulopathy apparently results from the release of procoagulant substances by the tumor cells. DIC may be seen in other forms of AML but is uncommon in them.


Question 3
3
What is the molecular abnormality associated with t(15;17), and how does it relate to disease pathogenesis and treatment?
Show answer

t(15;17) results in rearrangement of the retinoic acid receptor alpha gene on chromosome 17 with the PML gene on chromosome 15. Retinoic acid is important for granulocyte maturation, and the rearrangement disrupts that pathway. The neoplastic cells are arrested at the promyelocyte stage of differentiation. Treatment with pharmacologic doses of all-trans-retinoic acid (ATRA) can overcome this maturational block and cause the neoplastic cells to mature. This treatment also ameliorates DIC. Although remission may be obtained with ATRA, treatment with conventional chemotherapy is necessary as well to prevent certain relapse.

The most important feature of AML, which allows its distinction from other disorders, is that it consists of a proliferation of immature cells, usually myeloblasts. The term myeloid encompasses granulocytic, erythroid, megakaryocytic, and monocytic cells, however, and AML can be composed of immature cells of any of these myeloid lineages (e.g., monoblasts, megakaryoblasts). Also, some AMLs retain the capacity for limited differentiation. The most important differential diagnosis with AML is ALL. In general, they may be easily distinguished on the basis of morphologic, cytochemical, and immunophenotypic features. The image illustrates the cytologic differences between myeloblasts and lymphoblasts in Wright-stained smears. Compared to lymphoblasts (right), myeloblasts (left) tend to have more cytoplasm, finer chromatin, and more prominent nucleoli. In addition, they often contain cytoplasmic granules and/or Auer rods.

Question 1
1
What is the primary criterion for a diagnosis of acute leukemia?
Show answer

The primary criterion for a diagnosis of acute leukemia is 20% blasts in blood or bone marrow. The proliferating cells of acute promyelocytic leukemia, although not strictly blasts, are considered as such for the purpose of diagnosis.


Question 2
2
What are Auer rods, and what is their significance?
Show answer

Auer rods may be thought of as fused neutrophil primary granules. They are always abnormal and, except for occasional cases of myelodysplastic syndromes, are essentially pathognomonic of AML. Many cases of AML do not contain Auer rods, however.


Question 3
3
Why is it necessary to distinguish between AML and ALL?
Show answer

The therapy employed is very different for these two diseases.


Image 3

Blood smear, acute leukemia, myeloperoxidase stain - High power; flow cytometric results

In some cases, cytology by itself does not permit the differentiation of acute myeloid leukemia (AML) from acute lymphoblastic anemia (ALL), and additional special studies may be necessary. One analysis involves testing for the presence of two key enzymes: myeloperoxidase and terminal deoxynucleotidyl transferase (TdT). Myeloperoxidase is an enzyme present in the primary granules of granulocyte precursors. A positive myeloperoxidase stain in blasts (left) is diagnostic of AML. TdT is a nuclear enzyme that plays a key role in the acquisition of immunologic diversity in lymphocytes. TdT expression, usually assessed by flow cytometry (right), in conjunction with absence of myeloperoxidase staining, suggests ALL. Another special study that may aid in the differentiation of ALL from AML is the analysis of cluster designation (CD) antigens. The presence of blasts that express CD10, CD19, CD20, and CD22 but not surface immunoglobulin would support a diagnosis of precursor B ALL.

World Health Organization (WHO) classification of acute myeloid leukemia (AML)

In contrast to the previous French-American-British (FAB) grading system that classified AML by morphology alone, the WHO classification attempts to divide AML into biologically relevant categories. These categories are: (1) AML with genetic aberrations; (2) AML with myelodysplastic (MDS)-like features; (3) therapy-related AML; and (4) AML not otherwise specified. The most important level of classification is whether the AML possesses a recurrent cytogenetic abnormality, as shown in the table. As illustrated in the case of acute promyelocytic leukemia, these AMLs with recurrent cytogenetic abnormalities often have distinctive biologic, prognostic/therapeutic, and (in some cases) even histomorphologic features. The second category of AML is associated with multilineage dysplasia. These AMLs are thought to be related to myelodysplastic syndromes (see Image 7) and carry a poor prognosis. Another important and distinct category includes AMLs that arise as a consequence of prior chemotherapy/radiation. These possess characteristic cytogenetic abnormalities and usually have a very poor prognosis. Finally, the remainder of cases are not otherwise categorized and are subject to morphologic classification similar to that previously used in the FAB classification (see Images 5 and 6). The 2016 WHO classification adds even more categories of AML with genetic aberrations. As more is learned about the molecular genetics underlying the subtypes of this disease, the hope is to be able to better stratify prognoses and individualize therapy.

Those cases of AML that are not otherwise classified in the World Health Organization classification are assigned a morphologic classification that employs similar criteria as the FAB classification. The details of this morphologic classification are not important, but the basic principles of this morphologic classification are worth mentioning. The two main features used in classifying AML not otherwise classified are the degree of differentiation of the neoplastic cells (Image 5) and the myeloid lineages represented (Image 6). This image illustrates examples of AML classified according to the degree of maturation along the granulocytic lineage:

Minimally differentiated AML: In this disorder the myeloblasts show almost no overt evidence of maturation, even lacking myeloperoxidase expression. Flow cytometry is necessary to demonstrate that these are myeloid rather than lymphoid blasts.

AML without maturation: This type of AML is composed of myeloblasts that show clear myeloid differentiation (e.g., cytoplasmic granules, Auer rods, myeloperoxidase expression) but little or no maturation to more mature neutrophilic forms.

AML with lineage-specific maturation: This particular case shows monocytic maturation.

AMLs may differentiate along any of the myeloid lineages, including granulocytic, monocytic, erythroid, and megakaryocytic, and this differentiation forms the basis for classifying the remainder of cases of AML. Thus, AML with both granulocytic and monocytic components is known as acute myelomonocytic leukemia; AML with exclusively monocytic differentiation is known as acute monocytic leukemia; AML with erythroid differentiation is known as acute erythroid leukemia; and AML with predominantly megakaryocytic differentiation is known as acute megakaryoblastic leukemia.

Blood and bone marrow smears, myelodysplastic syndromes (MDSs) - High power

MDSs are a diverse group of clonal myeloid disorders characterized by two main features: (1) maturation defects leading to ineffective hematopoiesis (cytopenias in the face of marrow hyperplasia) of one or more myeloid cell lines, and (2) morphologic dysplasia resulting from abnormal cellular maturation. Many cases also have increased myeloblasts in the bone marrow. The range of dysplastic features that one sees is broad, and detailed discussion is beyond the scope of this case study, but several common findings are illustrated in the image. The top left image is a neutrophil with two distinct round, abnormally condensed nuclear lobes (so-called pseudo Pelger-Huët) and cytoplasm that is virtually devoid of granules. The platelet in the upper right is giant (much larger than the red cells) and is totally agranular. The megakaryocyte in the lower left is abnormally small and has multiple distinct round nuclei rather than multiple connected lobes. Finally, the large erythroid precursor in the lower right has an abnormally lobulated nucleus.

Question 1
1
How are myelodysplastic syndromes distinguished from myeloproliferative disorders?
Show answer

While both types of disorders characteristically have hypercellular bone marrows, the hematopoiesis of myeloproliferative disorders is effective, producing increased peripheral counts (at least initially), whereas that of myelodysplastic syndromes is ineffective, producing blood cytopenias.


Question 2
2
What is the natural history of MDS, and what are some prognostic factors?
Show answer

MDS may be primary or related to toxic drug or radiation therapy (t-MDS). The clinical course of MDS varies widely, from gradually worsening cytopenias to rapidly progressive marrow failure to transformation to AML. The prognosis, as well as the rate of transformation to AML, depends on multiple factors, including the bone marrow blast percentage, chromosomal abnormalities (monosomies 5 and 7, del 5q, 7q, 20q, and trisomy 8), and severity of cytopenias. Because of the propensity for MDS to transform to AML, these disorders have in the past been called pre-leukemias. AML arising from MDS confers a poor prognosis.
