Endocarditis and Septic Shock

Learning Objectives

  • Shock
    • 1. Define shock, know the three general categories of shock, and understand the general pathophysiology of shock.
    • 2. Know the pathophysiology of septic shock.
    • 3. Know the three stages of shock and the morphologic changes within the organs of the body.
  • Hemostasis
    • 1. Understand the consequences of hemorrhage (based upon location and amount).
    • 2. Define normal hemostasis and thrombosis, and understand the differences between them.
    • 3. Know the general sequence of events in hemostasis.
    • 4. Know the three antithrombotic properties and three prothrombotic properties of endothelium, and list their main mediators.
    • 5. Know the three general reactions platelets undergo and list their main mediators.
    • 6. Review the coagulation cascade.
    • 7. Know the three main anticoagulants, and understand the role of plasmin.
  • Thrombogenesis
    • 1. Know the Virchow triad (factors predisposing to thrombus formation), and understand each factor's role in the formation of thrombi.
    • 2. Know the common primary and high-risk factors for thrombosis secondary to hypercoagulable states, and understand the mutation in factor V Leiden.
    • 3. Know the morphology of thrombi (arterial and venous), the distinguishing features between arterial and venous thrombi, and the possible fates of thrombi.
  • Infarction
    • 1. Define infarct.
    • 2. Know the morphology of an infarct (red versus white, septic versus bland), understand the progression of an infarct, and understand the consequences of an infarct (depending upon the location).
    • 3. Know the four major determinants that influence the development of an infarct, and understand the pathophysiology behind each.
  • Disseminated Intravascular Coagulation
    • 1. Define disseminated intravascular coagulation, and understand that it is a secondary process.

Scenario

A 25-year-old IV drug abuser presents with high fever, chills, and rigors. On examination, he is found to have a murmur in the tricuspid valve area. His face and extremities look flushed, and his blood pressure is 90/60 mm Hg. Laboratory evaluation reveals an elevated leukocyte count with polymorphonuclear leukocytosis. Blood cultures grew gram-negative bacteria. He is treated with IV antibiotics. His blood pressure continues to decrease, and urinary output declines to 100 mL/24 hr. He develops a bleeding diathesis from venipuncture sites and mucosal surfaces. He dies 3 days later.

Image 1

Vegetations
Opened right atrium

Heart, view of the tricuspid valve from the right atrial side - Gross

The cusps of the valve are obscured by the presence of large irregular and friable masses of thrombi, also called vegetations. The vegetations, or thrombi, form as a result of underlying damage to the valvular endocardium (endocarditis). Tricuspid endocarditis is typically caused by organisms (bacteria, fungi) that gain access to the venous circulation. The microorganisms reach the right atrium and lodge on the tricuspid valve, causing inflammation (endocardial injury), providing an altered endocardial surface on which thrombosis occurs. Such thrombi, or vegetations, are infected.

Question 1
1
Which patients typically develop right-sided endocarditis?

Right-sided endocarditis is most commonly seen in IV drug abusers via use of nonsterile needles


Question 2
2
What might be the consequences of infective endocarditis with formation of vegetations on the right side of the heart?

Embolism to the lung, causing infarction, may occur. Because the vegetations are infected, the necrotic areas can be infected, leading to formation of abscesses. Spread to the bloodstream may cause septicemia.

Question 1
1
Why did this patient develop shock?

This patient had infective endocarditis with gram-negative septicemia. The shock resulted from release from the bacteria of endotoxins that triggered the release of inflammatory mediators such as IL-1 and tumor necrosis factor (TNF).


Question 2
2
How did this patient develop shock?

TNF plays an important role in the development of septic shock by promoting the release of IL-1, IL-6, IL-8, and nitric oxide, initiating a cytokine cascade. These mediators, in low to moderate quantities, may lead to local inflammation and systemic effects, such as fever. When present in higher quantities, they promote the development of shock by causing systemic vasodilation, impaired myocardial contractility, and widespread endothelial injury, which may lead to DIC.

Question 1
1
What are the major morphologic changes in multiple organ failure in a patient who dies of shock?

Morphologic changes are as follows: Kidneys: acute tubular necrosis; Brain: laminar cortical necrosis; Lungs: shock lung (diffuse alveolar damage) with hyaline membranes (seen mainly in septic shock); Heart: foci of necrosis, hemorrhage, contraction band necrosis; Gastrointestinal system: hemorrhages; Liver: central hemorrhagic necrosis, fatty change.


Question 2
2
What is the pathophysiology of septic shock?

A distributive effect occurs. Systemic vasodilation leads to peripheral pooling of blood and consequent reduction in visceral blood flow.

Normal cortex
Cortex with laminar necrosis

Brain, cortical laminar necrosis - Gross, coronal section

Compare the thinned and discolored cerebral cortex on the left side with the relatively preserved cortex on the right side. Neurons in different areas of the brain have different susceptibilities to global ischemic damage, such as may occur in shock. Most patients with shock who are diagnosed and treated early have no or only mild ischemic damage to the brain, which may result only in a confusional state. More severe, prolonged, and irreversible shock can lead to laminar cortical necrosis.

Question 1
1
How does this picture differ from a thrombotic/embolic infarct?

A thrombotic/embolic infarct in the brain is a localized lesion present in the area supplied by the occluded vessel. In contrast, laminar cortical necrosis is due to global hypoxic change, as in shock, and affects a large part of the cortical ribbon.

This mitral valve is severely damaged and distorted as a result of previous rheumatic heart disease. The valve is markedly thickened because of fibrosis. Because of damage to the endocardial layer, a large thrombus (vegetation) has formed and is attached to the valve surface. It contains fibrin in which bacteria are enmeshed. This is called infective endocarditis. Bacterial endocarditis also affects the adjacent atrial and ventricular surfaces.

Question 1
1
What complications may arise from this lesion?

Infected vegetations on the mitral valve may embolize systemically and cause infarcts, abscesses, or septicemia. In addition, the mitral valve, chordae tendineae, or papillary muscles may rupture, causing ventricular failure.

Question 1
1
How is fibrin formed? What are the categories of coagulant proteins?

Fibrin is derived from its precursor, fibrinogen, a proenzyme. This is one of three types of coagulant proteins. The other two are enzymes (e.g., active coagulation factors) and cofactors (e.g., factors V and VIII, tissue factor). The latter are also called reaction accelerators.


Question 2
2
What are the components of a vegetation? What are the predisposing conditions for their formation?

A vegetation is an intracardiac thrombus. They often form on the valves. They form when the valves are hemodynamically abnormal or there is injury to the endocardium. Vegetations may occur secondary to diseases such as rheumatic fever, lupus, or congenital heart diseases. Hypercoagulable states sometimes associated with chronic illnesses can also predispose to cardiac vegetations.

Figure 12-24 Comparison of the four major forms of vegetative endocarditis. The rheumatic fever phase of rheumatic heart disease (RHD) is marked by small, warty vegetations along the lines of closure of the valve leafl ets. Infective endocarditis (IE) is characterized by large, irregular masses on the valve cusps that can extend onto the chordae (see Fig. 12-25 A ). Nonbacterial thrombotic endocarditis (NBTE) typically exhibits small, bland vegetations, usually attached at the line of closure. One or many may be present (see Figure 12-26 ). Libman-Sacks endocarditis (LSE) has small or medium-sized vegetations on either or both sides of the valve leaflets.

Could this patient develop a cerebral infarct? What would be the appearance of such an infarct of the brain?

Heart, mitral valve, nonbacterial thrombotic endocarditis - Gross

In some chronically ill patients (e.g., patients with cancer), there is hypercoagulability of the blood. In such patients, fibrin deposition and consequent vegetation formation occur on normal valves. These vegetations do not contain bacteria or other microorganisms, and they do not destroy the valve. The valve retains its normal shiny, smooth surface and is not distorted. The risk of systemic embolism and its consequences remains, however.

Question 1
1
Compare this picture with that of infective endocarditis of the mitral valve.

Infective endocarditis occurs on previously damaged valves (e.g., in rheumatic heart disease), or it may occur on normal valves when the organisms are very virulent and can cause valve destruction. The valves are either previously damaged by disease or are damaged by the endocarditis itself. By contrast, in nonbacterial thrombotic endocarditis, the valve appears to be undamaged.

Question 1
1
What are the most common causes of DIC?

Common causes include complications of pregnancy, widespread carcinomatosis, sepsis, and major trauma.


Question 2
2
What is the spectrum of the clinical course of DIC?

The clinical presentation and course of DIC vary depending on the cause and the extent of individual organs involved. Acute DIC, dominated by a bleeding diathesis, is associated with obstetric complications, septic shock, or major trauma. Chronic DIC, dominated by thrombotic complications, is associated with carcinomatosis.


Question 3
3
What are the common morphologic changes seen in DIC?

Morphologic changes include microthrombi in many organs, such as brain, heart, lungs, and kidney; microinfarcts in kidney and brain; hyaline membranes in lungs; adrenal hemorrhage in meningococcemia (Waterhouse-Friderichsen syndrome); necrosis of the pituitary gland (Sheehan syndrome); and fragmented red blood cells and thrombocytopenia on the blood smear.


Image 12


Figure 14-27 Pathogenesis of disseminated intravascular coagulation - Diagram

As described in the diagram, DIC is characterized by activation of the coagulation sequence, leading to the formation of microthrombi throughout the microcirculation of the body. The signs and symptoms depend on the etiology and the extent to which individual organs are affected. In addition to tissue hypoxia and infarction caused by microthrombi, there is consumption of platelets, fibrin, and coagulation factors along with activation of the fibrinolytic system, leading to a bleeding diathesis, as has occurred in this patient.

Question 1
1
What are the two major triggers of DIC?

The two major triggers of DIC are release of tissue factor and endothelial injury.

Pathophysiology of disseminated intravascular coagulation.


Question 2
2
What are some common sources of tissue factor that can trigger DIC?

Common sources include major trauma to tissues, the placenta in obstetric complications, mucus released by adenocarcinomas, granules of leukemic cells in acute promyelocytic leukemia, bacterial endotoxins that stimulate release of tissue factor from monocytes, and IL-1 and tumor necrosis factor (TNF) that stimulate expression of tissue factor on endothelial cell surfaces.


Question 3
3
What are some common triggers of endothelial injury in DIC?

Microorganisms (e.g., meningococci, rickettsiae), temperature extremes (e.g., heatstroke, burns), and antigen-antibody complexes (e.g., systemic lupus erythematosus) are common triggers of endothelial injury.


Question 4
4
What is the mechanism of DIC in sepsis?

Bacterial endotoxins promote increased synthesis and release of tissue factor from monocytes. Activated monocytes also release IL-1 and TNF, which increase the expression of tissue factor on endothelial cells while decreasing the expression of thrombomodulin on endothelial cells. Increased tissue factor activates the clotting system. Reduced thrombomodulin levels decrease protein C activation. There is simultaneous activation of the clotting system and inhibition of coagulation control, leading to the formation of widespread microthrombi.


Question 5
5
What is the pathophysiology of bleeding in DIC?

Bleeding in DIC occurs as a result of the consumption of coagulation factors during the formation of widespread microthrombi. Widespread activation of the fibrinolytic system aggravates bleeding further. Plasmin cleaves not only fibrin but also factors V and VIII. In addition, fibrinolysis leads to the formation of fibrin degradation products, which inhibit thrombin, platelet aggregation, and fibrin polymerization.
