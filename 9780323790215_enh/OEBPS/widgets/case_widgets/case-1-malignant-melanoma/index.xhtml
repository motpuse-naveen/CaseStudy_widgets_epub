Scenario

A 51-year-old man sees his physician because of growth and change in color of an abdominal mole that, according to his statement, has been present since his birth. The mole has always had an oval shape with even, brown pigmentation. Recently, the patient noticed changes at the edge of the mole. The shape has become irregular, and some areas show dark discoloration. He denies pain or itching. This is the first time that he has seen a physician for this type of complaint. He has no family history of malignant melanoma.

Question 1
1
What are the “ABCs” of melanoma?
Show answer

The “ABCs” refer to physical examination findings that raise suspicion for melanoma rather than a benign nevus: A – asymmetry; B – irregular borders; C – variegated color; D – increasing diameter; E – evolution or change over time


Question 2
2
Which nonmelanocytic disorders of the skin can manifest as pigmented lesions?
Show answer

Seborrheic keratoses are common pigmented lesions, occurring most frequently in middle-aged and older individuals. Basal cell carcinomas usually manifest as a pearly papule or nodule with dilated blood vessels (telangiectasias), but they can also occur as an ulcerated, eczematous, or pigmented lesion. Vascular tumors (hemangioma, Kaposi sarcoma) often have a blue-brown color.

Question 1
1
What is the relationship between this thickness and the prognosis?
Show answer

The thickness of a melanoma, measured histologically between the granular layer of the epidermis and the deepest melanoma cell, is a very important factor that determines the prognosis of a melanoma. This thickness is called the Breslow thickness.

Question 1
1
Describe the differences between normal skin and this patient's skin.
Show answer

Normal skin contains pink collagen bundles. This slide shows severely sun-damaged skin with thickened, blue-gray elastic fibers. This change is called solar elastosis and is probably the result of abnormal dermal elastic fiber synthesis by sun-damaged fibroblasts.


Question 2
2
Which other skin lesions are often surrounded by sun-damaged skin?
Show answer

Basal cell carcinomas, squamous cell carcinomas, and actinic keratoses are often surrounded by sun-damaged skin.


Question 3
3
Are all melanomas found on sun-damaged skin?
Show answer

No. Melanomas can develop on non–sun-exposed skin as well as non-skin sites such as the mucosa of the gastrointestinal or genitourinary tract or the uvea of the eye. This highlights the complex combination of environmental and genetic risk in melanomas.

Skin, malignant melanoma in epidermis – Medium power

The epidermis contains a proliferation of cytologically atypical melanocytes with enlarged irregular nuclei and prominent nucleoli. Melanocytes are present along the basal layer in nests and singly (lentiginous spread). As seen in this case, malignant melanomas characteristically contain individual melanocytes spreading vertically throughout all levels of the epidermis (pagetoid spread).

Question 1
1
Which cell types can be found in the epidermis? Where are they located, and what is their function?
Show answer

The three main epidermal cell types are squamous epithelial cells (keratinocytes), melanocytes, and Langerhans cells. Keratinocytes are present in all epidermal layers. They not only protect the body from fluid loss and mechanical injury, but they also are important in the production of regulatory cytokines. Melanocytes are found between the keratinocytes of the basal layer of the epidermis. They produce melanin. This brown pigment is used as an endogenous screen to protect the DNA against damage by sunlight. Langerhans cells are dendritic cells, located in the upper layers of the epidermis. They are antigen-presenting cells of probable monocytic derivation.

Skin, malignant melanoma – High power

The cells in malignant melanoma are typically large with abundant dusty gray cytoplasm and melanin pigment. The nuclei are round with coarse chromatin and prominent nucleoli. The morphologic spectrum of malignant melanoma is vast and frequently causes diagnostic difficulty at metastatic sites when the primary lesion is occult or has regressed.

Question 1
1
Describe the morphological differences between melanoma cells and benign melanocytes of a nevus.
Show answer

Benign melanocytes lack pleomorphism and mitotic figures and are usually considerably smaller than the cells in a malignant melanoma. Nucleoli are generally not visible in benign melanocytes, whereas the cells in a malignant melanoma often contain prominent red nucleoli.


Question 2
2
What molecular changes are present in melanoma, and how do these changes guide patient treatment?
Show answer

A wide variety of mutations are found, but it is not completely clear which mutations are driving the molecular progression of melanoma. Activating mutations in BRAF and NRAS, which promote cell growth and survival, are present in 50% and 20% of tumors, respectively. Mutations in MEK have also been described. Cell cycle gene CDKN2A is mutated in 10% of sporadic melanomas but in up to 40% of familial melanomas. BRAF and MEK inhibitors have been shown to prolong survival in certain patients with melanoma and the corresponding mutations. Additionally, immunogenic therapy with anti-PD1 and anti-CTLA4 antibodies has shown encouraging results.

Targeted Therapy Box – Melanoma: Bright Hopes for a Dark Tumor

Melanoma: Bright Hopes for a Dark Tumor

Metastatic melanoma is a lethal disease. If a lesion cannot be recognized at a stage at which primary resection is adequate for control, the median survival is 7 months—even less if more than a single metastasis is present. As a consequence, melanoma is responsible for greater than three quarters of the mortality associated with skin cancers, amounting to roughly 9000 deaths annually in the United States and 48,000 worldwide. A distressing reality is that even small and apparently completely excised lesions also can harbor occult metastases at the time of original resection; in the absence of good adjuvant prophylaxis, late recurrence is always a concern.


Fortunately, the armamentarium available to fight back is growing, using a combination of augmented immunity and new chemotherapeutic agents that disrupt molecular pathways involved in proliferation of melanomas.


In the former category, topical imiquimod at the site of primary excision can activate cutaneous immune cells via Toll-like receptor 7, leading to local elaboration of a variety of pro-inflammatory cytokines. Systemically, immunomodulation is accomplished through interferon or interleukin-2 therapies, although these have been effective in only a small subset of patients. More encouraging, the introduction of ipilimumab—anti-CTLA-4 (cytotoxic T lymphocyte-associated antigen 4) monoclonal antibodies—has improved survival in metastatic melanoma by 20%, putatively by blocking the negative regulation of cytotoxic T cells through this surface receptor. Unfortunately, not only do these therapies have significant adverse side effects, but they can be very expensive and only rarely effect a cure.


Recent advances have greatly improved current understanding of the mutations that drive melanoma growth. These have been instrumental in developing targeted therapies. Melanoma cells seem to be “addicted” to MAPK (mitogen-activated protein kinase) pathways for growth, maintenance, drug resistance, and progression. Indeed, 40% to 60% of melanomas (and 7% to 8% of all cancers) carry activating mutations in the B-Raf serine-threonine protein kinase that is a component of the MAPK signaling; a remarkable 90% of these mutations involve a specific substitution of glutamic acid for valine at amino acid 600 (V600E). The high prevalence of B-Raf V600E in melanomas has allowed the development of kinase inhibitors specific for the mutant enzyme. In trials of two such inhibitors, vemurafenib and dabrafenib, complete or partial regression was observed in the vast majority of tumors carrying the mutated B-Raf, with almost double the duration of progression-free survival.


Still, this improvement does not equal a cure, and tumors eventually develop resistance through secondary N-Ras mutations, upregulation of other surface receptor tyrosine kinases, or B-Raf truncations. The success of these approaches, however, points to an eventually much brighter prognosis for such a dark malignancy.

Lymph node with metastatic melanoma – Medium power

The sentinel lymph node contains very similar malignant cells. The presence or absence of metastases to lymph nodes is the most important prognostic factor in early-stage melanomas. Nodal status is also important in determining subsequent treatment.

Question 1
1
Describe the sentinel node procedure.
Show answer

The patient is injected at the site of the scar of the malignant melanoma with a blue dye, a radioactive substance, or a combination of both. The lymph node draining this site (the sentinel lymph node) absorbs the dye/radioactive substance. The lymph node turns blue and can be identified by color, or it can be identified by the presence of radioactivity.


The sentinel lymph node directly drains the site of the tumor. If it is uninvolved by metastatic disease, it is unlikely that malignant melanoma has spread to other lymph nodes, and the patient can be spared regional lymphadenectomy.

Skin, dysplastic nevus – Clinical presentation

This pigmented macule with irregular borders was excised and found to contain a melanocytic proliferation with mild architectural and cytologic abnormalities. The nomenclature for such lesions is controversial and may be called an atypical or dysplastic nevus.

Question 1
1
What is the relationship between dysplastic nevi and malignant melanoma?
Show answer

This topic is controversial and not well understood. The risk of developing a malignant melanoma in an isolated dysplastic nevus is very low, but it does occur. Patients with a condition known as familial atypical multiple mole and melanoma syndrome often have more than 100 nevi, many of them dysplastic. These patients have a lifetime risk of malignant melanoma that approaches 100%. Some believe that dysplastic nevi represent a precursor lesion of melanoma. Others believe that dysplastic nevi merely serve as a marker for generalized increased risk of the developing melanoma.

Figure 25-5 Potential steps of tumor progression in dysplastic nevi. A, Lentiginous melanocytic hyperplasia. B, Lentiginous junctional nevus. C, Lentiginous compound nevus with abnormal architectural and cytologic features (dysplastic nevus). D, Early melanoma, or melanoma in radial growth phase (large dark cells in epidermis). E, Advanced melanoma (vertical growth phase) with malignant spread into the dermis and vessels. The risk of malignant transformation of any single dysplastic nevus is small, but appears to be higher than that of typical nevi.

Question 1
1
Is there an increased melanoma risk for this patient?
Show answer

In this small lesion, there is no appreciable increased melanoma risk. In large congenital melanocytic nevi, covering a total leg or the whole trunk, there is an increased melanoma risk.


Question 2
2
Is a nevus a neoplasm or a type of hyperplasia?
Show answer

A nevus is a benign neoplasm. Molecular studies have shown that nevi harbor activating mutations in BRAF and NRAS.


Question 3
3
Describe the difference between a junctional nevus, dermal nevus, and compound nevus.
Show answer

A junctional nevus has an accumulation of melanocytes only at the dermal-epidermal junction. A dermal nevus contains melanocytes only in the dermis. A compound nevus is composed of both junctional and dermal melanocytes.

Figure 25-4 Maturation sequence of nondysplastic melanocytic nevi. A, Normal skin shows only scattered dendritic melanocytes within the epidermal basal cell layer. B, Junctional nevus. C, Compound nevus. D, Dermal nevus. E, Dermal nevus with neurotization, a change that is also referred to as maturation. Nevi may exist at any stage in this sequence for variable periods of time, although many are believed to progress through this sequence.
