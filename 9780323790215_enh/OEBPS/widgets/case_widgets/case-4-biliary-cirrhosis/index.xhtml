Scenario

A 38-year-old white woman presented to her physician, complaining of constant itching and fatigue. The patient had had problems with pruritus for a long time. Pruritus occurred during the night, sometimes awakening her from sleep. There had been a slow but steady increase in the severity of the problem. Her history was negative for alcohol use, hepatitis, blood transfusion, and intravenous drug use. She had not had any recent illnesses or surgical procedures. Physical examination revealed a mildly obese woman in no distress. Excoriations were present over her upper arms and chest. There was mild jaundice, and small yellow papules were present about her palpebral fissures; larger cutaneous nodules were present over her elbows. There were no ascites. Her liver percussed to 14 cm and extended 5 cm below the right costal margin. Her liver was firm and had a smooth edge. Her spleen tip was palpable.

An abdominal sonogram showed a homogeneous liver, normal gallbladder without stones, and no dilated ducts. The pancreas appeared normal. A liver biopsy was performed. The patient was treated with oral cholestyramine and ursodeoxycholic acid.

Despite therapy over 6 years, the patient's liver disease progressed to cirrhosis, and a liver transplant was performed. The transplanted liver allograft began functioning well when, over several days, mild liver enlargement and tenderness were noted, and laboratory results revealed an elevated bilirubin and elevated aminotransferase levels. A liver biopsy was performed, and, based upon the results, steroid immunosuppression was given, resulting in improvement of the patient's condition. She went on to do well with her liver transplant.

Question 1
1
What group of individuals develop primary biliary cirrhosis?
Show answer

Primary biliary cirrhosis is a chronic liver disorder, found most commonly in white women (women:men ratio of 9:1), that results in chronic inflammatory destruction of intrahepatic bile ducts, eventually leading to development of biliary cirrhosis.

Question 1
1
What laboratory tests are particularly useful in diagnosing primary biliary cirrhosis? What are the postulated mechanisms for this disease?
Show answer

In primary biliary cirrhosis, the presence of a conjugated hyperbilirubinemia, that is, elevated total and direct bilirubin, along with an elevation of alkaline phosphatase out of proportion to minimally elevated hepatic aminotransferases (AST and ALT), is indicative of a cholestatic pattern of liver disease. Autoantibodies are a significant feature of this disease. Antimitochondrial antibodies are found in over 90% of cases. A liver biopsy is required to establish the diagnosis and exclude other diseases that can cause cholestasis. While there is much evidence to support an autoimmune etiology in primary biliary cirrhosis, the inciting antigens are not known, and a definitive cause of the disorder has not been found.

Question 1
1
Name some causes of biliary cirrhosis other than primary biliary cirrhosis.
Show answer

Other causes include primary sclerosing cholangitis; extrahepatic biliary atresia; and processes that lead to chronic obstruction of the biliary tract, such as an impacted gallstone in the common bile duct, biliary strictures, or tumors of the biliary tree or pancreas.

Biliary tree, biliary atresia - Gross

Biliary atresia, a common cause of neonatal cholestasis, results from progressive destruction of the hepatic and common bile ducts. In this image, the common bile duct is reduced to a thin band of fibrous tissue extending from the hilum of the liver, which is green due to the presence of cholestasis, to the duodenal area. This leads to the development of cirrhosis in the first year of life. It is the most common cause of referral for liver transplantation in children.

Image 8

Hepatic and intrahepatic ducts
Gallbladder

Gallbladder and bile ducts, primary sclerosing cholangitis - Endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatography (ERCP) image

ERCP is a radiographic technique used to visualize the extrahepatic and intrahepatic biliary and pancreatic ducts. An endoscope, the large white structure in the lower right portion of the image, is passed through the esophagus and stomach into the duodenum in order to cannulate the ampulla of Vater. Radiographic dye is then injected into the ampulla in order to outline the extrahepatic and intrahepatic bile and pancreatic ducts. The pattern produced in primary sclerosing cholangitis is virtually diagnostic in the appropriate clinical setting. ERCP shows a beaded column of radiographic dye due to numerous areas of stricture of the bile ducts with dilation of the ductular segments between the strictured areas.

Question 1
1
Can you name some other conditions in which ERCP would be useful, and what ERCP would show?
Show answer

ERCP is excellent for imaging the extrahepatic bile ducts as well as the pancreatic duct and the larger portion of the intrahepatic bile ducts. In addition to primary sclerosing cholangitis, it is useful in the evaluation of strictures, obstructing gallstones, and tumors of the biliary tract, periampullary region, and pancreas.

Bile ducts (note surrounding fibrosis)
Portal vein
Hepatic artery

Liver, primary sclerosing cholangitis - High power

Concentric periductular fibrosis around bile ducts (referred to as onion skinning), leading eventually to destruction and stricture of affected bile ducts, is seen in primary sclerosing cholangitis. In between the areas of stricture, bile ducts become ectatic and inflamed. The liver in primary sclerosing cholangitis eventually becomes cirrhotic.

Question 1
1
With what disease affecting the intestinal tract is primary sclerosing cholangitis associated?
Show answer

Primary sclerosing cholangitis is a disease of unknown etiology, often associated (in approximately two thirds of cases) with inflammatory bowel disease, primarily ulcerative colitis. Primary sclerosing cholangitis is preponderant in young men.
