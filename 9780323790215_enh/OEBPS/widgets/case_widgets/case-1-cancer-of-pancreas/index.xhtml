Scenario

A 62-year-old man presented to his physician complaining of yellow eyes and weight loss. The patient had noted a decrease in his appetite and a decrease in his body weight of about 30 lb over the past 6 months. The patient admitted to loose bowel movements, which were greasy, clay colored, and malodorous. He had a 45-pack-year history of smoking and a history of heavy alcohol use for many years. There was no history of chemical or toxin exposure. The physical examination revealed a thin, frail man with prominent bitemporal muscle wasting. His eyes and skin were yellow. The chest and cardiac examinations were unremarkable except for a mild increase in the anteroposterior chest diameter and mildly decreased breath sounds. His liver percussed to 11 cm, and a firm, irregular edge was palpable below the right costal margin. No spleen was felt.

An abdominal sonogram revealed dilated common bile and hepatic ducts, a dilated gallbladder containing sludge, and multiple masses in the liver. The pancreas was not visualized due to overlying bowel gas. A computerized tomography scan of the abdomen confirmed the same findings as well as a 5-cm mass in the head of the pancreas. An endoscopic retrograde cholangiopancreatogram showed a stricture of the common bile duct just above the ampulla of Vater and dilation of the common bile and hepatic ducts proximal to the stricture. A stent was placed across the stricture after brushings of the strictured distal common bile duct were taken, which later revealed malignant cells consistent with carcinoma. Blood for CA 19-9 antigen level was drawn; the result was 800 U/mL. Informed of the results, the patient declined further therapy and was offered palliative care. He expired at home several months later.

An autopsy revealed an enlarged liver with numerous tan masses of tumor distributed throughout, some with focal areas of necrosis and hemorrhage. Scattered similar tan nodules were noted in the lungs and right adrenal gland. The common bile duct was dilated, and the distal portion showed invasion and near-total narrowing of the lumen by a poorly defined scirrhous mass in the head of the pancreas. The remainder of the pancreas was firm and fibrotic with loss of the normal lobular architecture.

Question 1
1
By what routes do malignant tumors metastasize to the liver?

Most tumors metastasize to the liver hematogenously through the portal vein or hepatic artery. Biliary tract tumors directly and contiguously spread to the liver.

Question 1
1
From which organs do mucin-producing adenocarcinomas arise?

Mucin-producing adenocarcinomas arise from organs such as the lung, gastrointestinal tract, pancreas, breast, and ovary. Some adenocarcinomas, such as those from the prostate and kidney, are most often not mucin secreting.

Question 1
1
Can the location of this tumor be related to the patient's signs, symptoms, and laboratory abnormalities?

Pancreatic adenocarcinomas occur in the head of the pancreas in about 60% of cases, while 15% to 20% of cases occur in the body and 5% occur in the tail of the pancreas. In 15% to 20% of cases, the tumor is so extensive and diffuse throughout the pancreas that it is impossible to determine the site of origin. Carcinomas involving the head of the pancreas manifest somewhat earlier due to obstruction of the common bile duct. However, by the time jaundice appears, the tumor is usually unresectable. The location of this tumor in the head of the pancreas is responsible for severe obstructive jaundice. This is supported by conjugated hyperbilirubinemia with a marked elevation of alkaline phosphatase and gamma-glutamyl transpeptidase. In addition, the absence of urobilinogen in urine indicates nearly complete obstruction of bile flow into the gut.


Question 2
2
Did this patient have any clinical features of chronic pancreatitis? What are some other complications of chronic pancreatitis?

Yes. This patient had evidence of fat malabsorption, manifested as greasy, clay-colored, foul-smelling stools (steatorrhea). Other complications of chronic pancreatitis are pancreatic pseudocysts, pancreatic calcifications, and, with destruction of islet cells, diabetes mellitus.


Question 3
3
Is the presence of pancreatitis and carcinoma in this patient coincidental or etiologically related?

Chronic pancreatitis is associated with only a slightly increased risk of cancer, so the finding is most likely coincidental.


Question 4
4
What are some of the causes of acute and chronic pancreatitis?

Chronic pancreatitis results in destruction of the exocrine and, eventually, endocrine portions of the pancreas. It usually follows repeated bouts of acute pancreatitis that are due to alcohol ingestion or obstruction of the pancreatic duct. Other causes include tropical pancreatitis, hereditary pancreatitis, and idiopathic cases.

In acute pancreatitis acinar injury results in release of proteolytic enzymes, leading to a cascade of events including activation of the clotting cascade, acute and chronic inflammation, vascular injury, and edema. In most patients, complete resolution of the acute injury occurs with restoration of acinar cell mass. In chronic pancreatitis, repeated episodes of acinar cell injury lead to the production of profibrogenic cytokines such as transforming growth factor β (TGF- β) and platelet-derived growth factor (PDGF), resulting in the proliferation of myofibroblasts, the secretion of collagen, and remodeling of the extracellular matrix (ECM). Repeated injury produces irreversible loss of acinar cell mass, fibrosis, and pancreatic insufficiency.

Question 1
1
What are risk factors for developing pancreatic carcinoma?

Pancreatic carcinoma is a genetic disease, the result of multiple inherited and acquired genetic mutations. Risk factors for the development of pancreatic carcinoma include smoking and diets high in fats. The risk of developing pancreatic carcinoma in smokers is 1.5 times that in nonsmokers.

Question 2
2
Is there a recognized precursor lesion to pancreatic adenocarcinoma?

Yes. Panceatic intraepithelial neoplasia (PanIN) is analogous to the adenoma-carcinoma sequence in colorectal carcinoma and represents a precursor to pancreatic adenocarcinoma.

Figure 19-12 Model for the progression from normal ducts (far left) through PanINs (center) to invasive carcinoma (far right). Question 1
1
What symptoms result from failure of the exocrine pancreas?

Without adequate pancreatic exocrine secretions, patients develop malabsorption and diarrhea. Vitamin deficiency of the fat-soluble vitamins (K, A, D, and E) develop due to the malabsorption. Some research suggests that patients with low levels of vitamin D are at an increased risk for breast, prostate, and colon cancer.

Vitamin D: Lighting the Way Beyond Calcium Homeostasis

The major function of vitamin D is to facilitate the absorption and metabolism of calcium to support activities such as neuromuscular transmission and bone mineralization. Thus, the minimum daily requirement of 200 to 400 international units (IU), corresponding to 5 to 10 μg, was established as the amount necessary to prevent rickets and osteomalacia. However, that amount may be insufficient to sustain the other important activities supported by vitamin D. The major source (90%) of vitamin D in humans is the ultraviolet B-driven conversion of endogenously synthesized 7-dehydrocholesterol precursors to cholecalciferol (vitamin D3). This is an important factoid, because in northern latitudes or in dark-skinned persons (melanin blocks the relevant light wavelengths)—or in cloistered medical students, children with thick coats of sunscreen, or heavily veiled women—supplementary dietary needs may be greater. The metabolism of vitamin D involves the following steps:

  • Vitamin D3 is hydroxylated to its 25-hydroxy form through the cytochrome P-450; this is subsequently converted by renal tubular cells to 1,25-dihydroxy-vitamin D3, the most active form. Further 24-hydroxylation inactivates the molecule.
  • 1,25-Dihydroxy-vitamin D3 is essentially a steroid hormone; it acts by binding an intracellular nuclear vitamin D receptor (VDR), which then forms heterodimers with the retinoid X receptor (RXR). These vitamin D-VDR-RXR complexes bind to vitamin D response elements and drive protein transcription.
  • 1,25-Dihydroxy-vitamin D3 also can bind plasma membrane vitamin D receptors that directly activate protein kinase C and open calcium channels.

Virtually all cells—not just those involved in calcium homeostasis—express VDR. Of interest, macrophages, keratinocytes, and various epithelia (breast, colon, and prostate) can synthesize 1,25-dihydroxy-vitamin D3. In macrophages, activation of Toll-like receptors increases the synthesis of both VDR and 1,25-dihydroxy-vitamin D3, inducing the production of antimicrobial peptides such as cathelicidins, thus improving innate immunity. In all, more than 200 additional genes are regulated by vitamin D—including those that modulate proliferation, apoptosis, and angiogenesis. Epidemiologic data quite persuasively demonstrate that low 1,25-hydroxy-vitamin D3 levels correlate with a 30% to 50% greater incidence of colon, prostate, and breast cancers. Of interest, the vitamin D-inactivating 24-hydroxylase enzyme (CYP24A1) often is overexpressed in tumor cells. As a result of multiple epidemiologic studies, there is a movement to increase the daily minimal supplementation of vitamin D to greater than 1000 IU, especially in the winter in northern latitudes. Moreover, drugs that target the 24-hydroxylase CYP24A1 show promise in the therapy of certain epithelial malignancies. Clearly, ultraviolet light exposure should be balanced by concerns regarding cutaneous malignancy, and oral dosing should not be overdone lest vitamin D toxicity (and hypercalcemia) result. Overall, however, these considerations give all of us an excuse to spend more time in the sun and perhaps provide a greater impetus for selected vitamin supplementation.

Pancreatic pseudocysts are large cysts filled with pancreatic secretions and sometimes, as in this case, a few dark calculi as well. Pancreatic pseudocysts lack a true epithelial lining. Instead, their wall consists of a rim of fibrous tissue containing chronic inflammatory cells. They occur in the pancreas and are sometimes noted in the soft tissues just adjacent to it.

Question 1
1
What are the causes of pancreatic pseudocysts?

Pseudocysts of the pancreas are caused by pancreatic inflammation. They lack an epithelial lining and contain localized collections of pancreatic secretions. Pancreatic pseudocysts usually arise from pancreatitis. Some can be the result of abdominal trauma.
