Scenario

A 63-year-old man presented with urinary hesitancy, frequency, and nocturia. Digital rectal examination revealed a large, nodular, and rubbery prostate gland with no hard regions. The serum prostate-specific antigen (PSA) was 6 ng/mL (reference range, 0–4 ng/mL). Transrectal ultrasound examination was performed and prostatic biopsy specimens were obtained, which were negative for tumor (Images 1 and 2).

Fifteen years later, he presented with low back pain of several months' duration. Further questioning revealed recurrent urinary hesitancy with recent onset of dysuria. Digital rectal examination revealed a hard and irregular prostate gland. Laboratory data included alkaline phosphatase 386 U/L (reference range, 38–126 U/L) and PSA 103 ng/mL (reference range, 0–4 ng/mL). A transrectal biopsy of the prostate gland was performed (Images 3 and 4). A radionuclide bone scan revealed widespread hot spots, and a radiograph of the spine revealed numerous radiodense bony lesions (Image 5). Prostatectomy was performed and the patient received medical castration with leuprolide.

Question 1
1
Is nodular hyperplasia found most commonly in the central or peripheral portions of the prostate gland?
Show answer

Nodular hyperplasia is found in the central portions of the prostate gland, which explains its clinical association with urinary hesitancy.


Figure 21-30 Adult prostate. The normal prostate contains several distinct regions, including a central zone (CZ), a peripheral zone (PZ), a transitional zone (TZ), and a periurethral zone. Most carcinomas arise from the peripheral zone and may be palpable during digital examination of the rectum. Nodular hyperplasia, in contrast, arises from the more centrally situated transitional zone and often produces urinary obstruction.

Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia: Bigger Is Not Necessarily Better

Like death and taxes, progressive prostatic enlargement is nearly a certainty as males age (and not just humans—older male rats, rabbits, and dogs also are susceptible). Affecting 20% of men by age 40, the incidence of so-called benign prostatic hypertrophy (BPH) rises to 70% by age 60 and surpasses 90% by age 80. Moderate to severe symptoms occur in roughly a third of those so afflicted, resulting from compression of the prostatic urethra by the enlarged prostate. Patients classically experience urinary frequency, nocturia, difficulty in starting or stopping the urinary stream, and dysuria and are at a greater risk for urinary tract infections. In some cases, the presenting manifestation is acute urinary retention.


The key molecule in the pathogenesis of BPH is 5α-reductase (5AR) type 2. It converts circulating testosterone to dihydrotestosterone (DHT). DHT, in turn, forms a tight complex with nuclear androgen receptors within stromal and epithelial cells, driving the elaboration of a host of growth factors and their receptors. This results in proliferation of stromal as well as glandular cells and inhibition of apoptosis. The problem posed by prostatic enlargement is compounded by an increase in smooth muscle tone in the prostate stroma, urethra, and bladder neck.


BPH is treated by surgical and medical therapies:


  • Transurethral resection of the prostate (TURP) or updated variations on that theme (using lasers, microwaves, radiofrequency energy, or high-intensity ultrasound) remain the surgical gold standard. These modalities work by physically removing or otherwise ablating tissue adjacent to the urethra.
  • Because of the increased smooth muscle tone, drugs that relax smooth muscle contraction, such as α-adrenergic receptor antagonists, also are beneficial. Agents that act selectively on the α1A receptor (expressed in the prostate, urethra, and bladder, but not elsewhere), such as tamsulosin, improve urinary flow by relaxing the smooth muscles, with minimal side effects.

The current, more elegant approach to treating BPH involves blocking the production of DHT through inhibitors of 5AR such as finasteride and dutasteride. Without DHT, the prostatic stroma and epithelium lose their proliferative signals and even involute as antiapoptotic signals are removed. As an extra benefit, some 5AR inhibitors that block the type 1 enzyme (found in skin and liver) also can be effective for the treatment of alopecia (hair loss)—truly an example of better living through chemistry!

Question 1
1
How do malignant prostatic glands differ from benign prostatic glands in histologic appearance?
Show answer

Malignant prostatic glands tend to be small, back to back, and infiltrating; they have a single cell layer and prominent nucleoli. Benign prostatic glands tend to be larger, have a lobular configuration, and are lined by two cell layers.


Image 4

Carcinomatous glands
Intervening desmoplastic stroma

Prostate gland, adenocarcinoma - High power

Malignant prostatic glands are lined by a single layer of cells that typically display large nucleoli (as opposed to a two-cell layer without large nucleoli in benign glands; see Image 2). The intervening stroma is often desmoplastic, which is responsible for the rock-hard nature of many prostatic adenocarcinomas.

Question 2
2
What type of cell produces bone in osteoblastic metastases?
Show answer

Tumor cells do not produce bone; they activate benign osteoblasts to lay down bone. New bone formation is accompanied by increased serum alkaline phosphatase levels, as seen in this patient.

Question 1
1
What tumor marker would likely be elevated in patients with prostate cancer, and how specific is it?
Show answer

The serum level of prostate-specific antigen (PSA), a product of prostatic epithelial cells, is elevated in prostate cancer; however, this is not specific for cancer. PSA levels may also be elevated in patients with nodular hyperplasia of the prostate gland or prostatitis. Serum PSA levels by themselves cannot be used for a definitive diagnosis of prostate cancer.

Question 1
1
What are some common metastatic sites for prostatic adenocarcinoma?
Show answer

Pelvic lymph nodes, bone, and lungs are common metastatic sites.


Question 2
2
Why is leuprolide prescribed to patients with advanced prostate cancer?
Show answer

Leuprolide is a gonadotropin-releasing hormone (Gn-RH) agonist. Continuous Gn-RH agonism suppresses luteinizing hormone production and testicular androgen synthesis, leading to pharmacologic castration and improved control of the cancer.
