Scenario

The patient is a 51-year-old white man who presented 10 years before surgery with a history of heartburn, regurgitation, and epigastric pain. Endoscopy was performed, and a large erythematous area involving the distal esophagus was noted. Biopsy specimens were taken and showed features of reflux esophagitis. The patient was treated with antireflux drugs and given a follow-up appointment in 1 year. The patient returned 3 years later, complaining of dysphagia, heartburn, and epigastric pain. Endoscopy was performed again and revealed that the normal white squamous mucosa lining the distal esophagus was replaced by pink columnar mucosa. Biopsy specimens were taken, which subsequently showed metaplastic columnar epithelium containing intestinal goblet cells. Because of the diagnosis of Barrett esophagus, the patient was enrolled in a surveillance program, and yearly endoscopic procedures were recommended. Endoscopy showed extensive Barrett esophagus 6 years before surgery, and biopsy specimens showed features of dysplasia. He failed to return for subsequent surveillance endoscopy. The patient was admitted with increasing dysphagia 1 month before surgery. An upper GI series (radiographs) revealed distal narrowing of the esophagus. Endoscopic examination of the esophagus revealed an ulcerating mass in the distal esophagus. A biopsy specimen was obtained. The patient was taken to surgery, where an esophagogastrectomy was performed.

Esophagus, reflux esophagitis - Medium power

The most common cause of esophagitis is the reflux of gastric and duodenal contents; the resulting lesion is known as reflux esophagitis. Endoscopically, the esophageal mucosa may show erythema and prominent vascular markings, hemorrhage, and ulcers. Endoscopic biopsy specimens show intraepithelial eosinophils and neutrophils, basal zone hyperplasia, and elongation of lamina propria papillae. This image shows basal zone hyperplasia, characterized by a basal layer greater than 15%, and elongation of papillae that extend into the outer one third of the epithelium. The intraepithelial eosinophils and neutrophils are not evident at this magnification.

Question 1
1
What are the major causes of reflux esophagitis?
Show answer

Reflux of gastric and duodenal contents is the major cause of reflux esophagitis. Many factors play a role: (1) the presence of a sliding hiatal hernia, which is the most common; (2) heavy alcohol use; (3) heavy tobacco use; (4) increased gastric volume; (5) decreased efficacy of the lower esophageal sphincter; (6) pregnancy; (7) central nervous system depressants; and (8) hypothyroidism. It has been estimated that 10% of adults in the United States have had symptoms of reflux esophagitis. Although it is difficult to obtain a true prevalence of symptoms of gastroesophageal reflux in childhood, it is common in infants and children.

Esophagus, reflux esophagitis - High power

This image shows numerous intraepithelial eosinophils and neutrophils and normal squamous epithelial cells. Endoscopic punch biopsy specimens are small and poorly oriented; however, the presence of intraepithelial eosinophils and neutrophils is an important finding and allows diagnosis of reflux esophagitis. Patients may experience regurgitation of sour gastric contents, heartburn, hematemesis, melena, or dysphagia. Occasionally, patients experience attacks of severe chest pain that may be mistaken for a “heart attack.”

Question 1
1
What are other causes of esophagitis?
Show answer

Other causes of esophagitis include eosinophilic esophagitis (which can be in the differential with reflux); Crohn disease; ingestion of irritants (e.g., alcohol, corrosive acids); infections in immunosuppressed hosts by fungi (e.g., Candida) or viruses (e.g., cytomegalovirus, herpes); uremia; radiation therapy; graft-versus-host disease; and cytotoxic anticancer therapy.


Image 3

Metaplastic columnar epithelium
Goblet cells

Esophagus, metaplastic columnar epithelium of Barrett esophagus - Low power

Endoscopically, Barrett esophagus is recognized as red velvety mucosa located above the gastroesophageal junction. This image shows the esophageal squamous epithelium replaced by an intestinal type of metaplastic columnar epithelium containing goblet cells. The columnar epithelium of Barrett esophagus is a complication of long-standing gastroesophageal reflux, occurring in 11% of patients with reflux esophagitis. In Barrett esophagus, the distal squamous mucosa is replaced by metaplastic columnar epithelium as a response to prolonged injury. The intestinal type of metaplastic columnar epithelium may be associated with a neoplastic mucosal change known as dysplasia. Patients with Barrett esophagus tend to have a long history of heartburn and other reflux symptoms.

Image 4

Dysplastic glands
Inflamed lamina propria

Esophagus, dysplasia - Medium power

Crucial to the evaluation of patients with Barrett esophagus is the recognition of associated dysplasia, the precursor of malignancy. Dysplasia is recognized by the presence of cytologic and architectural abnormalities. The dysplastic glands resemble adenomatous glands typically seen in adenomatous polyps of the colon. This image shows the metaplastic columnar epithelium completely replaced by dysplastic epithelial cells with enlarged, crowded, and stratified hyperchromatic nuclei. In some areas, the glands are lined by cells that maintain their basal polarity, and in other areas, the glands show cells migrating toward the lumen.

Question 1
1
What is the definition of a Barrett esophagus?
Show answer

In Barrett esophagus, the squamous mucosa is replaced by metaplastic columnar epithelium as a response to prolonged reflux-induced injury in the lower part of the esophagus. Use of the term Barrett esophagus requires that endoscopic and histologic evidence of this metaplastic columnar epithelium be present.

Question 1
1
What are the major complications of reflux esophagitis?
Show answer

The potential complications of severe reflux esophagitis are (1) ulcer, (2) bleeding, (3) development of stricture, and (4) development of Barrett esophagus.

Esophagus, adenocarcinoma in Barrett esophagus - High power

This image shows a glandular tumor composed of irregular back-to-back glands. Most tumors arising in Barrett esophagus are mucin-producing. Note the presence of squamous epithelium adjacent to the adenocarcinoma. About 10% of patients who have Barrett esophagus develop adenocarcinoma of the esophagus. Most, if not all, of these carcinomas are preceded by the metaplasia-dysplasia-cancer sequence.

Question 1
1
In which portion of the esophagus do adenocarcinomas arise? Why?
Show answer

Adenocarcinomas arise predominantly in the lower third of the esophagus. This is because adenocarcinomas develop from reflux-associated Barrett Esophagus, which occurs in the distal esophagus. Conversely, squamous cell carcinomas occur predominantly in the upper to middle portions of the esophagus.

Esophagus, superficially invasive adenocarcinoma in Barrett esophagus - Gross, mucosal surface

This esophagogastrectomy specimen shows an irregular gastroesophageal junction. The distal esophagus shows a tan, granular zone of Barrett esophagus. Compare with the white squamous mucosa proximal to the displaced squamocolumnar junction. Note the presence of a small ulcer, consistent with an early invasive adenocarcinoma invading the submucosa. Superficially invasive adenocarcinomas (confined to the mucosa or involving the mucosa and submucosa with or without lymph nodes involved by cancer) arising in a Barrett esophagus are uncommonly encountered. Early diagnosis with definitive resection improves 5-year survival to more than 80%.

Esophagus, advanced adenocarcinoma in Barrett esophagus - Gross, mucosal surface

This esophagogastrectomy specimen shows a large, irregular, ulcerating tumor with heaped-up margins at the gastroesophageal junction. Note the pink, heaped-up metaplastic columnar epithelium and the adjacent white squamous mucosa. Endoscopic biopsy specimens for the evaluation of specialized columnar-type epithelium or associated dysplasia should be taken from the interface between white squamous mucosa and the pink, heaped-up Barrett epithelium. Most adenocarcinomas arising in a Barrett esophagus occur in white men older than 40 years. Patients usually present because of dysphagia, weight loss, bleeding, chest pain, and vomiting.

1
What do heaped-up margins indicate?
Show answer

Heaped-up margins around a lesion are usually a sign of tumor invasion into adjacent tissue.

Question 1
1
What factors predispose to esophageal squamous cell carcinoma?
Show answer

The most important factors are smoking and alcohol consumption. Other factors include fungal contamination of food; dietary nitrites; dietary deficiencies in vitamin A, vitamin C, and riboflavin; and long-standing esophagitis.

Question 1
1
What is the usual location of esophageal squamous cell carcinomas?
Show answer

Of esophageal squamous cell carcinomas, 20% occur in the upper third, 50% occur in the middle third, and 30% occur in the lower third of the esophagus.

Ulcerating tumor

Esophagus, squamous cell carcinoma - Gross, mucosal surface

The middle portion of the esophagus shows a large necrotic ulcer that can excavate deeply into surrounding structures (e.g., tracheobronchial tree, aorta). Three morphologic patterns are described: (1) protruded (60%), (2) flat (15%), and (3) ulcerated (25%). These tumors occur more frequently in black men and produce dysphagia (difficulty in swallowing) and obstruction. These tumors are lethal; the 5-year survival rate is about 9% for all patients with esophageal squamous cell carcinoma.
