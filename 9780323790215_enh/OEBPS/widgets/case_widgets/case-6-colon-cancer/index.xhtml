Scenario

A 60-year-old woman was in good health until she tripped and fell in her home and sustained a lumbar compression fracture of L1 and L2. Unexplained anemia was discovered when she was admitted to the hospital. On physical examination, the patient was a well-nourished white woman in no acute distress. Her conjunctivae were pale. The musculoskeletal examination revealed bilateral lower extremity weakness. Abdominal, cardiovascular, and respiratory examinations were unremarkable. Rectal examination showed guaiac-positive stools. On admission, the following laboratory results were noted:

TEST RESULT
Hemoglobin 10 g/dL
Hematocrit 31%
MCV 61 µm3
MCHC 28 g/dL
Serum ferritin 2 ng/mL

Fecal hemoccult (fecal occult blood [FOB]) tests were positive.

She was discharged after 3 days, and her internist initiated a work-up for anemia. Iron replacement therapy was started. Upper gastrointestinal x-ray studies revealed a normal esophagus, stomach, duodenum, and small bowel. Colonoscopy showed a 4-cm polypoid, exophytic cecal mass. A small polyp measuring 1 cm was also present in the transverse colon. Biopsy of the cecal mass showed a malignant lesion. The polyp was removed by endoscopic polypectomy; it showed the features of a pedunculated, benign, neoplastic polyp. A partial colectomy was performed. The resected specimen contained a polypoid mass, 5×5 cm. Histologic examination revealed a malignant tumor. Twenty-eight lymph nodes were negative for tumor cells. The tumor was classified as a T2N0M0. The postoperative course was uneventful.

A colonoscopy 1 year after surgery was negative, and colonoscopy 2 years after resection revealed a normal ileocolonic anastomosis and a 1-cm adenomatous polyp in the sigmoid colon. Her CBC remained normal after surgery. Carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA) was 1.5 ng/mL postoperatively (normal <3). Four years after surgery, her CEA value increased to 60, alkaline phosphatase was 202 U/L (normal 30-115 U/L) and aspartate transaminase (AST) was 240 U/L (normal 5-35 U/L). A liver-spleen scan revealed multiple masses in the liver.

Targeted Therapy Box – Aspirin and NSAIDS: Relief more than pain

Tumors of the Appendix

Aspirin and NSAIDs: Relief More Than Pain

Years of epidemiologic and interventional study have convincingly demonstrated that routine use of aspirin or other cyclooxygenase (COX) inhibitors reduces the risk of developing colorectal carcinoma in the general population. In patients with familial adenomatous polyposis, who have a markedly increased risk of colon cancer due to APC mutations, nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) objectively reduce the size and number of existing polyps, thereby mediating primary colon cancer chemoprevention. In murine models involving human gene-like APC mutations, concurrent COX-1 or COX-2 deficiency reduces the incidence of malignancy by 80%. NSAIDs also have been shown to decrease cancer-specific mortality after diagnosis, particularly for patients with tumors that overexpress the COX-2 isoform (90% of colorectal cancers). This finding suggests that beyond inducing colon cancers, COX activity (or some other effect mitigated by NSAIDs) also promotes tumor progression.

Many of the chemopreventive and chemotherapeutic effects of NSAIDs are attributable to direct COX inhibition. This makes sense in light of the fact that the COX-driven generation of prostaglandin E2 and thromboxane A2 can be tumorigenic, because these pro-inflammatory mediators also can influence epithelial proliferation, angiogenesis, and metastatic potential. Thus, it’s hardly surprising that the irreversible nonselective COX inhibitor aspirin, as well as the selective and reversible COX-2 inhibitors (e.g., celecoxib), provides therapeutic efficacy. Administration of ω3-polyunsaturated fatty acids (eicosapentaenoic acid), which are metabolized by COX to anti-inflammatory resolvins (at the expense of fewer pro-inflammatory metabolites), also is beneficial.

On the other hand, some of the NSAID benefits appear to be COX-independent. Celecoxib analogues or sulfone metabolites of sulindac that do not inhibit COX-1 or -2 also are effective in preventing tumors in mouse models. Thus, aspirin—and to a lesser extent the other NSAIDs—can inhibit NFκB activation, promote β-catenin degradation, and downregulate survivin, all leading to the apoptosis of neoplastic cells. Although these actions of aspirin and NSAIDs provide plausible explanation of their antineoplastic activity, they also raise the question of why this efficacy is restricted to colonic tumors.

Regardless of the actual mechanisms involved, it is only slightly ironic that such an old (and inexpensive) drug like aspirin can have such a salubrious payoff. Certainly in the setting of previous malignancy or existing high-risk APC mutations, the small risk of gastrointestinal hemorrhage from aspirin is far outweighed by the benefits. Moreover, if the true beneficial pathways can be elucidated, a drug lacking the existing NSAID side effects can’t be far behind.

Are all polyps neoplastic?
Show answer

No. Polyps can result from focal hyperplasia of the mucosa. Hyperplastic polyps do not have malignant potential.


Question 2
2
What variables determine the likelihood of malignant change in a polyp?
Show answer

Three interrelated features determine the risk of cancerous transformation: polyp size, histologic architecture, and severity of dysplasia. (1) Cancer is rare in tubular adenomas less than 1 cm in diameter. (2) The likelihood of cancer is high (about 50%) in sessile villous adenomas that are greater than 4 cm in diameter. (3) Severe dysplasia is likely to progress to cancer. Such dysplasias are found in villous areas. Of all these, size is the most important factor.

Colon, villous adenoma - Gross, mucosal surface

A villous adenoma is generally sessile, has a broad base, and may measure 10 cm in diameter. Villous adenomas have a propensity for the rectum and rectosigmoid colon. The presence of invasive carcinoma correlates with the size of the polyp.

Question 1
1
What types of mutations are likely to be present in such a lesion?
Show answer

There is progressive accumulation of mutations, which is called the “adenoma to carcinoma sequence.” In this scheme, mutations of the APC gene (resulting in homozygous loss of this tumor suppressor gene) are believed to occur first. (Patients with familial adenomatous polyposis syndrome are born with loss of one copy of the APC gene in all somatic cells.) As the adenomas enlarge, mutations in the RAS proto-oncogene and LOH 18q (Smad4) tumor suppressor genes occur. Eventually, mutations of TP53 and several other genes are superimposed. In this large sessile villous adenoma, it is likely that the APC, LOH 18q, and RAS genes have been affected.

Figure 17-49 Morphologic and molecular changes in the adenoma-carcinoma sequence. Loss of one normal copy of the tumor suppressor gene APC occurs early. Individuals born with one mutant allele are therefore at increased risk of developing colon cancer. Alternatively, inactivation of APC in colonic epithelium may occur later in life. This is the “first hit” according to the Knudson hypothesis ( Chapter 7 ). The loss of the intact second copy of APC follows (“second hit”). Other changes, including mutation of KRAS, losses at 18q21 involving SMAD2 and SMAD4 , and inactivation of the tumor suppressor gene TP53 , lead to the emergence of carcinoma, in which further mutations occur. Although there seems to be a temporal sequence of changes, the accumulation of mutations, rather than their occurrence in a specific order, is most critical.

Question 2
2
Is there a form of hereditary colonic cancer that is not associated with polyposis?
Show answer

Yes. Hereditary nonpolyposis colon carcinomas (HNPCC) arise in patients born with mutational loss of one copy of a DNA mismatch-repair gene. Although these individuals do not have polyposis (innumerable polyps), they do develop right-sided colon cancers, generally as a result of evolution through sessile serrated adenomas.

1
What is the typical presentation of patients with right-sided colon cancer?
Show answer

Right-sided colon cancers are polypoid, fungating masses that do not cause obstruction because of the large capacity of the cecum and ascending colon. As in this case scenario, they often manifest with iron-deficiency anemia. Sometimes the anemia causes vague symptoms, such as easy fatigability.

Colon, cecal adenocarcinoma - Gross, mucosal surface

Tumors in the proximal colon tend to grow as polypoid, fungating, ulcerating masses. Obstruction is uncommon. About 25% of colon carcinomas are located in the cecum or ascending colon. Cecal and right colon cancers most often come to clinical attention by the appearance of fatigue, weakness, and iron-deficiency anemia. Note the adjacent pedunculated adenomatous polyp. Most colon cancers develop from adenomatous polyps (the adenoma-carcinoma sequence).

Question 1
1
What is the significance of the patient's increased carcinoembryonic antigen (CEA) value 4 years after surgery?
Show answer

Colon carcinomas produce various tumor antigens (e.g., CEA) that can be detected in the blood. CEA is not specific for colon cancer; CEA levels may be elevated in patients with carcinomas of the lung, breast, ovary, urinary bladder, and prostate and in patients with numerous non-neoplastic disorders, including alcoholic cirrhosis, pancreatitis, and ulcerative colitis. Because elevated levels have little specificity, they are of greatest use to assess possible recurrence after resection of a primary tumor. If total removal has been accomplished, the serum CEA level disappears. Return of CEA positivity is a highly reliable indicator of recurrence of the primary neoplasm.
