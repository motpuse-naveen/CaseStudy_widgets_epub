Learning Objectives
  • Systemic Lupus eEythematosus (SLE)
    1. Describe the genetic, nongenetic, and immunologic factors in the etiology of SLE.
    2. Be familiar with criteria for the diagnosis of SLE.
    3. List common clinical manifestations of lupus (present in >50% of patients).
    4. Describe the role of antinuclear antibodies (ANAs) and immune complexes in the pathogenesis of renal disease in SLE.
    5. Briefly describe the pathology in the joints, central nervous system, serosal surfaces, heart, and lung.
    6. Describe the long-term prognosis of lupus.
    7. Define chronic discoid LE, subacute cutaneous LE, and drug-induced LE.
  • Laboratory Diagnosis of Connective Tissue Diseases
    1. Know five ANA patterns, including the significance of nucleolar and centromere patterns, the importance of DNA and Sm antibodies in SLE, the importance of low serum complement levels in SLE versus the other connective tissue diseases, and the sensitivity of U1-RNP antibodies for mixed connective tissue disease.
    2. Select and interpret the appropriate autoantibody and complement tests for SLE, rheumatoid arthritis, scleroderma, dermatomyositis-polymyositis, Sjogren syndrome, and MCTD.
    3. List the diseases associated with the depression of complement (C3 or C4 or both) and indicate changes in C4 and C3 associated with each of these diseases.

Scenario

A 20-year-old woman presented with complaints of fever for 2 weeks; pleuritic chest pain; and intermittent joint pain in her hands, feet, and knees. On examination, she was free of skin lesions. She had a pleural friction rub; a slightly swollen tender right knee; and enlarged axillary, cervical, and inguinal lymph nodes. A chest film showed a small right pleural effusion; joint films were normal except for a small amount of soft tissue swelling in the right knee. Hemoglobin was 10 g/dL, and urinalysis revealed 4+ proteinuria with white blood cells, red blood cells, and red cell casts in the urine sediment. A VDRL test was positive. A test for antitreponemal antibodies was negative. Blood urea nitrogen (BUN) and creatinine were slightly elevated, and moderate hypoalbuminemia and hypergammaglobulinemia were present. An antinuclear antibody (ANA) test was positive at a titer of 1:2560 and was reported as showing a speckled pattern. A double stranded DNA (dsDNA) antibody determination by the Crithidia luciliae assay was positive at 1:320, and an extractable nuclear antigen (ENA) antibody determination was reported as RNP (U1-RNP) and Sm antibody present. A C4 level was less than 10 mg/dL (reference range 12-45 mg/dL), and a C3 level was less than 40 mg/dL (reference range 88-192 mg/dL). The patient was treated with prednisone. After 4 weeks, the urine protein was 2+, the sediment had cleared, and BUN and serum creatinine had returned toward normal.

Image 1

Skin, malar (butterfly) rash of systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) - Clinical presentation

This is the typical malar rash of SLE. The butterfly area refers to the bridge of the nose and cheeks. The rash also frequently involves the trunk and extremities. The rash is often incited or exacerbated by sunlight or other stresses that cause increased circulation to the skin. Other skin signs may include blisters, maculopapular lesions, urticaria, and ulcerations.

9. Hematologic disorder Hemolytic anemia: With reticulocytosis, or

Leukopenia: <4.0 × 109 cells/L (4000 cells/mm3) total on two or more occasions, or

Lymphopenia: <1.5 × 109 cells/L (1500 cells/mm3) on two or more occasions, or

Thrombocytopenia: <100 × 109 cells/L (100 × 103 cells/mm3) in the absence of offending drugs

Anti-Sm: Presence of antibody to Sm nuclear antigen, or

Positive finding of antiphospholipid antibodies based on (1) an abnormal serum level of IgG or IgM anticardiolipin antibodies, (2) a positive test for lupus anticoagulant using a standard test, or (3) a false-positive serologic test for syphilis known to be positive for at least 6 months and confirmed by negative Treponema pallidum immobilization or fluorescent treponemal antibody absorption test
11. Antinuclear antibody An abnormal titer of antinuclear antibody by immunofluorescence or an equivalent assay at any point in time and in the absence of drugs known to be associated with drug-induced lupus syndrome

Question 1
1
Considering the etiology of the symptoms in this disease, characterize what might be seen histopathologically in the skin.
Show answer

The skin in lupus shows apoptotic keratinocytes, vacuolar degeneration of the basal layer of the epidermis, a thickened basement membrane, and perivascular mononuclear infiltrates in the dermis.


Image 2

Kidney, chronic lupus nephritis - Gross

Note the nodular-to-granular appearance of the cortex. The effect is distributed across the entire organ, suggesting a diffuse process. Consider the chronic mechanism that leads to the granular surface. Remember the vital target structure that the kidney represents in systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE).

Question 1
1
Why did this patient have a positive VDRL test?
Show answer

The VDRL test detects antibodies against the phospholipid cardiolipin. Patients with syphilis develop antibodies against cardiolipin, and this test is positive in many cases of syphilis. In lupus, patients develop many types of autoantibodies, including some that react with phospholipids such as cardiolipin. Antibodies specific for Treponema pallidum are absent, however. In SLE, a positive VDRL test is referred to as biologically false-positive for syphilis. Lupus patients (or any patients) who have antiphospholipid antibodies (“antiphospholipid antibody syndrome”) have increased risk for thrombosis and for fetal loss, perhaps because of binding of the antibodies to phospholipids of vascular endothelium.

Question 1
1
What are the major cells involved in the proliferative process?
Show answer

The cells that proliferate are mesangial cells and endothelial cells. Hypercellularity is also contributed to by neutrophil infiltration. In some cases (not this one) with severe glomerular injury, the epithelial cells lining the Bowman capsule may also proliferate.


Question 2
2
What is the inciting event leading to this proliferation?
Show answer

The proliferation is in response to cellular injury mediated by the deposition of antigen-antibody complexes in the mesangium and on the basement membrane.

Kidney, lupus nephritis - High power

The spectrum of histopathologic changes in lupus nephritis is extremely wide, and this is complicated by the fact that many changes are sequential and often not synchronized in all glomeruli. In progressive disease, the end result is the destruction of glomeruli. One sees elements of early destruction here. Fibrinoid necrosis is marked by loss of nuclei, loss of normal glomerular constituents, and the presence of scattered neutrophils and karyorrhectic debris. Crescents may also be seen in lupus nephritis.

Question 1
1
What is the significance of antinuclear antibody (ANA) tests in this patient?
Show answer

Positive tests for ANA, although not specific for lupus, are highly sensitive for the diagnosis of systemic lupus erythematosis (SLE), being positive in greater than 95% of patients. Antibodies specific for lupus include anti-dsDNA and anti-Sm antigen. The combination of these two, as seen in this patient, supports the diagnosis of SLE.

Kidney, systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE), glomerulus with immune complexes shown by immunofluorescence (IF) - High power

Note the lumpy-bumpy fluorescence in glomerular basement membranes. Frozen tissue sections of kidney are overlaid with an antibody (e.g., against IgG) that has been labeled with a fluorescent tag. Question 1
1
What is the IF staining pattern in SLE?
Show answer

SLE typically displays “full house” IF staining for IgG, IgA, IgM, C3, and C1q.


Question 2
2
What might be responsible for the irregular lumpy distribution of IgG in SLE compared with the next Image 8 (Goodpasture disease)?
Show answer

In SLE, immune complexes formed in the circulation or in situ are deposited along the basement membrane. These appear as discrete humps. By contrast, in Goodpasture disease, the antibodies are directed against the basement membrane itself, and they deposit all along its surface. These produce a linear pattern.


Question 3
3
Can you relate the low complement levels seen in this patient to the pathogenesis of SLE?
Show answer

SLE is an immune complex disease in which immune complexes deposited in tissues activate complement. Complement is consumed, and its levels in the serum are reduced. Complement levels tend to return to normal when the disease enters spontaneous or therapy-induced remission.


Image 8

Kidney, Goodpasture disease, glomerulus with immunofluorescence (IF) - High power

The pattern of immunofluorescence (IF) shown here can be compared with that seen in systemic lupus erythmatosis (SLE) (previous Image 7). Note the smooth, linear distribution of the antibody here compared with the irregular pattern in SLE.

Question 1
1
What is the mechanism of disease in Goodpasture disease?
Show answer

In Goodpasture disease, the antibody is directed expressly against an antigen in the basement membrane intrinsic to the kidney itself.


Question 2
2
Contrast the type of immune hypersensitivity reactions underlying the renal disease in SLE and Goodpasture disease. Correlate this to the IF staining pattern.
Show answer

Circulating immune complex deposition in the capillary loops in SLE is a type III hypersensitivity reaction and results in granular IF staining. Direct auto-antibody binding of basement membrane proteins in Goodpasture disease is a type II hypersensitivity reaction and results in linear IF staining.

Lungs with chronic pleuritis, interstitial pneumonitis, and fibrosis in systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) - Gross

SLE is a systemic disease. Multiple organ systems are affected. Although serositis is a common occurrence in SLE, interstitial changes can also be present. 1
What is responsible for the opacity of the pleura?
Show answer

Fibrosis of the pleura causes its opacity.


Question 2
2
What would you expect the duration of this process to have been?
Show answer

Because there is fibrosis, the process must have been chronic.


Question 3
3
What other serosal surface might be of clinical importance in SLE?
Show answer

Pericardium could be involved, producing a fibrinous pericarditis.


Question 4
4
With what clinical symptom might a patient with serositis present?
Show answer

Pleural inflammation (serositis) causes chest pain accentuated by breathing. On auscultation, there is a pleural friction rub.

In this case, the immune complexes are formed in the circulation and deposited in tissue. In other circumstances, the antigen may first become planted in a tissue (e.g., glomerular basement membrane), and the antibodies then bind to the antigen. This is called in situ immune complex formation.


Question 1
1
Can you name a situation in which immune complex formation occurs in the wall of the blood vessel?
Show answer

In the Arthus reaction, immune complexes form in the vessel wall. Morphologically, polyarteritis nodosa resembles an Arthus reaction.
