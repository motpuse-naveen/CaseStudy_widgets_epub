Scenario

A 62-year-old man was admitted to the hospital with a chief complaint of "shortness of breath." For several months before to admission, the patient had noted a gradual onset of weakness and fatigue and a 15-lb weight loss. When he became short of breath on climbing one flight of stairs, he came to the emergency department. In the emergency department, a chest x-ray revealed diffuse interstitial markings. He denied a history of smoking and exposure to beryllium, irradiation, coal dust, or asbestos. Oral temperature was 37° C, blood pressure was 120/80 mm Hg, pulse was 80 beats/min, and respirations were 20/min. The remainder of the physical examination was unremarkable. Laboratory data were as follows: WBC and differential counts were normal. Hematocrit, hemoglobin, urinalysis, and a serum chemistry profile were normal. Pulmonary function tests showed diminished total lung capacity (TLC) and forced vital capacity (FVC), decreased diffusing capacity, and mildly decreased forced expiratory volume at 1 second (FEV1).

The patient had a dry cough and was unable to produce sputum. His PPD skin test response was negative at 48 hours. An open-lung biopsy was performed. Over the ensuing few years, the patient's lung function progressively deteriorated, and he eventually required continuous oxygen. He died, and an autopsy was performed.

Lungs, diffuse interstitial pulmonary fibrosis - Radiograph

Numerous conditions causing interstitial fibrosis can manifest with this radiographic picture. There is a diffuse increase in linear markings, which are particularly prominent at the lung base. If an etiologic agent is not found for this condition, as in this case, the patient is said to have idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis.

1
How does the radiographic appearance of the lung differ from that in a patient with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease with emphysema?
Show answer

In emphysema, the lungs are often hypertranslucent and the diaphragm is depressed.


Image 2

Lung, idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis - Gross, cut surface

This piece of lung shows numerous cystic spaces outlined by dense yellow-white fibrosis, resulting from gradual destruction of the normal lung architecture. This pattern is referred to as honeycomb lung.

Question 1
1
Compare and contrast the features of this image with the features of emphysema.
Show answer

Fibrosis is not a feature of emphysema. In pulmonary fibrosis, the dilated airspaces are rigid because they frequently are surrounded by fibrosis. In contrast to pulmonary fibrosis, the dilated air spaces in emphysema collapse.


Image 3

Lung, idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF) - Low power

IPF is a clinicopathologic syndrome characterized by progressive interstitial pulmonary fibrosis and respiratory failure. The histologic correlate of IPF is a pattern of fibrosis (seen here) referred to as usual interstitial pneumonia (UIP). UIP is both a disease entity and a pattern of injury seen in other interstitial lung diseases. This pattern of injury consists of patchy interstitial fibrosis that results in markedly thickened alveolar septa containing chronic inflammatory cells. Some of the septa have a normal thickness. The fibrotic lesions exhibit “temporal heterogeneity,” meaning that there are fibroblastic lesions of different ages present. The pulmonary vessels show sclerotic thickening as a result of hypoxia and subsequent pulmonary hypertension. Small thromboemboli are present in some of the small vessels, but there are no infarcts.

How are interstitial lung diseases categorized?
Show answer

Chronic interstitial pulmonary diseases are a heterogeneous group of disorders characterized predominantly by inflammation and fibrosis of the pulmonary inter¬stitium. Many of the entities are of unknown cause and pathogenesis, and some have an intra-alveolar as well as an interstitial component. There is frequent overlap in histologic features among the different conditions. Question 1
1
What pathogenetic mechanisms are believed to cause pulmonary interstitial fibrosis?
Show answer

The mechanism of pulmonary fibrosis is not entirely understood. In pneumoconiosis, the most important mechanism relates to toxicity of the inhaled dust to alveolar macrophages, which are stimulated to release chemotactic factors that attract additional macrophages and neutrophils to the alveolus. Stimulated resident macrophages and recruited monocytes and neutrophils release proteases and oxidants that damage parenchymal cells and connective tissue. Macrophages are a source of fibronectin and growth factors, both of which contribute to proliferation of fibroblasts and deposition of collagen. In diseases such as sarcoidosis, hypersensitivity pneumonitis, and berylliosis, T cells are important mediators of the inflammatory response, and they release cytokines that stimulate macrophages to release mediators as described. IPF is likely a multifactorial disorder initiated by environmental exposure in patients with genetic predisposition and aberrant mechanisms of repairing recurrent alveolar epithelial cell injuries.


Figure 15-13 Proposed pathogenic mechanisms in idiopathic pulmonary fi brosis. Environmental factors that are potentially injurious to alveolar epithelium interact with genetic or aging-related factors that place epithelium at risk to create a persistent epithelial injury. Factors secreted from injured/activated epithelium, possibly augmented by factors released from innate and adaptive immune cells responding to “danger” signals produced by damaged epithelium, activate interstitial fi broblasts. There is some evidence that these activated fi broblasts exhibit signaling abnormalities that lead to increased signaling through the PI3K/AKT pathway. The activated fi broblasts synthesize and deposit collagen, leading to interstitial fi brosis and eventual respiratory failure.


Question 2
2
How can the illustrated changes in the lung explain the patient's pulmonary function tests?
Show answer

The thickened septa restrict the ability of the lungs to expand (decreased FVC) and block diffusion of oxygen and CO2 between alveoli and capillaries. They do not impede airflow significantly, however (only mildly decreased FEV1).

Question 1
1
What are the functions of type I and type II pneumocytes?
Show answer

Type I pneumocytes are involved in gaseous exchange. Type II pneumocytes have many functions, the best known being surfactant production. They are also the precursors of alveolar type I pneumocytes, are involved in the metabolism and detoxification of inhaled pollutants, and are able to communicate with immune cells.


Image 6

Lungs, silicosis - Radiograph

This x-ray shows multiple small miliary densities throughout both lung fields and two large nodules in the mid-right lung. These nodular densities represent progressive massive fibrosis. Progressive massive fibrosis can occur as a component of any of the pneumoconioses and represents more severe disease. Typically, silicosis is more prominent in the apices of the lungs.

What occupations are associated with silicosis?
Show answer

Silicosis is the most prevalent chronic occupational disease in the world. Many occupations are associated with silicosis risk, including sandblasting and many forms of mining.

Image 7

Lung, silicotic nodule - Low power

Silicosis develops after many years of inhalation of crystalline silicon dioxide (silica). In its early stages, it consists of multiple tiny nodules, which grow larger and contain considerable scar tissue. Large nodules may undergo central softening and cavitation. As the silicotic nodules increase in size and number, several smaller ones may coalesce to form large hyalinized nodules containing abundant scar tissue.

Question 1
1
How do these changes compare with those found in asbestosis?
Show answer

The pulmonary fibrosis of asbestosis is characterized by the presence of asbestos bodies and the absence of large nodules. Striking pleural changes are often present in asbestosis.

1
Which of these changes are due to smoking, asbestos, or both?
Show answer

Pleural plaque is due to asbestos, linear fibrosis is due to asbestos, emphysema is due to smoking, and carcinoma is due to both.


Question 2
2
What types of malignant tumors are associated with asbestos exposure?
Show answer

Lung carcinoma, mesotheliomas (usually pleural, but occasionally peritoneal), and laryngeal and other extrapulmonary neoplasms (rare) are associated with asbestos exposure. The most commonly occurring cancer related to asbestos exposure is lung carcinoma, not mesothelioma.

What histologic type of tumor likely developed in this patient?

Because of its peripheral location, this tumor is more likely to be an adenocarcinoma.

Asbestos is a family of related fibers formed from crystalline hydrated silicates. Occupational exposure to asbestos results in pleural thickening (usually localized, occasionally generalized), and interstitial pulmonary fibrosis. Eventually, malignant tumors may develop.

Question 1
1
Does smoking increase the chances of developing the malignancies associated with asbestos?
Show answer

Smoking increases the risk of lung carcinoma but not of mesothelioma. Asbestos workers who smoke have 10 times the risk of lung cancer compared to with asbestos workers who do not smoke.

Image 11
Asbestos body

Lung, asbestos body - High power

An asbestos body is being engulfed by a macrophage. Although many asbestos bodies are engulfed by macrophages, macrophages are unable to dispose of them and initiate inflammatory and fibrogenic mediators. Failure of macrophages to dispose of asbestos fibers plays a major role in their pathogenicity.

Question 1
1
What are the components of an asbestos body?
Show answer

Asbestos bodies appear as golden brown fusiform or beaded rods with a translucent center; they are asbestos fibers coated with an iron-containing proteinaceous material.


Question 2
2
What happens to the fiber after ingestion by the macrophage?
Show answer

The macrophages coat the fibers with ferritin and other proteinaceous materials because the macrophages cannot digest the fibers.


Image 12

Lung, asbestos body - Very high power

Characteristically, asbestos bodies have knobbed ends and beading along their lengths. The golden color is due to iron-containing protein that wraps the fiber. Other inorganic particles also may become coated with iron-protein complexes. Collectively, these (and asbestos bodies) are called ferruginous bodies.

Question 1
1
What symptoms occur in patients with sarcoidosis?
Show answer

Frequently patients have no symptoms. Cough and dyspnea would reflect pulmonary involvement. Fever, fatigue, and weight loss may be present. Involvement of lacrimal and salivary glands may cause dryness of the eyes and mouth. Skin lesions in the form of plaques or nodules may be present.

Question 1
1
What does the presence of granulomas suggest regarding the possible pathogenesis of sarcoidosis?
Show answer

Sarcoidosis remains a disease of unknown etiology. The presence of granulomas suggests chronic exposure to some agent that persists for a lengthy period, however, either an infectious agent or some noninfectious particulate material. Such agents probably cause a delayed hypersensitivity reaction and, consequently, granuloma formation. In support of this hypothesis, lungs in sarcoidosis contain high levels of CD4+ T cells in the lung that secrete TH1-dependent cytokines such as IFN-γ and IL-2 locally.


Question 2
2
What special procedures must be done before making a histologic diagnosis of sarcoidosis?
Show answer

Although various tests for sarcoidosis are used, none is specifically diagnostic. Frequently, sarcoidosis is a clinicopathologic diagnosis of exclusion for noncaseating granulomas without an infectious cause or history suggestive of pneumoconiosis. Before making a diagnosis of sarcoidosis, tests such as stains and culture for fungi and Mycobacterium tuberculosis must be done to exclude a specific cause for granuloma formation.

Question 1
1
Are there extrapulmonary lesions in sarcoidosis? What organs are commonly involved?
Show answer

Although almost any organ may be involved, common sites include the eye, salivary glands, skin, spleen, and liver.
