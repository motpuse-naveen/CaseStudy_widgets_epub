Learning Objectives
  • Seborrheic Keratoses
    • 1. Describe seborrheic keratoses in terms of their occurrence (frequency, age, and localization) and outcome.
    • 2. Describe the gross and histologic features of seborrheic keratoses.
    • 3. Know what factor is thought to be responsible for the numerous seborrheic keratoses sometimes seen as a paraneoplastic syndrome.
  • Epithelial Cysts
    • 1. Describe the pathogenesis of epithelial cysts and where they are usually located.
    • 2. Describe the differences between an epidermal inclusion cyst, a pilar cyst, and a dermoid cyst in terms of histology and occurrence.
  • Adnexal Tumors
    • 1. Describe the different normal adnexal structures in the skin.
    • 2. Know the common tumors arising from these adnexal structures.
    • 3. Describe the clinical significance of these adnexal tumors.

Scenario

A 55-year-old man sees his physician because of a firm, light brown papule on his back. The lesion is round, has a diameter of 14 mm, and a surface 3 mm above the surrounding skin level. The patient worries that he might have a malignant melanoma. There is no bleeding, itching, or pain.

Image 1
Dark brown lesions with greasy-appearing, granular surface
Smaller tan lesions

Skin, seborrheic keratoses - Clinical presentation

The largest lesion is dark brown, and the characteristic greasy-appearing, granular surface is clearly elevated above the surrounding skin level. The surrounding smaller lesions vary in color from light to deep brown. Over a period of years, seborrheic keratosis may become numerous, particularly on the trunk.

Question 1
1
What must be considered when numerous seborrheic keratoses occur explosively?
Show answer

The occurrence of multiple seborrheic keratoses over a short time is called the sign of Leser-Trélat, a paraneoplastic syndrome seen in association with carcinomas of the gastrointestinal tract. This is most likely caused by the production of transforming growth factor–alpha by malignant cells.


Image 2
Epidermis of normal thickness
Thickened epidermis
Elevation above the surrounding skin

Skin, seborrheic keratosis - Very low power

This characteristic low-power magnification of a seborrheic keratosis shows a thickened epidermis, elevating the lesion above the surrounding skin.

Question 1
1
What molecular changes are present in seborrheic keratoses?
Show answer

Activating mutations in FGFR3, a receptor tyrosine kinase, are commonly found in seborrheic keratoses and are thought to promote tumor growth.


Image 3
Papillary dermis
Small, inconspicuous keratinocytes
Pseudohorn cyst

Skin, seborrheic keratosis – Medium power

The lesion consists of a thickened (acanthotic) epidermis composed of epithelial cells with scant cytoplasm resembling the immature squamous cells of the epidermal basal layer (basaloid cells). Small cystlike structures containing lamellated pink keratin are present within this proliferation (pseudohorn cysts).

Question 1
1
Do the cells of a sebborrheic keratosis resemble the cells of an adnexal structure?
Show answer

The name seborrheic keratosis may suggest an origin from sebaceous glands, but this is incorrect. The term seborrheic refers to the clinical appearance of the lesions. The cells of a seborrheic keratosis most resemble basal cells of the normal epidermis.


Question 2
2
What is the difference between true epithelial cysts and the "pseudohorn cysts" of seborrheic keratosis?
Show answer

In a true epithelial cyst, the cystic cavity does not have any connection to the epithelial surface. In contrast, in a seborrheic keratosis, the pseudohorn cysts are actually epithelial invaginations. They appear as cysts only because of the plane of tissue sectioning.


Image 4
Well-circumscribed, firm nodule

Skin, epithelial cyst – Clinical presentation

Epithelial cysts are common lesions, presenting as well-circumscribed, firm nodules. Traumatic rupture may cause pain. Epidermal inclusion cysts can be very small and multiple.

Question 1
1
What is the name of multiple, very small, epidermal inclusion cysts, usually occurring on the faces of young individuals?
Show answer

Multiple, small, epidermal inclusion cysts are called milia.


Image 5
Dermis
Epithelial lining
Strands of keratin

Skin, epithelial cyst – Low power

This cyst is filled with strands of loose "basket weave" keratin and lined by epithelium that resembles normal epidermis. Adnexal structures are absent.

Question 1
1
Which histologic type of epithelial cyst is this?
Show answer

This is an epidermal inclusion cyst. This epithelial cyst is lined by epithelium resembling normal epidermis and filled with keratin.


Question 2
2
How do the histologic findings change if the cyst ruptures?
Show answer

Rupture causes keratin leakage, which incites an extensive inflammatory reaction with granuloma formation.


Question 3
3
Name three other histologic types of epidermal cysts.
Show answer

Pilar or trichilemmal cysts are lined by epithelium resembling follicular epithelium and are filled with homogeneous compact keratin lacking a granular layer. A dermoid cyst is a cyst lined by squamous epithelium with adnexal structures. A steatocystoma is an uncommon epithelial cyst lined by squamous epithelium with sebaceous glands.


Image 6
Multiple small flesh-colored papules

Skin, syringomas – Clinical presentation

This woman has multiple syringomas, which typically manifest as flesh-colored small papules surrounding the eye.

Question 1
1
To which group of tumors do syringomas belong?
Show answer

Syringomas are a type of benign adnexal (appendage) tumor. They are neoplasms with differentiation toward the ducts of the eccrine sweat gland.


Image 7
Ear
Large, partly coalescing nodules on the scalp

Scalp, cylindromas – Clinical presentation

A cylindroma is another example of a benign adnexal tumor. These tumors are most likely of apocrine or eccrine derivation and usually occur on the forehead and scalp. Coalescence of tumors on the head may cause a hatlike growth.

Question 1
1
When the scalp is covered completely by cylindromas, what is this called?
Show answer

When the scalp is covered completely by cylindromas, it is referred to as a turban tumor.
