Scenario

A 75-year-old man presented with a 6-month history of early satiety. He reported that he had experienced upper abdominal discomfort for many years. Physical examination revealed mild epigastric tenderness. Esophagogastroduodenoscopy showed a large ulcerated mass involving the lesser curvature of the stomach. A biopsy specimen of the mass was taken, and, based on the specimen, a gastrectomy was performed.

Stomach, chronic atrophic gastritis - Low power

In normal gastric mucosa, the glands lie in a back-to-back configuration. In this atrophic mucosa, there is loss of gastric glands. In atrophic gastritis, intestinal metaplasia is often seen, characterized by the formation of intestinal-type epithelium with goblet cells. The mucin in the goblet cells stains pink with special stain (e.g., mucicarmine stain), which does not stain gastric-type epithelial cells. Patients with extensive atrophic gastritis are more likely to develop dysplasia, characterized by glands with closely packed, hyperchromatic, pseudostratified nuclei.

Question 1
1
Compare chronic atrophic gastritis with H. pylori–associated gastritis.
Show answer

Chronic atrophic gastritis represents less than 10% of chronic gastritis. Unlike H. pylori gastritis, it spares the antrum and is associated with hypergastrinemia. It is not due to an infectious cause but rather due to antibodies against parietal cells and intrinsic factor. It leads to decreased vitamin B12 absorption and subsequent pernicious anemia.

Gastric hyperplastic polyp - Gross

Most (90%) gastric polyps are non-neoplastic, either hyperplastic or fundic gland polyps. Most hyperplastic polyps are single, small, and sessile; however, some may approach 2 to 3 cm in size. Of polyps, 25% are multiple. The low-power view shows cystically dilated glands, characteristic of hyperplastic polyps. The high-power view shows cystically dilated glands lined by hyperplastic surface epithelium (foveolar epithelium). Although previously rare, the fundic gland polyp is now thought by some authors to be the most commonly encountered gastric polyp. The increase in incidence of this sporadic form is due to its association with proton pump inhibitor therapy. These polyps are believed to be benign except in the setting of familial adenomatous polyposis (nonsporadic), in which case dysplasia can occur. In fundic gland polyps, the underlying glandular component becomes hyperplastic. The result is cystically dilated, oxyntic glands that may be lined by parietal cells or chief cells or both.

Question 1
1

Show answer

Fundic gland polyps are commonly seen in patients receiving proton pump inhibitor therapy.

Stomach, adenomatous polyp - Gross, mucosal surface

Of gastric polyps, 5% to 10% are true neoplasms, and many undergo malignant transformation. Gastric adenomas are usually single, sessile, 3 to 4 cm in size, and located in the antrum. The low-power view shows the polypoid nature of the adenoma and the dark-staining adenomatous (dysplastic) epithelium. The higher power view shows the adenomatous glands lined by proliferative dysplastic epithelium, which has malignant potential. At the time of diagnosis, 40% of gastric adenomas contain a focus of carcinoma.

Question 1
1
In what pathologic background do gastric adenomas develop?
Show answer

Gastric adenomas almost always develop in the background of atrophic chronic gastritis and intestinal metaplasia.

This is an example of an early gastric carcinoma. An ulcer with surrounding nodularity is observed on the lesser curvature of a gastric resection specimen. Low-power histologic examination reveals that the tumor involves only the mucosa and submucosa; it fits the definition of early gastric carcinoma. Question 1
1
What is the 5-year survival rate for early gastric carcinoma?
Show answer

The 5-year survival is 90% to 95% after surgical resection.


Question 2
1
What mutations are present in sporadic intestinal-type gastric adenocarcinoma?
Show answer

Loss of function mutations in the APC gene, gain of function mutations in the gene encoding beta catenin, TGF beta mutations, and CDKN2A mutations have all been seen in sporadic gastric cancer.

Question 2
2
What type of carcinoma is this? What genetic mutation characterizes this tumor type?
Show answer

This is a diffuse signet-ring type carcinoma. The loss of E-cadherin expression through a number of different mechanisms is a key step in the development of this tumor. Of note, E-cadherin – which promotes cell-cell junctions - is also lost in lobular breast cancer. Both tumors share a similar discohesive signet-ring morphology.

Stomach, gastric lymphoma (MALToma) - Gross, mucosal surface

In this image, the gastric folds are enlarged and thickened. There is mural thickening with effacement of the overlying mucosal folds and focal ulceration. A histologic section shows a dense lymphoid infiltrate. The lymphocytes have a tendency to invade the epithelium, producing lymphoepithelial lesions, a characteristic feature of this type of lymphoma. The lymphoepithelial lesions are better visualized with a cytokeratin stain.
