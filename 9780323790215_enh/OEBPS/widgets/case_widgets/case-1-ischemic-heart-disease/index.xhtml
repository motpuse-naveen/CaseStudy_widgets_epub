Ischemic Heart Disease

Learning Objectives

Atherosclerosis

  • 1. Understand the general properties of vascular endothelial and smooth muscle cells. Understand how these cells respond to injury.
  • 2. Describe the natural history of coronary artery atherosclerosis and the associated clinical syndromes. Know what level of obstruction defines critical stenosis.
  • 3. List four key risk factors for atherosclerosis. Understand how atherosclerotic lesions cause clinical events.
  • 4. Describe the basic structure of an atherosclerotic plaque. Describe the gross and microscopic appearance of atherosclerotic plaques.
  • 5. List factors that are important in the initiation and growth of atherosclerotic plaques. Understand the roles of endothelial cells, smooth muscle cells, macrophages, and platelets in plaque formation.
  • 6. List the ways that lipoprotein oxidation contributes to the formation of atherosclerotic plaques.
  • 7. Compare and contrast atherosclerosis, Mönckeberg medial calcific sclerosis, and arteriolosclerosis.

Ischemic Heart Disease

  • 1. Review the blood supply of the heart. Know what myocardial regions are supplied by each coronary artery and how to determine which coronary artery is dominant.
  • 2. Define ischemia. List causes of impaired oxygen supply and increased oxygen demand.
  • 3. List the clinical syndromes associated with ischemic heart disease. Understand the role of the following:
    • a. fixed coronary artery obstruction
    • b. coronary artery thrombosis
    • c. acute plaque change
    • d. vasospasm
  • 4. Know the morphologic changes associated with:
    • a. stable angina
    • b. unstable angina
    • c. variant angina
    • d. myocardial infarction (MI)
  • 5. Describe the pathologic correlates of sudden death.
  • 6. Define acute MI. List the sequential effects of ischemia. Describe the timing of the wave front of irreversible myocardial damage after total coronary artery occlusion and the implications for the success of thrombolytic interventions.
  • 7. Know how long it takes for a typical infarct to be grossly recognizable to a trained pathologist. Know how long it takes before histopathologic changes are recognizable in an infarct.
  • 8. Describe how an evolving MI can be altered by thrombolysis and reperfusion. Describe the common pathologic changes in reperfused infarcts.
  • 9. Compare and contrast transmural versus subendocardial infarction.
  • 10. Compare and contrast tests of creatine kinase (CK) and troponin with regard to timing, sensitivity, and specificity in acute MI. Describe how sequential tests for CK-MB are used in the clinical setting to rule out acute MI. Understand when CK-MB is unreliable in ruling out acute MI. Describe the major tissue sources of the three CK isoenzymes (CK-MM, CK-MB, and CK-BB).
  • 11. Describe the specificity and time course of troponin as it relates to the diagnosis of acute MI.
  • 12. List important complications of acute MI. Know when the patient is at greatest risk for myocardial rupture.

Scenario

A 40-year-old diabetic woman was evaluated in the emergency department for chest pain. She had a history of hypertension and a 30-pack-year smoking history. Her medications included antihypertensives and cholesterol-lowering agents. She had a prior admission several years ago for a small, uncomplicated, myocardial infarct. She had had angina for many years, averaging one bout of angina a week. Her usual angina lasted 10 to 15 minutes and was relieved by nitroglycerin. A cardiologist attempted angioplasty several years ago. This procedure relieved her symptoms for 6 months, but eventually exercise-induced angina returned. There were no clinical changes until 2 weeks before her emergency department admission, when she began having daily anginal attacks that lasted more than 30 minutes. In the hour before her admission, she had awakened with severe chest pain, nausea, and dyspnea. There had been severe unrelenting pain for 45 minutes, and it had not been relieved by nitroglycerin. Vital signs were heart rate 105 beats/min, blood pressure 100/50 mm Hg (usual was about 155/95 mm Hg), and temperature 100° F. She was obese and diaphoretic (sweating profusely) with pale skin and labored respirations. Rales were heard over both lung fields. An ECG and serial cardiac markers were ordered.

Serial cardiac serum marker study - Laboratory values

Serum creatine kinase (CK) is only slightly elevated on admission. The time course of CK elevation is consistent with that expected for infarct-related release of CK. CK-MB increases and decreases in parallel with CK, as does the CK index, with a peak at 24 hours. In this case, total CK and CK-MB seem to peak at the same time. In most cases, CK-MB peaks about 6 hours before total CK and returns to normal levels about 12 to 24 hours before total CK. Elevated cardiac troponins, particularly cardiac troponins I and T, are highly specific markers of myocardial injury. Note that troponin levels rise in parallel with CK-MB and reach a maximum at the same time that CK-MB and the CK index peak. In contrast, troponin levels remain persistently elevated after CK-MB and the CK index have returned to normal.

CK index - Graph

The CK and CK index rapidly increase together in a time course consistent with acute myocardial infarction (MI). Even at 8 hours, the laboratory suggests myocardial injury. CK index is a calculated parameter that evaluates the amount of CK-MB relative to the amount of total CK. In MI, CK-MB makes up a high percentage of total CK, so the CK index increases with CK-MB. In skeletal muscle injury, CK can be very high, but CK-MB is only a tiny portion of the total CK, so the CK index remains.

1
What are some of the clinical manifestations associated with the presence of an unstable atheromatous plaque?
Show answer

Clinical manifestations associated with the presence of an unstable atheromatous plaque include unstable angina, acute myocardial infarction (MI), and sudden cardiac death.


Question 2
2
What kind of angina did this patient have in the week before she presented? Compare the pathology of stable angina and unstable angina. What events may have occurred in her coronary arteries?
Show answer

This patient has a long history of stable angina. The pathology of stable angina is that of a tight (>70%) atherosclerotic plaque in a major epicardial coronary artery. The character of her angina seems to have changed dramatically in the weeks before her admission. Her symptoms suggest she has developed unstable angina. Unstable angina is often pre-MI angina and reflects some type of acute plaque change in many cases. Acute coronary artery occlusion and subsequent MI are often precipitated by some change in a stenotic but stable atherosclerotic plaque. The change may be rupture of a plaque or hemorrhage into a plaque. If the thrombogenic lipid core comes into contact with blood, a thrombus may form acutely and completely occlude the artery, leading to acute MI. Studies suggest that some patients with unstable angina have a component of coronary artery spasm. Arterial spasm is also important in variant angina (Prinzmetal angina).

Targeted Therapy Box: Statins: Not just about cholesterol anymore

Statins: Not Just About Cholesterol Anymore

The liver controls plasma cholesterol levels through three interrelated mechanisms:

  • Hepatocytes clear cholesterol-rich low-density lipoprotein (LDL) from the plasma through cell surface LDL receptors, which release cholesterol into the cytoplasm.
  • Intracellular cholesterol in turn negatively regulates the activity of steroid regulatory element-binding protein-1 (SREBP-1), a cholesterol-sensitive transcription factor that normally enhances the expression of LDL receptors and the enzyme 3-hydroxymethyl-3-glutaryl (HMG)-CoA (HMG-CoA) reductase.
  • HMG-CoA reductase carries out the rate-limiting step in cholesterol synthesis; thus, when intracellular cholesterol levels fall, SREBP-1 drives the synthesis of HMG-CoA reductase, and cholesterol synthesis rises.

Statins belong to a widely used class of drugs that reduce cholesterol synthesis by inhibiting HMG-CoA reductase. Through the feedback loops just outlined, this increases the activity of SREBP-1 and the synthesis of LDL receptors, leading to increased clearance of LDL cholesterol from the plasma. Statins typically yield 10% to 50% reduction in plasma LDL. This translates into up to 60% reduction in cardiac events and 20% reduction in stroke in persons deemed to be at risk due to elevated levels of LDL cholesterol.

Of note, however, the cardiovascular benefits of statins consistently exceed the benefits anticipated on the basis of the extent of cholesterol lowering. For example, hypercholesterolemia has no statistically significant association with stroke, yet clinical studies have consistently shown that statin treatment reduces the incidence of stroke. Moreover, recent studies showed that stains reduce the incidence of clinically significant cardiovascular disease even in individuals with normal or very low cholesterols levels.

The foregoing observations suggest that protective mechanisms other than cholesterol reduction are afoot. As it turns out, HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors also block the formation of lipid precursors involved in the prenylation of a number of important intracellular proteins. Thus, the functions of both Ras (a key signaling molecule) and Rab (a regulator of membrane protein transport) depend on prenylation, which promotes the membrane association of these proteins. Through these and other less well-characterized “off-cholesterol” mechanisms, statins have a number of beneficial effects, including increased expression of the transcription factor KLF-2, which has anti-inflammatory effects in T cells and endothelial cells. Based on the important role in inflammation in atherosclerosis, it is easy to imagine how these effects of statins could contribute to a lowering of risk for cardiovascular events.

Statin therapy is not without side effects: A low incidence of rhabdomyolysis (severe autocatabolism of skeletal muscle) has been reported, along with hepatic metabolic derangements. These effects also are independent of its cholesterol-lowering activities and may be related to the activation of ubiquitin and proteasome degradation pathways.

Question 3
3
What are some of the complications that are encountered after acute myocardial infarction (MI)?
Show answer

The major complications of acute MI include the following: Common complications: Cardiac arrhythmias (75%-95%), sudden death (20%), cardiogenic shock (10%-15%), left ventricular aneurysm, and mural thrombosis (15%-40%); Uncommon complications: Myocardial rupture, papillary muscle rupture, reinfarction, infarct extension/expansion, postinfarction pericarditis, and Dressler syndrome.

Question 5
5
What factors determine infarct size? What factors determine the risk for post-MI complications?
Show answer

The factors that determine eventual infarct size and the factors that determine the risk for complications are nearly identical and include size of the myocardial bed at risk and its metabolic/oxygen needs, duration of coronary artery obstruction, coronary artery anatomy, dominance/collaterals, site of coronary artery obstruction, severity of obstruction, and rate at which obstruction developed.


Question 6
6
How much myocardium must a patient lose before signs and symptoms of cardiogenic shock manifest? What does this indicate about prognosis?
Show answer

Signs and symptoms of severe pump failure/cardiogenic shock generally occur with large infarcts (>40% of the left ventricle involved). Cardiogenic shock indicates a poor prognosis (70% mortality) and accounts for two-thirds of hospital deaths.

The wave front of myocardial necrosis - Schematic

Ultrastructural evidence of irreversible myocardial damage can be detected 20 to 40 minutes after occlusion of a major epicardial coronary artery. Wavy fiber change can be detected with the light microscope at 1 to 3 hours, and classic histologic features of necrosis are established by 4 to 12 hours. The subendocardial zones become irreversibly damaged first. There is progressive loss of the myocardial zone at risk over the next 24 hours, with most of the damage occurring in the first 4 to 6 hours.

Question 2

What would thrombolysis accomplish in this patient if it were given within 20 to 40 minutes of the patient's first symptoms? What would it accomplish if it were given 6 to 12 hours later? What would it accomplish if it were given 1 day later? What are the sequential effects of ischemia?
Show answer

Thrombolysis would be most beneficial if given within 30 minutes of the onset of symptoms, perhaps even preventing irreversible injury. Question 3
3
Is myocardial necrosis necessary to get arrhythmias?
Show answer

Irreversible myocardial damage is not necessary to get arrhythmias. The majority of survivors of sudden death resuscitated early in the course of events do not develop myocardial infarction. After a region of myocardium becomes ischemic, there is a change to anaerobic glycolysis within seconds. Over the following several minutes, myocardial pH decreases, and high-energy phosphates are depleted. Myocardial function is lost within minutes, and myocardial ionic pumps become unstable, leading to arrhythmias.

Image 7

Contraction bands
Neutrophils

Heart, contraction band necrosis - High power

Contraction bands are bright eosinophilic bands of condensed contractile proteins that run at right angles to the long axis of the cardiac myocyte. They form in zones of myocardium irreversibly injured by ischemia and then reperfused after angioplasty or clot-lysing drugs, such as tissue plasminogen activator. Hemorrhage and contraction bands are common in reperfused infarcts.

Figure 12-16 Effects of reperfusion on myocardial viability and function. Following coronary occlusion, contractile function is lost within 2 minutes and viability begins to diminish after approximately 20 minutes. If perfusion is not restored (A), then nearly all myocardium in the affected region suffers death. B, If flow is restored, then some necrosis is prevented, myocardium is salvaged, and at least some function can return. The earlier reperfusion occurs, the greater the degree of salvage. However, the process of reperfusion itself may induce some damage (reperfusion injury), and return of function of salvaged myocardium may be delayed for hours to days (postischemic ventricular dysfunction or stunning).

Infarct zone
Rupture channel

Heart, post—myocardial infarction (MI) rupture of left ventricular free wall - Gross, cut surface

A myocardial rupture has occurred at an interface between necrotic and spared myocardium. The infarct zone is pale. The zone of rupture is dark red-black. There is left ventricular hypertrophy except in the zone of infarct, which shows significant thinning. Myocardial rupture is an uncommon but catastrophic and rapidly fatal complication of acute MI. The pericardium rapidly fills with blood (hemopericardium). When the pericardium fills with blood, the right ventricle cannot fill, and the patient rapidly loses cardiac output. Hypertension, age 60 years or older, and female gender are risk factors for myocardial rupture.

Question 1
1
At what time after MI are patients at the greatest risk for rupture of an acute MI?
Show answer

Patients are at greatest risk during the first 3 to 7 days, when coagulative necrosis, neutrophilic infiltration, and lysis of the myocardial connective tissue have appreciably weakened the infarcted myocardium (mean, 4-5 days; range, 1-10 days).

Heart, left ventricular aneurysm with thrombus - Gross, endocardial surface

The aneurysm is a large oval zone located toward the apex of the left ventricle. An aneurysm forms when a region of the left ventricular wall is weakened by infarction and then thinned by the constant pressure of systolic contractions. Aneurysms at any site are often areas of abnormal flow and are predisposed to thrombosis. The aneurysm in this case has been filled by an organizing thrombus. Left ventricular thrombi can generate systemic emboli. Ventricular wall aneurysms may also cause congestive heart failure and dangerous arrhythmias.

CK index - Graph

CK index is useful in situations where there is extensive skeletal muscle injury, such as trauma and substance abuse. In patients with trauma from a motor vehicle accident, cardiac contusion (related to steering-wheel injury) may coexist with skeletal muscle injury.
