Scenario

A 45-year-old Caucasian male with little previous medical care presented to a clinic with complaints of fatigue, joint pain, and numbness in his feet. On physical exam the sclerae were yellow, and his skin was a bronze color despite denying spending much time in the sun. Spider angiomas were present on the abdomen.

Pertinent laboratory values included a fasting glucose level 180 mg/dL; albumin 2.9 g/dL; serum iron 187 ug/dL; ferritin 1144 ng/dL; saturation percentage of transferrin 96.3%; and total iron-binding capacity 194 ug/dL.

A subsequent CT scan showed a cirrhotic liver. A biopsy was performed that showed micronodular cirrhosis with increased iron stores on Prussian blue stain. The patient underwent liver transplantation.

Question 1
1
What is the difference between primary and secondary hemochromatosis (secondary iron overload or hemosiderosis), and what is the pathogenesis of primary hemochromatosis?
Show answer

Primary hemochromatosis is an autosomal recessive inherited disorder resulting in progressive iron absorption through the gastrointestinal tract with accumulation of iron in a variety of organs, including the liver. Without treatment, the disorder leads to cirrhosis and hepatic failure. With involvement of other organs, patients can also develop cardiac failure and various endocrine insufficiency states. Patients with hemochromatosis and cirrhosis are at an increased risk for developing hepatocellular carcinoma. Secondary iron overload or secondary hemochromatosis that results in hemosiderosis of the liver can be due to a variety of causes, including massive blood transfusions and severe hemolytic anemias (e.g.,thalassemia major).

Question 1
1
What is deficient in Wilson disease?
Show answer

Wilson disease is caused by a recessively inherited mutation in the ATP7B gene (over 30 different mutations currently described). ATP7B encodes a transcript for the transmembrane copper-transporting ATPase located on the canicular membranes of hepatocytes. The deficiency of the transmembrane copper-transporting ATPase leads to copper accumulation in the liver. Eventually, copper spills over into the circulation and causes damage in other tissues.


Image 4

Kayser-Fleischer rings, which are present in patients with Wilson disease, are green to brown copper deposits present in the periphery of the Descemet membrane of the cornea.


Image 5
PAS-positive AAT globules

Liver, alpha1-antitrypsin (AAT) deficiency, Periodic acid–Schiff (PAS) stain - High power

In AAT deficiency, AAT accumulates in the cytoplasm of hepatocytes. Due to an inherited metabolic defect, the protein is not secreted and thus accumulates in hepatocytes. This is apparent as eosinophilic globular material in the cytoplasm of hepatocytes in a PAS-with-diastase-stained section.

Question 1
1
What is the genetic abnormality in patients with AAT deficiency?
Show answer

AAT, a protease inhibitor (Pi), has many variant forms, which are grouped and designated alphabetically. AAT protein is expressed codominantly from both alleles; thus, an individual's AAT genotype and phenotype are denoted by the alphabetic designation of the protein type(s) expressed by his alleles; for example, PiMZ (heterozygote for M and Z types) or PiMM or PiZZ (homozygotes). PiZZ homozygotes have only very low serum levels of AAT (typically less than 10% of normal) because the PiZ protein cannot be secreted by hepatocytes. Retained protein leads to cellular damage and decreased liver function. Patients also develop emphysematous lung disease.

Question 1
1
Why are hepatic infarcts rare?
Show answer

The liver has a dual blood supply (supplied by both the hepatic artery and portal vein), so it is uncommon to sustain enough ischemic damage to lead to infarction.

Question 1
1
What are some causes of hepatic vein obstruction and thrombosis?
Show answer

Hepatic vein obstruction, or Budd-Chiari syndrome, can be seen in hypercoagulable states such as polycythemia vera, pregnancy, the postpartum state, oral contraceptive use, malignancies, inherited coagulation defects, antiphospholipid syndrome, and paroxysmal nocturnal hemoglobinuria.

Microvesicular steatosis is present, manifest as numerous small holes in hepatocytes. This complication can occur in the third trimester of pregnancy. It is related to abnormalities of intramitochondrial fatty acid metabolism.
