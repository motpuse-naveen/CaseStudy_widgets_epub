Scenario

The patient is a 55-year-old white man who presents to the emergency department with chest pain and labored breathing. His temperature is 98.6° F. He is overweight and smokes two packs of cigarettes a day. His ECG and serum cardiac marker profile reveal changes compatible with an acute myocardial infarction. His complete blood count (CBC) shows the following:

Hemoglobin 13 g/dL
WBCs 15,500/mm3
Platelets 250,000/mm3

A differential white blood cell count was included in the CBC report, showing 80% segmented neutrophils (polymorphonuclear neutrophils), 10% band neutrophils, 7% lymphocytes, and 3% reactive lymphocytes.

Question 1
1
What does the presence of neutrophilia indicate?

Neutrophilia is a useful laboratory indicator of an acute inflammatory response. In particular, neutrophilia is a sensitive finding in patients with pyogenic bacterial infections, which are the cause of neutrophilia most often encountered in clinical practice. Neutrophilia may be seen, however, in states of tissue necrosis, such as myocardial infarction, owing to the release of inflammatory mediators from damaged tissue. In addition, a mild neutrophilia may be seen in any situation of acute stress, secondary to an epinephrine-induced demargination of neutrophils within the blood vessels. Glucocorticoid administration is also a common cause of mild neutrophilia.

Bone marrow smear, maturation of neutrophils - High power

The different cells seen in the maturation sequence of the neutrophil are identified (100×), from a myeloblast to a segmented neutrophil. The overall maturation scheme of the neutrophilic line consists of nuclear shrinkage and segmentation with concurrent condensation of the chromatin. This process begins with the formation of a functionally immature myeloblast and culminates in the development of a phagocytic segmented neutrophil. This maturation takes about 15 days and occurs in the bone marrow. About half of this time is spent in the so-called mitotic compartment. The cells of this compartment—myeloblast, promyelocyte, myelocyte—multiply (i.e., are mitotically active) and undergo maturation. In the last stage of the mitotic compartment, the myelocyte begins to replace its primary (nonspecific) granules with secondary (specific) granules. The final three cellular stages of maturation are the so-called “storage compartment.” Cells in this pool consist of metamyelocytes, band neutrophils, and segmented neutrophils or “polys” (polymorphonuclear neutrophils).

Question 1
1
When are these cells released into the blood?

These cells remain in the marrow until the segmented forms are preferentially released into the peripheral blood.


Question 2
2
What are the mechanisms underlying neutrophilia?

The first phase of a neutrophilic leukocytosis is a simple demargination of the blood marginal pool; this does not represent an increase in peripheral neutrophils but merely a redistribution. This is a mild neutrophilia consisting of mature cells (segmented neutrophils and a few bands). In more severe reactions, there is a release of the marrow storage pool of mainly mature neutrophilic forms, and this may produce a more pronounced neutrophilia. At this time, less mature forms such as metamyelocytes may be released into the blood. Finally, if a sustained neutrophilic leukocytosis is required, the marrow increases its production of neutrophils and potentially releases even less mature forms into the blood (myelocytes and even promyelocytes). This is called a “left shift”.


Question 3
3
What is a "left shift," and what is its significance?

“Left shift” is a colloquial term for the presence of immature granulocytes in the peripheral blood. An increase in circulating band forms is of debatable diagnostic significance (except perhaps in newborns) and may be a feature of neutrophilia from various causes. The presence in the blood of significant numbers of less mature forms (metamyelocytes, myelocytes, and promyelocytes) generally indicates a more severe reaction, however, such as to a bacterial infection. There is considerable variability in this response from patient to patient. Myeloblasts are only rarely seen in the blood as a reactive phenomenon, and they usually indicate the presence of serious bone marrow pathology, such as myeloproliferative disorders, myelodysplastic syndromes, or acute leukemia.

Peripheral blood smear, neutrophilia - Low power

This image shows a more pronounced leukocytosis than in Image 1; the reported white blood cell count was 75×109/L. A higher power image shows that this consists of neutrophilic cells, including segmented forms, bands, metamyelocytes, and myelocytes. This sort of extreme reaction is generally termed a leukemoid reaction owing to its resemblance to chronic myelogenous leukemia (CML). This term is commonly reserved for neutrophilic reactions greater than 50×109/L. Typically, but not always, immature granulocytes are prominent in leukemoid reactions. Leukemoid reactions are an idiosyncratic reaction to various stimuli, including infection, severe hemolysis or hemorrhage, various malignancies, and severe burns.

Question 1
1
How may a leukemoid reaction be distinguished from CML?

Leukemoid reactions generally have fewer circulating immature granulocytes, and they lack the basophilia, circulating blasts, and splenomegaly that are nearly always seen in CML. Also, leukemoid reactions, being reactive proliferations, lack any cytogenetic abnormalities (e.g., the Philadelphia chromosome). Finally, leukemoid reactions only rarely exceed 100×109/L, whereas counts greater than this are commonly seen in CML. Generally, clinical findings also help distinguish these disorders.

Peripheral blood smears, circulating granulocytes - High power

The three frames in this image (100×) illustrate the different granulocytes that are seen commonly in the peripheral blood. Granulocytes are leukocytes, or white blood cells, that contain cytoplasmic granules. Frame (a) shows a basophil with the obvious large, basophilic granules typical of these granulocytes. The nuclear segmentation of these cells is often difficult to appreciate because of the heavy cytoplasmic granulation. Basophils are the least common of the granulocytes in the peripheral blood. Basophilia (increased number of basophils) is frequently noted in myeloproliferative disorders. The most abundant of the circulating granulocytes is the neutrophil (b). The specific granules of these cells are difficult to resolve individually with the light microscope; they appear as uniform, pale orange staining of the cytoplasm. An increase in their number (neutrophilia) may be seen in various conditions, including infections and tissue necrosis (as in myocardial injury). The large, red granules of eosinophils (c) are readily apparent in this image. Eosinophilia, an increase in eosinophils, is shown in the next Image 5.

Question 1
1
What are some examples of non-neoplastic eosinophilia?

Eosinophilia may be seen in allergic reactions (including asthma and drug reaction), parasitic infestation, and skin diseases, as well as in response to some neoplasms.

Peripheral blood smear, reactive lymphocytes in infectious mononucleosis - High power

This image is an example of infectious mononucleosis caused by acute Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) infection. This is a common, self-limited disorder that usually affects children or young adults. In adults, lymphocytes normally account for less than 50% of the leukocyte differential count, and their absolute numbers do not exceed 4×109/L. In addition, most lymphocytes in healthy individuals manifest a resting or mature appearance, characterized by small cells with round nuclei, inconspicuous or absent nucleoli, and a narrow rim of cytoplasm. In infectious mononucleosis, there are increased numbers of reactive lymphocytes (also known as atypical lymphocytes). As illustrated, these cells are commonly larger with more abundant pale or deeply basophilic cytoplasm, and they often have distinct nucleoli.

Question 1
1
Are reactive lymphocytes specific for infectious mononucleosis?

No. Small numbers of reactive lymphocytes are a normal finding in the peripheral blood, and mild increases may be seen in a wide variety of reactive states. However, when these are prominently increased (>10% of leukocytes) in association with lymphocytosis, this is quite specific for infectious mononucleosis in adolescents and young adults.


Question 2
2
What is the nature of the reactive lymphocytes in infectious mononucleosis?

In EBV mononucleosis, the virus infects B cells. These infected B cells express viral antigens on their surfaces that incite a cytotoxic T-cell reaction. The reactive lymphocytes seen in the blood are actually the cytotoxic T cells, not the infected B cells.


Figure 8-13 Outcomes of Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) infection. In an individual with normal immune function, infection is usually either asymptomatic or leads to mononucleosis. In the setting of cellular immunodefi ciency, the proliferation of infected B cells may be uncontrolled, leading to the development of B-cell neoplasms. In other instances, persons without overt evidence of immunodeficiency develop EBV-positive tumors, which are usually (but not always) also derived from B cells. One secondary genetic event that collaborates with EBV to cause B-cell transformation is a balanced 8;14 chromosomal translocation, which is seen in Burkitt lymphoma. EBV is also implicated in the pathogenesis of nasopharyngeal carcinoma, Hodgkin lymphoma, and certain other rare non-Hodgkin lymphomas.

Question 3
3
What are the serologic findings allowing a diagnosis of EBV mononucleosis?

Most patients with infectious mononucleosis caused by EBV have elevated heterophil antibodies. These are nonspecific IgM antibodies that react with beef, sheep, and horse erythrocytes. These are commonly detected with a rapid slide test known as the Monospot. In the rare patient who does not have positive heterophil antibodies, more specific serologic tests may be performed to detect the presence of specific antibodies against the virus itself. Most helpful in the acute disease is the presence of IgM antibodies directed against the EBV viral capsid antigen.

Question 4
4
What are the diagnostic possibilities in a patient with an infectious mononucleosis–like syndrome who lacks heterophil antibodies?

As detailed previously, some patients with EBV infectious mononucleosis lack heterophil antibodies, but these patients may still be shown to possess specific anti-EBV antibodies. Occasional patients with an infectious mononucleosis–like syndrome lack evidence of EBV infection. Most of these patients are found to have acute cytomegalovirus (CMV) infection or, rarely, are found to be infected with other viral or parasitic agents. The infectious mononucleosis–like syndrome caused by CMV is notable for a lack of cervical adenopathy and pharyngitis.
