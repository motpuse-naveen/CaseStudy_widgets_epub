Learning Objectives
  1. Know the basic principles of the nomenclature of benign and malignant processes.
  2. Describe the morphologic changes associated with poorly differentiated tumors; define and understand the usage of the terms anaplasia, pleomorphism, nuclear atypia, loss of polarity, abnormal mitoses, and tumor giant cells.
  3. Understand the clinical significance of invasiveness and metastasis.
  4. Describe the anatomic pathways used by tumors in metastatic spread. Know which pathways are more commonly used by carcinomas versus sarcomas.
  5. Understand the concepts of dominant oncogenes and proto-oncogenes.
  6. Review the principles of signal transduction in cells. Be familiar with the different classes of growth factors, growth factor receptors, signal-transducing molecules, and nuclear regulatory factors.
  7. Describe the concept of DNA-damage checkpoints and the roles played by P53 and CKI in the DNA damage response.
  8. Compare and contrast the different mechanisms of oncogene activation (point mutations, overexpression, gene amplification).
  9. Understand the normal functions of the RAS, ABL, ERB-B2, MYC, beta-catenin, and cyclin D1 gene products; the types of genetic alterations found in these genes; and the types of neoplasms that carry alterations in these genes.
  10. List some inherited syndromes associated with a genetic predisposition to cancer.
  11. Compare and contrast clinical features of cancer in patients with an inherited predisposition versus sporadic cancer in terms of multiple primary tumors, age at onset, and genetic alterations.
  12. Compare and contrast the kinetics of tumor growth with the kinetics of tumor cell cycle progression, and understand how this relates to tumor growth fraction.
  13. Understand the therapeutic implications of growth fraction in tumors.
  14. List some of the host factors (angiogenesis factors, hormones) that affect tumor cell growth, and describe how they might be used as targets for therapeutic intervention.
  15. Distinguish between initiation and promotion of carcinogenesis.
  16. Be familiar with the general principles, value, procedures, and applications of biopsy, exfoliative and aspiration cytology, and frozen section.
  17. List some examples of special markers used to diagnose cancer by immunohistochemistry and flow cytometry.
  18. Define tumor grade and clinical stage.
  19. Define paraneoplastic syndrome, and know examples of tumors associated with endocrinopathies, osseous changes, and vascular and hematologic changes.

Scenario

A 41-year-old woman presented with a mass in her left breast. During a self-examination, the patient initially noticed a tiny nodule, which doubled in size during the last 2 months before presentation. Family history is significant for a maternal aunt who died of breast cancer at age 59; no other cancer-affected relatives are noted. The patient has two young daughters. Physical examination revealed a mass, which was non-fixed, in the left breast. No signs of edema or nipple changes were evident. A single palpable axillary lymph node, which was also mobile, was found. Mammography confirmed the presence of a mass 2.5 cm in diameter. CT scans of the chest and abdomen revealed no masses in the lungs, liver, adrenal glands, kidneys, spleen, or ovaries. A bone scan was negative as well. Serum calcium was elevated at 12.5 mg/dL, without specific signs or symptoms, and serum PTH was slightly low.

The patient underwent a modified radical mastectomy to remove the tumor, including axillary lymph node dissection. Of the 14 lymph nodes removed, one was completely replaced by tumor cells, and three others showed microscopic involvement. The carcinoma cells stained intensely for HER2/neu amplification by FISH and were positive for the estrogen and progesterone receptors. Based on clinical staging, the patient was given adjuvant radiation therapy to the left breast and axilla as well as Herceptin and an aromatase inhibitor. The patient presented 10 months later with complaints of headaches and blurred vision. Cerebrospinal fluid was analyzed.

Question 1
1
Based on your knowledge of the influence of estrogen on cells that express this receptor, how might this finding be exploited therapeutically?
Show answer

Because tumor cells can proliferate by binding estrogen, three therapeutic strategies are used to interrupt this process: (1) use of tamoxifen (which acts as an anti-estrogen in breast tissue) to interrupt the binding of estrogen to its receptor; (2) aromatase inhibitors (which prevent aromatization of steroids to estrogen in the ovary) to prevent estrogen production; or (3) salpingo-oophorectomy, particularly in patients with BRCA1 or BRCA2 mutations, to deplete the supply of estrogen and to prevent serous tubal/ovarian cancers that arise in these patients.


Question 2
2
How is immunohistochemical staining done?
Show answer

Immunohistochemical staining requires an antibody against the target protein to be detected. The antibody is conjugated to an enzyme (e.g., horseradish peroxidase) that converts a colorless compound into a chromogen. In this way, any antibody binding to the tissue can be visualized.

What are the two types of chromosomal structures that represent amplified DNA in cancer cells?
Show answer

The two types are amplified gene integrated into one of the normal chromosomes and expanded as a homogeneously staining region (HSR); and amplified gene present as extrachromosomal DNA copies, (termed double minutes).

The epidermal growth factor receptor family of tyrosine kinases includes this protein.

Targeted Therapy Box: Gazing into the Crystal Ball: Gout Therapies Present and Future

Her2 Now: Targeted Therapy for

Breast Cancer Breast cancer is the most common malignancy in women, with a lifetime risk of roughly one in eight. It is second only to lung cancer as a cause of female cancer deaths. However, breast cancer is not just one monolithic disease; survival statistics (and potential therapies) are associated with different tumor characteristics, including estrogen and progesterone hormone receptor status, aneuploidy, and specific heritable mutations (such as BRCA1 and BRCA2 genes related to DNA repair). In particular, overexpression of the human epidermal growth factor receptor type 2 (Her2, also called Her2/neu or c-ErbB-2) occurs in 20% to 30% of invasive breast cancers and is associated with a poor prognosis. Fortunately, Her2 overexpression also provides a target for an effective therapy.

Her2 is one of four related transmembrane epidermal growth factor receptors with intrinsic tyrosine kinase activity. When these receptors form homo- or heterodimers after ligand binding, downstream kinase signaling promotes proliferation through a MAP kinase pathway and blocks apoptosis through AKT-dependent mechanisms. Significantly, the extracellular portion of Her2 can dimerize in the absence of ligand; cleavage of the extracellular portion of Her2 can leave a trans cellular fragment that can continue to signal. Consequently, overexpression of Her2 can lead to unabated kinase activity through either autodimerization or proteolysis.

Her2 overexpression occurs as a consequence of gene amplification; this is usually demonstrated by immunohistochemical staining showing increased membrane protein expression and is confirmed by fluorescence in situ hybridization (FISH) showing the replicated gene sequence. Of interest, although Her2 overexpression occurs with some frequency in breast cancer, it is only rarely seen in other malignancies.

Recognition of Her2 overexpression in a subset of breast cancers led to the development of a monoclonal antibody, trastuzumab, which can block Her2 dimerization or cleavage. Trastuzumab also may promote Her2 internalization and degradation, or kill tumors through antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity. If there is no Her2 overexpression, the monoclonal antibody provides no particular benefit. Regardless of the mechanism, patients receiving a course of the monoclonal antibody in addition to standard chemotherapies show almost 50% better response rates, with significantly improved disease-free and overall survival. Indeed, trastuzumab was the first monoclonal antibody to prolong life for patients with epithelial malignancies.

Cardiotoxicity is the only significant side effect, developing in about 4% of patients; it is attributable to the role that Her2 signaling apparently plays in myocardial damage repair. The one other limitation of trastuzumab is that it does not cross the blood-brain barrier; the central nervous system thus becomes a sanctuary for metastatic disease. Fortunately, small molecule oral Her2-specific tyrosine kinase inhibitors (lapatinib) are in the pipeline.

Question 1
1
How does the presence of carcinoma cells in the cerebrospinal fluid influence tumor staging? How does it influence grading?
Show answer

The presence of cells in the cerebrospinal fluid indicates distant metastases, and the tumor stage is increased to stage IV. Because the cells still apparently have a differentiated feature (mucin secretion), the tumor grade is not affected by this finding.


Question 2
2
What might be the cause of hypercalcemia in this patient?
Show answer

Hypercalcemia may occur because of (1) osteolysis resulting from primary or metastatic bone lesions or (2) the production of hypercalcemic hormonal substances by extraosseous neoplasms. Paraneoplastic syndromes are clinical features that cannot be explained by the local or regional spread of a tumor or by the production of hormones indigenous to the tissue from which the tumor arose. Hypercalcemia is an extremely common paraneoplastic syndrome. Serum PTH should be low with hypercalcemia. In this case, hypercalcemia is associated with an appropriately low PTH level and no obvious bone invasion; this suggests that this neoplasm produces PTH-related protein. Hypercalcemia resulting from osteolysis secondary to bone metastasis is not considered a paraneoplastic syndrome.
