Scenario

A 25-year-old white woman presents to the emergency department with a painful swollen right calf. She has been taking oral contraceptive pills for the last 6 months. On physical examination, the right calf is tender and 5 cm larger in diameter compared with the left calf. On further questioning, she states that her mother had a similar problem of a swollen leg at a young age and had suddenly died of lung failure at age 45. Some other family members on the paternal side also had similar histories of swollen legs.

Image 1

Mechanism of action of antithrombin - Schematic

The clinical features of the case suggest the presence of a deep vein thrombosis (DVT) of a calf vein. This is consistent with a hypercoagulable state in a young woman, especially with a strong family history of clotting. There are three natural anticoagulants: antithrombin (AT), protein C (PC), and protein S (PS). The mechanism of action of AT is illustrated here. It is the most potent inhibitor of thrombin and factor Xa. When AT comes into contact with factor IIa/Xa (serine proteases), it undergoes a conformational change and binds to factor IIa/Xa. This neutralizing activity of AT is enhanced 5000-fold to 10,000-fold by heparin, which is the most commonly used anticoagulant in clinical practice.

Image 2

Mechanism of action of proteins C and S - Schematic

Free thrombin in plasma gets bound to thrombomodulin on endothelial cells. The thrombin-thrombomodulin complex converts protein C (PC) to activated protein C (APC). APC inactivates factor Va and factor VIIIa to factor Va(i) and factor VIIIa(i), reducing thrombin generation. Protein S (PS) is a cofactor for APC.

Question 1
1
What is the consequence of inherited deficiencies of antithrombin (AT), PC, or PS?

Deficiency of any of these natural anticoagulants causes a hypercoagulable state. However, only 10% of patients with thrombophilia (hypercoagulable state) have a deficiency of these natural anticoagulants.

Question 2
2
What is the relationship of oral contraceptive use to the problems this patient is having?

Oral contraceptive pills induce a hypercoagulable state, which is more severe when there is another underlying hypercoagulable state (especially a hereditary one).


Image 3

Factor V Leiden mutation - Schematic

Factor V Leiden (FVL) mutation accounts for 25% to 50% of thrombophilia cases, making this genetic defect one of the most common causes so far identified. The incidence of this genetic mutation in the general population is approximately 2%. There is a point mutation in factor V at position 506 such that Gln replaces Arg. This is the cleavage site on factor Va for activated protein C (APC) to inactivate factor Va. Because of mutation at the cleavage site, APC is unable to cleave factor Va to its inactive form, resulting in the continued presence of factor Va in the circulation, which leads to continued generation of thrombin, ultimately causing a thrombotic state. Other genetic defects causing thrombophilia are a prothrombin gene mutation (PT20210) and hyperhomocysteinemia (HHC). PT20210 is responsible for 5% to 10% of deep vein thrombosis (DVT). HHC is associated with thrombophilia, especially with arterial thrombosis. HHC is acquired because of folate/vitamin B12 deficiency or results from an inherited methylene tetrahydrofolate reductase (MTHFR) deficiency. HHC is implicated in atherosclerotic thrombosis and is responsible for 5% to 10% of cases of DVT and less than 5% of cases of thrombophilia.

Question 1
1
What are some acquired causes of hypercoagulability?

Acquired HHC and antiphospholipid antibody syndrome (APS) are two acquired causes of hypercoagulability. APS consists of two separate entities known as lupus anticoagulant (LA) and anticardiolipin antibody (ACA). A patient can have LA or ACA, or both. APS is as common as FVL.


Question 2
2
How do the various factors associated with hypercoagulability interact?

A patient can have a combination of a genetic defect and APS or more than one genetic defect leading to clinically severe thrombophilia. Thrombophilia in young patients (generally < 50 years old) can manifest in the form of DVT of the lower limbs, especially recurrent; as pulmonary embolism; as DVT at unusual sites, such as mesenteric and axillary veins; and as arterial thrombosis (especially the retinal artery) in some patients.

Question 3
3
What laboratory tests should be ordered, and when should they be ordered to make a diagnosis?

Laboratory investigation of thrombophilia should be done only in young patients or someone who has unusual thrombosis. Elderly patients with other risk factors, such as prolonged bed rest, major surgery, or trauma, should not be investigated. The investigation should include all of the natural anticoagulants, antithrombin (AT), protein C (PC), protein S (PS), activated protein C resistance (APCR) (a functional test for FVL), lupus anticoagulant (LA), and anticardiolipin antibody (ACA). The reason for doing a panel of tests is that many patients have more than one defect, and knowing that helps in deciding the duration of anticoagulation therapy, genetic counseling, and family study, as well as in giving appropriate prophylactic anticoagulation therapy for high-risk situations such as major surgery and prolonged bed rest.


Question 4
4
What is the major side effect of anticoagulant therapy?

The major side effect of anticoagulation therapy is bleeding. The bleeding tendency correlates with the degree of prolongation of the prothrombin time. Oral anticoagulation therapy needs to be monitored very carefully. Warfarin (Coumadin) is contraindicated in pregnancy, especially in the first trimester, when it is associated with a dysmorphic fetus.
