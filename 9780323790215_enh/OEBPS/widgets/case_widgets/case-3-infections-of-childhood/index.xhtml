Infections of Childhood

Learning Objectives

  • 1. Know the following about infections with Epstein-Barr virus (EBV):
    • a. the pathogenesis of EBV infection and the immune response of the immunocompetent host to the viral infection
    • b. the histopathologic changes in lymphoid tissue and peripheral blood in infectious mononucleosis
    • c. the spectrum of neoplastic or pre-neoplastic disorders associated with EBV infection in the immunosuppressed host
  • 2. Identify the virus associated with the Warthin-Finkeldy cell, and describe the morphology of the cell.
  • 3. Understand the clinical and pathologic features of Bordetella pertussis infection.
  • 4. Describe the characteristic rash and the viral inclusions of varicella-zoster virus infection, and recognize the clinical presentations of chicken pox and shingles.
  • 5. Identify the pathologic findings in genital herpes simplex virus infection, the mode of perinatal transmission, and the characteristic findings in the neonate.
  • 6. Describe the characteristic pathologic findings in congenital syphilis, congenital cytomegalovirus infection, and congenital parvovirus infection.

Scenario

A 17-year-old high school student was seen by his physician because he had a severe sore throat and fever for 3 days and was not getting better. The physician performed a physical examination. The patient's blood pressure and respiratory rate were normal. His temperature was 102°F, and his skin was warm and dry without a rash. His tonsils were markedly enlarged with overlying exudates. Tender lymph nodes were palpated in the cervical, axillary, and inguinal areas (Image 1). Abdominal examination was significant for an enlarged liver and spleen. The physician ordered a CBC that showed increased numbers of white blood cells and many atypical lymphocytes (Image 2). A rapid heterophil antibody test was positive. The patient was given an analgesic/antipyretic and sent home to rest. Over the next 2 weeks, his sore throat, fever, and lymphadenopathy resolved. He returned to school, slowly regaining his strength over the next 3 months.

Marked lymphoid hyperplasia

Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) lymphadenitis - Clinical presentation

Massive lymphadenopathy is characteristic of infectious mononucleosis. The lymphoid enlargement occurs as a result of proliferation of both infected B lymphocytes and T lymphocytes reacting to the infection.

Question 1
1
What causes obstructive sleep apnea in some patients with infectious mononucleosis?

Tonsillar lymphoid hyperplasia may result in tonsillar enlargement, producing airway obstruction.


Figure 8-13 Outcomes of Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) infection. In an individual with normal immune function, infection is usually either asymptomatic or leads to mononucleosis. In the setting of cellular immunodeficiency, the proliferation of infected B cells may be uncontrolled, leading to the development of B-cell neoplasms. In other instances, persons without overt evidence of immunodeficiency develop EBV-positive tumors, which are usually (but not always) also derived from B cells. One secondary genetic event that collaborates with EBV to cause B-cell transformation is a balanced 8;14 chromosomal translocation, which is seen in Burkitt lymphoma. EBV is also implicated in the pathogenesis of nasopharyngeal carcinoma, Hodgkin lymphoma, and certain other rare non-Hodgkin lymphomas.


Image 2

Peripheral blood smear, infectious mononucleosis - High power

A normal lymphocyte is about the size of a red blood cell and has little cytoplasm. The reactive lymphocyte in this image is seen in infectious mononucleosis; however, it is not specific for infectious mononucleosis. Reactive T lymphocytes may be seen in a variety of clinical conditions, including a wide range of viral infections. A reactive lymphocyte has an enlarged nucleus with abundant cytoplasm that is indented by the surrounding red blood cells. These are circulating T lymphocytes. The B lymphocytes remain in the lymphoid tissue and may actually be found in parenchymal organs, such as the liver, where they produce hepatitis.

Question 1
1
Is Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) found in circulating T lymphocytes?

EBV is not found in circulating T lymphocytes. EBV infects B lymphocytes, in which it resides in a latent state for the remainder of the patient's life.

Image 3

Agglutination

Heterophil test for infectious mononucleosis

This test to detect heterophil antibodies is an easy confirmatory test for acute Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) infection in older children, adolescents, and adults. In infectious mononucleosis, patients develop antibodies called heterophil antibodies. These antibodies are not specific for EBV, but they attach to sheep and horse red blood cells. This test shows the ability of the patient's serum to agglutinate horse red blood cells. This is a positive result, which is visualized by the technologist as clumping of the material on the slide.

Question 1
1
What other serologic test can be used to diagnose acute EBV infection?

In an acute EBV infection, an IgM antibody to the viral capsid antigen of EBV (IgM-VCA) will increase.


Question 2
2
What other viral infection can cause a mononucleus-like infection that is heterophil antibody negative?

The other infection is cytomegalovirus.


Image 4

Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) serologic response - Graph

A characteristic serologic response is seen with EBV infection. It helps differentiate acute infection from convalescence. Serology is used in young children and infants if EBV infection is suspected because the heterophil test is frequently negative in this group. Also, approximately 5% of adolescents and young adults with infectious mononucleosis do not produce heterophil antibodies, so serologic evaluation of these patients is necessary to confirm the diagnosis.

Antibodies:

-IgG VCA - IgG antibody to the EBV viral capsid antigen

-IgM VCA - IgM antibody to the viral capsid antigen

-EA - Antibody to the EBV early antigen

-EBNA - Antibody to EBV nuclear antigen

Tonsil, Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) tonsillitis - High power

EBV induces a proliferation of B lymphocytes in the lymphoid tissue. When EBV infects B lymphocytes, they become transformed. Histologically, these transformed lymphocytes are large and atypical, and they frequently have prominent nucleoli. The inset is a higher magnification of the EBV-infected B lymphocytes. The tonsils of the patient in the case would look like this during the acute infection. Question 1
1
What neoplasms are associated with EBV infection?

EBV is involved in the pathogenesis of multiple types of lymphoid neoplasms (e.g., Burkitt lymphoma, some types of Hodgkin lymphoma, plasmablastic lymphoma, post-transplant lymphoproliferative disorder, Nasal-type NK/T cell lymphoma) and non-lymphoid neoplasms (e.g., nasopharyngeal carcinoma, EBV-related smooth muscle tumors, some gastric carcinomas).

Question 1
1
What type of virus causes measles (rubeola)?

Rubeola is caused by paramyxovirus infection. Immunization for measles has been available for decades, and the disease predominantly occurs in third-world countries.


Image 7

Fifth disease (parvovirus infection) - Clinical presentation

The bright red cheeks on this 15-month-old child are characteristic of infection by parvovirus B19. The virus causes a very mild illness in healthy children (compare with the fetus in the following Image 8). A characteristic "slapped cheek" rash is seen in many cases. The disease is mild in healthy children because they are able to compensate for the anemia produced by parvovirus infection of red blood cells. However, in children with impaired hemoglobin production, parvovirus B19 may produce a severe anemia, requiring transfusions.

Question 1
1
In which hemoglobin disorders can parvovirus cause severe anemia?

The aplastic crisis of sickle cell anemia is caused by parvovirus B19. Patients with other hemoglobin disorders, such as hemoglobin SC disease, thalassemia, and hereditary spherocytosis, may develop aplastic crises with parvovirus B19 infection as well.

Question 1
1
Is congenital parvovirus infection an ascending infection or is it acquired transplacentally?

Congenital parvovirus infection is acquired by the fetus in utero secondary to transplacental passage of the virus from mother to fetus. This occurs at the time of maternal viremia.

Congenital cytomegalovirus (CMV) infection - Clinical view; Brain, coronal section, gross

The classic features of congenital CMV infection are shown in these images. There is a skin rash and hepatosplenomegaly, clinical features that may also be seen in babies with infections from other members of the TORCH group (toxoplasmosis, rubella, herpes, other, including syphilis). In disseminated CMV infections, CMV inclusions are characteristically seen in a variety of parenchymal, epithelial, and endothelial cells. The virus causes cellular enlargement, as shown in the high-power view. The virus causes ependymitis and destruction of cerebral parenchyma, as seen here. Babies with congenital CMV infection may appear normal at birth, only to present with hearing loss in childhood. Other infants with congenital CMV infection may have growth failure with a wasted appearance, as shown here, or may be hydropic. Microcephaly may also be present.

Question 1
1
Where would you expect the calcifications in the central nervous system to be in a patient with severe congenital CMV infection?

CMV produces an ependymitis, so the calcifications are typically periventricular.

Question 1
1
Which is the more common method of transmission of HSV infection from mother to infant — ascending through the cervix, or transplacental?

Perinatal HSV infection, either type 1 or type 2, is usually acquired at the time of delivery through an infected birth canal; therefore, this is considered ascending rather than transplacental. This is in contrast to most other perinatal viral infections, which are acquired via a hematogenous (transplacental) dissemination from the mother.


Question 2
2
What is the most important factor determining the clinical course of a neonatal HSV infection?

The clinical course of neonatal HSV infection is dependent upon whether the maternal infection is primary or recurrent. If the maternal infection is recurrent, protective antibody passes to the fetus through the placenta, and only 3% of the infants contract the virus. Most of these infants are mildly affected, but disseminated infection may occur in this group, particularly in the face of prematurity. In pregnancies in which a primary infection occurs in the mother, there is a 50% risk for neonatal infection if the infant is delivered through an infected birth canal because of lack of protective maternal antibodies. The infants usually become symptomatic 5 to 8 days after delivery.

Question 1
1
Which organs are most likely to be involved by fibrosis in congenital syphilis?

Fibrosis most commonly occurs in the liver, pancreas, and lungs, but it may be present in multiple organs. The typical morphology of congenital syphilis in the brain is that of a chronic meningitis.
