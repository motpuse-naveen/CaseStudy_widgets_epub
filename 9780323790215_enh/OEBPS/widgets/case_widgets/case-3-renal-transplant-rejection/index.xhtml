Renal Transplant Rejection

Learning Objectives

  • 1. Know the cell types that stimulate rejection (i.e., are immunogenic).
  • 2. Describe the cellular immunology of CD4+ and CD8+ T cells, focusing on their relationship to class I and class II human leukocyte antigen (HLA) antigens and effector functions (e.g., delayed type hypersensitivity, cytotoxic T cells).
  • 3. Distinguish between the causes and morphology of hyperacute, acute, and chronic graft rejection of the kidney.
  • 4. Know the methods of increasing graft survival (e.g., HLA matching, immunosuppressive therapy). Know the immunologic mechanisms affected by these methods.

Scenario

A 24-year-old man with bilateral polycystic disease of the kidney developed hypertension and chronic renal failure. Both kidneys were removed, and he was treated with renal dialysis until a kidney donor could be found. He had no siblings. He typed human leukocyte antigen (HLA)-A 1/2, HLA-B 5/8, and HLA-DR 3/4. A search for a donor in the Organ Donor Directory was finally successful after 3 years, when a cadaver donor became available that typed HLA-A 1/2, HLA-B 5/8, and HLA-DR 3/4. The patient did well for 6 weeks, but an increasing creatinine level was noted. A renal biopsy specimen showed numerous lymphocytes in the interstitium with invasion of the renal tubular epithelial cells (tubulitis). His doses of tacrolimus, mycophenolate, and prednisone were increased. The patient’s renal functions returned to normal, and he did very well except for occasional bouts of infections, including cryptococcal meningitis.

Question 1
1
What are the mechanisms and timing of this reaction?
Show answer

Hyperacute rejection occurs within minutes to hours after renal transplantation. The mechanism is preformed antibodies against the graft antigens; these antibodies rapidly react with the endothelium when the vascular anastomoses are made. Endothelial damage by antibody and complement triggers thrombosis.


Question 2
2
Why would the vasculature be the primary target in this form of rejection?
Show answer

When antibodies enter the graft, they encounter the donor endothelium; the reaction occurs primarily in the vasculature.


Question 3
3
What do the paleness and thinness of the cortex indicate?
Show answer

These suggest severe ischemia and possibly coagulative necrosis of the cortex owing to vascular thromboses.

1
What type of rejection does the course of this patient suggest?
Show answer

This patient began to show signs of rejection in 6 weeks. This is typical of acute rejection.


Question 2
2
Why did he have rejection despite the matching for HLA antigens?
Show answer

The donor/host match was exact for HLA-A, HLA-B, and HLA-DR. The reasons for rejection could be mismatches for (1) variations in subsets of HLA-A, HLA-B, or HLA-DR not detected by serologic methods used; (2) HLA-C, HLA-DQ, HLA-DP, or other remaining MHC genes not evaluated; or (3) minor histocompatibility antigens, which are numerous and are composed of peptide–class I heavy chain (shared between donor and host) or peptide–class II molecules (shared between donor and host). Molecules encoded by minor histocompatibility loci are thought to be immunogenic through intracellular processing and proteolytic cleavage with subsequent presentation of resulting peptides by class I and class II MHC molecules. More practically, the patient may have been noncompliant with his immunosuppressive medications.


Question 3
3
Why is this patient at risk for opportunistic infections?
Show answer

Immunosuppressive therapy is necessary to prevent rejection of the allograft. However, this makes the patient susceptible to infection. A common infection in the transplanted kidney is polyoma nephropathy. However, other extra-renal infections can also occur, such as the cryptococcal meningitis described in the case.

Question 1
1
What is the difference in the histologic manifestations of cellular versus humoral rejection?
Show answer

Cellular rejection is manifested as an interstitial infiltrate of lymphocytes and macrophages. These lymphocytes are T cells. Humoral rejection manifests as endothelial injury with thrombosis and resultant coagulative necrosis.


Figure 6-32 Recognition of alloantigens in organ grafts. A, In the direct pathway, donor class I and class II MHC antigens on antigen-presenting cells in the graft (along with costimulators, not shown) are recognized by host CD8 + cytotoxic T cells and CD4 + helper T cells, respectively. CD4 + cells proliferate and produce cytokines (e.g., IFN- γ ), which induce tissue damage by a local inflammatory reaction. CD8 + T cells responding to graft antigens differentiate into CTLs that kill graft cells. B, In the indirect pathway graft antigens are picked up, processed, and displayed by host APCs and activate CD4 + T cells, which damage the graft by an inflammatory reaction and stimulate B lymphocytes to produce antibodies. An example of the reaction to kidney allografts is shown, but the same principles are applicable to all solid organ grafts.
