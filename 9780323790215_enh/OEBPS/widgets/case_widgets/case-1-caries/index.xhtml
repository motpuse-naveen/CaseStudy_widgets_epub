Learning Objectives
  • 1. Explain how tooth plaque destroys the tooth enamel and the dentin.
  • 2. Explain how caries results in localized inflammation in the jaw surrounding the root apex (periapical disease).
  • 3. Explain how periapical disease may lead to submucosal or subcutaneous swelling.
  • 4. Explain the relationship between dental caries and lesions of the facial skin.
  • 5. Explain why periapical inflammation heals spontaneously after removal of the diseased tooth, in contrast with a radicular cyst, which does not heal after tooth removal.

Scenario

A 25-year-old man is seen in the dermatology clinic for evaluation of persistent "proud flesh" in the facial skin covering the mandible. Several attempts to excise the lesion have been unsuccessful, with the lesion recurring after some weeks. Because of the multiple excisions and recurrences, a scar has developed adjacent to the proliferating granulation tissue. The patient recalls a period of intense tooth pain 6 months ago. This period of intense pain lasted for 2 weeks. Thereafter, the pain subsided, but he noticed a swelling in the cheek at the area adjacent to the involved tooth. This swelling regressed over several weeks. After a symptomless period, a skin lesion developed, the persistent character of which provoked the patient to see the dermatologist.

Question 1
1
Describe the normal histology of the tooth and surrounding structures.
Show answer

The bulk of a tooth consists of dentin, a peculiar form of mineralized collagenous matrix akin to bone but without cells. Instead of cells, the dentin has tiny canals, the so-called tubuli that occupy the entire width of the dentin, and that contain cytoplasmic extensions of the odontoblasts. These odontoblasts are columnar cells that line the inner side of the dentin and are interposed between the dentin and the loose fibrovascular tissue that forms the most centrally located part of the tooth, the dental pulp. At its outer side, the part of the tooth that is exposed to the oral environment is capped by enamel, which is a product of epithelial cells (the ameloblasts) that consists of a highly mineralized keratinous matrix. The proportion of mineral (hydroxyapatite) is so high that enamel does not survive the decalcification step that is needed to make histologic sections. The only way it can be studied is by making ground sections. In hematoxylin and eosin–stained sections from paraffin-embedded material, no enamel is present except in very young teeth that, owing to incomplete maturation, contain a higher proportion of organic material in their enamel.


The part of the tooth that is not covered by enamel is covered by a thin layer of acellular mineralized collagenous matrix, the root cementum. This tissue connects the tooth with the collagenous fibers—the periodontal ligament—that anchor the tooth root in its bony socket, the alveolus.


The mucosal strip that surrounds the neck of the tooth is called the gingiva. At its outer side, it consists of parakeratinized, multilayered squamous epithelium. Its inner side consists of multilayered epithelium that sticks to the enamel surface of the tooth. This structure is called junctional epithelium. The core of the gingiva consists of collagenous tissue that is continuous with the collagen fibers of the periodontal ligament. The attachment of the junctional epithelium to the tooth surface forms a seal by which bacteria from the oral cavity are prevented from invading the soft tissues that surround the neck of the tooth. This barrier is highly vulnerable because the epithelium easily dislodges from the tooth surface, giving entrance to bacteria from the plaque into the tooth-supporting tissues.

Question 2
2
Describe the progression from carries to periapical cyst.
Show answer

Caries, or tooth decay, starts with demineralization of the tooth enamel because of an acidic environment created by plaque bacteria. Initially, the enamel becomes porous, but subsequently the crystalline structure of the enamel collapses and a cavity is formed. When the border between enamel and dentin is reached, the cytoplasmic extensions of the odontoblasts are stimulated. As a response to this stimulus, the odontoblasts form reactive dentin to protect the pulp against bacteria and their noxious products. This response is futile, however. Bacteria invade the dentinal tubuli and, owing to proteolysis, the dentin becomes soft and friable. If left untreated, the tooth decay reaches the dental pulp, which reacts with inflammation. This inflammatory change in time extends through the root canal and spreads into the periapical area of the jaw bone. Periapical disease results, which may remain localized or may form a fistulous tract to open at a mucosal surface or at the facial skin. Submucosal or subcutaneous abscesses may also form. Initially, the periapical lesion consists of granulation tissue—hence its name, periapical granuloma (not a granuloma in the strict sense—ie, a lesion composed of epithelioid cells and multinucleated giant cells). The periapical inflammatory process stimulates epithelial remnants in the periapical part of the periodontal ligament. Liquefaction necrosis in the center of these epithelial nests creates a fluid-filled cavity with an inner epithelial lining, a lesion called a radicular or periapical cyst. This cyst may cause the same complications as mentioned previously for the periapical granuloma with one important difference. The periapical granuloma may heal spontaneously after removal of the tooth. The cyst does not heal, but must be removed surgically because the epithelial proliferation and necrosis form an inflammatory nidus.
