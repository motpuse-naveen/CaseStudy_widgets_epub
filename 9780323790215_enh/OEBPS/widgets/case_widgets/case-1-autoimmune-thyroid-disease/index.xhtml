Scenario

Two cases are provided. Compare their immunopathogenesis, morphology, and clinical features.

Case A

A 43-year-old woman presents with a 2- to 3-month history of nervousness, increased sweating, decreased tolerance to heat, palpitations, fatigue, weight loss with increased appetite, and increased bowel movement frequency. She also has puffiness around her eyes, and she has noted episodes of double vision, also of 2 to 3 months’ duration. She is otherwise healthy, takes no medications, and leads an active working life. This is her first episode of hyperthyroidism.

Palpation of her neck reveals a diffusely enlarged thyroid gland, representing a goiter of 70 to 80 g. She has a regular pulse of 105 beats/min and typical but minimal eye signs. Serum levels are: triiodothyronine, 354 ng/dL (reference range 52-160 ng/dL); thyroxine, 13.9 μg/dL (reference range 5.5-12.3 μg/dL); Thyroid stimulating hormone (TSH ), <0.05 μU/mL (reference range 0.05-5 μU/mL); and free thyroxine, 2 ng/mL (reference range 0.7-1.8 ng/mL).

Case B

A 24-year-old woman presents with “swelling” in her neck. On careful questioning, she reports being “tired and cold all the time,” and it is determined that these symptoms developed insidiously over a period of 4 to 5 years. Physical examination reveals a diffusely enlarged thyroid gland. Laboratory tests reveal thyroid hypofunction and high serum titers of antithyroid (antimicrosomal and antithyroglobulin) antibodies.


Image 1


Hyperthyroidism with exophthalmos - Clinical presentation

Exophthalmos is a characteristic sign of hyperthyroidism, and it is a useful sign for diagnosis when present. Eye changes may have an infiltrative and a noninfiltrative component. The noninfiltrative component reflects hyperthyroidism-induced sympathetic stimulation of the levator palpebrae superioris muscles of the eyelids. The infiltrative component is much more common, consisting of deposition of glycosaminoglycans and a mononuclear cellular infiltration of the orbital soft tissues and extraocular muscles. In severe cases, corneal ulceration results, and loss of sight may occur.

What thyrotoxic symptoms might this patient have?
Show answer

Symptoms of thyrotoxicosis include palpitations, weight loss, increased appetite, nervousness, increased sweating, and decreased heat tolerance. Symptoms result from increased metabolic rate and sympathetic overactivity.

Question 1
1
What is the mechanism of enlargement?
Show answer

Increased TSH signaling, which is initiated by a mechanism described later, causes G protein–coupled receptor activation, production of cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP), and modulation of gene expression, leading to increased thyroid follicular cell mitogenesis.


Figure 24-8 Homeostasis in the hypothalamus-pituitary-thyroid axis and mechanism of action of thyroid hormones. Secretion of thyroid hormones (T 3 and T 4 ) is controlled by trophic factors secreted by both the hypothalamus and the anterior pituitary. Decreased levels of T 3 and T 4 stimulate the release of thyrotropin-releasing hormone (TRH) from the hypothalamus and thyroidstimulating hormone (TSH) from the anterior pituitary, causing T 3 and T 4 levels to rise. Elevated T 3 and T 4 levels, in turn, feed back to suppress the secretion of both TRH and TSH. TSH binds to the TSH receptor on the thyroid follicular epithelium, which causes activation of G proteins, and cAMP-mediated synthesis and release of thyroid hormones (T 3 and T 4 ). In the periphery, T 3 and T 4 interact with the thyroid hormone receptor (TR) to form a hormonereceptor complex that translocates to the nucleus and binds to so-called thyroid response elements (TREs) on target genes to initiate transcription.

Question 1
1
What is the molecular basis of these changes?
Show answer

Patients with Graves disease develop autoantibodies to the TSH receptor. These antibodies stimulate the TSH receptor, causing increased cellular signaling resulting in hypertrophy, hyperplasia, and increased secretion of T3 and T4.


Figure 6-16 Mechanisms of antibody-mediated injury.

A, Opsonization of cells by antibodies and complement components and ingestion by phagocytes.

B, Infl ammation induced by antibody binding to Fc receptors of leukocytes and by complement breakdown products.

C, Antireceptor antibodies disturb the normal function of receptors. In these examples, antibodies to the acetylcholine (ACh) receptor impair neuromuscular transmission in myasthenia gravis, and antibodies against the thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH) receptor activate thyroid cells in Graves disease.

Image 7


Thyroid gland, Hashimoto thyroiditis - Gross, cut surface

This specimen represents the thyroid from Case B. Hashimoto thyroiditis is a destructive, autoimmune, inflammatory disease of the thyroid gland. There are many similarities in the course of this disease and that of Graves disease. Some patients with Hashimoto thyroiditis pass through a period of hyperthyroidism, and patients with Graves disease may go on to develop destruction of the thyroid gland with resulting hypothyroidism. Note the pale gray appearance of the cut surface and the accentuation of the lobular structure.

Question 1
1
What do you think is causing enlargement of this thyroid gland?
Show answer

In Hashimoto disease, enlargement of the thyroid gland occurs because of infiltration by lymphoid cells.


Question 2
2
What would be the gross appearance of a thyroid gland after a very long period of chronic inflammation?
Show answer

Prolonged chronic inflammation causes destruction of follicles and replacement by scar tissue, causing the thyroid gland to shrink.

Question 1
1
When germinal centers are formed, what is the likely nature of the lymphoid infiltrate?
Show answer

The presence of germinal centers indicates infiltration by B lymphocytes.


Question 2
2
What does the presence of germinal centers indicate?
Show answer

The presence of germinal centers indicates that there is local production of an immune response, mainly involving B cells.


Question 3
3
Is this disease cell mediated or antibody mediated?
Show answer

Hashimoto thyroiditis is believed to be initiated by the development of autoreactive T cells, but B cells are also involved, and several autoantibodies develop.


Figure 24-10 Pathogenesis of Hashimoto thyroiditis. Breakdown of peripheral tolerance to thyroid autoantigens, results in progressive autoimmune destruction of thyroid cells by infi ltrating cytotoxic T cells, locally released cytokines, or by antibody-dependent cytotoxicity.


Question 4
4
Both Graves disease and Hashimoto thyroiditis result from the creation of autoantibodies targeting the thyroid. Why does Graves disease cause hyperthyroidism but Hashimoto thyroiditis cause hypothyroidism?
Show answer

The autoantibodies in Graves disease stimulate the TSH receptor, leading to thyroid hormone production and causing hyperthyroidism. In contrast, the autoantibodies in Hashimoto’s thyroiditis lead to destruction of the thyroid parenchyma, leading to tissue loss and hypothyroidism (though there may be an initial transient hyperthyroidism).

Question 1
1
When the inflammation subsides, what replaces the damaged thyroid follicles?
Show answer

Collagen replaces thyroid follicles.


Question 2
2
Do thyroid epithelial cells have the capacity to regenerate?
Show answer

Yes. They can regenerate.


Question 3
3
Review the classification of tissues into three categories based on their regenerative capacity.
Show answer

The three categories are labile cells, stable cells, and permanent cells. Labile cells (e.g., gut epithelium) are continuously dividing. Stable cells (e.g., liver, thyroid epithelium, endothelium) are not dividing but can enter the cell cycle. Permanent cells (e.g., neurons) either cannot divide or can divide only very rarely.

Question 1
1
What does the presence of collagen indicate about the duration of this inflammatory process?
Show answer

It indicates the presence of chronic inflammation, which caused tissue damage and subsequent repair.


Question 2
2
What factors determine whether a tissue damaged by inflammation is replaced by a scar or by normal cells of that tissue?
Show answer

This is determined by two factors. The first factpr is the type of cells: labile, stable, or permanent; the former two types can regenerate. The second factor is whether damage to the supporting framework has occurred. If the supporting stroma collapses, even if cells can regenerate, the normal tissue architecture cannot be replaced; this occurs in cirrhosis of the liver.

This image illustrates end-stage chronic thyroiditis on the left compared with a normal thyroid gland on the right.
