Learning Objectives
  • 1. Identify the two most common etiologic agents causing pelvic inflammatory disease and the pathologic changes they produce.
  • 2. Discuss the characteristic clinical and pathologic findings caused by herpes simplex virus (HSV) infections:
    • a. fever blisters
    • b. genital HSV infection
    • c. HSV encephalitis
    • d. disseminated neonatal HSV
  • 3. Describe the pathology of the cervix in Trichomonas infection, and discuss the utility of a saline preparation to visualize the protozoan.
  • 4. Describe the pathologic changes produced by Treponema pallidum.
  • 5. Define the stages of syphilis infection, and discuss the clinical features and characteristic laboratory changes in each stage. The laboratory tests are as follows:
    • a. VDRL (Venereal Disease Research Laboratory
    • b. RPR (rapid plasma reagin)
    • c. FTA-Abs (fluorescent treponemal antibody absorption)
    • d. MHA-TP (microhemagglutination assay for T. pallidum antibodies)
    • e. darkfield microscopy
  • 6. Describe the clinical features and pathologic changes produced by Chlamydia trachomatis.

Scenario

C.K. was a 21-year-old college student who presented to the student health service for her yearly pelvic examination and Pap smear. She had been sexually active and used oral contraceptives without barrier protection for birth control. She denied any problems. The physician noted a 1-cm shallow erythematous ulcer on her cervix, which was not tender to manipulation. The physician scraped the ulcer and placed the scrapings on a slide to examine under fluorescence microscopy (Images 1-3). An RPR test was 1:64, and the FTA-Abs test was reactive. Upon questioning about sexual contacts, C.K. stated that her boyfriend had had a rash about a month before, present over almost his entire body, but particularly on his palms (Image 4). C.K. was diagnosed with syphilis and was treated with penicillin and told to return to clinic in 2 weeks; at that time, the ulcer had resolved, and she felt well.

One year later, C.K. came to the clinic again, complaining of excruciating vaginal pain during sexual intercourse. The gynecologist did a pelvic examination, noting two discrete ulcers along the vaginal wall. A specimen from the ulcers was sent to the virology laboratory for culture. Image 5 shows the characteristic cytologic changes that were seen microscopically.


Image 1

Treponema pallidum on direct fluorescent antibody test - High power

Examination of scrapings from the cervical ulcer shows thin coiled organisms characteristic of Treponema pallidum. This test uses a fluorescent antibody specific for T. pallidum; a darkfield examination would look similar.

Question 1
1
Which tests are used to diagnose syphilis? Review the utility of the various tests in different stages of syphilis.
Show answer

In general, the serologic diagnosis of syphilis should consist of a nontreponemal test (VDRL or RPR) followed by confirmation with a treponemal-specific test (MHA-TP or FTA-Abs). Because of the high false-negative rate of the nontreponemal tests in tertiary syphilis, a specific treponemal test should be ordered to help confirm the diagnosis if the screening nontreponemal test is negative.

Skin, chancre of syphilis - Low power

A low-power view of a syphilitic chancre, typically seen in the genital region of an infected adult, shows ulceration with a dense inflammatory response in the dermis and subcutaneous tissue, giving the lesion a hazy blue color on hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) stained slides. The inset demonstrates lymphocytic and plasmacytic infiltration of a vessel wall, representing an early stage of obliterative endarteritis. Eventually, the vascular lumen will be obliterated, and the wall will undergo fibrosis. Obliterative endarteritis and plasmacytic infiltrates are the hallmark pathologic features of syphilis.

Question 1
1
What stains are necessary to visualize the spirochetes in tissue sections?
Show answer

Silver stains, not routine bacterial stains, are necessary to see the organisms in tissue sections. These are typically either the Steiner stain or the Warthin-Starry stain.

Question 1
1
What are the potential complications of PID?
Show answer

The acute complications of PID include peritonitis and bacteremia, which in turn may result in endocarditis, meningitis, and suppurative arthritis. The chronic sequelae of PID include infertility and tubal obstruction, ectopic pregnancy, pelvic pain, and intestinal obstruction due to adhesions between the bowel and pelvic organs.

Question 1
1
How does PID predispose to ectopic pregnancy?
Show answer

Acute salpingitis associated with PID often leads to chronic salpingitis, characterized by chronic inflammation and scarring of the fallopian tube fimbriae. The ovum may not be able to traverse the scarred tube, and a fertilized egg may therefore implant in a location outside of its normal intrauterine location.

Image 9

Uterine cervical cytology, Trichomonas, Papanicolaou stain (Pap smear) - High power

Trichomonads may be seen on a Papanicolaou stain of cervical scrapings, typically called a Pap smear clinically. Trichomonas infection of the cervix is usually diagnosed with a "wet prep." This is a preparation in which the gynecologist suspends cervical scrapings in a saline solution on a slide and examines them under the microscope directly. Trichomonads in saline preparations resemble white blood cells, so it is necessary to identify the motility of the protozoan to differentiate between the two
