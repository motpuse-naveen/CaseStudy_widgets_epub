Scenario

A 15-year-old girl presents at her physician's office complaining of multiple firm papules with a diameter ranging from 2 to 7 mm on the plantar surfaces of both feet. Some lesions are tender, especially on pressure sites. Other lesions are confluent, and several are located on opposite sides of adjacent toes ("kissing" lesions). All of the lesions show extensive hyperkeratosis that can be removed with a scalpel. The patient asks you to remove the lesions because of the discomfort they cause when she walks. Although aggressive therapy is usually not warranted with this type of lesion, the physician elects to use cryosurgery to treat this patient.

Kissing lesions

Skin, verruca plantaris – Clinical presentation

The plantar surface of the foot shows multiple rough papules and plaques with a rough surface. The opposite sides of the first and second toes and of the third and fourth toes show "kissing lesions." The location of these verrucous lesions is characteristic of verruca plantaris. If the lesions were located on the palms, it would be called verruca palmaris. The most common wart is the verruca vulgaris. Lesions can occur almost anywhere on the body, but most frequently they are located on the hands. The venereal wart (condyloma acuminatum) occurs on the penis, female genitalia, and urethra, as well as in the perianal region.

Question 1
1
What is the causative agent of all of these verrucous lesions?
Show answer

Verruca plantaris, verruca palmaris, verruca vulgaris, and condyloma acuminatum are all caused by infection with human papillomavirus (HPV).

Skin, verruca plantaris – Low power

At low magnification, a verruca plantaris is a symmetrical lesion composed of a thickened epidermis with hyperkeratosis and an undulating surface (papillomatosis). This particular lesion was widely excised.

Question 1
1
Why would someone excise a verruca?
Show answer

Verrucae are self-limited lesions, regressing spontaneously within 6 months to 2 years. Verrucae can be excised for cosmetic reasons, because of inconvenience to the patient, or if the clinical diagnosis is in doubt. In patients who are immunosuppressed, verrucous lesions are often numerous and more aggressive.

Skin, verruca plantaris – High power

The nuclei of infected keratinocytes are enlarged with irregular contours and surrounded by a sharply defined pale cytoplasmic halo. This phenomenon is called koilocytosis. The stratum granulosum is thickened, and parakeratosis is present. These histopathologic changes are characteristic of human papillomavirus (HPV) infection.

Skin, verruca plantaris, histochemical stain for HPV - High power

An immunohistochemical stain for PAN-HPV is shown. All types of HPV are positive with this stain. The brown color in the nuclei of the keratinocytes represents positive staining.

Question 1
1
Which subtypes of HPV are often detected in malignant and dysplastic HPV-induced lesions?
Show answer

HPV subtypes 5 and 8 have been implicated in cutaneous squamous cell carcinomas arising in immunocompromised individuals. HPV subtypes 16 and 18 are common in dysplastic lesions and squamous cell carcinomas arising in the uterine cervix.

Question 2
1
Why do HPV subtypes 5 and 8, commonly found in verruca, have low oncogenic potential compared to high-risk HPV subtypes 16 and 18?
Show answer

High-risk HPV subtypes produce protein E6, which can inhibit p53 function and lead to tumorigenesis. Conversely, subtypes 5 and 8 product a variant E6 protein that has no effect on p53.

Question 1
1
What infectious agent is most frequently the cause of impetigo?
Show answer

Impetigo is most frequently caused by Staphylococcus aureus.

Skin, staphylococcal scalded skin syndrome (SSSS) - Medium power

The skin specimen shows superficial blistering. The roof of the blister is the stratum corneum, and the plane of the split involves the granular layer. This is very similar to bullous impetigo.

Question 1
1
Why does this biopsy specimen represent SSSS and not impetigo bullosa?
Show answer

In impetigo bullosa, a local infection with Staphylococcus aureus is present. The bacteria are seen along with a brisk inflammatory response. The production of bacterial toxin cleaves the desmoglein 1 molecule and causes acantholysis. The biopsy specimen shown here displays blistering and acantholysis but does not show inflammation or bacteria. In SSSS, the bacterial exotoxin is elaborated in a different location and causes cutaneous manifestations at distant sites.
