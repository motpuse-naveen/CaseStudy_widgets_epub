Learning Objectives
  • Nephrotic Syndrome
  • 1. Describe the clinicopathologic features of the nephrotic syndrome.
  • 2. List the causes of nephrotic syndrome, and distinguish them in terms of:
    • a. incidence in adults versus children
    • b. primary (eg, minimal change) versus secondary (eg, diabetes mellitus) causes
  • Minimal Change Disease (MCD)
  • 1. Describe the characteristic light microscopic (LM), immunofluorescence (IF), and electron microscopic (EM) findings of MCD.
  • 2. Describe the suggested mechanism for proteinuria in MCD. Know what happens to the negative charge of the glomerular basement membrane (GBM).
  • 3. Describe the response to steroids in MCD.
  • Focal Segmental Glomerulosclerosis (FSGS)
  • 1. Describe the characteristic light microscopic and EM findings of FSGS.
  • 2. Explain how patients with this pattern differ from patients with MCD in terms of response to steroids, progression of renal disease, hematuria, hypertension, and selectivity of proteinuria.
  • 3. Understand that FSGS may be idiopathic or secondary (eg, in human immunodeficiency virus [HIV] infection).
  • Membranous Glomerulonephritis (MGN)
  • 1. Define MGN, and distinguish between primary and secondary forms.
  • 2. Discuss the proposed immunologic mechanisms of MGN.
  • 3. Describe the morphologic glomerular changes of MGN by LM, IF, and EM.
  • 4. Discuss the salient clinical features (onset and course) of MGN.
  • Membranoproliferative Glomerulonephritis (MPGN)
  • 1. Describe the characteristic light microscopic features of MPGN.

Scenario

A previously healthy, 35-year-old man complained of recent fatigue and swelling of his feet and ankles. He also noted puffy eyes. Physical examination revealed an afebrile man with lower extremity edema extending to the knee, periorbital edema, and a small amount of ascites.

Laboratory results were as follows:

Urinalysis: Protein 4+
Glucose negative
Blood negative
Bilirubin negative
Microscopy: Rare RBCs, no WBCs, many oval fat bodies
Creatinine 0.8 mg/dL
BUN 18 mg/dL
Albumin 2.8 g/dL
Hematocrit 40%
Liver function tests Normal
Triglycerides 400 mg/dL
Cholesterol 375 mg/dL
24-Hour urine protein 11.2 g/24 h

BUN, Blood urea nitrogen; RBCs, red blood cells; WBCs, white blood cells.

Drug history was negative. Antinuclear antibody, human immunodeficiency virus, and hepatitis serologies were negative.

The patient developed right flank pain the following day. Ultrasound examination revealed renal vein thrombosis. A renal biopsy was performed.

Question 3
3
Based on the results of light microscopy, immunofluorescence, and EM of the renal biopsy specimen, what is the patient's diagnosis?
Show answer

The biopsy specimen reveals a membranous glomerulopathy with subepithelial deposits of electron-dense, immune complex–type material by EM. By light microscopy, this is illustrated by lack of increased glomerular cellularity and uniformly thickened GBMs. A silver stain shows spikes of GBM material projecting between the deposits, a feature better understood on the EM photograph. The immunofluorescence is typical of membranous glomerulonephritis, showing granular, peripheral capillary loops staining with immunoglobulin G and complement 3.


Figure 20-12 D, Diagrammatic representation of membranous nephropathy.


Question 4
4
What is the difference between primary and secondary membranous glomerulopathy?
Show answer

Primary membranous glomerulopathy is an autoimmune disease linked to human leukocyte antigen (HLA) alleles such as HLA-DQA1 and caused by autoantibodies to a renal antigen. Phospholipase A2 receptor has emerged as the most common autoantigen in recent years.

Secondary membranous glomerulopathy is a chronic immune complex–mediated disease wherein antibodies are formed against either endogenous or exogenous antigens. The most common associations are underlying metastatic tumors (melanoma and carcinoma of lung or colon), drugs (nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs, gold, penicillamine, captopril), infections (hepatitis B, hepatitis C, syphilis, malaria, schistosomiasis), and systemic lupus erythematosus.

Question 1
1
What are the general differences in the etiology of nephrotic syndrome in children versus adults?
Show answer

In children, primary glomerular disease accounts for 95% of the cases, the most common cause being MCD. By comparison, primary glomerular diseases are responsible for only 60% of cases in adults; membranous glomerulonephritis is the second most common primary renal disease in adults, after focal segmental glomerulosclerosis.

Question 3
3
What is the pathogenesis of MCD?
Show answer

The pathogenesis of MCD is not fully understood. There is no evidence of anti-GBM antibodies or immune complexes. There is evidence supporting the hypothesis that systemic immune dysfunction somehow results in visceral epithelial cell damage (podocytopathy), leading to effacement of foot processes and secondary alterations in the GBM resulting in proteinuria.

Figure 20-7 Epithelial cell injury. The postulated sequence is a consequence of antibodies specific to epithelial cell antigens, toxins, cytokines, or other factors causing injury; this results in foot process effacement and sometimes detachment of epithelial cells and protein leakage through defective GBM and filtration slits.

1
What would immunofluorescence (IF) show in this segmentally sclerotic focus?
Show answer

IF would be positive in the sclerotic focus, which would stain with antibodies to immunoglobulin M (IgM) and complement 3 (C3).


Question 2
2
What would IF show in a nonsclerotic part?
Show answer

IF would be negative in the nonsclerotic part.


Question 3
3
Is FSGS the result of immune complex injury?
Show answer

No. The basic lesion in primary FSGS is disruption of visceral epithelial cells, possibly by a circulating cytokine. The sclerosis and entrapment of plasma protein, including IgM and C3, are nonspecific. They result from hyperpermeability at sites of epithelial cell damage.

Question 1
1
Is FSGS a descriptive term, a clinicopathologic entity, or both?
Show answer

FSGS is both a descriptive term and a clinicopathologic entity, which can be divided into primary and secondary forms. Descriptively, FSGS implies sclerosis of some, but not all, glomeruli (ie, focal), and in the affected glomerulus, only a portion of the capillary tuft is involved (ie, segmental). Secondary FSGS exhibits these changes in patients with immunoglobulin A nephropathy, human immunodeficiency virus infection, heroin abuse, sickle cell disease, and massive obesity. Alternatively, primary FSGS is currently considered an idiopathic process related to visceral epithelial cell damage (podocytopathy), but recent research implicates a circulating factor or mutations of proteins composing the slit diaphragm.


Question 2
2
What is the difference in the clinical presentation and prognosis of minimal change disease (MCD) versus FSGS in children?
Show answer

FSGS and MCD manifest with nephrotic syndrome (MCD is more common). It is essential to distinguish them because their course and prognosis are different. Most cases of FSGS are less responsive to steroid treatment. Also, in contrast to the benign course of MCD, 50% of cases with FSGS end up with renal failure in 10 years.

What is proliferating in MPGN?
Show answer

Mesangial cells, capillary endothelial cells, monocytes, and lymphocytes proliferate in MPGN. The term endocapillary proliferation refers to the combination of capillary endothelial cell and leukocyte proliferation.

Show answer

The double contour appearance is caused by an apparent splitting of the basement membrane. This split appearance results from the intercalation of mesangial cell processes into the peripheral capillary loops.

Question 2
2
What are the types of membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis (MPGN) and their histologic findings?
Show answer

There are two types of primary MPGN. Type I shows a granular pattern of immunoglobulin G (IgG) and complement 3 (C3) deposition by IF. The immune complexes are subendothelial by electron microscopy (EM).


Figure 20-5 Localization of immune complexes in the glomerulus: (1) subepithelial humps, as in acute glomerulonephritis; (2) epimembranous deposits, as in membranous nephropathy and Heymann nephritis; (3) subendothelial deposits, as in lupus nephritis and membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis; (4) mesangial deposits, as in IgA nephropathy. EN, Endothelium; EP, epithelium; GBM, glomerular basement membrane; LD, lamina densa; LRE, lamina rara externa; LRI, lamina rara interna; MC, mesangial cell; MM, mesangial matrix. (Modifi ed from Couser WG: Mediation of immune glomerular injury. J Am Soc Nephrol 1:13, 1990.)

Type II (dense deposit disease) is characterized by C3 but not IgG in a granular-linear pattern on the basement membrane and as aggregates in the mesangium. On EM, there is a ribbon of electron-dense deposits within the GBM. The two types are distinguished best by EM.


Question 3
3
What is the pathogenesis of Types I and II MPGN?
Show answer

Type I MPGN is a pattern of glomerular injury that results from the activation of both the classical and alternative complement pathways in response to immune complex deposition.


Figure 20-17 C, Schematic representation of patterns in the two types of membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis. In type I there are subendothelial deposits; type II is characterized by intramembranous dense deposits (dense-deposit disease). In both, the basement membranes appear split when viewed in the light microscope.

Type II MPGN (dense deposit disease) belongs to a group of disorders called C3 glomerulopathies, and results from overactivation of the alternative complement system. Seventy percent of patients have a circulating autoantibody termed C3 nephritic factor that prevents inactivation of C3 convertase and favors activation of C3 and hypocomplementemia. Some patients have also been found to have mutations in alternative complement pathway proteins such as factor H.

Figure 20-18 The alternative complement pathway in dense deposit disease (Type II MPGN). Note that C3NeF, an antibody present in the serum of individuals with membranoproliferative glomerulonephritis, acts at the same step as properdin, serving to stabilize the alternative pathway C3 convertase, thus enhancing C3 activation and consumption, causing hypocomplementemia.
