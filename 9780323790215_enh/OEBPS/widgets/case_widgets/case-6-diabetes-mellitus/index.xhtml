Diabetes Mellitus

Learning Objectives

  • 1. Understand the major metabolic effects of insulin and glucagon, which is essential in understanding some of the metabolic aberrations in diabetes mellitus.
  • 2. Understand the classification of diabetes mellitus as defined by the National Diabetes Data Group. In addition, understand the clinical and biochemical definitions of impaired glucose tolerance and gestational diabetes mellitus.
  • 3. Be able to differentiate between insulin-dependent diabetes mellitus (IDDM, type 1 diabetes) and non–insulin-dependent diabetes mellitus (NIDDM, type 2 diabetes) with respect to pathogenesis and presentation.
  • 4. Describe the pathogenesis of the two acute metabolic complications of diabetes.
  • 5. Describe the pathogenesis of the long-term complications of diabetes, especially the vascular complications. List the major organs involved by microvascular and macrovascular disease. Describe the morphologic changes in various organs, particularly:
    • a. pancreas.
    • b. small and large blood vessels.
    • c. kidneys.
    • d. retina.
  • 6. Know the test of choice for monitoring control of diabetes in patients who do not measure their blood glucose levels regularly at home.

Scenario

A 12-year-old girl came to the emergency department with fatigue, weight loss, polydipsia, polyuria, vomiting, and drowsiness. On examination, she was found to have a blood pressure of 90/55 mm Hg, a pulse rate of 110 beats/min, cold extremities, and deep, sighing respirations (Kussmaul respiration).

Laboratory investigation at initial presentation produced the following results:

Urine glucose 4+
Urine ketone 3+
Plasma glucose 600 mg/dL
Sodium 130 mEq/L
Potassium 5.8 mEq/L
Bicarbonate 8 mEq/L
pH 7.25
PCO2 28 mm Hg
Blood urea nitrogen 5.8 mEq/L

PCO2, partial pressure of carbon dioxide.

After management of this acute episode, the patient was treated with twice-daily insulin therapy.

Twenty-five years later, at age 37, the patient presented to the emergency department with acute substernal chest pain that radiated to her neck and down her left arm. This pain lasted for at least 30 minutes, and was associated with nausea and sweating. Investigations done at this time revealed that her serum markers of myocardial injury were increased. Her creatine kinase MB isoenzyme (CK-MB) was 25 ng/mL (reference range, 0–3 ng/mL), her total CK was 500 U/L (reference range, 38–120 U/L), and her troponin I was 9.4 ng/mL (reference range, 0.05–0.5 ng/mL). An electrocardiogram revealed evidence of a myocardial infarction (ST segment changes and Q waves). After a 7-day stay in the hospital, she was discharged and asked to continue insulin therapy.

She was lost to follow-up, but presented 2 years later with generalized edema. On physical examination, there was no clinical evidence of cardiac failure. Neurologic examination revealed muscle weakness and loss of sensation in both lower extremities. She also had bilateral visual impairment. Examination of her retinas with an ophthalmoscope revealed microaneurysms in retinal vessels. All of her peripheral pulses were palpable. Laboratory tests revealed elevations of blood urea nitrogen and serum creatinine (both indicating renal failure). Urine examination revealed glucose 2+ and massive proteinuria.

Question 2
2
How has our increased understanding of the pathophysiology of neovascularization led to a new treatment for diabetic retinopathy?
Show answer

Diabetic retinopathy has traditionally been treated with laser photocoagulation to prevent new vessel growth. However, the signaling protein vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF) has been discovered to play a major role in the process of neovascularization. VEGF inhibitors have been developed and are now an effective treatment option for patients with diabetic retinopathy.

Question 1
1
What are long-term complications of diabetes mellitus?
Show answer

The long-term complications of diabetes mellitus include the following:

  • 1. Diabetes affects blood vessels of all sizes. Accelerated atherosclerosis (macroangiopathy) affects all of the usual sites—aorta, carotid arteries, peripheral arteries, and coronary arteries. Microangiopathy, or small vessel disease, affects capillaries throughout the body, but important clinical effects are seen in the kidneys and eyes. Diabetics have a 100-fold increase in atherosclerotic narrowing of medium-sized arteries such as the femoral and popliteal, and an attendant increase in gangrene.

Block of AGEs: Unlinking Glucose from Diabetic Complications

Roughly a third of diabetic patients will suffer significant cardiovascular complications within 5 to 10 years of their initial diagnosis. Ischemic heart disease, stroke, and peripheral vascular disease not only are major contributors to the morbidity and mortality associated with chronically elevated blood glucose but also account for the lion’s share of the clinical costs.


  Some of the pathology is attributable to intracellular mayhem. Thus, in cells like neurons and those of the endothelium, where insulin is not required to drive glucose transport, extracellular hyperglycemia causes intracellular hyperglycemia through mass action. Subsequent enzymatic reduction of the elevated intracellular glucose load causes a hyperosmolar state and—by consuming NADPH—a severe oxidative stress. Intracellular hyperglycemia also activates protein kinase C, leading to the production of profibrotic and proangiogenic cytokines.

  The other major consequence of hyperglycemia is nonenzymatic glycation of intra- and extracellular proteins to generate an irreversibly modified advanced glycation end-product (AGE). By binding to specific vascular wall and inflammatory cell receptors, AGEs are potently procoagulant and pro-inflammatory. The glycation chemistry also causes protein cross-linking that reduces matrix turnover and traps molecules in the interstitium (e.g., proatherogenic low-density lipoproteins).

  In view of the cellular and chemical effects of hyperglycemia, it is easy to see how the cardiovascular system takes a beating. Both large and small vessels are overall more prone to thrombosis and also to the development of accelerated atherosclerosis; in addition, protein cross-linking (with increased matrix synthesis) engenders changes that result in a stiffened and noncompliant vasculature. Such effects in the coronary circulation render the heart relatively ischemic, and cross-linking of myocardial collagen adversely affects both systolic contraction and diastolic relaxation.

  The current major therapeutic approach to diabetes focuses on reducing circulating glucose levels, either by improving insulin production or insulin responsiveness or—failing those—by administering exogenous insulin. Although this may control ongoing glycemia, patients are left with the accumulated backlog of AGEs and cross-linked proteins—which are slow to degrade and a cause of persistent cellular dysfunction.

  Thus, there is substantial enthusiasm for a new class of small-molecule cross-link “breakers” (e.g., aminoguanidine, alagebrium); these can chemically interact with carbonyl intermediates to prevent formation of irreversibly cross-linked adducts. As it turns out, these agents also chelate divalent cations that contribute to cellular oxidation reactions and are potent scavengers of oxidizing sugars and free radicals. By whatever mechanism, in both animal models and early human trials, a number of these molecules have been shown to improve arterial compliance, endothelial function, diastolic relaxation, and myocardial contractility. Although they may not be able to reverse severe end-stage vascular damage, AGE breakers hold substantial promise as agents for adjunctive therapy to limit the long-term cardiovascular complications of glycation-associated cross-linking.

Question 2
2
What is the pathogenesis of type 1 versus type 2 diabetes?
Show answer

Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune disease resulting in destruction of beta cells in the pancreatic islets. The autoimmune response is probably triggered by some unidentified environmental agent (eg, virus, chemical, toxin). In genetically predisposed individuals, beta cell injury leads to autoimmunity. At least one gene that regulates this phenomenon is linked to the human leukocyte antigen D (HLA-D) region. The importance of an environmental trigger is underscored by the fact that, even in identical twins, the concordance rate of type 1 diabetes is only 50%. Type 2, or non–insulin-dependent, diabetes is genetically and biochemically distinct from type 1 diabetes. Although there is a strong genetic predisposition, there is no evidence of autoimmunity or HLA linkage. It usually starts in adults (>30 years old), and 80% of the patients are obese. The deficiency of insulin is mild relative to that of type 1 diabetes, and the major factor, at least at the onset, seems to be insulin resistance (ie, the target tissues are resistant to the action of insulin).

Question 3
3
How is insulin secretion regulated, and how is this pathway targeted pharmacologically?
Show answer

Glucose enters pancreatic beta cells through the "glucose-sensing" insulin-independent glucose transporter-2 and is metabolized to adenosine triphosphate (ATP) via cellular respiration. An increase in blood glucose leads to elevated intracellular ATP, which inactivates the ATP-sensitive potassium pump. This inactivation leads to membrane depolarization, which causes Ca2+-induced release of stored insulin granules. The class of drugs known as sulfonylureas bind to and inhibit the ATP-sensitive potassium pump and thus increase release of insulin.

Question 2
2
What is the biochemical basis of diabetic ketoacidosis?
Show answer

The biochemical basis of diabetic ketoacidosis is as follows:

1. Shift in the hormonal balance in favor of the counter-regulatory hormones—glucagon, epinephrine, growth hormone, and cortisol—over insulin, often resulting from some form of stress.

2. Hyperglycemia results from decreased glucose uptake by tissues and increased gluconeogenesis and glycogenolysis.

3. Hyperglycemia induces an osmotic diuresis, which results in fluid and electrolyte loss.

4. Ketogenesis increases because of increased lipolysis triggered by the counter-regulatory hormones.

5. The free fatty acids that are released can be converted in the liver to ketone bodies.

6. Increased ketone bodies result in a metabolic acidosis, manifested as low pH and low bicarbonate, which is consumed in buffering the keto acids.

7. The metabolic acidosis stimulates the respiratory center to reduce the partial pressure of carbon dioxide (PCO2) to correct the acid–base disturbance; the resulting increased respiration, which is classic, is a deep, sighing respiration (Kussmaul respiration), which reduces the PCO2.
