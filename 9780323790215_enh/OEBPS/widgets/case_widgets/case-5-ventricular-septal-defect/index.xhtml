Scenario

A 2-month-old boy was brought for evaluation of poor feeding and "rapid breathing." Pregnancy and a home delivery were uncomplicated. In recent weeks, the mother had noted that the infant tended to sweat during feedings. At other times, his breathing seemed fast, and he was fussy. Physical examination showed a well-developed, irritable infant with a pulse of 160/min and a respiratory rate of 72/min. His skin and mucous membranes were pink. The precordium was hyperdynamic with systolic and diastolic murmurs. The liver was slightly enlarged. Biventricular hypertrophy was evident from the ECG. Echocardiography showed a large muscular ventricular septal defect with dilation of the left atrium and left ventricle. Cardiac catheterization showed moderate pulmonary hypertension with normal pulmonary vascular resistance. The infant was treated with digoxin and a diuretic, and his symptoms gradually resolved. Later, a palliative surgery was performed, and the patient did well postoperatively.

2
What is the major pathogenetic cause of congenital cardiac abnormalities?
Show answer

Sporadic genetic abnormalities are the major known causes of congenital heart disease. They result from single gene mutations, small chromosomal losses, and additions or deletions of whole chromosomes (trisomies and monosomies). They occur in a wide variety of protein types.

Question 1
1
Why does cyanosis develop in patients with large persistent VSDs?
Show answer

Increased volume and pressure load to the right ventricle and pulmonary circulation causes pulmonary hypertension and, ultimately, a reversal of the early left-to-right shunt to a right-to-left shunt, resulting in delivery of poorly oxygenated blood to the systemic circulation. This shunt reversal is known as Eisenmenger’s syndrome and is particularly dangerous in pregnant women with septal defects.


Question 2
2
What mechanism underlies the disappearance of the murmur in an infant with a VSD?
Show answer

The murmur disappeared because of spontaneous closure of the VSD, which occurs in 50% of patients. Closure may occur by fibrous obliteration progressing from the margins or by adherence of the tricuspid valve to the defect, where it acts as a patch.


Question 3
3
What was the cause of the diastolic murmur in the infant with the VSD?
Show answer

The diastolic murmur in the infant with the VSD was the result of the large volume of blood produced by the left-to-right shunt returning from the lungs to the left atrium and flowing across the mitral valve.



Image 3

Physiology of VSD - Schematic

In utero, the pulmonary circulation is a low-flow, high-resistance circuit. At birth, the lungs expand, and the lungs and pulmonary arteries become high-flow, low-resistance circuits. Pulmonary vascular resistance is normally much lower than the resistance of the systemic circulation. In fluid systems, flow is greatest along the path of least resistance. If a large VSD is present, left ventricular blood follows the path of least resistance, and a certain percentage is shunted into the right ventricle and pulmonary arteries. The clinical effects and functional significance of a VSD depend on its size. VSDs that are smaller than the aortic orifice have limited left-to-right shunts because the defect itself has some intrinsic resistance. Small VSDs, especially ones less than 0.5 cm, are often very loud and can close spontaneously. These children are generally asymptomatic and have normal chest radiographs and ECGs. Larger defects offer essentially no resistance to flow. They remain patent and allow a significant left-to-right shunt. Patients with large undiagnosed or uncorrected VSDs are at risk to develop secondary pulmonary vascular changes, chronic persistent elevations of pulmonary vascular resistance, and, eventually, right heart failure. The left ventricle and left atrium also experience volume overloading and hypertrophy.


Image 4
Tight stenotic lumen
Hypertrophic smooth muscle

Artery, small vessel changes in congenital heart disease, H&E, and elastic stains - Medium power

The media and intima of this small pulmonary artery are markedly thickened. The lumen of the vessel is much smaller than normal. This anatomic change is a consequence of the progressive increase in pulmonary blood flow that accompanies a large ventricular septal defect (VSD). At some point, these small vessel changes become irreversible, even if the VSD is repaired. After birth, pulmonary vascular resistance normally decreases rapidly. In patients with a medium-to-large-sized VSD, pulmonary vascular resistance decreases slowly. As pulmonary vascular resistance decreases, the degree of left-to-right shunt increases. In some cases, pulmonary blood flow can increase to be two to four times more than that in the systemic circuit. The smooth muscle in the walls of the pulmonary arteries and arterioles undergoes hypertrophy, and the intima thickens. (The elastic stain of the media in the right panel emphasizes the intimal thickness.) The resistance of the pulmonary circuit slowly increases. Increased pressure and flow also alter the right ventricle (see Image 3). There is often hypertrophy and dilation of the chamber. Progressive right ventricular failure can ensue. When pulmonary vascular resistance increases more than systemic resistance, the left-to-right shunt can reverse.

Question 1
1
Why is pulmonary hypertension less common in patients with ASD than ventricular septal defect?
Show answer

ASDs present an increased volume load to the pulmonary circulation, but at lower pressures than ventricular septal defects, and they are better tolerated by the pulmonary vasculature.

Heart, patent ductus arteriosus (PDA) - Gross

The ductus arteriosus is a structure that is normally patent in utero but closes after birth. It may remain patent in neonates who are hypoxic, particularly premature infants. A PDA may also be associated with ventricular septal defects, coarctation, or pulmonary or aortic stenosis.

Question 1
1
What type of shunt occurs in the early stages of a PDA?
Show answer

A left-to-right shunt occurs in the early stages of a PDA.

Question 2
2
What is the flow of blood in the ductus arteriosus before birth?
Show answer

Blood flows from the pulmonary artery to the aorta, bypassing the unoxygenated lungs. In healthy term infants, functional closure of the duct by constriction is complete within 1 to 2 days after birth due to increased arterial oxygenation, decreased pulmonary vascular resistance, and decreasing prostaglandin E2 levels. Complete irreversible closure occurs within the first few months of life.


Question 3
3
Why might a PDA be purposely preserved in an infant, and how could this be achieved pharmacologically?
Show answer

For an infant with a congenital heart malformation that lead to systemic or pulmonary outflow obstruction (such as aortic valve atresia), preservation of a patent PDA can be life-saving. This can be achieved through administering prostaglandin E.
