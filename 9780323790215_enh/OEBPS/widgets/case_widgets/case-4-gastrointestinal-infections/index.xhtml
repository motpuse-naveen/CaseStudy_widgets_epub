Identify the properties, particularly related to toxin production or invasive capabilities, of the organisms that produce the pathologic reactions.

Scenario

The patient, who had a several-day history of fever and cough productive of yellow-green sputum, was brought to the hospital from a nursing home. He was diagnosed as having pneumonia and was treated with a broad-spectrum antibiotic.

The patient initially improved, and his temperature became normal. However, 4 to 5 days after beginning treatment, he developed watery diarrhea and was again febrile. A stool sample was obtained with the following results:

  • Leukocytes (Image 1)
  • Ova and parasites — negative for Giardia and E histolytica
  • Culture — no Salmonella, Shigella, Campylobacter, Vibrio cholerae, or pathogenic Escherichia coli isolated
  • Enzyme immunoassay for Clostridium difficile toxin (Image 2)

Sigmoidoscopy revealed multiple yellow-white plaques overlying a hyperemic colonic mucosa (Image 3). Characteristic pathologic findings may be seen in Images 4, 5, and 6. The patient continued on IV antibiotics for pneumonia and began a course of oral vancomycin for C. difficile–induced pseudomembranous colitis. His diarrhea resolved, and he was discharged in good health to return to the nursing home.

Question 1
1
Are neutrophils in the stool more indicative of a viral or a bacterial infection?
Show answer

Neutrophils are more commonly present in bacterial infection than in viral infection. Most viruses that cause diarrhea affect the small intestine and do not cause ulceration or invade the mucosa. Cytomegalovirus (CMV) is one virus that does not follow this rule because it causes ulceration secondary to ischemic necrosis and direct invasion. However, CMV is not a common cause of infectious diarrhea in the immunocompetent host. A list of common gastrointestinal bacterial pathogens is included here.

Question 1
1
What is the mechanism of disease production by Clostridium difficile?
Show answer

The major factors that contribute to the pathogenesis of C. difficile–induced pseudomembranous colitis are:

  • 1. Alteration of the normal intestinal bacterial flora.
  • 2. Toxin production by rapidly growing C. difficile at a time when the growth of the other, normal, competing intestinal organisms are suppressed. C. difficile produces two toxins; toxin A is an enterotoxin, and toxin B is a cytotoxin. The toxins cause direct damage to the epithelium and also result in localized vascular thrombosis, which causes further epithelial destruction.
  • 3. Age-related susceptibility. Neonates and infants up to 1 year of age commonly harbor C. difficile in their normal intestinal flora without deleterious effects. Population-based studies have shown a 20- to 100-fold increased incidence of C. difficile toxin-positive stools in the 60+-year-old age group compared to the 10- to 20-year-old age group. In this patient, the normal flora of the gastrointestinal tract was altered by antibiotic therapy, which allowed for overgrowth of C. difficile.

Question 1
1
How do pseudomembranes form in this type of infection?
Show answer

The toxins produced by Clostridium difficile cause epithelial damage, which results in sloughing of the epithelium. In addition, there is an intense inflammatory response and formation of an exudate. The necrotic epithelium and the inflammatory exudate together form the pseudomembrane.

Question 1
1
How does the pathologic alteration here differ from that seen in cholera?
Show answer

The colon in patients with cholera is edematous without any other pathologic changes. Cholera is also a toxin-mediated disease, but epithelial destruction does not occur.


Question 2
2
What is the pathophysiologic mechanism of cholera infection?
Show answer

Cholera toxin A enters the intestinal epithelial cells and actives the stimulatory G protein Gαs, leading to constitutive adenylate cyclase activation. This causes elevated cyclic adenosine monophosphate (cAMP), which opens cystic fibrosis transmembrane conductance regulator (CTRF) chloride channels with resultant flux of chloride into the lumen. This creates an osmotic gradient that forces water into the lumen, resulting in massive diarrhea.

Figure 17-27 Cholera toxin transport and signaling. After retrograde toxin transport to the endoplasmic reticulum (ER), the A subunit is released by the action of protein disulfi de isomerase (PDI) and is then able to access the epithelial cell cytoplasm. In concert with an ADP-ribosylation factor (ARF), the A subunit then ADP-ribosylates G s α , which locks it in the active, GTP-bound state. This leads to adenylate cyclase (AC) activation, and the cAMP produced opens CFTR to drive chloride secretion and diarrhea.

Question 1
1
Why is this disease called pseudomembranous colitis instead of membranous colitis?
Show answer

The material forming the pseudomembrane consists of fibrin, neutrophils, and cellular debris, which is different from a membrane, which is composed of epithelial cells.

Small intestine, normal and viral gastroenteritis - Medium power

Viruses invade the enterocytes directly, causing destruction of the small intestinal epithelial cells. Typically, mild villous blunting (atrophy) is present. Severe blunting, as seen here, is described in infants with rotavirus infection. Along with the villous blunting, hyperplasia of the crypts occurs in an attempt to regenerate the epithelium. The high-power view shows the cellular composition of the lamina propria, which comprises lymphocytes and plasma cells. These cells are normal constituents of the duodenum and may be increased in viral gastroenteritis, as shown here.

Question 1
1
How does this picture differ pathologically from bacterial enterocolitis?
Show answer

Bacterial enterocolitis incites a neutrophilic response in contrast to viral enteritis, which typically induces a mononuclear cell response.

Question 1
1
Would one expect these pathologic alterations with Campylobacter enteritis?
Show answer

Yes. Acute bacterial enterocolitis is most commonly associated with Campylobacter, Shigella, Salmonella, or toxigenic Escherichia coli.


Image 9

Giardia

Duodenum, giardiasis - High power

This high-power view of the tip of a duodenal villus shows Giardia within the lumen and attaching to the epithelium. Giardia lamblia does not secrete toxins; they cause damage by decreasing expression of brush border enzymes, causing mechanical damage to the brush border and inducing apoptosis of small bowel epithelial cells.

Question 1
1
What are the typical morphologic changes of giardiasis?
Show answer

Giardiasis typically is associated with mild villous blunting in the small intestine and a mixed infiltrate in the lamina propria, although the morphologic changes may vary from none to severe villous blunting.

Colon, amebic enterocolitis - Low power

Amebae have cytotoxic enzymes and burrow into the colon wall. They are stopped by the muscularis propria and continue to spread in the submucosa, forming a characteristic flask-shaped ulcer. The neck of the ulcer is the initial site of entry of the organism, and the bottom of the flask results from the burrowing and spreading of the organisms.

Question 1
1
What is the infectious form of Entamoeba histolytica, the cysts or the trophozoites?
Show answer

The cysts are the infectious form of the organism. These are swallowed, followed by release of the trophozoites.


Image 11

Amebic trophozoites

Colon, amebic enterocolitis - High power

The amebae are seen within cellular debris and fibrin. Amebic organisms may be mistaken for histiocytes. They differ from histiocytes in that amebae have smaller nuclei with mature karyosomes.

Question 1
1
What organs are locations for disseminated Entamoeba histolytica?
Show answer

The liver is the typical location for amebic abscesses. Rarely, the amebae may disseminate to the lungs and brain.
