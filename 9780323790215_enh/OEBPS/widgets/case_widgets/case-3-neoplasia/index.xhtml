Scenario

A 60-year-old man presented to his primary care physician with complaints of headache and weakness in his left arm over the past several weeks. The day before his appointment, he experienced two episodes of uncontrollable shaking in his left arm and leg, accompanied by development of weakness in his left leg. On physical examination, he was found to have mild papilledema, decreased strength in his left arm and leg, and brisk deep tendon reflexes on the left side of his body compared with the right. A CT scan of his head revealed a ring-enhancing mass in the right frontal region (Image 1). He was referred to a neurosurgeon, who admitted him to the hospital for a biopsy.

Surgical exploration revealed a soft, partially necrotic mass expanding the right frontal lobe; a biopsy specimen was obtained (Images 2-4). After histologic diagnosis, the patient began radiation therapy. Despite therapy, the mass continued to grow and was associated with progression of left-sided weakness and seizure activity. He died following a seizure approximately 6 months after his initial diagnosis.

Postmortem examination revealed diffuse flattening of gyral crests and narrowing of sulci, more pronounced on the left side. Cut sections of the brain revealed that the right frontal lobe was expanded by a hemorrhagic, ill-defined mass that extended into the contralateral hemisphere. The microscopic features of the lesion were similar to the features noted in the biopsy specimen obtained 6 months earlier.

Head, right frontal lobe brain lesion – CT scan with contrast enhancement

CT scan shows a bright, ring-shaped lesion surrounding a hypodense center in the right frontal lobe. The bright appearance, or contrast enhancement, of the ring-like lesion is due to leakage of intravascular contrast material into the tissue. Question 1
1
What is the cause of the uncontrollable shaking of the left arm and leg that the patient experienced the day before seeing his primary care physician? Why does the patient have left-sided hyperreflexia?
Show answer

The shaking in this case was a primary motor seizure caused by involuntary firing of neurons in the right motor cortex, triggered by the adjacent right frontal lobe lesion. Because most axons from these cortical motor neurons cross in the medulla oblongata before descending in the spinal cord, their stimulation causes abnormal involuntary movement in the left arm and leg. The hyperreflexia is caused by an overall decrease in upper motor neuron input to anterior motor neurons in the spinal cord during periods between seizures.


Question 2
2
Why does this patient have headaches?
Show answer

The patient has headaches because of a generalized increase in intracranial pressure. The presence of papilledema, noted during his initial evaluation, is also an indicator of increased intracranial pressure.


Question 3
3
What types of lesions can produce the ring-enhancing appearance seen in this scan?
Show answer

The ring-enhancing appearance of this lesion is typical of glioblastoma and other high-grade gliomas. Other processes can produce an identical radiographic appearance, including other types of neoplasms (e.g., lymphomas, metastatic carcinoma), abscesses, central nervous system toxoplasmosis, and organizing infarcts.

Question 1
1
What is the diagnosis in this case?
Show answer

The diagnosis in this case is glioblastoma multiforme (GBM).


Question 2
2
What features led to this diagnosis?
Show answer

Astrocytic differentiation, infiltrative growth, cellular pleomorphism, and mitotic activity in this tumor characterize it as a high-grade astrocytic neoplasm. Necrosis and endothelial proliferation allow classification as a GBM.


Question 3
3
What are the clinical characteristics of this tumor?
Show answer

GBM is the most common and most aggressive of the primary central nervous system parenchymal tumors. Although usually encountered in older individuals, GBM can occur at any age, including infancy and childhood. These tumors may occur anywhere in the brain or spinal cord, but they arise most commonly in the cerebral hemispheres in adults. In contrast, many pediatric GBMs involve the brainstem. GBMs may arise de novo (primary GBMs) or may evolve from better differentiated infiltrating astrocytic neoplasms (secondary GBMs).


Question 4
4
What are some other examples of astrocytic neoplasms?
Show answer

The two most common forms of astrocytic tumors are (1) infiltrating astrocytic neoplasms and (2) pilocytic astrocytomas. Infiltrating astrocytic neoplasms, as the name implies, grow into the native brain parenchyma in an infiltrative fashion. The infiltrating astrocytomas are traditionally divided into three grades based on histologic features: diffuse astrocytoma, an intermediate-grade lesion known as anaplastic astrocytoma, and, at the most aggressive end of the spectrum, the highly aggressive GBM. Pilocytic astrocytomas, discussed subsequently, tend to be better circumscribed lesions than the infiltrating astrocytomas, and they generally have a much more favorable prognosis.

Question 1
1
Is this lesion malignant? What is meant by the term anaplastic astrocytoma?
Show answer

Although this lesion lacks histologically aggressive features (mitotic activity, vascular proliferation, and necrosis), diffuse astrocytomas typically progress, albeit much more slowly than glioblastomas. The natural history of diffuse astrocytoma may progress along two distinct pathways. Some lesions remain well differentiated and continue to grow, slowly allowing survival for many years. In other cases, the tumor may undergo transformation to an anaplastic astrocytoma or GBM with a much shorter survival time. GBM arising from a lower grade lesion can be distinguished from a de novo GBM by the presence of an adjacent lower grade component.


Question 2
2
What molecular genetic changes are found in GBM?
Show answer

Four molecular subtypes of GBM are now recognized. The classic subtype harbors PTEN mutations, chromosome 10 deletions, and EGFR amplifications. The proneural subtype shows p53 and IDH1/2 mutations and is common in secondary GBMs. The neural subtype shows overexpression of neural markers NEFL, GABRA1, SYT1, and SLC12A5. The mesenchymal subtype exhibits NF1 deletions and overexpression of genes in the NF-ΚB pathway.

Question 1
1
What is the most common site for pilocytic astrocytomas? What are some of the major differences between pilocytic astrocytomas and infiltrating astrocytomas?
Show answer

Pilocytic astrocytomas most commonly occur in the cerebellum. Pilocytic astrocytomas may occur in children and adults, but they are much more common in children. In contrast to the infiltrating astrocytomas, pilocytic astrocytomas tend to be comparatively circumscribed and can sometimes be completely resected. If completely removed, their prognosis is quite good, with survival rates higher than 90%. When pilocytic astrocytomas arise in areas not amenable to surgical resection (e.g., hypothalamus, thalamus), the prognosis is much more guarded.


Question 2
2
What are two other types of glial neoplasms?
Show answer

The astrocytic neoplasms, as a group, account for most primary central nervous system tumors. Other types of glial neoplasms include oligodendrogliomas and ependymomas. Oligodendrogliomas are infiltrating neoplasms, occurring most commonly in the cerebral hemispheres and composed of cells with rounded nuclei and inconspicuous cell processes reminiscent of normal oligodendroglial cells. Well-differentiated and anaplastic variants of oligodendroglioma occur and are important to distinguish from astrocytic neoplasms because of the greater sensitivity of some oligodendroglial tumors to certain forms of chemotherapy. For this reason, cytogenetic studies to identify deletions at 1p (common in oligodendrogliomas) are commonly used to confirm the oligodendroglial nature of a glial neoplasm. Ependymomas are neoplasms composed of cells that mimic, to varying degrees, the morphology of normal ependymal cells. As might be anticipated, ependymomas usually arise within, or adjacent to, the ventricular system.

Meningiomas are common central nervous system tumors that arise from the cells lining the arachnoid mater (arachnoid cap cells, or meningothelial cells). They are usually attached to the dura mater and tend to displace, rather than infiltrate, adjacent brain parenchyma. The tumor illustrated here was attached to the falx cerebri and manifested as a circumscribed, parasagittal mass. Because of their slow growth, many meningiomas reach a very large size before coming to clinical attention.

A wide variety of histologic patterns may be encountered in these tumors.

The last form of primary central nervous system tumor to be considered in this group is the medulloblastoma, a neoplasm composed of primitive, pleuripotential cells, likely originating from the external granular cell layer of the cerebellum. Question 1
1
Describe the molecular genetics of medulloblastoma.
Show answer

There are four molecular classes of medulloblastoma. The WNT type shows abnormalities in the WNT signaling pathway and has an excellent prognosis. The SHH type shows mutations in sonic hedgehog signaling pathway, occurs in younger patients, may show MYC amplification, and has an intermediate prognosis. Group 3 medulloblastoma often shows MYC amplification and isochromosome 17, occurs in young patients, and has a poor prognosis. Group 4 medulloblastoma shows isochrosome 17, usually without MYC amplification, and has an intermediate prognosis.

Medulloblastomas typically extend from the cerebellum into the subarachnoid space or fourth ventricle, or both, and can disseminate extensively throughout the subarachnoid space. Because of this, some medulloblastomas require irradiation of the entire neuraxis.

Brain, metastatic carcinoma – Gross, coronal section

This section contains at least three metastatic deposits. Carcinomas that metastasize to the brain parenchyma reach the brain via arterial channels. Similar to other embolic processes, parenchymal metastases are most commonly encountered in the territory of the middle cerebral artery, often implant at gray-white junctions, and are often multiple. They are typically well circumscribed. This patient's primary tumor was in the lung.

Question 1
1
What are common sites of primary tumors that metastasize to the brain?
Show answer

Although any visceral carcinoma can metastasize to the brain parenchyma, the lung is the most common primary site. An estimated 15% to 20% of cases of lung cancer manifest with neurologic abnormalities. Other important primary sites include breast, skin (melanomas), and, less commonly, gastrointestinal tract.


Question 2
2
Name three familial syndromes that can present with brain tumors.
Show answer

Tuberous sclerosis, von Hippel Lindau, and neurofibromatosis can all present with a variety of central nervous system (CNS) and non-CNS tumors.


Question 3

What are two other noncancerous mass lesions that can present in the CNS?
Show answer

Both tuberculosis and sarcoidosis can present as mass lesions in the CNS.
