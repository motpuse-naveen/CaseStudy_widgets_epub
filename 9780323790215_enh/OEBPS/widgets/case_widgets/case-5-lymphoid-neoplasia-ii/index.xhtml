Scenario

A 66-year-old man has noticed nontender enlarged lymph nodes in his neck and inguinal region of 5 months’ duration. He also tells you that he has lost 5 to 7 lb in the last 2 months. He denies fever or night sweats. His complete blood count (CBC) is unremarkable; specifically, his lymphocyte count is not elevated. Radiographic examination reveals retroperitoneal adenopathy. A large lymph node from the neck region is removed.

Image 1

Lymph node, follicular lymphoma, low grade - Very low power

Follicular lymphoma is the most common type of non-Hodgkin lymphoma in the United States (40% to 50% of cases). It tends to produce widespread, painless, slowly enlarging lymphadenopathy that may wax and wane. It is generally disseminated at presentation and has a high frequency of bone marrow involvement. Although indolent (median survival is 7 to 9 years), it is considered incurable. The natural history is not significantly modified by therapy, so a common approach to treatment is observation until symptoms necessitate chemotherapy and anti-CD20 immunotherapy. Follicular lymphomas closely recapitulate normal germinal centers architecturally and cytologically. At low power in this image, one may appreciate the characteristic uniform, closely packed, follicular structures of follicular lymphoma.

Question 1
1
What characteristic cytogenetic abnormality is seen in follicular lymphoma?

t(14;18) is seen in follicular lymphoma.


Question 2
2
What is the molecular consequence of this translocation, and how may it relate to the pathogenesis and clinical features of this disease?

t(14;18) results in juxtaposition of the BCL2 gene on chromosome 18 and the immunoglobulin heavy chain gene on chromosome 14. This juxtaposition results in overexpression of the BCL2 gene, whose product, BCL2 protein, has antiapoptotic effects. Follicular lymphomas are neoplasms with decreased apoptosis rather than increased proliferation, resulting in a slow accumulation of long-lived cells. Because most chemotherapeutic agents are most effective against rapidly proliferating cells, the slowly proliferating cells of follicular lymphoma are difficult to eradicate fully.

Follicular lymphoma, predominantly small cleaved cells - High power

The nodules of follicular lymphoma are composed of a mixture of small, cleaved cells (centrocytes) and large, noncleaved cells (centroblasts), closely resembling the normal cytologic spectrum seen in normal germinal centers. The relative proportions of the two cell types constitute the basis for the common method of grading of follicular lymphoma. Although the clinical significance of follicular lymphoma grading is controversial, generally tumors with more large cells have a higher proliferative rate and are considered to be more aggressive.

Image 3

Diffuse large cell transformation

Follicular lymphoma, small cleaved cell - High power

Part of the natural history of follicular lymphoma is transformation to a more aggressive subtype, usually diffuse large cell type, as illustrated here. This transformation occurs in 30% to 50% of cases, although it is generally believed that if a patient with follicular lymphoma survives long enough, he or she eventually experiences transformation.

Question 1
1
What is the prognosis of transformed follicular lymphoma?

The prognosis is poor. The median survival is less than 1 year. If the large cell transformation is successfully treated, the follicular lymphoma commonly recurs.


Question 2
2
Can other types of transformation occur?

Yes. Rarely, follicular lymphomas can acquire a MYC translocation and transform to a Burkitt-like lymphoma.

Chronic lymphocytic leukemia/small lymphocytic lymphoma (CLL/SLL)

CLL is the most common form of leukemia in the United States and Europe. CLL/SLL is a disease of older individuals with a male predominance. It commonly involves lymph nodes and is distinguished from SLL only on the basis of whether there is a peripheral blood lymphocytosis. The tumor cells are identical in virtually all respects in these two disorders, and they are commonly considered a single disease with a spectrum of manifestations. The cells of CLL/SLL are small and very round and uniform, with scant cytoplasm and indistinct nucleoli. These tumors are incapable of forming follicles, and they infiltrate tissue in a diffuse pattern. Similar to follicular lymphoma, this tumor has a low proliferation rate and accumulates slowly over long periods. Similar to follicular lymphoma, CLL/SLL may transform to a more aggressive neoplasm in a few cases.

Question 1
1
What is a distinctive immunophenotype characteristic of CLL/SLL?

In addition to the expression of typical B-cell markers such as CD19, CD20, and surface immunoglobulin, the cells also aberrantly express the T-cell–associated antigen CD5. This antigen is expressed on a small normal B-cell population in adults that is thought to be involved with autoimmunity. Among B-cell lymphomas, CD5 is expressed primarily in CLL/SLL and mantle cell lymphoma.


Question 2
2
How do patients with CLL/SLL commonly present clinically?

CLL/SLL is commonly an incidental finding in an asymptomatic older patient. When symptoms are present, they include generalized lymphadenopathy, fatigue, anorexia, and weight loss.


Question 3
3
What is the primary determinant of prognosis in CLL/SLL?

Traditionally, clinical stage is the primary determinant. The staging of CLL depends on such factors as the presence of lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly, and cytopenias. More recently, other biologic features of CLL/SLL have been discovered that may be more important than clinical staging, including the presence or absence 11q and 17p deletions and expression of Zap-70. Symptomatic patients are generally treated with “gentle” chemotherapy and anti-CD20 immunotherapy. Approximately 5% to 10% of patients undergo Richter transformation to DLBCL with a poor prognosis.


Image 5

Mantle cell lymphoma (MCL) - High power

Although MCL is not an indolent neoplasm and is uncommon (4% of non-Hodgkin lymphoma), it is discussed here because of pathologic similarities to follicular lymphoma and chronic lymphocytic leukemia/small lymphocytic lymphoma (CLL/SLL) and because it illustrates another example of a chromosomal translocation that is important in pathogenesis. MCL is usually composed of small, slightly irregular (clefted) cells arranged in a diffuse, nodular, or mantle zone (surrounding reactive germinal centers) pattern. Similar to follicular lymphoma and CLL/SLL, it is usually disseminated at presentation with frequent involvement of blood and bone marrow. Despite its innocuous histologic appearance and its resemblance to indolent lymphomas, MCL is an aggressive tumor with median survivals of only a few years. Similar to the indolent tumors CLL/SLL and follicular lymphoma (and in contrast to other aggressive B-cell tumors), it is considered incurable.

Question 1
1
What translocation is commonly seen in MCL? What is the consequence of this translocation?

t(11;14) is commonly seen in MCL. This translocation results in juxtaposition of the immunoglobulin heavy chain gene on chromosome 14 and the cyclin D1 gene on chromosome 11, resulting in overexpression of the latter gene. Cyclin D1 is a cell cycle regulator, active in the G1 phase of the cell cycle. Overexpression of this protein is believed to be important in the pathogenesis of this disease. Immunohistochemical staining for cyclin D1 is also an important diagnostic clue to the diagnosis of MCL.


Question 2
2
From what stage of B-cell differentiation is MCL thought to derive?

MCL is thought to derive from the pregerminal center, specifically the primary follicle/mantle zone cells.

Stomach, mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue (MALT) lymphoma (extranodal marginal zone lymphoma) - Low power

Lymphomas of MALT are unique among indolent B-cell lymphomas because they are generally localized at presentation and may be cured with local therapy. They are thought to arise as a result of chronic immune stimulation. Over time, there is a “polyclonal to monoclonal transition” in which a B-cell clone merges after unknown mutation(s). With time, more characteristic mutations are acquired and the tumor progresses. These tumors have among the best prognoses of all non-Hodgkin lymphomas. The tumors manifest in extranodal sites as mucosal/submucosal infiltrates of small cells with slightly irregular nuclei and moderately abundant pale cytoplasm. These cells are thought to be related to marginal zone cells (a normal B-cell compartment seen best in the spleen). A characteristic feature of these lymphomas is their tendency to invade epithelial structures to produce so-called lymphoepithelial lesions.

Gastrointestinal Disease Case 3, Image 7 (stomach, MALT lymphoma, gross)

Question 1
1
What are the most common sites of MALT lymphoma?

The stomach, salivary glands, orbit, thyroid gland, and lung are the most common sites.


Question 2
2
What infectious agent has been implicated as a causative factor in gastric MALT lymphoma?

Helicobacter pylori has been implicated. It is thought that gastric mucosal infection with this bacterium causes recruitment of lymphoid tissue to the stomach, and that prolonged immune stimulation predisposes to the outgrowth of clonal B cells. In early stages of disease, gastric MALT lymphomas, although clonal, are not truly autonomous and still require antigenic stimulation for growth. Antibiotic therapy to eradicate H. pylori often cures the lymphoma. Chronic immune stimulation has also been implicated as a causative factor in other MALT sites, such as the salivary glands and the thyroid gland. In these instances, the stimulation results from autoimmune disease (e.g., Sjögren syndrome, Hashimoto thyroiditis), however, rather than an exogenous agent.

Question 3
3
Are there any common cytogenetic translocations seen in MALT lymphomas?

The most common abnormality is t(11;18), involving the API2 and MALT1 genes. This translocation produces a fusion protein that activates NFKB signalling. t(11;18) is seen in 30% of MALT lymphomas. In the stomach, its presence is associated with high stage and refractoriness to antibiotic therapy.

Skin, mycosis fungoides (MF) - Low power

Approximately 15% of non-Hodgkin lymphomas in the United States are of peripheral T-cell origin (in contrast to thymic T-cell/lymphoblastic processes, discussed in previous cases). Classification of peripheral T-cell lymphomas has been generally unsatisfactory. Most are quite aggressive clinically. One of the few peripheral T-cell lymphomas that are characteristically indolent, and the only one common enough in the United States to warrant discussion here, is MF. MF is a primary cutaneous T-cell lymphoma composed of CD4+ cells that manifests with localized or generalized cutaneous patches, plaques, or tumors. It may clinically mimic various inflammatory skin disorders. The infiltrates of MF in the skin are primarily localized in the epidermis and upper dermis. The tendency to invade the epidermis is known as epidermotropism. This epidermotropism sometimes may produce characteristic clusters of neoplastic cells within clear spaces in the epidermis, known as Pautrier microabscesses. The neoplastic cells of MF commonly have highly convoluted nuclei, producing a cerebriform appearance.

Question 1
1
What is the prognosis of MF?

The prognosis is relatively good with a median survival of 8 to 9 years. Similar to indolent B-cell lymphomas, MF is commonly disseminated at presentation and is essentially incurable. Also, a small proportion of tumors may transform to a more aggressive subtype.


Question 2
2
What is the difference between MF and Sézary syndrome?

Sézary syndrome is a variant of MF that is characterized by generalized erythroderma and prominent leukemic involvement by tumor cells. MF and Sézary syndrome are considered different expressions of the same disease.


Question 3
3
What are possible reasons for the striking epidermotropism seen in MF?

Possible reasons include the presence of skin-specific homing molecules on the lymphoma cells or dependence on skin-specific factors for growth and survival.

Hodgkin lymphoma (HL), Reed-Sternberg (RS) cells - High power

Hodgkin cells are the malignant cells of HL. These neoplastic cells are generally far outnumbered in the tumor mass by reactive cells such as small lymphocytes, eosinophils, plasma cells, and histiocytes. This contrasts with non-Hodgkin lymphomas (NHLs), in which the neoplastic cells generally form the bulk of the tumor. The prototype Hodgkin cell is the RS cell. These images show classic binucleate RS cells with surrounding small lymphocytes, a plasma cell, and an eosinophil. The World Health Organization recognizes 5 types of HL: (1) nodular sclerosing, (2) mixed cellularity, (3) lymphocyte-rich, (4) lymphocyte depleted, and (5) lymphocyte predominant. In the first four forms, referred to as classic HL, the RS cells show a similar immunophenotype with lack of expression of CD45 (leukocyte common antigen) and B-cell and T-cell markers. Instead, they express the activation marker CD30 and the myelomonocytic antigen CD15. The neoplastic cells of lymphocyte-predominant HL, on the other hand, are positive for CD45 and B-cell markers such as CD20 and lack CD15 and CD30.

Question 1
1
What are some general characteristics of HL?

Overall, HL accounts for less than 1% of new cancers in the United States, spreads in a contiguous lymph node distribution (in contrast to NHL), and has an average age at diagnosis of 32 years. It is characterized by the presence of a distinctive neoplastic cell, the RS cell. These neoplastic cells are mixed with a variable number of inflammatory cells.

Question 2
2
Current evidence supports what cell of origin for HL?

All forms of HL seem to be derived from B cells.


Question 3
3
What virus has been linked to the pathogenesis of HL?

The Epstein-Barr virus genome can be identified in RS cells in 40% to 50% of cases, particularly in the mixed cellularity subtype (>90% EBV+). It is clonally integrated, suggesting that it is not a passenger virus. Epidemiologic studies have documented an increased risk of HL in patients with a history of infectious mononucleosis.

Hodgkin lymphoma, nodular sclerosis - High power

A higher magnification of the nodular lymphoid areas from the previous Image 10 shows small lymphocytes and several Hodgkin cells. The latter cells are large, have prominent small nucleoli and pale-staining cytoplasm, and appear to be lying in clear spaces, or lacunae. The appearance of these lacunar cells is due to artifactual cytoplasmic retraction in formalin-fixed tissue. Classic binucleate Reed-Sternberg cells are infrequent.

Question 1
1
What are some general characteristics of nodular sclerosis HL? Do these characteristics differ from those of mixed cellularity HL?

Overall, nodular sclerosis HL is the most common HL subtype. It is more common in women and is likely to produce a mediastinal mass. It is a disease of adolescents or young adults, and it is known to have an excellent prognosis, especially when the disease is limited (low clinical stage), which is the typical presentation. Mixed cellularity HL is more common in men, has a bimodal age distribution (young adults and adults>55 years old), and commonly manifests at a higher stage. Although the natural history of mixed cellularity HL is more aggressive, current therapeutic regimens have erased the prognostic differences when corrected for stage at presentation.

Hodgkin lymphoma, lymphocyte predominant (LPHL) - Low power

This image shows the typical nodular low-power appearance of LPHL. This appearance differs from nodular sclerosis in that fibrous bands are not present surrounding the nodules. A higher power image shows an infiltrate of small lymphocytes and admixed large atypical cells. The latter cells have an appearance similar to popcorn (popcorn, or lymphocytic and histiocytic [L&H] cells). Classic Reed-Sternberg cells are rare or absent in this subtype of Hodgkin lymphoma (HL). In this subtype of HL, the reactive infiltrate is restricted to lymphocytes and histiocytes. This is usually a disease of young men, and its prognosis is excellent. In some cases of LPHL, the disease is stable without therapy. Nodular sclerosis and mixed cellularity HL are often curable, but without therapy they are generally rapidly progressive.
