Small Cell Lung Cancer

Learning Objectives

  • 1. List the cell types present in the various parts of the tracheobronchial tree. Be familiar with various forms of cellular differentiation and their possible relationship to specific types of lung cancer.
  • 2. Describe the relationship between lung cancer and cigarette smoking and other environmental hazards (e.g., radon).
  • 3. Describe the four major histologic forms of lung cancer and their biologic behavior patterns (relative to prognosis and therapy). Understand the importance of distinguishing non–small cell lung carcinoma and small cell lung carcinoma.
  • 4. Describe the usual histologic sequence of the development of lung cancers. Appreciate that lung cancers arise through a series of molecular events, and that some of these may be of diagnostic and prognostic importance.
  • 5. Describe the common location, typical histology, clinical manifestations, and cell of origin of bronchial carcinoid. Know whether its etiology is related to smoking.
  • 6. Know that the lung and pleura are common sites of metastatic spread of tumors arising elsewhere. Know which tumors frequently metastasize to the lungs.
  • 7. List the important causes of serous and inflammatory effusions in the pleural space.
  • 8. Describe the major primary tumor of the pleura and the environmental agent associated with it. Understand the role of smoking in asbestos-induced lung cancer and mesothelioma.
  • 9. List the major diseases associated with tobacco use and abuse.
  • 10. List the major cancer types associated with tobacco use.
  • 11. Understand the nature of the carcinogens and other harmful products in tobacco smoke and the mechanism by which they promote carcinogenesis.

Scenario

A chain-smoking, 48-year-old insurance company executive was referred to an endocrinologist with complaints of increasing facial hair, a florid complexion, weight gain, depression, and a productive cough. The level of free cortisol in his urine was very greatly increased. X-rays of his skull indicated that the pituitary fossa was of normal size. However, a chest x-ray demonstrated a large mass in the right lung, so he was sent to the Chest Clinic. Bronchoscopy revealed focal areas of thickening in the right upper and middle lobe bronchi and compression of the middle lobe bronchus. Several biopsy specimens were obtained, and a specimen of bronchial washing was sent for cytologic examination. The tumor cells were relatively small, tightly clustered, and had inconspicuous nucleoli. Immunohistochemical stains showed that the cells were positive for synaptophysin, chromogranin, Adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH) and calcitonin. Bone scans and biopsies were done. The patient was treated with combination chemotherapy. Within 8 weeks, the large chest mass was no longer evident on x-ray, and his original symptoms had almost completely disappeared. Urine cortisol levels returned to normal.

He returned 3 months later, complaining of severe headaches. A lumbar puncture was performed, and cells similar to those identified in the bronchial wash specimen were found in the cerebrospinal fluid. Despite further chemotherapy, his condition progressively declined, and he died 6 weeks later. At autopsy, multiple body sites were involved by tumor. Microscopic examination of the metastases revealed tumor with multiple forms of differentiation, including small cell, squamous cell, and adenocarcinoma elements.

Question 2
2
Is surgical resection a viable therapeutic option for limited-stage SCLC (i.e., SCLC tumor limited to one hemithorax)?
Show answer

Because most small cell carcinomas have metastasized prior to diagnosis, most centers do not routinely perform surgical resection of SCLC, even of tumors that appear to be limited to the lung by conventional staging procedures.

Question 1
1
Did this tumor originate from the respiratory epithelium?
Show answer

It almost certainly did, although some controversy exists about the cell of origin. Most authorities believe that small cell tumors arise from cells in the respiratory mucosa that are committed to endocrine cell differentiation.

1
Is nuclear molding diagnostic of this tumor type?
Show answer

No. However, it is a very characteristic feature. Other tumors with very high nuclear:cytoplasmic ratios (such as some lymphomas) may show nuclear molding.

Question 1
1
These granules are characteristic of what cell type?
Show answer

These are characteristic of neuroendocrine cells.


Question 2
2
What do the granules represent?
Show answer

They represent membrane-bound cytoplasmic storage sites of the secretory products of neuroendocrine cells.


Question 3
3
What other normal and malignant cells may contain similar granules?
Show answer

Neuroendocrine cells, which include C cells of the thyroid gland, islet cells of the pancreas, endocrine cells of the gastrointestinal tract, adrenal medulla cells, and the tumors that arise from them may contain similar granules.

Why is this condition regarded as a medical emergency?

Unless the compression is promptly relieved, it may lead to permanent spinal cord or nerve damage.

1
Are the bony metastases in SCLC osteoblastic or osteolytic?
Show answer

They may be of either type. While many tumors cause osteolytic metastases, the most common sources of osteoblastic metastases are SCLC and carcinomas of the prostate gland and breast.

Image 10

Single flask-shaped PEC
PECs, small cluster
Basement membrane
Bronchial lumen

Respiratory mucosa, pulmonary endocrine cells (PECs) - High power

Like mucosa in many organs, the respiratory mucosa contains neuroendocrine cells. PECs may occur singly or in small clusters (right). In the center panel, a single cell is depicted. The cell is flask shaped with its wide end resting on the basement membrane of the fetal bronchus. It sends a long process toward the bronchial lumen. Secretion of its dense-core granules is directed basally. These PECs are more numerous in fetal and neonatal life and relatively scant in normal adult lungs. They are believed to be the precursor cells of small cell lung cancer and bronchial carcinoid tumors. Exposure to carcinogens and other stimuli cause them to proliferate. In physiologic states, they secrete serotonin, calcitonin, and gastrin-releasing peptide. In tumors, they may ectopically secrete many other neuropeptides, including ACTH.

Question 1
1
What does ectopic secretion mean?
Show answer

This means secretion by a tumor cell of a product not made by the tumor cell's normal counterpart or precursor.


Question 2
2
What is a paraneoplastic syndrome? Did this patient have a paraneoplastic syndrome?
Show answer

Paraneoplastic syndromes are symptom complexes in cancer patients that cannot be explained by local or distant spread of the tumor or by elaboration of hormones indigenous to the tissue from which the tumor arose. They occur in about 10% of patients with advanced malignant disease. Small cell carcinomas may be associated with Cushing syndrome (ACTH production); other lung cancers may secrete parathyroid hormone-related protein and cause hypercalcemia.

At autopsy, metastases were found in many organs, including lung, lymph nodes, liver, adrenal glands, bone, bone marrow, and brain. At most locations, the tumor resembled the original tumor. 1
What is the significance of these findings?
Show answer

They probably indicate that the different types of lung tumors arise from a common epithelial precursor that can differentiate along multiple pathways.

Question 1
1
How can one distinguish primary pulmonary carcinomas from metastases?
Show answer

Solitary metastases may be difficult to distinguish from primary lung tumors. However, most metastases are multiple and involve both lungs and multiple lobes. Metastases are often peripheral, spheroidal, and do not grow from the respiratory epithelium.
