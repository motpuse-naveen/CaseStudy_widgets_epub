Periodontal Disease

Learning Objectives

  • 1. Understand the general histologic features of the teeth and their surrounding structures.
  • 2. Understand the changes in the periodontal structures caused by plaque and their sequelae for the integrity of the tissues that fix the teeth in their sockets.

Resources

  • 1. Pathologic Basis of Disease, 9th Ed., pp. 000–000.

Scenario

A 25-year-old woman is concerned because of gingival bleeding that even occurs after gentle toothbrushing. On inspection, the border of the gingiva is slightly swollen and red. Blood oozes from the gingival border. The teeth are covered with plaque. She is instructed to keep her teeth clean. Nevertheless, this advice is not heeded and, after several years, severe gingival retraction is observed and the upper parts of the tooth roots are exposed. Some teeth are mobile.

Question 2
2
Describe the pathophysiology of periodontal disease.
Show answer

Periodontal disease starts with inflammatory changes in the gingiva. Clinically, the gingiva is erythematous and exhibits diffuse swelling. Bleeding is easily elicited by even gentle toothbrushing. The condition is due to toxins from plaque bacteria that damage the attachment of the gingival epithelium to the tooth surface. This damage leads to inflammatory changes in the gingiva that eventually involve the bone of the alveolar socket. Destruction of this bone results in loosening and eventual loss of teeth. The initial phase that involves the gingiva only is called gingivitis. The condition is called periodontitis if the alveolar jaw bone is involved. Sometimes the inflammatory stimulus leads to exuberant formation of granulation tissue. A localized gingival swelling called an epulis may also occur. Depending on its composition, the epulis may be soft and edematous or more fibrous and firm. Generalized gingival swellings may also occur; they often are a symptom of more generalized diseases. In the case of hematopoietic neoplasms, they may be soft and edematous. If they are more fibrous, they may be idiopathic or drug related. Antiepileptic drugs, cyclosporine, and antihypertensive medications may also have these side effects.

Question 1
1
What is the mechanism that is responsible for this gingival retraction?
Show answer

Bacterial toxins from the plaque cause an inflammation of the gingiva. Dislodgment of the junctional epithelium from the enamel surface facilitates the entrance of bacteria and their metabolic products in the gingival tissues, and aggravates the inflammatory changes. Periodontal collagen fibers and bone of the alveolar socket are destroyed, and the epithelium proliferates downward in an attempt to cover the wound surface.
