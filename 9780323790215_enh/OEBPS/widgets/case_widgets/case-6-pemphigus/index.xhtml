Scenario

A 62-year-old man visits his physician with painful lesions on his soft palate. On inspection, there are multiple red areas of ulceration. The oral lesions prevent the patient from eating normally. A mouthwash is initially prescribed. The lesions do not disappear, and after a few weeks, round-to-oval vesicles and bullae also appear scattered on the patient's face, chest, and groin. A biopsy is performed, and the immunofluorescence pattern shows immunoglobulin deposition along the cell membranes of the epidermal keratinocytes in a fishnet-like pattern. The patient is subsequently treated with a combination of corticosteroids and azathioprine, resulting in disappearance of his skin lesions.

Skin, pemphigus vulgaris – Clinical presentation

The axilla shows large areas of ulceration. On the inner side of the upper arm, a few intact blisters are visible. Pemphigus vulgaris can be a life-threatening condition if untreated.

Question 1
1
What are the main complications of pemphigus vulgaris and other blistering diseases?
Show answer

Blistering diseases can be complicated by secondary infections and extreme loss of fluid. The oropharyngeal lesions in pemphigus vulgaris are particularly painful and make it difficult for patients to eat and drink.

Skin, pemphigus vulgaris – Medium power

The histology of pemphigus vulgaris shows an intraepidermal blister. The base of the blister consists of dermal collagen, covered by one layer of basal keratinocytes. The individual basal keratinocytes have separated from the upper layer of the epidermis and from each other. The separation is caused by acantholysis. A few acantholytic keratinocytes can be found in the blister.

Question 1
1
What is the difference between spongiosis and acantholysis?
Show answer

Spongiosis is caused by the presence of fluid within the intercellular spaces of the epidermis. The intercellular attachment sites (desmosomes) are not primarily affected. Only in severe spongiosis can the mechanical pressure on the desmosomes, followed by damage of the desmosomes, cause formation of intraepidermal vesicles. Acantholysis refers to the loss of attachment between the keratinocytes. This loss of attachment can be caused by autoantibodies against desmosomes, as in pemphigus, or by inherited dysfunction of desmosomal structures.

Skin, pemphigus vulgaris, immunofluorescence - High power

Direct immunofluorescence of pemphigus vulgaris shows deposition of immunoglobulin between the epidermal keratinocytes in a fishnet-like pattern.

Question 1
1
These immunoglobulins are reacting with which epidermal antigen?
Show answer

In pemphigus vulgaris, the main autoantibody reacts with desmoglein-3 present in desmosomes in the lower layers of the epidermis. This causes an intraepidermal (suprabasal) blister just above the basal layer of the epidermis.

Figure 25-28 Keratinocyte adhesion molecules and blistering inflammatory disorders. Knowledge of the proteins composing desmosomes and hemidesmosomes is key to understanding blistering disorders. Desmogleins 1 and 3 (Dsg1, Dsg3) are functionally interchangeable components of desmosomes, but have different distributions within the epidermis (left panel). The major structural proteins of desmosomes and hemidesmosomes are shown at right. In pemphigus vulgaris autoantibodies against Dsg1 and Dsg3 cause blisters in the deep suprabasal epidermis, whereas in pemphigus foliaceus the autoantibodies are against Dsg1 alone, leading to superficial, subcorneal blisters. In bullous pemphigoid autoantibodies bind BPAG2, a component of the hemidesmosomes, leading to blister formation at the level of the lamina lucida of the basement membrane. Dermatitis herpetiformis is caused by IgA autoantibodies to the fibrils that anchor hemidesmosomes to the dermis.


Question 2
2
In another clinicopathologic form of pemphigus, the antibodies react with a more superficial antigen. What is the antigen, and what is the form of pemphigus?
Show answer

In the superficial layers of the epidermis, desmoglein 1 is present. Antibodies against desmoglein 1 result in pemphigus foliaceus, which has very superficial blisters with a very thin blister roof. These lesions clinically appear as erythema and erosions; real blisters are hardly ever found because the thin blister roof is very friable.


Image 4
Tense bullae

Skin, bullous pemphigoid – Clinical presentation

In bullous pemphigoid, the primary lesions are tense bullae, often in an erythematous background. When the blisters rupture, the resulting lesions become crusted.

Question 1
1
What is the age of most patients with bullous pemphigoid?
Show answer

Patients with bullous pemphigoid are generally older individuals, 50 to 70 years old.

Bullous pemphigoid – Medium power

The histology of bullous pemphigoid shows a subepidermal blister with acantholysis. The plane of the split is between the basement membrane and the basal keratinocytes. The blister cavity and epidermis typically contain a mixed inflammatory infiltrate that includes variable numbers of eosinophils. Other causes of eosinophilic dermatitis with blister formation include insect bites, drug reactions, and allergic contact dermatitis.

Question 1
1
How can one differentiate between bullous pemphigoid and other causes of eosinophil-rich subepidermal blisters?
Show answer

The clinical history is important. Was there an insect sting bite? Does the patient use drugs that can cause bullous eruptions? The diagnosis has to be confirmed by direct and indirect immunofluorescence in which bullous pemphigoid is positive and the insect reaction and bullous drug eruption are negative.

Skin, bullous pemphigoid, immunofluorescence - High power

In bullous pemphigoid, direct immunofluorescence shows linear deposition of complement along the dermoepidermal junction. The antibodies in bullous pemphigoid are generally directed against hemidesmosomal glycoproteins within the epidermal basement membrane (BP230 and BP180).

Question 1
1
What is the difference between direct and indirect immunofluorescence?
Show answer

Direct immunofluorescence is done on a biopsy specimen of the skin of the patient. The skin is incubated with labeled complement or immunoglobulins. Indirect immunofluorescence is done with the antibody-containing serum of the patient. Control skin or other control epithelium is incubated with the patient's serum.
