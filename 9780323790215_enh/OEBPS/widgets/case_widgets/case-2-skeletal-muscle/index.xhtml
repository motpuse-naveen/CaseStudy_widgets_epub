Scenario

The parents of a 2-year-old boy were concerned about his difficulty walking and his inability to run because his 6-year-old sister had had no problems walking and running when she was 2 years old. The boy's mother was particularly worried because her mother's brother had died of a muscle disorder when he was 18. The parents took their son to his pediatrician for evaluation.

On physical examination, the pediatrician observed moderate weakness of the lower extremities without any noticeable atrophy. The child's calves were disproportionately large for their lack of strength. The child had difficulty standing from a seated position, and there was mild weakness of the upper extremities. The physician drew blood, performed a muscle biopsy, and advised the parents to return for a discussion of the results in 3 weeks.

The parents and child returned for the follow-up visit, and the pediatrician discussed the results with the parents. The blood studies revealed only an increased creatine kinase level (a muscle enzyme that is increased in muscle destruction). The muscle biopsy findings were reviewed (Images 1-3). The parents particularly wanted to know about the available therapy and prognosis. In addition, the pediatrician discussed the possibility of counseling and support groups for them.

Question 1
1
What is meant by the term myopathy?
Show answer

The term myopathy indicates a primary, or intrinsic, muscle abnormality, in contrast to the muscle changes that occur when normal innervation is interrupted (denervation atrophy). There are many different forms of myopathy, including inflammatory myopathies (e.g., polymyositis), metabolic myopathies (e.g., glycogen storage diseases), congenital myopathies, and muscular dystrophies.

Question 1
1
What is a muscular dystrophy?
Show answer

The term muscular dystrophy designates a heterogeneous group of hereditary disorders characterized by spontaneous chronic myofiber degeneration, weakness, and muscle atrophy. Although the cause of some muscular dystrophies remains obscure, it is now apparent that some muscular dystrophies are caused by mutations in genes encoding crucial structural proteins associated with muscle fiber membrane integrity or normal contractile activity, or both.


Figure 27-10 Relationship between the cell membrane (sarcolemma) and the sarcolemmal associated proteins. Dystrophin, an intracellular protein, forms an interface between the cytoskeletal proteins and a group of transmembrane proteins, the dystroglycans and the sarcoglycans. These transmembrane proteins have interactions with the extracellular matrix, including the laminin proteins. Dystrophin also interacts with dystrobrevin and the syntrophins, which form a link with neuronal type nitric oxide synthetase (nNOS) and caveolin. Mutations in dystrophin are associated with the X-linked muscular dystrophies; mutations in caveolin and the sarcoglycan proteins with the limb-girdle muscular dystrophies, which can be autosomal dominant or recessive disorders; and mutations in the α 2 -laminin (merosin) with autosomal recessive congenital muscular dystrophy.


Question 2
2
What is Duchenne muscular dystrophy?
Show answer

Duchenne muscular dystrophy is a severe form of muscular dystrophy caused by a mutation in the dystrophin gene, located on the short arm of the X chromosome. Dystrophin is a cytoplasmic protein that forms part of a protein complex involved in connecting the cytoskeleton of a muscle fiber to the extraskeletal cell membrane. Lack of dystrophin leads to increased intracellular calcium levels and ultimately cell death. Dystrophin gene mutations associated with a complete absence of dystrophin cause Duchenne muscular dystrophy, a disease that usually becomes manifest by age 5 and progresses relentlessly; patients typically become wheelchair-bound by their teens. Because dystrophin is expressed in cardiac and skeletal muscle, cardiac abnormalities (arrhythmias, congestive heart failure) are seen in some patients in addition to skeletal muscle weakness and atrophy. Most patients die of their disease by age 25 to 30, usually as a result of profound weakness, respiratory compromise, pulmonary infections, or cardiac decompensation.

Duchenne Muscular Dystrophy: When Less Is Maybe More

Duchenne muscular dystrophy (DMD) is a recessive X-linked form of striated muscle degeneration affecting roughly 1 in 3500 boys. It has an unremitting course and carries a dismal prognosis, typically with wheelchair dependency by age 12 and death by age 25 due to cardiomyopathy and respiratory complications. The fundamental defect is a mutation in the dystrophin gene that encodes a large structural protein with 79 exons. Frameshift or early-termination mutations in DMD lead to the total absence of functional dystrophin; lack of the dystrophin gives rise to a series of bimolecular changes that culminate in myocyte cell death.


Current treatment involves physical therapy and use of orthopedic appliances (e.g., braces) to maintain skeletal function for as long as possible. Anabolic corticosteroids can be a temporizing measure:They increase sarcomeric synthesis and efficiency of contraction. Eventually, however, myofiber loss supervenes. Likewise, β2-agonists increase muscle contraction and hence strength but do not affect disease progression.


Whereas total lack of dystrophin leads to DMD, the milder Becker muscular dystrophy (BMD) results from mutations that allow variable expression of smaller truncated dystrophin proteins. These mutations cause a loss of one or more exons. Thus, in BMD, a later onset of muscle changes is characteristic, with a more slowly progressive clinical disease. Recognition of this important difference between the two muscular dystrophies pointed the way to a radical exon-skipping therapeutic approach to treatment in which DMD is essentially converted to a form of BMD.


By identifying the relevant mutation in each case of DMD, site-specific antisense oligonucleotides (AONs) can be generated that induce the transcriptional apparatus to “leapfrog” over the defective exon, effectively splicing out the offending sequence. Although the resulting dystrophin is smaller than the full-length protein, it may retain much of its normal function. The AON approach has shown real promise in phase II trials, despite the need for repeated delivery of the oligonucleotides. Even better, and more remarkable, new systemically administered small-molecule agents (such as eteplirsen) are showing efficacy in inducing similar processing skips in some patients with DMD, thereby converting a potentially fatal disease to one that is milder and slowly progressive.



Question 3
3
What is Becker muscular dystrophy?
Show answer

Becker muscular dystrophy is also caused by mutation in the dystrophin gene. In contrast to Duchenne muscular dystrophy, in which dystrophin synthesis is virtually absent, patients with the Becker form of the disease are able to synthesize at least some dystrophin, usually in a truncated form. The clinical manifestations of Becker muscular dystrophy vary, but they typically are less severe than the manifestations of Duchenne muscular dystrophy, with a later onset and slower progression of symptoms. As in the case of Duchenne muscular dystrophy, cardiac abnormalities may occur in patients with Becker muscular dystrophy and sometimes dominate the clinical picture.

Question 1
1
What is polymyositis? Why is an accurate diagnosis of polymyositis critical?
Show answer

Polymyositis is a primary autoimmune inflammatory disorder of skeletal muscle. It occurs most often in adults, in whom it typically manifests with muscle weakness (usually proximal) and pain. CK levels are usually elevated, reflecting active myofiber injury, and there may be other systemic evidence of inflammation, such as an elevated erythrocyte sedimentation rate. In addition, many patients with polymyositis have anti-Jo antibodies, which are a specific serologic marker for this disorder. Polymyositis can manifest as an isolated process, but it can also be seen in association with other forms of autoimmune disease. In addition to skeletal muscle problems, patients with polymyositis may develop significant interstitial lung disease. A timely, accurate diagnosis of polymyositis is important because most cases respond well to immunosuppressive therapy.

Name one physical examination finding that would help make a diagnosis of dermatomyositis instead of polymyositis. What serious medical condition is common in patients with dermatomyositis?
Show answer

A heliotrope rash – a red or violet rash around the eyes – is particularly concerning for a diagnosis of dermatomyositis.


Up to 25% of patients with dermatomyositis also develop cancer. Common sites include cervix, lung, pancreas, breast, ovary, and gastrointestinal tract. In this situation, the dermatomyositis is considered a paraneoplastic condition associated with the underlying cancer.

This is an example of denervation atrophy, another common abnormality in muscle biopsy specimens. Denervation atrophy is characterized by the presence of variable numbers of atrophic fibers with an angular contour, sometimes occurring in groups (group atrophy). In contrast to the myopathies discussed previously, denervation atrophy is caused by loss of normal innervation in the muscle fibers rather than by an intrinsic abnormality in the muscle. Question 1
1
In addition to myopathies and denervation atrophy, what other type of disorder can be associated with muscle fiber weakness?
Show answer

Another important cause of muscle weakness is disease affecting the neuromuscular junction. The most common example of such a condition is myasthenia gravis, a disorder characterized by autoimmune-mediated injury of the acetylcholine receptors at the neuromuscular junction. Myasthenia gravis may manifest at any age, although most cases occur in adults. Thymic abnormalities, including hyperplasia and neoplasms (thymomas), are present in many patients with myasthenia gravis. The disease can be fatal if untreated owing to respiratory muscle compromise. Muscle biopsy specimens from patients with myasthenia gravis typically reveal varying degrees of disuse atrophy and occasional lymphoid aggregates but no other significant changes.
