Scenario

A 67-year-old man comes to the outpatient clinic with a lesion on his left cheek. The nodular lesion measures 12 mm at its widest diameter and has a pearly color with clearly visible blood vessels and a small crust on top of it. It has been present for 3 months, and, despite the use of ointments prescribed by the patient's family physician, it has not disappeared. Nearly every morning while the patient is washing his face, the crust comes off and the lesion starts bleeding. The family physician finally sends the patient to see a dermatologist with this question: Is this nonresolving lesion a form of skin cancer? The dermatologist suspects basal cell carcinoma and takes a small biopsy specimen from the edge of the lesion. The biopsy results confirm the diagnosis, and the patient subsequently undergoes definitive therapy.

A round, nodular, pearly tumor is present on the left cheek. The presence of dilated, subepidermal blood vessels (telangiectasias) is characteristic of basal cell carcinomas.

Question 1
1
What individuals have an increased risk for developing basal cell carcinomas?
Show answer

An increased risk for developing basal cell carcinomas is present in (1) lightly pigmented individuals with sun-damaged skin; (2) immunosuppressed patients; (3) patients with inherited defects in DNA replication or repair (e.g., xeroderma pigmentosum); and (4) patients with the nevoid basal cell carcinoma syndrome (Gorlin syndrome).


Question 2
2
What is Gorlin syndrome and what is the pathogenetic mechanism underlying tumor formation?
Show answer

Gorlin syndrome (nevoid basal cell carcinoma syndrome) is an autosomal dominant disease caused by a germline mutation in one copy of the PTCH gene, which encodes a protein important in regulating the sonic hedgehog (SHH) signaling pathway. The second hit (mutation in other PTCH gene) comes from UV ray-associated damage. Patients with this mutation develop multiple basal cell carcinomas during adolescence in addition to medulloblastomas, ovarian fibromas, odontogenic keratocysts, and other changes.


Figure 25-14 Normal and oncogenic hedgehog signaling. Left, Normally, PTCH and SMO form a receptor complex that can bind sonic hedgehog (SHH). In the absence of SHH, PTCH blocks SMO activity. When SHH binds PTCH, SMO is released to trigger a signal transduction cascade that leads to activation of GLI1 and other transcription factors. Right, Mutations in PTCH, and less often in SMO, allow SMO to signal without SHH binding and produce constitutive activation of GLI1. GLI signaling is a characteristic feature of sporadic basal cell carcinomas and tumors associated with the nevoid basal cell carcinoma (Gorlin) syndrome.

Nests of small hyperchromatic cells resembling those present in the normal basal layer of the epidermis (basaloid cells) bud off from the epidermis and infiltrate the dermis. The dermis adjacent to the tumor nodules is hypercellular and has a vague myxoid character.



Question 1
1
What are the large clefts between the tumor nests and the stroma?
Show answer

During the tissue fixation process, the stroma retracts from the tumor nests, creating artifactual clefts.


Question 2
2
What histologic significance do these clefts have?
Show answer

The presence of clefts can be very helpful in differentiating basal cell carcinomas from other adnexal tumors, such as trichoepitheliomas.

Question 1
1
What is the risk of metastasis from a basal cell carcinoma?
Show answer

Although basal cell carcinomas may invade locally, they rarely metastasize. The risk of metastasis is less than 0.01%.

Skin, basal cell carcinoma, superficial type - Clinical presentation

The superficial subtype of basal cell carcinoma can have a different presentation. They are not nodular, manifesting as erythematous plaques with a scale or crust. These lesions may also be pigmented.

Question 1
1
What is the main clinical differential diagnosis of superficial basal cell carcinoma?
Show answer

The clinical differential diagnoses of superficial basal cell carcinoma include eczematous dermatitis, Bowen's disease, fungal infection, and, if the lesion is pigmented, malignant melanoma.

Skin, basal cell carcinoma, superficial type – Low power

In a superficial basal cell carcinoma, the tumor nests are much smaller than in a nodular basal cell carcinoma. The nests are also more superficial and connect with the surface epithelium. Superficial basal cell carcinomas are thought to be less aggressive than their nodular counterparts.

Question 1
1
What is the typical growth pattern of a superficial basal cell carcinoma?
Show answer

A superficial basal cell carcinoma often has a multifocal growth pattern with the tumor extending over several square centimeters of skin surface.

Skin, actinic keratosis – Clinical presentation

This image shows sun-damaged skin on the balding scalp of an elderly man. The tan-brown, poorly defined lesion with a scaly crust is actinic keratosis. The most important factor in the pathogenesis of these common lesions is chronic sun exposure.

Question 1
1
What other factors may induce actinic keratosis?
Show answer

Ionizing radiation, hydrocarbons, and arsenicals may also induce actinic keratosis.

Question 1
1
What is the natural history of actinic keratosis?
Show answer

Although actinic keratoses are considered premalignant lesions, some cases spontaneously regress if left untreated. Many cases remain stable, and a few cases progress to invasive squamous cell carcinoma.

Scalp, squamous cell carcinoma – Clinical presentation

This large actinic keratosis is present on the balding scalp of an elderly man. In the center, a nonhealing ulcer is present, consistent with superimposed squamous cell carcinoma.

Question 1
1
Why are immunosuppressed patients at high risk for developing squamous cell carcinomas of the skin?
Show answer

Immunosuppressed patients have an increased incidence of squamous cell carcinoma, basal cell carcinoma, and melanoma. Decreased immunosurveillance of aberrant antigen expression reduces the ability to identify and counteract premalignant changes. In addition, many immunosuppressive drugs used in chemotherapy and other conditions are directly carcinogenic. Finally, in the case of squamous cell carcinoma, immunosuppressed patients have decreased protection against human papillomavirus, a causative agent in many squamous cell carcinomas of the skin.

Question 1
1
What other skin tumor is characterized by well-differentiated but atypical squamous epithelium and is surrounded by an inflammatory infiltrate?
Show answer

Histologically, a keratoacanthoma can be very hard to distinguish from a well-differentiated squamous cell carcinoma. Among other things, the clinical history can be particularly useful. Keratoacanthomas typically have a natural course characterized by rapid growth and spontaneous regression.
