Scenario

A 52-year-old woman presents with the chief complaint of a 6-month history of progressive muscle weakness and a skin rash. In particular, she has noticed increased difficulty in climbing stairs and getting out of a chair. She has also had more difficulty raising items to high shelves, and lately she has even had problems keeping her arms raised while combing her hair. Symptoms have not been accompanied by myalgias. In addition, she has noted a 10-lb weight loss.Physical examination is remarkable for a diffuse purple/red discoloration of the skin over her cheeks, nose, and eyelids and extending to her chest. Examination also confirms the proximal muscle weakness.

Laboratory findings show an increase in the following: creatine kinase, AST, and ALT. Myoglobin is positive in the urine. Serology shows no anti-DNA antibodies, but it is positive for anti–Jo-1 antibodies.

Question 1
1
In the context of proximal muscle weakness, how does the rash contribute to making a clinical diagnosis?

The presence of a rash with proximal muscle weakness suggests a diagnosis of dermatomyositis.



Question 2
2
What if only the muscle weakness were present?

The diagnosis would be polymyositis. These two entities are related, and they are grouped under inflammatory myopathies


Image 2


Skeletal muscle, polymyositis - Medium power

Note the disruption of the muscle fascicle, inflammatory infiltrate, and muscle necrosis. The process is a combination of damage to skeletal muscle fibers and an inflammatory infiltrate. Consider the components of the inflammatory infiltrate and the major cause of damage to the skeletal muscle.

Question 1
1
What type of hypersensitivity does this represent?

Polymyositis is believed to result from cytotoxic (CD8+) T cell–mediated injury to muscle cells.


Question 2
2
How would the histopathology differ in dermatomyositis?

In dermatomyositis, there is primary damage of small blood vessels rather than muscle cells. Typically, a few layers of atrophic muscle fibers are present at the periphery of the fascicles.


Question 3
3
What is the mechanism of damage in dermatomyositis?

It is believed to be mediated by antibodies that damage small blood vessels in the muscles.


Question 4
4
What is the typical clinical presentation of polymyositis? Which muscle groups are affected first?

The proximal large muscles are affected first. These include muscles around the shoulder and pelvis. The involvement is bilateral.


Question 5
5
Is there an autoantibody that is specific for this condition?

Yes. The Jo-1 antibody, present in 25% of patients, is specific for inflammatory myopathies


Question 6
6
What biochemical evidence of muscle damage may be detected by a blood test?

Release of muscle enzymes such as creatine kinase causes elevated levels in the blood.
