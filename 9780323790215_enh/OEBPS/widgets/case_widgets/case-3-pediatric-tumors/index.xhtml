Scenario

A 19-month-old boy with 2 days of abdominal pain was found to have a palpable mass in the left upper quadrant of his abdomen. Additionally, his mother had noted that he had not been gaining weight lately. Physical examination revealed a well-developed, thin boy in no acute distress. He was afebrile with normal vital signs. Mild pitting edema of his feet was noted. He was neurologically intact. A complete blood count and differential, serum chemistries, and urinalysis were normal for age.

A sonogram and a computed tomography scan showed a solid left upper quadrant abdominal mass. The left adrenal gland was unrecognizable, and the radiologist believed that this was the most likely source of the growth, although a mass arising in the upper pole of the left kidney could not be excluded. Other abdominal organs were unremarkable. A skeletal survey and bone scan were negative. A left iliac bone marrow aspiration and biopsy specimen showed no metastatic tumor. Urine was sent for tests for vanillylmandelic acid, which was 121 mg/g creatinine (reference range for age, 0–19 mg/g), and for homovanillic acid, which was 97 mg/g creatinine (reference range for age, 0–9 mg/g).

Ultimately, laparotomy revealed a 10-cm tumor, apparently replacing the left adrenal gland and upper pole of the left kidney. Because no plane of dissection was visible between the kidney and the mass, the surgeon performed a left nephroureterectomy with the tumor resection. One renal hilar lymph node and three para-aortic nodes were also biopsied.

The surgical pathologist bisected the 554-g mass in a coronal plane, revealing a hemorrhagic tumor distinct from the upper pole of the kidney; the adrenal gland was not visualized (see Image 1). Tumor grew down the renal and adrenal veins. Tumor was present at the margin of resection. Slides showed a monotonous small round cell tumor with little differentiation except for an occasional rosette (see Image 2). Histochemical markers for cells of neuroendocrine origin were positive. Samples of fresh tumor tissue for cytogenetic analysis showed 46,XY,del(1)(p36) and double minutes (see Image 3). Studies of the tumor revealed that the N-Myc proto-oncogene was highly amplified (>100 copies). The lymph nodes were normal.

The child was treated on a high-intensity chemotherapy protocol and has had no recurrence of disease during 3 years of follow-up.

Question 2
2
What is the explanation of the interest in vanillylmandelic acid (VMA) and homovanillic acid (HVA) in this case?
Show answer

VMA and HVA are metabolites in the synthesis of epinephrine, and are often elevated in the urine of patients with neuroblastoma, ganglioneuroblastoma, ganglioneuroma, and pheochromocytoma. About 90% of neuroblastomas produce catecholamines and have detectable VMA or HMA or both in the urine. These metabolites may be used to confirm a diagnosis or to follow a patient for recurrence of disease after initial resection. In this case, VMA and HVA were elevated. Some poorly differentiated neuroblastomas have normal values. Absence of VMA production is an indicator of poor prognosis, but probably is not independent of other markers of poor differentiation, such as histologic immaturity.

Which pediatric tumors show a small round blue cell morphology?
Show answer

Lymphoma, Ewing sarcoma/primitive neuroectodermal tumor, neuroblastoma, rhabdomyosarcoma, and retinoblastoma show a small round blue cell morphology.


Question 2
2
What is the relationship of histology and prognosis in this tumor?
Show answer

Neuroblastoma is the prototype for relating pediatric tumor histology, specifically degree of differentiation, to tumor behavior. The case presented here represents the poorly differentiated end of the spectrum. When mature Schwann cells predominate over differentiating neuroblastic nests, the tumor is termed a ganglioneuroblastoma, and when the tumor is composed solely of mature Schwann cells and mature ganglion cells, it is termed a ganglioneuroma. Although the immature neuroblastoma is highly malignant, the completely mature ganglioneuroma is benign. Differentiating neuroblastomas and ganglioneuroblastomas exhibit intermediate behavior.

Question 3
3
Why was the high-intensity chemotherapy protocol chosen for this patient?
Show answer

This child has many poor prognostic indicators, including (1) age greater than 18 months old, (2) a poorly differentiated tumor with high mitotic activity, (3) cytogenetic evidence of deletion of part of the short arm of chromosome 1, and (4) N-Myc amplification. Although he was technically of low stage (stage II), his oncologists were worried by the presence of residual microscopic disease in the renal vein. Consequently, they elected to treat him on a higher intensity protocol.

Question 1
1
Describe the pathogenetics of retinoblastoma (RB).
Show answer

Two mutations of the RB locus on chromosome 13q14 lead to neoplastic proliferation of the retinal cells. In the sporadic form, both RB mutations in the tumor-founding retinal cell are acquired. In the familial form, all somatic cells inherit one mutated copy of the RB gene from a carrier parent, and as a result only one additional RB mutation in a retinal cell is required for complete loss of RB function.

Question 1
1
Describe the role of retinoblastoma (RB) in regulating the cell cycle.
Show answer

Hypophosphorylated RB in complex with the E2F transcription factors binds to DNA, recruits chromatin-remodeling factors, and inhibits transcription of genes whose products are required for the S phase of the cell cycle. When RB is phosphorylated by the cyclin D and E complexes, it releases E2F. The latter then activates transcription of S-phase genes. The phosphorylation of RB is inhibited by cyclin-dependent kinase inhibitors, because they inactivate cyclin–cyclin-dependent kinase (CDK) complexes. Virtually all cancer cells show dysregulation of the G1-S checkpoint as a result of mutation in one of four genes that regulate the phosphorylation of RB; these genes are RB1, CDK4, the genes encoding cyclin D proteins, and CDKN2A (p16).
