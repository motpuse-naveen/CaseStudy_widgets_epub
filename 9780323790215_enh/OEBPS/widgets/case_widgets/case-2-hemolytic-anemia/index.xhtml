Learning Objectives

Hemolytic Anemias: General

  • 1. Name the three general types of intrinsic erythrocyte abnormalities that lead to increased hemolysis.
  • 2. Name the commonly seen extrinsic abnormalities that lead to premature red blood cell destruction.
  • 3. Compare and contrast extravascular and intravascular hemolysis.
  • 4. Know the feature that the red blood cells of the various primarily extravascular hemolytic anemias have in common. Understand the role of the spleen in the hemolytic process.
  • 5. List the laboratory tests that are indicative of increased red blood cell destruction and increased red blood cell production. Know why the serum haptoglobin level decreases in hemolytic states.
  • 6. Describe the peripheral blood and bone marrow changes seen in response to excessive red blood cell destruction.

Hemolytic Anemias Due to Intrinsic Red Cell Defects: Disorders of Hemoglobin

  • 1. List and distinguish the two main categories of hemoglobin abnormalities (hemoglobinopathies and thalassemias) that often lead to clinical problems.
  • 2. List two common hemoglobinopathies associated with an amino acid substitution.
  • 3. Understand the role of hemoglobin electrophoresis in the diagnosis of hemoglobinopathies.
  • 4. Describe the natural history of individuals with HbSS.
  • 5. Distinguish between reversibly and irreversibly sickled cells.
  • 6. List and explain the postulated mechanism of the three most commonly seen complications in sickle cell anemia patients.
  • 7. List the variables that affect sickling, and describe how these variables may explain the different clinical expression of disease in the following situations:
    • a. heterozygous HbAS
    • b. homozygous HbSS
    • c. newborn homozygous HbSS
    • d. double heterozygous HbSC
  • 8. Explain briefly the classification, clinical behavior, and the type of genetic defect in:
    • a. α -thalassemia
    • b. β-thalassemia
  • 9. Understand the mechanism of anemia in severe ß-thalassemia.
  • 10. List the long-term complications of ß-thalassemia major.
  • 11. Describe the findings in the peripheral blood smear in homozygous ß-thalassemia and in thalassemia minor.

Hemolytic Anemias Due to Intrinsic Red Cell Defects: Membrane and Enzyme Defects

  • 1. Describe common hematologic findings, the inheritance pattern, and therapeutic options for hereditary spherocytosis (HS).
  • 2. Describe how spherocytes form in HS. Understand the role of the spleen in HS.
  • 3. Understand the basis of the osmotic fragility test used to diagnose HS.
  • 4. Know the most common red cell enzyme deficiency associated with hemolytic anemia. Describe a common clinical scenario for hemolysis related to G6PD deficiency.
  • 5. Understand the mechanism of hemolysis in G6PD deficiency.

Hemolytic Anemias Due to Extrinsic Red Cell Defects

  • 1. Compare and contrast warm antibody and cold agglutinin hemolytic anemias with respect to mechanisms of red cell destruction and predisposing causes. Describe the Coombs test.

  • 3. Explain the mechanism of hemolysis in infectious diseases. List three infections that cause hemolysis.

Scenario

The patient is a 15-year-old African American boy who presents to the emergency department with severe abdominal pain. His mother informs you that these painful episodes began when he was about 1 year old. She also tells you that the patient, his brother, her husband, and she have been told they have the “sickle gene.” However, only her husband has experienced similar painful episodes (pain crises).

Physical examination reveals a soft abdomen and intact bowel sounds. The patient’s pulse is rapid, and his blood pressure is 125/90 mm Hg. His temperature is 98.8° F. CBC shows the following:

Hb 8 g/dL
WBC 13,500/mm3
Plt 350,000/mm3

Microscopic examination of the peripheral blood smear reveals red blood cell changes as seen in Image 1; in addition, there is significant polychromasia. Most of the white blood cells are neutrophils (segmented forms and bands).

Question 1
1
How are hemolytic anemias classified?

Hemolytic anemias are classified as intrinsic (intracorpuscular) versus extrinsic (extracorpuscular).


Question 2
2
How are the morphologic abnormalities in the red blood cells seen here related to polychromasia and this patient’s abdominal pain?

Red blood cells in this smear are sickled and are prone to undergo hemolysis in the spleen; in addition, patients with sickle cell anemia have episodes of vascular occlusion. The hemolysis prompts the bone marrow to rev up production of red cell precursors, resulting in reticulocytosis and polychromasia. The vascular occlusions cause painful episodes (crises).


Question 3
3
What is the pathophysiology of vaso-occlusive crises in these patients?

The pathophysiology of vascular occlusions and resulting organ damage is not fully understood. It is thought that sickle Hb causes red cell membrane damage. Such cells, even if not sickled, have increased stickiness to endothelium and tend to retard blood flow in the microcirculation. In the sluggish blood flow, there is greater deoxygenation of HbS, and sickling is facilitated. The abnormal cells accumulate and obstruct blood flow.


Figure 14-7 Pathophysiology of sickle cell disease.


Question 4
4
Why did this patient have no noticeable clinical problems before age 1?

High levels of HbF present in red blood cells during infancy inhibit polymerization of HbS.


Question 5
5
Why does his father have abdominal crises, but his mother and brother do not?

His mother and brother have HbAS (i.e., they have sickle cell trait). His father probably has HbSS too.


Question 6
6
What is the correlation between the severity of the patient’s anemia and the number of irreversibly sickled cells (ISCs)? Why?

ISC number correlates with hemolysis because ISCs undergo hemolysis in the spleen owing to decreased deformability. In this and other hemolytic disorders characterized by decreased red cell deformability, the abnormal red blood cells have reduced ability to traverse the narrow slits between the splenic cords and sinuses. They are sequestered in the spleen and ultimately destroyed.


Question 7
7
How is the severity of clinical manifestations affected by the red blood cell concentration of HbS and by the presence of other types of hemoglobins?

Many factors affect the severity of sickle cell disease, including intracorpuscular concentration of HbS. Homozygotes with HbSS have nearly 100% HbS and full-blown sickle cell disease; heterozygotes with HbAS have a condition called sickle cell trait, which is virtually asymptomatic and causes no hematologic abnormalities. Increased hemoglobin F in HbSS disease inhibits the polymerization of HbS by decreasing its concentration within the cell. The situation in HbSC disease (inheritance of an S and a C gene) is more complex. It is commonly stated that HbC copolymerizes with HbS. Experimental evidence suggests, however, that HbC does not interact with HbS to any greater extent than HbA. The sickling tendency in these patients is attributed to cellular dehydration caused by HbC. HbSC is generally a less severe disease than HbSS disease, but it is more severe than S trait.

Question 1
1
What are the major categories of intrinsic red blood cell abnormalities?

The categories are hemoglobin disorders, membrane disorders, and enzyme deficiencies.


Question 2
2
How are ISCs formed?

ISCs are the result of permanent membrane damage related to multiple bouts of polymerization-depolymerization of intracellular hemoglobin S.



Question 3
3
What is the cause of decreased red cell survival in HbC disease?

The red blood cells in HbC disease are dehydrated because of abnormalities of ion flux across the membranes. Dehydration leads to increased rigidity and, ultimately, removal by the spleen.

Hemoglobin electrophoresis on agarose gel

Hemoglobin electrophoresis is a commonly used laboratory test for qualitative evaluation of hemoglobinopathies. Hemoglobin molecules in an alkaline solution have a net negative charge and move toward the anode in an electrophoretic system. Differences in the charges of various types of hemoglobin result in different mobilities, causing their separation. Lanes 1 and 2 are control lanes. Lane 1 has adult (HbA) and fetal (HbF) hemoglobins, whereas lane 2 has HbA, HbS, and HbC. Lanes 5 and 7 are normal individuals containing only HbA. Lanes 3 and 8 are from patients with sickle cell trait, showing HbA and HbS. Lane 6 is a case of sickle cell disease with total absence of HbA; HbS is the predominant hemoglobin, with HbF making up the rest. Lane 4 is an individual with HbC disease who has only HbC.

Blood smears, polychromasia and reticulocytes - High power

The diagnosis of hemolysis requires demonstration of increased red blood cell destruction associated with evidence of increased marrow red blood cell production. One way to document increased red blood cell production is by assessment of the reticulocyte count. The Wright-stained smear on the left shows polychromatophilic red blood cells in the peripheral blood. (The term polychromasia refers to the bluish tinge of these cells.) These red blood cells have been recently released from the bone marrow and still contain significant amounts of RNA, which is the reason for the bluish tinge. (RNA stains blue with the Wright stain.) On the right is a reticulocyte stain. In this procedure, RNA is precipitated inside the erythrocytes. Because precipitated RNA gives the reticulated appearance shown, the cell is called a reticulocyte. Although all polychromatophilic cells are reticulocytes, not all reticulocytes are polychromatophilic. Only the younger reticulocytes appear overtly polychromatophilic. These cells remain as reticulocytes in circulation for about 24 hours. Because they have been recently released from the marrow, the number of circulating reticulocytes reflects the ability of the marrow to respond to the challenges of anemia. If an individual is anemic and has an elevated reticulocyte count, the likely explanation for the anemia is hemolysis; the red blood cells are being destroyed outside the bone marrow.

Question 1
1
What does an anemia with a low reticulocyte count signify?

Anemia with a low reticulocyte response signifies a production problem within the marrow.


Question 2
2
What laboratory test results may be used to document increased red blood cell destruction?

Increased serum indirect bilirubin, increased serum lactate dehydrogenase, and decreased serum haptoglobin may be used to document increased red blood cell destruction.

Target cells

Blood smear, β-thalassemia major - High power

β-thalassemia major results from absence or near-absence of beta chain production as a result of homozygous mutations in both beta globin genes. This disorder is characterized by profound transfusion-dependent anemia. In adequately transfused patients, there is normal early development, but normal secondary sexual development does not occur because of endocrine dysfunction resulting from iron overload. These patients commonly die in early adulthood from cardiac dysfunction related to iron deposition in the myocardium. Inadequately transfused β-thalassemia major causes a wide variety of severe symptoms, including stunted growth, wasting, and various bony abnormalities and fractures (owing to massive compensatory marrow expansion). These patients die in childhood. Typically, the blood smear in these patients shows profound anisopoikilocytosis, including hypochromasia and numerous target cells, and erythroblastosis.

Figure 14-12 Pathogenesis of β -thalassemia major. Note that the aggregates of unpaired α -globin chains, a hallmark of the disease, are not visible in routinely stained blood smears. Blood transfusions are a double-edged sword, diminishing the anemia and its attendant complications, but also adding to the systemic iron overload.

Question 1
1
How do thalassemias differ from structural hemoglobin disorders?

Thalassemias result from underproduction of normal globin chains, whereas structural hemoglobin disorders result from relatively normal production of abnormal globin chains. In other words, thalassemia is a quantitative disorder of hemoglobin production, while structural hemoglobin disorders represent a qualitiative disorder of hemoglobin production. States in which alpha globin chains are underproduced are called α-thalassemias, whereas states with underproduction of beta globin chains are called β-thalassemias.


Question 2
2
What is the pathogenesis of the anemia in the thalassemic states?

Although underproduction of hemoglobin contributes to the microcytosis and hypochromia seen in these disorders, the degree of anemia is directly related to the degree of imbalance between the production of alpha and beta globin chains. The excess, normally produced globin chains (either alpha or beta, depending on the type of thalassemia) precipitate in the red blood cells and cause a shortened red blood cell life span. The thalassemias are a form of hemolytic disorder.

Blood smear, ß-thalassemia minor (trait) - High power

At the opposite end of the spectrum from β-thalassemia major is β-thalassemia minor, resulting from heterozygous mutation in one beta globin gene. This is a mild, asymptomatic, hypochromic, microcytic anemia. The red blood cells in this image are mildly microcytic and hypochromic. They exhibit mild anisocytosis and some poikilocytosis. The largest cell in the center of the field has numerous basophilic inclusions; this is referred to as basophilic stippling, which is thought to represent aggregates of polyribosomes. This feature is characteristically seen in β-thalassemia minor, but it may also be seen in other types of anemias. β-thalassemias intermediate in severity between thalassemia major and thalassemia minor are appropriately termed thalassemia intermedia.

Question 1
1
How does one distinguish β-thalassemia minor from iron deficiency anemia?

Hemoglobin A2 is elevated in β-thalassemia minor (trait) and normal in the other microcytic anemias.

Blood smear, hereditary spherocytosis - High power

This image shows reticulocytes (largest cells with slightly grayish color), normal mature red blood cells (intermediate-sized cells with an area of central pallor), and spherocytes (smallest cells, which stain darkly and lack central pallor). Spherocytes result from membrane damage owing to either a problem extrinsic to the red blood cells or an abnormality intrinsic to the red blood cells. Hereditary spherocytosis, which is shown in this image, is caused by a genetic defect in the red blood cell membrane. An abnormality of membrane skeletal proteins causes progressive loss of membrane, making the red blood cells less deformable. The red blood cells get trapped within the cords of the splenic red pulp and are destroyed by the splenic macrophages. This is an example of extravascular hemolysis. Spherocytes are not unique to hereditary spherocytosis and are seen in other hemolytic states, particularly in immune hemolysis.

Question 1
1
What are the inheritance pattern, red blood cell defect, mechanism of hemolysis, and treatment in this disorder?

This disease is caused by genetic defects (most commonly autosomal dominant) in the red blood cell membrane cytoskeleton. A variety of mutations may be present, but mutations in ankyrin, band3 and spectrin are some of the most common. Abnormal red blood cells get trapped within the cords of the splenic red pulp and are destroyed by macrophages. Transfusions, splenectomy, or both are sometimes necessary.

Figure 14-2 Role of the red cell membrane skeleton in hereditary spherocytosis. The left panel shows the normal organization of the major red cell membrane skeletal proteins. Various mutations involving α -spectrin, β -spectrin, ankyrin, band 4.2, or band 3 that weaken the interactions between these proteins cause red cells to lose membrane fragments. To accommodate the resultant change in the ratio of surface area to volume these cells adopt a spherical shape. Spherocytic cells are less deformable than normal ones and therefore become trapped in the splenic cords, where they are phagocytosed by macrophages. GP, Glycophorin.

Blood smear, G6PD deficiency - High power

This hemolytic state is related to a different intrinsic red blood cell defect; specifically, an enzyme deficiency. This image shows a blood smear from a patient with G6PD deficiency who has been challenged by an oxidant stress. The abnormal red blood cell near the center of the image has the appearance of an apple core and is sometimes referred to as a bite cell. The pathophysiology of G6PD deficiency involves precipitation of denatured hemoglobin in the form of Heinz bodies. These precipitates are removed by splenic macrophages, causing the bite cell configuration observed on this blood smear. These pitted cells are prone to leak hemoglobin in the circulation, which explains the hemoglobinuria, a hallmark of intravascular hemolysis, noted in these patients. Eventual removal of these red blood cells from the circulation by macrophages in the spleen, indicating an extravascular hemolytic component, contributes to the red blood cell destruction in these cases. The second feature shows a special Heinz body stain in a patient with a severe form of G6PD deficiency after an oxidant challenge. The Heinz bodies are the dark inclusions at the periphery of the red blood cells. A special stain is required because the routine Wright stain used on blood smears does not stain Heinz bodies.

Question 1
1
What is the etiology of G6PD deficiency?

G6PD deficiency is an X-linked recessive trait commonly seen in African American men (10%) that results from an abnormal form of the enzyme glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase, which is responsible for producing nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate (NADPH) to reduce glutathione and prevent cellular oxidative damage. This deficiency results in episodic hemolysis during times of oxidative stress (e.g., infections, sulpha drugs, fava beans).


Figure 14-5 Role of glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase (G6PD) in defense against oxidant injury. The disposal of H 2 O 2 , a potential oxidant, is dependent on the adequacy of reduced glutathione (GSH), which is generated by the action of the reduced form of nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NADPH). The synthesis of NADPH is dependent on the activity of G6PD. GSSG, Oxidized glutathione.

Blood smear, immune hemolysis, warm antibody type - Medium power

An example of an extrinsic insult causing a hemolytic state is immune-mediated hemolysis. In this case, the blood smear reveals mainly spherocytes and polychromatophilic cells. Spherocytes in this condition are formed as a result of partial loss of the red blood cell membrane caused by attempted phagocytosis of immunoglobulin-coated red blood cells. The rigid spherocytes are removed in the spleen (i.e., extravascular hemolysis). So-called warm antibodies, which may be autoantibodies or alloantibodies, are responsible. (The terms cold and warm when referring to antibodies relate to the temperature at which the antibody binds most avidly to the red blood cell antigen.)

Question 1
1
What are causes of warm (auto)antibody hemolytic anemias?

Causes are primary (idiopathic) or secondary (lymphomas, leukemias, cancers, autoimmune disorders, drugs).




Question 2
2
What is the isotype of the antibody in such cases?

The isotype of the antibody is IgG.



Image 11

Red cell agglutination

Blood smear, hemolytic anemia, cold agglutinin type - Medium power

The presence of a cold agglutinin antibody is another example of an extrinsic red blood cell insult resulting in immune-mediated hemolysis. The blood smear in these cases often shows characteristic clumping of the red blood cells, as shown here. In contrast to warm antibodies described in the previous Image 10, cold agglutinin antibodies are always autoantibodies. The antibody is most often IgM.

Question 1
1
What is the cause of hemolysis in this process?

The in vivo agglutination property of these antibodies is not the cause of the hemolysis; rather, their ability to fix complement on the red blood cell surface at relatively low temperatures results in intravascular hemolysis. In addition, at 37° C, IgM antibody is released from the cell surface, leaving a coating of C3b. This is an opsonin, and such opsonized red blood cells are destroyed by monocyte-macrophages. There is extravascular hemolysis as well.

What is the most common cause of infection-induced hemolytic anemia?

Question 2
2
