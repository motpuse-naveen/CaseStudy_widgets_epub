Scenario

The patient is a 72-year-old white man with a history of homelessness, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, chronic alcohol abuse, chronic dementia, and multiple episodes of upper gastrointestinal (GI) bleeding. He was admitted to the hospital with complaints of lightheadedness, syncope, and abdominal pain. The patient was a thin elderly man in no acute distress. On admission, his blood pressure was 96/72 mm Hg, pulse was 104 beats/min, respiratory rate was 24/min, and temperature was 98.9° F. His conjunctivae were pale. The chest and heart examination were unremarkable. The abdominal examination revealed mild epigastric pain on palpation. The rectal and prostate examinations were unremarkable; however, black stool, which tested positive for fecal blood, was present. The remainder of the physical examination was unremarkable. On admission, the following laboratory results were noted:

Test Result
Hemoglobin 4.1 gm/dL
Hematocrit 12.9%
Albumin 2.6 gm/dL
WBC 11,900/mm3
Ethanol <10 mg/dL (normal: <10mg/dL)

An ECG was unremarkable; likewise, chest x-ray and x-rays of kidneys, ureters, and bladder (KUB film) were unremarkable.

The patient was admitted to the medical intensive care unit, where a nasogastric tube lavage produced coffee-ground gastric contents that tested positive for blood. He was transfused with 6 U of packed RBCs, which increased his hematocrit to 38% (normal 40% to 52%). An upper GI tract endoscopy was performed, which showed a large (5 x 5 cm) gastric ulcer in the antrum along the lesser curvature. Biopsy specimens were taken of the ulcers and surrounding mucosa. The biopsy specimens showed necrotic debris consistent with an ulcer, and the surrounding mucosa showed acute and chronic inflammation and organisms consistent with Helicobacter pylori. There was no evidence of malignancy. The patient was treated with acid-suppressive therapy and antibiotics and was discharged 5 days after admission. He was given instructions not to take aspirin or drink alcohol.

The patient was readmitted 2 weeks later with a history of abdominal pain, weight loss, constipation, and fever. Laboratory data revealed a urinary tract infection resulting from Klebsiella, which was treated with antibiotic therapy. His hemoglobin was 8.2 g/dL, and his hematocrit was 27.5%. He was transfused with 2 U of packed RBCs and discharged 1 week later on antibiotics and acid-suppressive therapy. He was readmitted for two additional episodes of GI bleeding. Over a 2-year period, he received a total of 20 U of packed RBCs. He was taken to surgery for a vagotomy and antrectomy with Billroth II reconstruction. He was discharged to a nursing home 2 weeks after surgery.

Question 1
1
What is the relationship between chronic gastritis, H. pylori, and chronic peptic ulcer?
Show answer

Chronic infection, especially with H. pylori, represents the most common form of chronic gastritis. The mucosa shows an intense acute and chronic inflammatory infiltrate and progressive atrophy of the mucosal glands. Patients secrete excess acid and have a low intragastric pH. H. pylori is also associated with gastric and duodenal ulcer disease and with gastric cancer. H. pylori infection of gastric mucosa is present in nearly 100% of patients with duodenal ulcer and 70% of patients with gastric ulcer. H. pylori infection is a major factor in the pathogenesis of peptic ulcer. H. pylori induces an intense inflammatory and immune response and increased production of proinflammatory cytokines. H. pylori secretes a urease that breaks down urea to form toxic compounds such as ammonium chloride and monochloramine. Thrombotic occlusion of surface capillaries is promoted by a bacterial platelet-activating factor. Other antigens, including lipopolysaccharides, recruit inflammatory cells to the mucosa. The chronically inflamed mucosa is more susceptible to acid-peptic injury and peptic ulceration. In addition, chronic inflammation of the mucosa is possibly important in the pathogenesis of gastric carcinoma and a low-grade gastric lymphoma, also known as MALToma (MALT = mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue).

Question 1
1
What are the complications of chronic peptic ulcers?
Show answer

The complications of peptic ulcer disease are (1) bleeding, (2) perforation, (3) penetration into an adjacent viscus, (4) obstruction from edema or from scarring of the pylorus or duodenum, and (5) intractable pain. Malignant transformation does not occur in duodenal ulcers and is extremely rare in gastric ulcers.

Image 7

Acute ulcers
Normal stomach

Stomach, acute gastric ulcers - Gross, mucosal surface

Acute ulcers are multiple, small, circular mucosal defects that typically measure less than 1 cm. The ulcer base is stained dark brown by acid digestion of extravasated blood. In an ulcer, necrosis involves the entire thickness of the mucosa. When the necrosis involves only the superficial mucosa, the lesion is an erosion. Acute gastric ulcers are usually not associated with fibrosis, which characterizes a chronic peptic ulcer. Acute, or stress, ulcers are most commonly encountered in patients with shock, burns, sepsis, intracranial conditions, and aspirin use. About 1% to 4% of patients admitted to hospital intensive care units acutely develop superficial gastric erosion or ulcers, which may be associated with lethal bleeding. The most important determinant of clinical outcome is the ability to correct the underlying condition.

Bowel infarction is an uncommon but serious disorder that has a 50% to 75% death rate because the time between onset of symptoms, sepsis, and perforation is short. Older individuals are vulnerable because of preexisting cardiac and vascular disease and adhesions from previous abdominal surgery. Bowel infarction is an important cause of an acute abdomen.

Question 1
1
What are the causes of bowel infarction?
Show answer

Bowel infarction results from ischemia of the intestines, caused either by mechanical obstruction to blood flow (e.g., arterial thrombosis or embolism from the heart, volvulus, stricture) or by reduced perfusion (e.g., cardiac failure, shock).


Question 2
2
What is a “watershed area,” and what are the watershed areas of the colon?
Show answer

Watershed areas are the anatomic zones at the end of their respective vascular supplies that are therefore especially susceptible to ischemia. The major watershed area in the colon is the splenic flexure, a major site for ischemic damage. The sigmoid colon and rectum are also fairly susceptible to ischemia.

Why does the area of infarction appear hemorrhagic?
Show answer

The area of infarction appears hemorrhagic either because of reperfusion into the damaged area after resolution of the obstruction (“reperfusion injury”) or from the extensively anastomosing branches of the mesenteric arteries.

Question 1
1
What are the clinical features of carcinoid syndrome?
Show answer

Carcinoid syndrome results largely from the synthesis and secretion of serotonin (5-hydroxytryptamine) by the tumor cells. The syndrome occurs in 1% of all patients with carcinoid tumors and 20% of patients with widespread metastases. The symptoms are (1) vasomotor disturbances (flushing of skin); (2) intestinal hypermotility (diarrhea); (3) asthmatic bronchoconstrictive attacks (cough, wheezing); (4) systemic fibrosis (in the heart, pulmonary and tricuspid valve stenosis; endocardial fibrosis in the right ventricle; retroperitoneal and pelvic fibrosis); and (5) hepatomegaly (owing to metastases).

Question 1
1
What other endocrine syndromes may occur with carcinoid tumors?
Show answer

Because of their neuroendocrine origin, carcinoids may elaborate various hormones, including gastrin, insulin, and adrenocoticotropic hormone. The corresponding syndromes are Zollinger-Ellison syndrome, insulinoma, and Cushing syndrome. Carcinoids can also occur in Multiple Endocrine Neoplasia Syndrome Type I (MENI ).
